P vs NP

Research guide. Complexity classes P and NP, NP-completeness, the Cook–Levin theorem, and the implications of resolving this Clay Millennium Problem.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Computational models and complexity classes

Complexity theory classifies decision problems by the resources needed to solve them. A decision problem is a language \(L \subseteq \{0,1\}^*\); the answer is "yes" or "no" for each input \(x\).

P (polynomial time) is the class of problems solvable by a deterministic Turing machine in time polynomial in the input length \(|x|\). NP (nondeterministic polynomial time) is the class of problems for which a "yes" answer has a certificate verifiable in polynomial time.

Formal definition of NP: \(L \in \mathrm{NP}\) if there exists a polynomial \(p\) and a polynomial-time verifier \(V\) such that: \[ x \in L \iff \exists\, w \text{ with } |w| \leq p(|x|) \text{ and } V(x, w) = 1. \] The witness \(w\) is the certificate.

Prerequisite: discrete mathematics, basic algorithms, and familiarity with Turing machines. Related: Boolean Algebra, Karnaugh Mapping.

2) The P vs NP question

Clearly \(\mathrm{P} \subseteq \mathrm{NP}\): if we can solve a problem in polynomial time, we can verify by solving and comparing. The central question:

P vs NP: Does \(\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}\)? Equivalently: is every problem whose solution can be verified quickly also solvable quickly?

Most researchers believe \(\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}\), but no proof is known in either direction. A proof would be among the deepest results in mathematics and computer science, with immediate practical consequences for cryptography, optimization, and automated reasoning.

Common misconception: P vs NP is not about "finding versus checking" in a philosophical sense — it is a precise statement about asymptotic polynomial bounds on deterministic Turing machines versus verifiers with access to witnesses.

3) NP-completeness and reductions

A problem \(A\) is NP-hard if every problem in NP reduces to \(A\) in polynomial time. A problem is NP-complete if it is in NP and NP-hard. NP-complete problems are the hardest problems in NP: if any one admits a polynomial-time algorithm, then \(\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}\).

Polynomial-time reduction: \(A \leq_p B\) means there is a polynomial-time computable function \(f\) such that \(x \in A \iff f(x) \in B\). Reductions compose and preserve membership in P.
NP-complete problem Description
SAT Is a Boolean formula satisfiable?
3-SAT SAT restricted to clauses of 3 literals
Clique Does a graph contain a clique of size \(k\)?
Hamiltonian Cycle Does a graph have a cycle visiting each vertex once?
Subset Sum Is there a subset of integers summing to target \(t\)?
Integer Programming Feasibility of linear constraints over \(\mathbb{Z}\)

4) The Cook–Levin theorem

Cook's theorem (1971) and Levin's independent work (1973) established that SAT is NP-complete. This was the first NP-completeness result and provided a foundation for thousands of subsequent completeness proofs.

Proof sketch: Let \(L \in \mathrm{NP}\) with verifier \(V\) running in time \(p(|x|)\). For input \(x\), construct a Boolean formula \(\Phi_x\) that encodes the computation of \(V\) on some witness \(w\). The formula is satisfiable iff a valid witness exists. The construction is polynomial in \(|x|\) because \(V\) runs in polynomial time.

The proof uses computation histories: each step of the Turing machine is expressed as local constraints on Boolean variables representing tape cells, states, and head positions. Circuit satisfiability (Circuit-SAT) and 3-SAT follow by standard reductions.

Key idea: NP-completeness transforms a universal question (P vs NP) into concrete combinatorial problems. Showing any one NP-complete problem is in P settles the entire question.

5) The complexity landscape beyond P and NP

Understanding P vs NP requires context. Related classes:

  • co-NP: complements of NP problems (validity of Boolean formulas).
  • PH (Polynomial Hierarchy): alternations of quantifiers over polynomial predicates.
  • PSPACE: problems solvable in polynomial space; \(\mathrm{P} \subseteq \mathrm{NP} \subseteq \mathrm{PSPACE}\).
  • EXP: problems solvable in exponential time.

Barrier results

Relativization (Baker–Gill–Solovay), natural proofs (Razborov–Rudich), and algebrization (Aaronson–Wigderson) show that many proof techniques cannot resolve P vs NP without new ideas.

Average-case hardness

Cryptography requires problems hard on average, not just worst-case. Worst-case to average-case reductions exist for some problems under cryptographic assumptions.

Status: No barrier prevents \(\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}\); the barriers only limit known techniques. The question remains fully open.

6) Implications of P = NP or P ≠ NP

If P = NP If P ≠ NP (expected)
Polynomial algorithms for SAT, scheduling, protein folding (decision versions) No general polynomial algorithm for NP-complete problems
Breakage of most classical public-key cryptography (RSA, ECC) if reductions are efficient Cryptography based on one-way functions remains viable
Revolution in automated theorem proving and mathematical discovery Heuristic and approximation methods remain essential
Optimal solutions to many engineering design problems become feasible at scale Approximation algorithms and metaheuristics dominate practice
Engineering note: Even if \(\mathrm{P} \neq \mathrm{NP}\), NP-complete problems arise constantly in circuit design (Karnaugh Mapping addresses small instances), logistics, and Optimization. Polynomial-time approximation schemes (PTAS) and SAT solvers are practical responses to hardness.

7) Research connections

Proof complexity

Lower bounds on proof length in propositional proof systems connect to circuit lower bounds and P vs NP. Resolution, cutting planes, and Frege systems are active research areas.

Circuit complexity

Superpolynomial lower bounds for Boolean circuits would separate complexity classes. Razborov–Rudich natural proofs explain why monotone circuit lower bounds have stalled.

Quantum complexity

BQP (bounded-error quantum polynomial time) may solve some NP problems faster, but is not believed to contain NP. Shor's algorithm factors integers in BQP, threatening RSA regardless of P vs NP.

Fine-grained complexity

Under SETH (Strong Exponential Time Hypothesis), many problems have nearly optimal algorithms. Conditional lower bounds refine the P vs NP landscape.

Related: Cryptography, Millennium Prize Problems, Optimization.
