Calculus (Power Calculations)

A-Level guide. Instantaneous and average power, AC power integrals, and an introduction to RMS values.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Power and energy in circuits

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred. In electrical circuits: \[ p(t) = v(t)\,i(t) \] where \( p \) is in watts (W), \( v \) in volts (V), and \( i \) in amperes (A).

Energy over a time interval is the integral of power: \[ E = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} p(t)\,dt \]

Key idea: For DC (constant \( v \) and \( i \)), \( P = VI \) directly. For AC, both \( v \) and \( i \) vary with time, so we distinguish instantaneous and average power.

See Calculus (Electrical Engineering) for AC circuit context and AC Power Calculator for numeric examples.

2) Instantaneous power

At any instant, power is the product of voltage and current at that moment. For a resistor with \( v = Ri \), power is always non-negative: \[ p = i^2 R = \frac{v^2}{R} \]

Worked example: If \( v(t) = 230\sqrt{2}\sin(100\pi t) \) V and \( i(t) = 10\sin(100\pi t) \) A (resistive load, in phase): \[ p(t) = 2300\sqrt{2}\sin^2(100\pi t) \text{ W} \] Power pulsates at twice the supply frequency — it is never negative for a pure resistor.
Reactive components: In capacitors and inductors, instantaneous power alternates positive and negative — energy is stored and returned each cycle rather than dissipated.

3) Average power over a period

The average power over one complete cycle of period \( T \) is: \[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T v(t)\,i(t)\,dt \]

For sinusoidal AC with peak values \( V_m \), \( I_m \) and phase difference \( \phi \): \[ v(t) = V_m\sin(\omega t), \qquad i(t) = I_m\sin(\omega t + \phi) \] using the identity \( \sin A\sin B = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)] \):

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{V_m I_m}{2}\cos\phi = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}\cos\phi \]

Interpretation: Only the in-phase component delivers net energy. The term \( \cos\phi \) is the power factor. When \( \phi = 90° \) (purely reactive), \( \bar{P} = 0 \).

4) AC power integrals — worked example

Evaluate average power for \( v = 100\sin(\omega t) \) V and \( i = 5\sin(\omega t + \pi/3) \) A over one period.

Step 1: Instantaneous power: \[ p(t) = 500\sin(\omega t)\sin(\omega t + \pi/3) \] Step 2: Use trig identity → \[ p(t) = 250\left[\cos(-\pi/3) - \cos(2\omega t + \pi/3)\right] \] \[ = 250\left[\tfrac{1}{2} - \cos(2\omega t + \pi/3)\right] \] Step 3: Integrate over \( T = 2\pi/\omega \). The oscillating term averages to zero: \[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T 250 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\,dt = 125 \text{ W} \] Alternatively: \( \bar{P} = \tfrac{100 \times 5}{2}\cos(\pi/3) = 250 \times 0.5 = 125 \) W.

5) RMS values — preview

The root mean square (RMS) value of a periodic function gives an equivalent DC value that would deliver the same average power to a resistor.

For current: \[ I_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T i^2(t)\,dt} \] For a sinusoid \( i = I_m\sin(\omega t) \): \[ I_{\text{rms}} = \frac{I_m}{\sqrt{2}} \]

Why RMS matters

Mains "230 V" is RMS, not peak. Peak voltage ≈ 325 V.

Meters and ratings use RMS for safe comparison with DC.

Power in terms of RMS

For resistive load: \( \bar{P} = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}} \)

General: \( \bar{P} = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}\cos\phi \)

Deriving RMS: Average power in a resistor: \( \bar{P} = I_{\text{rms}}^2 R \). Equating with the integral definition confirms \( I_{\text{rms}} = I_m/\sqrt{2} \) for sine waves.

6) Real, reactive, and apparent power

In AC systems, power is often split into components (preview for further study):

Quantity Symbol Formula Meaning
Real (active) power \( P \) \( V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\cos\phi \) Energy dissipated or converted (W)
Reactive power \( Q \) \( V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}}\sin\phi \) Energy stored/returned (VAR)
Apparent power \( S \) \( V_{\text{rms}}I_{\text{rms}} \) Total power capability (VA)

See Power Factor Calculator and Three-Phase Power for practical calculations.

7) Engineering applications

  • Billing: Energy meters integrate \( \int p(t)\,dt \) over time (kWh)
  • Component sizing: Resistors and cables must handle \( I_{\text{rms}} \), not peak current
  • Power factor correction: Adding capacitors improves \( \cos\phi \), reducing wasted current
  • Inverter efficiency: Calculating switching losses uses \( \int VI\,dt \) over each cycle
  • Maximum power transfer: Load matching for optimum \( \bar{P} \) (see Optimization)
Common mistake: Using peak values in \( P = VI \) for AC. Always use RMS values unless the question explicitly asks for instantaneous or peak power.
