Power Calculations in Electrical Engineering

Overview

Power calculation involves determining how much energy is transferred or converted in an electrical circuit over time. When voltage and current vary with time (as in AC circuits), the instantaneous power also varies. Calculus, specifically integration, helps compute the average power delivered over a time period.

Instantaneous Power

At any instant \( t \), the power \( p(t) \) delivered to a load is the product of the instantaneous voltage \( v(t) \) and current \( i(t) \):

\[ p(t) = v(t) \times i(t) \]

Average Power

The average power over a time period \( T \) is the integral of instantaneous power over that time, divided by \( T \):

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T v(t) \, i(t) \, dt \]

Example: Sinusoidal Voltage and Current

Suppose the voltage and current are sinusoidal:

\[ v(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t) \]
\[ i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t + \phi) \]

where \( V_m \) and \( I_m \) are peak voltage and current, \( \omega = 2\pi f \) is angular frequency, and \( \phi \) is the phase difference.

The instantaneous power becomes:

\[ p(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t) \times I_m \sin(\omega t + \phi) \]

Using the trigonometric identity \( \sin A \sin B = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)] \), we get:

\[ p(t) = \frac{V_m I_m}{2} \left[ \cos(\phi) - \cos(2\omega t + \phi) \right] \]

Integrating over one full period \( T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \):

\[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T p(t) \, dt = \frac{V_m I_m}{2} \cos(\phi) \]

The second term averages to zero over a full period.

Key Points

  • The average power depends on the phase difference \( \phi \). Maximum power transfer occurs when voltage and current are in phase (\( \phi = 0 \)).
  • If voltage and current are 90Â° out of phase (\( \phi = \pm \frac{\pi}{2} \)), the average power is zero (purely reactive circuit).
  • Calculus (integration) enables accurate power calculations for time-varying signals in electrical engineering.
