Power Wire Analysis — Help

What the calculator does, what it does not do, and why wire survival temperature and installation acceptance are different engineering concepts.

Calculator Standards reference Help

What ARP4404 does

SAE ARP4404 provides methodology for estimating conductor temperature (T2) based on circuit current, ambient temperature, wire size, and de-rating factors. It supports wire current carrying capacity analysis and comparison of AWG sizes for a given electrical load.

The calculator uses ARP4404 §9.3.4.2 (default) for T2 and allowable voltage drop U.

What ARP4404 does not do

  • It does not define installation acceptance temperature limits for aircraft zones.
  • It does not assess human factors, connector limits, clamp materials, or adjacent equipment survival.
  • It does not replace Design Authority requirements, system safety assessments, or certification data.
  • Passing against cable insulation rating TR alone does not prove installation suitability.

Wire survival vs installation acceptance

Wire survival (TR): The cable insulation rating defines the maximum conductor temperature the wire can withstand without insulation failure. Example: KP260 with TR = 260 °C and calculated T2 = 161 °C — the insulation survives.

Installation acceptance: Aircraft installations must protect personnel, connectors, clamps, structure, fluids, and EWIS safety margins. A conductor may be within TR but still unacceptable if it exceeds the project installation temperature limit defined by the Design Authority.

Enable Apply installation temperature assessment and enter a project-defined limit. TSAFE = MIN(TR, installation limit). Optional guidance presets are not certification limits.

What AS50881 covers

AS50881 addresses aircraft wiring installation practices: routing, separation, support, clamping, environmental exposure, and maintainability. It supports EWIS design but does not provide fixed numeric installation temperature limits for all aircraft zones.

What AC 43.13-1B covers

FAA Advisory Circular 43.13-1B Chapter 11 provides accepted methods for electrical installations including bundle derating (Fig 11-5), altitude derating (Fig 11-6), and free-air current (Fig 11-4). The calculator uses these charts for de-rating factors. AC 43.13 does not replace project-specific installation limits.

What SFAR 88 covers

SFAR 88 addresses fuel tank system safety and ignition source prevention. Wire routing and electrical ignition source control in fuel tank areas require dedicated analysis beyond conductor temperature alone.

What AC 25.981 covers

AC 25.981 provides guidance for transport category aircraft fuel tank ignition prevention, focusing on elimination of ignition sources in fuel systems rather than conductor temperature limits.

What EWIS means

EWIS (Electrical Wiring Interconnection System) encompasses wiring, connectors, clamps, protection, and routing as a safety-critical system. Installation assessment must consider fire propagation, maintenance access, failure modes, and system safety — not insulation rating alone.

Using the calculator

  1. Enter circuit parameters and select wire type.
  2. Review T2 in the analysis grid (ARP4404 calculation).
  3. With installation assessment off, compare T2 to TR (cable rating).
  4. With installation assessment on, enter the Design Authority installation limit; compare T2 to TSAFE.
  5. Review the Installation assessment panel and export Engineering Assessment in Excel or Word reports.

See also the Standards reference tab and the in-page How-to guide on the calculator.

The calculator determines conductor temperature using SAE ARP4404 methodology. Installation acceptance criteria are project-specific and shall be established by the Design Authority using applicable certification requirements, system safety assessments, EWIS requirements, environmental requirements and approved aircraft design data.
