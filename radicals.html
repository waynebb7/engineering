Radicals

GCSE guide. Square roots, cube roots, surd notation, simplifying surds, rationalising denominators, and exact values.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) What is a radical?

A radical (or root) is the inverse of a power. The square root of a number a is the value that, when squared, gives a.

  • √9 = 3 because 3² = 9
  • √25 = 5 because 5² = 25
  • Every positive number has two square roots: √9 = ±3
Notation: √ means square root. ∛ means cube root. At GCSE, √x usually means the positive (principal) root unless stated otherwise.

2) Square roots and cube roots

Square roots

√a × √a = a (for a ≥ 0)

  • √16 = 4
  • √2 ≈ 1.414 (not a whole number)

Cube roots

∛a × ∛a × ∛a = a

  • ∛8 = 2 because 2³ = 8
  • ∛27 = 3
  • Cube roots can be negative: ∛(−8) = −2
Link to indices: √a = a^(1/2) and ∛a = a^(1/3). See Powers for fractional indices.

3) Surd notation

A surd is a root that cannot be simplified to a whole number. We leave it in exact form using surd notation rather than rounding.

  • √2, √3, √5 are surds — they are irrational
  • √4 = 2 is not a surd — it simplifies to an integer
  • 2√3 means 2 × √3 — a coefficient times a surd
Common mistake: Writing √(a + b) = √a + √b. This is false. Example: √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5, but √9 + √16 = 3 + 4 = 7.

4) Simplifying surds

Factor the number under the root and take out any perfect square factors.

Rule: √(ab) = √a × √b
Worked example: √50 = √(25 × 2) = √25 × √2 = 5√2
Original Factorisation Simplified
√12 √(4 × 3) 2√3
√72 √(36 × 2) 6√2
√45 √(9 × 5) 3√5

Always simplify surds fully — examiners expect the largest square factor to be removed.

5) Adding and multiplying surds

Adding and subtracting

Only like surds (same root) can be combined.

3√2 + 5√2 = 8√2

7√3 − 2√3 = 5√3

√2 + √3 cannot be simplified further.

Multiplying surds

√a × √b = √(ab)

√2 × √8 = √16 = 4

2√3 × 4√5 = 8√15

Useful result: √a × √a = a. So √2 × √2 = 2, which is why rationalising denominators works.

6) Rationalising the denominator

Exam answers are usually written without surds in the denominator. Rationalising removes the root from the bottom of a fraction.

Case 1 — single surd: multiply top and bottom by the surd.
5/√2 = (5 × √2)/(√2 × √2) = 5√2/2
Case 2 — binomial surd (Higher): multiply top and bottom by the conjugate.
The conjugate of a + √b is a − √b.
3/(2 + √5) = 3(2 − √5)/[(2 + √5)(2 − √5)] = 3(2 − √5)/(4 − 5) = −3(2 − √5)
Common mistake: Cancelling incorrectly, e.g. writing √8/√2 = √8/2. Correct: √8/√2 = √(8/2) = √4 = 2.

7) Exact values

GCSE often asks for exact answers — left in surd form or as fractions of π — rather than decimals. This avoids rounding errors and links to Irrational Numbers.

Context Decimal (approx.) Exact form
Diagonal of unit square 1.414… √2
Hypotenuse: legs 1 and 2 2.236… √5
Side of square, area 18 cm² 4.24… 3√2 cm
Circle circumference, radius 5 31.4… 10π
Engineering note: Calculators give decimals, but design calculations often keep exact forms until the final step to preserve precision.
