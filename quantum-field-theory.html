Quantum Field Theory

Quantum guide. Fields as the fundamental degrees of freedom, Lagrangians that encode dynamics, and the path from QED to Feynman diagrams and renormalization.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) From particles to fields

In non-relativistic quantum mechanics, we track a finite number of coordinates. In relativistic physics, particle number is not conserved — pair creation and annihilation are routine. Quantum field theory (QFT) replaces particles with quantum fields: operator-valued functions \(\hat\phi(x)\) on spacetime that create and destroy quanta.

  • Each elementary particle type (electron, photon, quark) corresponds to an excitation of a field
  • The vacuum \(|0\rangle\) is the ground state of all fields — not empty space
  • Localized measurements detect particle-like quanta; propagation is wavelike
Bridge: Single-particle quantum mechanics is recovered as the low-energy limit of QFT. See Quantum Mechanics I and Wave–Particle Duality.

2) Classical fields and the action

Before quantisation, fields are classical functions \(\phi(x)\). Dynamics is encoded in an action functional:

\[S[\phi] = \int d^4x\;\mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu\phi)\]

where \(\mathcal{L}\) is the Lagrangian density. The principle of least action \(\delta S = 0\) yields the Euler–Lagrange equations:

\[\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\phi} - \partial_\mu\!\left(\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi)}\right) = 0\]

For a free scalar field, \(\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\phi)(\partial^\mu\phi) - \tfrac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2\) gives the Klein–Gordon equation \((\Box + m^2)\phi = 0\).

3) The Lagrangian formulation

The Lagrangian is the compact recipe for a theory. It specifies:

  • Kinetic terms — how fields propagate (derivatives \(\partial_\mu\))
  • Mass terms — \(m^2\phi^2/2\) for scalars; fermion masses couple left and right spinors
  • Interaction terms — couplings such as \(\lambda\phi^4\) or \(e\,\bar\psi\gamma^\mu\psi A_\mu\)

Symmetries

Continuous symmetries (via Noether's theorem) give conserved currents — charge, energy–momentum. Gauge symmetries constrain interactions and enforce consistency.

Lorentz invariance

Relativistic Lagrangians are built from Lorentz scalars: contracted indices, spinor bilinears, and field strength tensors \(F_{\mu\nu}\).

Vector calculus tools: Vector Calculus, Special Relativity.

4) Quantising the field

Promote \(\phi\) to an operator \(\hat\phi(x)\) satisfying equal-time commutation relations (bosons) or anticommutation relations (fermions). Mode expansion at fixed time:

\[\hat\phi(x) = \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\omega_k}} \left(a_k e^{-ik\cdot x} + a_k^\dagger e^{ik\cdot x}\right)\]

Here \(a_k^\dagger\) creates a particle with momentum \(k\); \(a_k\) annihilates one. The Hamiltonian \(H = \int d^3k\,\omega_k\,a_k^\dagger a_k\) counts quanta. Fermionic fields use anticommutators \(\{\psi_\alpha(x),\psi_\beta^\dagger(y)\} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})\) enforcing the Pauli exclusion principle.

Operator language: Dirac notation and Hilbert-space structure carry over — Dirac (Bra–Ket) Notation, Hilbert Spaces.

5) Interactions and scattering

Free theories are solved exactly; interactions are handled perturbatively. The interaction picture introduces a time-evolution operator:

\[S = T\exp\!\left(-i\int d^4x\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{int}}(x)\right)\]

Scattering amplitudes are matrix elements \(\langle f|S|i\rangle\) between incoming and outgoing asymptotic states. Wick's theorem rewrites time-ordered products of field operators as sums of contractions (propagators) — the algebraic heart of perturbative QFT.

Particle physics context: Nuclear and Particle Physics.

6) QED: quantum electrodynamics

QED is the quantum field theory of electrons (\(\psi\)) and photons (\(A_\mu\)). The Lagrangian density is:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{QED}} = \bar\psi(i\gamma^\mu D_\mu - m)\psi - \tfrac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}, \quad D_\mu = \partial_\mu + ieA_\mu\]

The coupling \(e\) is the elementary charge. QED is a gauge theory: the Lagrangian is invariant under local \(U(1)\) phase transformations \(\psi \to e^{ie\Lambda(x)}\psi\), with \(A_\mu \to A_\mu - \partial_\mu\Lambda\). This redundancy is essential — it forces the photon to be massless and ties the interaction vertex to the charge.

  • Tree-level processes: Compton scattering, pair annihilation, bremsstrahlung
  • Loop corrections: Lamb shift, anomalous magnetic moment \(g-2\) of the electron
  • QED is the most precisely tested theory in physics (\(\alpha \approx 1/137\))

7) Feynman diagrams (preview)

Feynman diagrams are pictorial bookkeeping for perturbation theory. Each diagram corresponds to a term in the amplitude; rules assign a factor to every vertex and internal line.

Elements

  • Lines — propagators (virtual particles)
  • Vertices — interaction couplings
  • External legs — real incoming/outgoing particles

QED example

Electron–photon vertex: \(-ie\gamma^\mu\). Electron propagator: \(i(\slashed{p}+m)/(p^2-m^2+i\varepsilon)\). Photon propagator: \(-ig_{\mu\nu}/(q^2+i\varepsilon)\) (Feynman gauge).

The optical theorem links imaginary parts of loop diagrams to total cross sections. Adding diagrams of increasing loop order systematically improves precision — but introduces divergences that must be tamed.

Preview: A single tree-level QED diagram for \(e^+e^- \to \mu^+\mu^-\) has one photon exchange; the one-loop vacuum polarisation correction modifies the effective coupling at different momentum scales.

8) Renormalization

Loop integrals in QFT often diverge. Renormalization is the systematic procedure for absorbing infinities into a finite number of measurable parameters (masses, couplings, field normalisations) while leaving finite, predictive observables.

  • Regularisation — temporarily modify integrals (dimensional regularisation, cutoff)
  • Counterterms — add bare parameters \(\delta m\), \(\delta e\) to cancel divergences
  • Renormalization group (RG) — how couplings run with energy scale \(\mu\): \(\mu\,\partial g/\partial\mu = \beta(g)\)

A theory is renormalizable if only finitely many counterterms are needed. QED is renormalizable; gravity is not (in four dimensions) — a hint of the difficulty of Quantum Gravity. The Standard Model, built on gauge QFT, is renormalizable and described further in Beyond the Standard Model.
