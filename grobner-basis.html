Grobner Basis

Postgraduate guide. Monomial orderings, Buchberger's algorithm, S-polynomials, elimination, and solving polynomial systems.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Polynomial rings and monomial orderings

Let \(R = k[x_1, \ldots, x_n]\) be a polynomial ring over a field \(k\). A monomial ordering \(\prec\) is a total order on monomials compatible with multiplication: if \(m \prec m'\) then \(m \cdot u \prec m' \cdot u\) for all monomials \(u\).

Common orderings:
  • Lexicographic (\(\mathrm{lex}\)): compare variables left to right — good for elimination.
  • Graded reverse lex (\(\mathrm{grevlex}\)): fast for Buchberger computations.
  • Graded lex (\(\mathrm{grlex}\)): total degree first, then lex tie-break.

The leading monomial \(\mathrm{LM}(f)\), leading coefficient \(\mathrm{LC}(f)\), and leading term \(\mathrm{LT}(f) = \mathrm{LC}(f)\cdot\mathrm{LM}(f)\) are defined relative to \(\prec\).

Prerequisite: Polynomials and Elementary Algebra.

2) Ideals and division algorithm

Given an ideal \(I = \langle f_1, \ldots, f_s\rangle \subset R\), the multivariate division algorithm reduces \(f\) modulo \(\{f_i\}\) to a remainder \(r\). Unlike the univariate case, the remainder depends on the order of divisors unless \(\{f_i\}\) is a Gröbner basis.

Key idea: A Gröbner basis \(G\) for \(I\) makes division canonical: \(f \in I\) iff its remainder modulo \(G\) is zero.
Worked example: With \(\mathrm{lex}\) order \(x \succ y\) and \(I = \langle x - y^2,\, y - 1\rangle\), reduction of \(x\) gives remainder \(1\), confirming \(x \equiv 1 \pmod{I}\).

3) Gröbner basis definition

A finite set \(G = \{g_1, \ldots, g_m\} \subset I\) is a Gröbner basis of \(I\) (with respect to \(\prec\)) if: \[ \langle \mathrm{LT}(g_1), \ldots, \mathrm{LT}(g_m)\rangle = \langle \mathrm{LT}(f) : f \in I\rangle \] Equivalently, every \(f \in I\) has \(\mathrm{LT}(f)\) divisible by some \(\mathrm{LT}(g_i)\).

Reduced Gröbner basis: Uniquely determined: monic generators with no term of \(g_i\) in \(\langle \mathrm{LT}(g_j) : j \neq i\rangle\).
Convention: Always state the monomial ordering. The same ideal has different Gröbner bases under different orderings.

4) Buchberger's algorithm and S-polynomials

For polynomials \(f, g\), the S-polynomial is: \[ S(f,g) = \frac{L}{\mathrm{LC}(f)\,\mathrm{LM}(f)}\,f - \frac{L}{\mathrm{LC}(g)\,\mathrm{LM}(g)}\,g, \quad L = \mathrm{lcm}(\mathrm{LM}(f), \mathrm{LM}(g)) \]

Buchberger's criterion: \(\{f_i\}\) is a Gröbner basis iff all \(S(f_i, f_j)\) reduce to zero modulo \(\{f_i\}\).
Algorithm sketch:
Step 1: Start with generators \(\{f_i\}\).
Step 2: Compute all S-polynomials, reduce remainders.
Step 3: Add nonzero remainders to the set; repeat until all S-reductions vanish.
Worked example: For \(I = \langle x^2 - y,\, xy - 1\rangle\) under \(\mathrm{lex}\) \(x \succ y\), Buchberger's algorithm adds \(x - y^3\) and \(y^3 - 1\), yielding a Gröbner basis that reveals \(y = 1\) and \(x = 1\) as the only solution over \(\mathbb{C}\).

5) Elimination and the Extension Theorem

With lexicographic ordering \(x_1 \succ \cdots \succ x_n\), the \(k\)-th elimination ideal is: \[ I_k = I \cap k[x_k, \ldots, x_n] \] Gröbner bases respect elimination: if \(G\) is a Gröbner basis of \(I\) in \(\mathrm{lex}\), then \(G \cap k[x_k, \ldots, x_n]\) generates \(I_k\).

Extension Theorem: Determines whether a partial solution \((a_k, \ldots, a_n)\) extends to a full solution of \(V(I)\). Critical for solving systems by back-substitution.

Related: Equations and Algebraic Geometry.

6) Geometry of Gröbner bases

The initial ideal \(\mathrm{in}_\prec(I) = \langle \mathrm{LT}(f) : f \in I\rangle\) encodes asymptotic geometry. For a radical ideal, \(\dim V(I) = \dim V(\mathrm{in}_\prec(I))\).

Shape lemma: For a zero-dimensional radical ideal in generic position, a \(\mathrm{lex}\) Gröbner basis has triangular form \(x_n - g_n,\, x_{n-1} - g_{n-1}, \ldots\), enabling explicit root computation.

Hilbert functions

Leading monomial ideals determine Hilbert series and degree of projective varieties.

Primary decomposition

Gröbner-based algorithms decompose ideals into primary components over \(k\).

7) Applications

Robotics and kinematics

Constraint equations for joint configurations reduce to solvable triangular systems via Gröbner bases.

Coding theory and cryptography

Decoding algorithms and multivariate polynomial cryptosystem analysis use Gröbner techniques.

Computer algebra

Systems like Singular, Macaulay2, and Mathematica implement optimised Buchberger variants (F4, F5).

Engineering design

Implicitisation of parametric surfaces and exact constraint solving in CAD benefit from elimination ideals.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Define a monomial ordering and state why \(\mathrm{lex}\) aids elimination.
  2. State the definition of a Gröbner basis in terms of leading terms.
  3. Write the S-polynomial \(S(f,g)\) and explain its role in Buchberger's criterion.
  4. Why does the multivariate remainder depend on divisor order in general?
  5. Compute a Gröbner basis for \(\langle x - y,\, y - 2\rangle\) under any ordering.
  6. What is the elimination ideal \(I_2\) for \(I = \langle x^2 + y - 1,\, xy - 2\rangle\)?
  7. Name two software packages that compute Gröbner bases.

For marking: specify the ordering, show S-reductions explicitly, and interpret solutions geometrically as points of \(V(I)\).
