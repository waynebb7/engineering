1) Time-independent perturbation theory

When the full Hamiltonian \(\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 + \lambda\hat{H}'\) is close to a solvable \(\hat{H}_0\), perturbation theory expands corrections in powers of the small parameter \(\lambda\). For non-degenerate eigenstates \(|n^{(0)}\rangle\) of \(\hat{H}_0\) with energies \(E_n^{(0)}\):

First-order energy correction: \[ E_n^{(1)} = \langle n^{(0)}|\hat{H}'|n^{(0)}\rangle \] First-order state correction: \[ |n^{(1)}\rangle = \sum_{k \neq n} \frac{\langle k^{(0)}|\hat{H}'|n^{(0)}\rangle}{E_n^{(0)} - E_k^{(0)}}\,|k^{(0)}\rangle \] Second-order energy: \[ E_n^{(2)} = \sum_{k \neq n} \frac{|\langle k^{(0)}|\hat{H}'|n^{(0)}\rangle|^2}{E_n^{(0)} - E_k^{(0)}} \]

Key idea: Perturbation theory works when \(\hat{H}'\) is "small" compared to the energy gaps of \(\hat{H}_0\). For degenerate states, use degenerate perturbation theory: diagonalise \(\hat{H}'\) within each degenerate subspace first.

Prerequisite: Quantum Mechanics I, Dirac Notation.

2) Fine structure of hydrogen

The Bohr energies \(E_n = -13.6\,\mathrm{eV}/n^2\) are not exact. Three corrections lift the \(n^2\) degeneracy (partially):

  • Relativistic kinetic energy: \(\hat{H}'_{\mathrm{rel}} \approx -\frac{\hat{p}^4}{8m^3c^2}\)
  • Spin–orbit coupling: \(\hat{H}'_{\mathrm{SO}} \propto \hat{\mathbf{L}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{S}}\)
  • Darwin term: contact interaction at \(r = 0\) for \(s\)-states

Using perturbation theory, the fine-structure correction to order \(\alpha^4\) (where \(\alpha = e^2/4\pi\varepsilon_0\hbar c \approx 1/137\) is the fine-structure constant) is \[ E_{njl} = -\frac{13.6\,\mathrm{eV}}{n^2}\left[1 + \frac{\alpha^2}{n^2}\left(\frac{n}{j+\frac{1}{2}} - \frac{3}{4}\right)\right] \] where \(j = l \pm \frac{1}{2}\) (with \(j = \frac{1}{2}\) when \(l = 0\)).

3) Scattering — introduction

In scattering experiments, a beam of particles approaches a target potential \(V(r)\). Far from the interaction region, the wave function is a superposition of an incident plane wave and an outgoing spherical wave: \[ \psi(\mathbf{r}) \xrightarrow{r\to\infty} e^{ikz} + f(\theta)\,\frac{e^{ikr}}{r} \] where \(k = \sqrt{2mE}/\hbar\) and \(f(\theta)\) is the scattering amplitude.

The differential cross section gives the probability per unit solid angle: \[ \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = |f(\theta)|^2 \] The total cross section is \(\sigma = \int |f(\theta)|^2\,d\Omega\).

In the Born approximation (weak potential, high energy): \[ f(\theta) \approx -\frac{m}{2\pi\hbar^2}\int V(\mathbf{r}')\,e^{i\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{r}'}\,d^3r' \] where \(\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{k}_f - \mathbf{k}_i\) is the momentum transfer.

Common mistake: The cross section has units of area (m\(^2\) or barns), not probability. It measures the effective "target size" that removes flux from the incident beam.

4) Identical particles and quantum statistics

Particles of the same type are fundamentally indistinguishable. Swapping two identical particles cannot change any physical observable. This forces the total wave function to be either symmetric (bosons) or antisymmetric (fermions) under exchange:

Symmetric (bosons, integer spin): \(\psi(\ldots, \mathbf{r}_i, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_j, \ldots) = +\psi(\ldots, \mathbf{r}_j, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_i, \ldots)\)

Antisymmetric (fermions, half-integer spin): \(\psi(\ldots, \mathbf{r}_i, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_j, \ldots) = -\psi(\ldots, \mathbf{r}_j, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_i, \ldots)\)

Pauli exclusion principle: No two fermions can occupy the same quantum state. This explains the periodic table, white dwarf stability, and the structure of metals.

For two non-interacting fermions in a box, the ground state places one particle in \(n = 1\) and one in \(n = 2\), giving total energy \(E_1 + E_2 = 5E_1\). Two bosons can both occupy \(n = 1\), giving \(2E_1\) — Bose–Einstein condensation at low temperature.

5) Variational method

When exact solutions are unavailable, the variational principle provides an upper bound on the ground-state energy. For any normalised trial state \(|\psi_\mathrm{trial}\rangle\): \[ E_0 \leq \frac{\langle\psi_\mathrm{trial}|\hat{H}|\psi_\mathrm{trial}\rangle}{\langle\psi_\mathrm{trial}|\psi_\mathrm{trial}\rangle} \] Minimise over adjustable parameters to approach \(E_0\).

Example: hydrogen ground state with trial \(\psi = Ae^{-\alpha r}\). Minimising \(\langle\hat{H}\rangle\) gives \(\alpha = 1/a_0\) and \(E = -13.6\,\mathrm{eV}\) — the exact answer, because the trial form is correct.

Strengths

Works for complex systems (molecules, many-body). No small-parameter requirement. Foundation of modern computational chemistry and VQE algorithms.

Limitations

Gives an upper bound only for the ground state. Excited states require orthogonal trial functions or constrained optimisation. Accuracy depends on trial wave function quality.

6) Time-dependent perturbation theory

When \(\hat{H}(t) = \hat{H}_0 + \hat{H}'(t)\) and \(\hat{H}'\) is time-dependent (e.g. an oscillating electric field), transitions between eigenstates of \(\hat{H}_0\) occur. To first order, the transition amplitude from \(|i\rangle\) to \(|f\rangle\) is \[ c_f(t) = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_0^t \langle f|\hat{H}'(t')|i\rangle\,e^{i\omega_{fi}t'}\,dt' \] where \(\omega_{fi} = (E_f - E_i)/\hbar\).

For monochromatic perturbation \(\hat{H}'(t) = \hat{H}'_0\cos(\omega t)\), transitions are resonant when \(\omega \approx \omega_{fi}\) (absorption) or \(\omega \approx -\omega_{fi}\) (stimulated emission). This underpins spectroscopy and laser physics.

Fermi's golden rule: For constant \(\hat{H}'\) turned on slowly, the transition rate to a continuum of final states is \[ \Gamma_{i\to f} = \frac{2\pi}{\hbar}|\langle f|\hat{H}'|i\rangle|^2\rho(E_f) \] where \(\rho(E_f)\) is the density of final states.

Applications: Quantum Optics, Quantum Control and Calibration.

7) WKB approximation and semiclassics

The WKB method (Wentzel–Kramers–Brillouin) approximates solutions when the de Broglie wavelength varies slowly compared to the potential: \[ \psi(x) \approx \frac{A}{\sqrt{p(x)}}\exp\!\left(\pm\frac{i}{\hbar}\int^x p(x')\,dx'\right), \quad p(x) = \sqrt{2m(E - V(x))} \]

WKB explains quantum tunneling through classically forbidden regions and connects bound-state quantisation to Bohr–Sommerfeld conditions: \[ \oint p\,dq = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)h \]

Breakdown: WKB fails near classical turning points where \(p = 0\). Match to Airy functions (connection formulas) at turning points for accurate results.

Tunneling applications: Quantum Tunneling, scanning tunnelling microscopy, \(\alpha\)-decay.
