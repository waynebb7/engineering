1) Why we need quantum physics

By the late 1800s, classical physics explained most of everyday experience — mechanics, thermodynamics, and Maxwell's electromagnetism. Three experiments at the atomic scale refused to fit:

  • Black-body radiation: classical theory predicted infinite ultraviolet emission (the "ultraviolet catastrophe").
  • Photoelectric effect: light ejects electrons only above a threshold frequency, not intensity.
  • Atomic spectra: atoms emit light at discrete wavelengths, not a continuous rainbow.

Planck (1900) proposed that energy is exchanged in quanta \(E = hf\). Einstein (1905) extended this to light itself. Bohr (1913) quantised electron orbits. By the 1920s, de Broglie, Schrödinger, Heisenberg, and Dirac built the full quantum framework we still use today.

Key idea: Quantum physics is not an optional add-on to classical physics — it is the correct description whenever particles, energies, or times are small enough that discreteness and interference matter.

2) The photoelectric effect

When light shines on a metal surface, electrons may be ejected. Classical wave theory predicts that brighter light (higher intensity) should eject electrons with more kinetic energy. Experiment shows something different:

  • No electrons are emitted below a threshold frequency \(f_0\), regardless of intensity.
  • Above \(f_0\), the maximum kinetic energy depends on frequency, not intensity.
  • Electron emission begins almost instantly when light arrives.

Einstein explained this by treating light as quanta (photons) of energy \[ E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \] where \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm{J\,s}\) is Planck's constant. One photon gives all its energy to one electron. The photoelectric equation is \[ hf = \phi + K_{\max} \] where \(\phi\) is the metal's work function (minimum energy to free an electron) and \(K_{\max}\) is the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons.

Common mistake: Intensity controls how many photons arrive per second (and thus the photocurrent), not the energy of individual photons. Only frequency sets \(E = hf\).

Related: Wave–Particle Duality, Quantum Optics.

3) The double-slit experiment

Send particles (photons, electrons, or even large molecules) one at a time through two narrow slits onto a detector screen. After many arrivals, an interference pattern of bright and dark fringes appears — exactly as if each particle passed through both slits as a wave.

For light of wavelength \(\lambda\) and slit separation \(d\), constructive interference at angle \(\theta\) satisfies \[ d\sin\theta = m\lambda, \quad m = 0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots \]

de Broglie (1924) proposed that all matter has an associated wavelength \[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} \] where \(p = mv\) is momentum. Electrons in a double-slit experiment produce the same fringe pattern as light, proving that particles interfere with themselves.

Measurement changes the outcome: If you detect which slit each particle passes through, the interference pattern vanishes. Which-path information and wave behaviour are mutually exclusive.

Deeper treatment: Superposition and Measurement, Uncertainty Principle.

4) Energy levels and quantisation

Atoms absorb and emit light only at specific wavelengths. Each spectral line corresponds to a transition between discrete energy levels \(E_n\). The photon energy matches the level difference: \[ \Delta E = E_{\mathrm{upper}} - E_{\mathrm{lower}} = hf \]

Bohr's model of hydrogen (1913) quantised angular momentum \(L = n\hbar\) with \(n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\) and predicted energy levels \[ E_n = -\frac{13.6\,\mathrm{eV}}{n^2} \] The ground state (\(n = 1\)) has \(E_1 = -13.6\,\mathrm{eV}\); ionisation requires at least 13.6 eV. Transitions to \(n = 2\) produce the Balmer series (visible hydrogen lines).

5) Wave functions — a preview

Modern quantum mechanics replaces definite trajectories with a wave function \(\psi(x, t)\) (or \(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)\) in three dimensions). The probability density of finding a particle near position \(x\) is \[ P(x, t) = |\psi(x, t)|^2 \] Normalisation requires \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\psi|^2\,dx = 1\): the particle must be somewhere.

For a free particle with definite momentum \(p = \hbar k\), the plane wave \[ \psi(x) = Ae^{ikx} \] has constant \(|\psi|^2 = |A|^2\) — it is not normalisable over all space. Physically realisable states are wave packets: superpositions of plane waves localised in space.

What \(\psi\) is not: The wave function is not a physical wave in the material sense. It is a probability amplitude. We never measure \(\psi\) directly — only \(|\psi|^2\) (or related expectation values).

Notation and formalism: Dirac Notation, Postulates of Quantum Mechanics.

6) Photons vs electrons — same rules, different scales

Photons

Massless, always travel at \(c\). Energy \(E = hf\), momentum \(p = h/\lambda\). No charge; bosons (integer spin). Interference seen in optics and interferometry.

Electrons

Mass \(m_e = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\,\mathrm{kg}\). de Broglie wavelength \(\lambda = h/p\) matters at atomic scales. Fermions (spin \(\frac{1}{2}\)). Wave nature confirmed by electron diffraction (Davisson–Germer, 1927).

Both obey the same interference and quantisation rules. The difference is which physical constants dominate: for photons it is \(c\) and \(h\); for electrons it is \(m_e\) and \(\hbar\).

Nuance: "Wave–particle duality" does not mean a particle literally splits in two. It means a single quantum entity is described by a wave function whose squared magnitude gives measurement statistics.
