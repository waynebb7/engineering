Quantum Mechanics I

Quantum guide. Undergraduate first course: the time-dependent and time-independent Schrödinger equation, bound states in standard potentials, angular momentum, and an introduction to the hydrogen atom.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) The Schrödinger equation

The central equation of non-relativistic quantum mechanics governs how the wave function evolves in time. For a particle of mass \(m\) in potential \(V(\mathbf{r}, t)\): \[ i\hbar\frac{\partial\psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H}\psi \] where the Hamiltonian operator is \[ \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}, t) \]

For time-independent potentials \(V(\mathbf{r})\), separate variables with \(\psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \psi(\mathbf{r})\,e^{-iEt/\hbar}\) to obtain the time-independent equation \[ \hat{H}\psi = E\psi \] Bound states yield discrete eigenvalues \(E_n\); scattering states yield a continuous spectrum.

Key idea: Solving \(\hat{H}\psi = E\psi\) is an eigenvalue problem. Each eigenfunction \(\psi_n\) is a stationary state with definite energy \(E_n\); the full time dependence is the phase factor \(e^{-iE_n t/\hbar}\).

Prerequisites: Quantum Physics Intro, Dirac Notation, Differential Equations.

2) Infinite square well

A particle of mass \(m\) is confined to \(0 < x < a\) with \(V = 0\) inside and \(V = \infty\) outside. Inside the well, \(\hat{H}\psi = E\psi\) becomes \[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} = E\psi \] with boundary conditions \(\psi(0) = \psi(a) = 0\).

Normalised energy eigenfunctions and eigenvalues: \[ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right), \quad E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2ma^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \]

3) Quantum harmonic oscillator

Potential \(V(x) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2\) models molecular vibrations, phonons, and the electromagnetic field modes. Defining \(\hat{a} = \sqrt{m\omega/2\hbar}\,(\hat{x} + i\hat{p}/m\omega)\) (lowering operator), the Hamiltonian becomes \[ \hat{H} = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{a}^\dagger\hat{a} + \frac{1}{2}\right) = \hbar\omega\left(\hat{N} + \frac{1}{2}\right) \]

Energy eigenvalues are equally spaced: \[ E_n = \hbar\omega\left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \] The ground state \(n = 0\) has zero-point energy \(\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\). Eigenfunctions in the position basis involve Hermite polynomials \(H_n(\xi)\) with \(\xi = x\sqrt{m\omega/\hbar}\).

Ladder operators: \(\hat{a}|n\rangle = \sqrt{n}\,|n-1\rangle\) and \(\hat{a}^\dagger|n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\,|n+1\rangle\). This algebraic method avoids solving the differential equation directly and generalises to field theory.

Related: Quantum Tunneling (finite barriers).

4) Angular momentum

Orbital angular momentum operators \(\hat{\mathbf{L}} = \hat{\mathbf{r}} \times \hat{\mathbf{p}}\) satisfy \([\hat{L}_i, \hat{L}_j] = i\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}\hat{L}_k\). Simultaneous eigenstates of \(\hat{L}^2\) and \(\hat{L}_z\) are labelled by quantum numbers \(l\) and \(m\): \[ \hat{L}^2|l,m\rangle = l(l+1)\hbar^2|l,m\rangle, \quad \hat{L}_z|l,m\rangle = m\hbar|l,m\rangle \] with \(l = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\) and \(m = -l, -l+1, \ldots, l-1, l\).

In spherical coordinates, the angular part of \(\psi_{nlm}(r,\theta,\phi) = R_{nl}(r)\,Y_l^m(\theta,\phi)\) is a spherical harmonic \(Y_l^m\). The radial equation depends on the potential.

Orbital angular momentum

Integer \(l\); describes motion in central potentials. \(2l+1\) states per \(l\). Example: \(l = 1\) gives \(p\)-orbitals (\(m = -1, 0, +1\)).

Spin angular momentum

Half-integer or integer \(s\); intrinsic, not from spatial motion. Electron has \(s = \frac{1}{2}\), \(m_s = \pm\frac{1}{2}\). See Spin and Pauli Matrices.

5) Hydrogen atom — introduction

The Coulomb potential \(V(r) = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\) for a proton–electron system is separable in spherical coordinates. The radial equation yields energy eigenvalues \[ E_n = -\frac{m_e e^4}{2(4\pi\varepsilon_0)^2\hbar^2}\cdot\frac{1}{n^2} = -\frac{13.6\,\mathrm{eV}}{n^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots \] matching the Bohr model but with the correct degeneracy and wave functions.

Each level \(n\) has degeneracy \(n^2\) (accounting for \(l = 0, \ldots, n-1\) and \(m\)). The ground state \(\psi_{100} \propto e^{-r/a_0}\) has Bohr radius \[ a_0 = \frac{4\pi\varepsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_e e^2} \approx 0.529\,\text{Å} \]

Selection rules: Electric dipole transitions require \(\Delta l = \pm 1\) and \(\Delta m = 0, \pm 1\), explaining which spectral lines appear in hydrogen emission.

Fine structure and perturbations: Quantum Mechanics II.

6) Operators, observables, and expectation values

Every measurable quantity corresponds to a Hermitian operator \(\hat{A}\). The expectation value in state \(|\psi\rangle\) is \[ \langle A \rangle = \langle\psi|\hat{A}|\psi\rangle \] Measurement of \(\hat{A}\) yields eigenvalue \(a_n\) with probability \(|\langle a_n|\psi\rangle|^2\).

7) Solving problems — a systematic approach

Bound states

1. Write \(\hat{H}\) in appropriate coordinates.
2. Separate variables if possible.
3. Apply boundary conditions.
4. Normalise \(\psi\).
5. Compute \(\langle x \rangle\), \(\langle p \rangle\), etc.

Key theorems

Virial theorem: For homogeneous \(V \propto r^n\), \(\langle T \rangle = \frac{n}{2}\langle V \rangle\).
EHF theorem: Ground state minimises \(\langle\hat{H}\rangle\).
Orthogonality: Distinct eigenstates with different \(E\) are orthogonal.

Common mistake: Forgetting that \(\hat{p}\) is Hermitian only when boundary terms vanish — always check that \(\psi\) goes to zero (or satisfies periodicity) at boundaries.
