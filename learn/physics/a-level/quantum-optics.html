1) Quantisation of light

In quantum optics, each mode of the electromagnetic field is a quantum harmonic oscillator. For a single mode with annihilation operator \(a\) and frequency \(\omega\): \[ H = \hbar\omega\left(a^\dagger a + \frac{1}{2}\right) \] The number operator \(\hat{n} = a^\dagger a\) counts photons in that mode.

Canonical commutation relation: \[ [a, a^\dagger] = 1 \] Position and momentum of the field quadratures do not commute — quantum fluctuations are intrinsic, not measurement noise.

Key idea: Light comes in discrete energy packets \(\hbar\omega\), but a laser field is not a Fock state of definite photon number — it is a coherent superposition of number states.

Prerequisites: Optics and Photonics, Postulates of Quantum Mechanics, Complex Numbers.

2) Fock (number) states

Fock states \(|n\rangle\) are eigenstates of photon number: \[ \hat{n}|n\rangle = n|n\rangle, \quad a|n\rangle = \sqrt{n}\,|n-1\rangle, \quad a^\dagger|n\rangle = \sqrt{n+1}\,|n+1\rangle \] The vacuum \(|0\rangle\) has zero photons but non-zero field fluctuations (zero-point energy \(\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\)).

A single-photon state \(|1\rangle\) is the building block for Photonic Quantum Computing and QKD. Detecting individual photons requires avalanche photodiodes or superconducting nanowire single-photon detectors.

Common mistake: A dim laser pulse is not a single-photon source. It contains a Poissonian mixture of Fock states \(|n\rangle\) with mean \(\langle n \rangle\).

3) Coherent states

A coherent state \(|\alpha\rangle\) is an eigenstate of the annihilation operator: \[ a|\alpha\rangle = \alpha|\alpha\rangle \] Expanded in the Fock basis: \[ |\alpha\rangle = e^{-|\alpha|^2/2} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\alpha^n}{\sqrt{n!}}|n\rangle \] Mean photon number \(\langle \hat{n} \rangle = |\alpha|^2\); photon-number statistics are Poissonian.

Coherent states minimise uncertainty in a balanced quadrature and closely approximate laser light above threshold. They form an overcomplete, non-orthogonal basis — the closest quantum analogue to a classical electromagnetic wave with well-defined phase and amplitude.

Displacement operator: \(|\alpha\rangle = D(\alpha)|0\rangle\) where \(D(\alpha) = \exp(\alpha a^\dagger - \alpha^* a)\) displaces the vacuum in phase space.

4) Quadratures and squeezing (preview)

Field quadratures \(X = \frac{1}{2}(a + a^\dagger)\) and \(P = \frac{i}{2}(a^\dagger - a)\) satisfy \(\Delta X \cdot \Delta P \geq 1/4\) (in units with \(\hbar = 1\)). Coherent states saturate this bound equally in both quadratures.

Squeezed states redistribute uncertainty — reducing noise in one quadrature at the expense of the other. Squeezed light improves sensitivity in Quantum Metrology and gravitational-wave detectors (LIGO).

Phase-space picture connects to Superposition and Measurement and continuous-variable quantum information.

5) Beam splitters

A lossless beam splitter couples two optical modes. On the annihilation operators: \[ \begin{pmatrix} a_1' \\ a_2' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix} \] A 50:50 splitter has \(\theta = \pi/4\). Beam splitters are unitary — they preserve total photon number in the two-mode system.

For dual-rail photonic qubits, \(|0\rangle \equiv |1\rangle_a|0\rangle_b\) and \(|1\rangle \equiv |0\rangle_a|1\rangle_b\): a beam splitter implements a Hadamard-like operation on the photon path degree of freedom.

Linear optics: Networks of beam splitters and phase shifters implement any unitary on \(n\) modes — the backbone of integrated photonic circuits and boson sampling.

Photonic computing: Photonic Quantum Computing, Quantum Gates.

6) Hong–Ou–Mandel effect

When two indistinguishable photons enter a 50:50 beam splitter — one on each input port — they always exit together on the same output port. The probability of coincidence (one photon per output) vanishes: \[ P_{\text{coincidence}} = 0 \] This is the Hong–Ou–Mandel (HOM) dip, a direct signature of bosonic quantum statistics.

The two-photon input \(|1\rangle_1|1\rangle_2\) is symmetric under exchange; only the antisymmetric path combination couples to the coincidence outputs, but bosons forbid antisymmetric occupation. The destructive interference eliminates coincidence counts.

Engineering use: HOM interference enables photonic Bell measurements, entangling gates via post-selection, and high-visibility tests of photon indistinguishability (spectrum, timing, polarisation).
Visibility: Partial distinguishability fills in the HOM dip. Fidelity of photonic gates depends critically on spectral and temporal mode matching.

7) Cavity QED — introduction

Cavity quantum electrodynamics (cavity QED) studies atoms (or artificial atoms) interacting with quantised light confined in a high-finesse cavity. The simplest model is the Jaynes–Cummings Hamiltonian: \[ H = \hbar\omega_c a^\dagger a + \frac{\hbar\omega_a}{2}\sigma_z + \hbar g(a\sigma_+ + a^\dagger\sigma_-) \] where \(\omega_c\) is the cavity frequency, \(\omega_a\) the atomic transition, and \(g\) the coupling strength.

  • Weak coupling: atom and cavity exchange excitations faster than decay — Rabi oscillations.
  • Strong coupling: \(g\) exceeds cavity and atomic decay rates — vacuum Rabi splitting.
  • Strong coupling regime: enables single-photon nonlinearities and atom–photon entanglement.

Cavity QED platforms underpin trapped-ion and superconducting circuits (circuit QED is the microwave analogue) and provide a route to deterministic photon–atom interfaces.

Quality factor: Cavity finesse \(\mathcal{F} = \pi\sqrt{\mathcal{R}}/(1-\mathcal{R})\) sets photon lifetime \(\tau = Q/\omega_c\). High \(Q\) cavities enhance coupling per photon.

Related hardware: Superconducting Qubits, Trapped-Ion Systems.

  3. Why does a 50:50 beam splitter map \(|1\rangle_1|0\rangle_2\) into an equal superposition of both output paths?
  4. Explain in words why bosons exhibit the HOM dip but fermions would show enhanced coincidence.
