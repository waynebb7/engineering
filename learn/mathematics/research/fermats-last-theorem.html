1) The statement and historical context

Fermat's Last Theorem (FLT) asserts: for integers \(n \geq 3\), the equation \[ x^n + y^n = z^n \] has no nonzero integer solutions \((x, y, z)\). Fermat claimed a proof in the 1630s (margin note in Arithmetica); the first complete proof was achieved by Andrew Wiles (1995), with Richard Taylor.

Early progress: Euler proved \(n = 3, 4\). Dirichlet and Legendre handled \(n = 5, 7\). Kummer's work on regular primes (using cyclotomic fields) proved FLT for infinitely many exponents but not all.
Exponent / case Status before 1995
\(n = 3, 4\) Euler, Fermat (infinite descent)
Regular primes Kummer (1847); covers e.g. \(n = 5, 7, 11, 13\)
\(n < 4 \times 10^6\) Computational (Wagstaff, others)
All \(n \geq 3\) Wiles (1995) via modularity

Prerequisite: Elementary Algebra, Algebraic Number Theory, Algebraic Number Theory.

2) Reduction to elliptic curves (Frey, Serre, Ribet)

Suppose \((a, b, c)\) is a counterexample to FLT with \(\gcd(a,b,c) = 1\) and exponent prime \(p \geq 5\) (Wiles's case; other exponents follow from this). The Frey curve is \[ E : y^2 = x(x - a^p)(x + b^p). \] This elliptic curve over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is semistable and (conjecturally) should not exist if FLT holds.

Serre's \(\varepsilon\)-conjecture (1986): The Galois representation \(\rho_{E,p} : \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)\) attached to \(E\) is irreducible and modular (comes from a modular form of weight 2 and level \(N\)).
Ribet's level-lowering (1986): If \(\rho_{E,p}\) is irreducible and \(E\) is semistable, then \(\rho_{E,p}\) is modular of level 2 (i.e. comes from a modular form of level 2). A modular form of level 2 cannot exist (no cusp forms), yielding a contradiction.
Logical chain: FLT follows from: (i) Frey curve is semistable, (ii) modularity lifting (Wiles), (iii) Ribet's level lowering. Wiles proved (ii) for semistable curves, completing the argument.

3) Elliptic curves and Galois representations

An elliptic curve over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is a smooth projective curve \(E : y^2 = x^3 + ax + b\) with \(a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\) and discriminant \(\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0\). The set \(E(\mathbb{Q})\) of rational points forms a finitely generated abelian group (Mordell–Weil theorem).

For a prime \(p\), the \(p\)-torsion \(E[p] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^2\) gives a Galois representation \[ \rho_{E,p} : \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p). \] The Frobenius element at a good prime \(\ell\) has characteristic polynomial \(T^2 - a_\ell T + \ell\) where \(a_\ell = \ell + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_\ell)\).

Semistable reduction

\(E\) is semistable if it has good reduction outside a set of primes dividing the conductor \(N\), and bad reduction is multiplicative (Kodaira type I\(_m\)). The Frey curve is semistable.

Conductor

The minimal \(N\) such that \(E\) has good reduction outside primes dividing \(N\). For the Frey curve, \(N = abc\) (product of the FLT counterexample primes).

4) Modular forms and the modularity theorem

A modular form of weight \(k\) and level \(N\) is a holomorphic function \(f : \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{C}\) satisfying \[ f\left(\frac{az+b}{cz+d}\right) = (cz+d)^k f(z), \quad \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \Gamma_0(N), \] with growth conditions at cusps. A cusp form vanishes at all cusps.

Modularity theorem (Taniyama–Shimura–Weil, now proved): Every elliptic curve \(E/\mathbb{Q}\) is modular: there exists a weight-2 cusp form \(f\) of level \(N\) (the conductor of \(E\)) such that \[ a_\ell(f) = a_\ell(E) \quad \text{for all but finitely many primes } \ell. \] Equivalently, the \(L\)-function \(L(E, s)\) equals \(L(f, s)\) (up to factors at bad primes).
Hecke operators: \(T_\ell f = \sum_{[a : b] \in \mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_\ell)} f\left(\frac{a z + b}{\ell}\right)\). Eigenforms of Hecke operators correspond to Galois representations with controlled ramification.

5) Wiles's proof outline

Wiles proved the modularity lifting theorem for semistable elliptic curves: if \(\rho_{E,3}\) (or \(\rho_{E,5}\) with auxiliary techniques) is irreducible and modular, then \(E\) itself is modular. The proof proceeds via Galois deformation theory.

Step 1 — Universal deformation ring: Let \(\mathcal{R}\) be the universal deformation ring of \(\bar{\rho} = \rho_{E,p}\) (unramified outside \(N\), with prescribed ramification at \(p\)). Mazur's deformation theory controls \(\mathcal{R}\).
Step 2 — Hecke algebra: Let \(\mathbf{T}\) be the Hecke algebra of modular forms of level \(N\). A modular Galois representation \(\bar{\rho}\) arises from an eigenform, giving a surjection \(\mathbf{T} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{O}\) (the coefficient ring).
Step 3 — \(\mathcal{R} = \mathbf{T}\): Wiles constructs a surjection \(\mathbf{T} \to \mathcal{R}\) and proves \(\mathcal{R}\) is a complete intersection of the expected dimension. By level-lowering arguments and numerical criteria (Wiles's numerical criterion / patching by Taylor–Wiles), \(\mathcal{R} \cong \mathbf{T}\). This identifies the universal deformation with a Hecke algebra, proving modularity.
Initial gap: Wiles's first manuscript (1993) had a gap in the Euler system argument. Taylor and Wiles patched it using a second auxiliary prime. The final proof appeared in 1995.

6) Generalizations and modern arithmetic geometry

Full modularity

Breuil, Conrad, Diamond, and Taylor (2001) extended modularity to all elliptic curves over \(\mathbb{Q}\), not only semistable ones. The modularity theorem is now fully proved.

ABC conjecture

FLT is a consequence of the ABC conjecture (Mochizuki's claimed proof remains disputed). The Frey curve argument shows deep connections between Diophantine equations and curve geometry.

Fermat quotients

\(q_p(a) = \frac{a^{p-1} - 1}{p} \bmod p\) connects FLT to Iwasawa theory and class numbers of cyclotomic fields.

Langlands program

FLT is a special case of reciprocity between Galois representations and automorphic forms. See Automorphic Forms and Perfectoid Spaces for modern extensions.

BSD connection: The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture relates \(L(E,1)\) to the rank of \(E(\mathbb{Q})\). Modularity provides analytic continuation and functional equations for \(L(E,s)\).

7) Research connections and frontier directions

Post-Wiles arithmetic geometry studies: Serre's uniformity conjecture (open), generalized Fermat equations \(x^p + y^q = z^r\), modularity of abelian varieties, and the Langlands correspondence for \(\mathrm{GL}_n\) over number fields.

Related genius-level topics: Perfectoid Spaces, Cohomology (Galois cohomology, Selmer groups), Complex Kleinian Groups (arithmetic lattices), Galois Theory.
