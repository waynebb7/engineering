1) Graph colouring and the four-colour problem

A proper colouring of a graph \(G = (V, E)\) assigns colours to vertices so that adjacent vertices receive different colours. The chromatic number \(\chi(G)\) is the minimum number of colours required.

The Four Colour Problem (Francis Guthrie, 1852): Can every planar map be coloured with four colours so that adjacent regions share no colour? Equivalently:

Four Colour Theorem (4CT): Every planar graph satisfies \(\chi(G) \leq 4\).

Planar graphs are exactly those drawable in the plane without edge crossings. The dual formulation — regions as vertices, adjacency as edges — is equivalent to vertex colouring of the dual graph.

Prerequisite: Topology, basic graph theory, and Combinatorics.

2) Historical reductions and partial results

Early progress reduced 4CT to checking specific configurations:

  • Five Colour Theorem (Heawood, 1890): \(\chi(G) \leq 5\) for all planar \(G\), proved by Kempe chains (an ingenious but flawed approach for four colours).
  • Kempe's flawed proof (1879): Assumed Kempe chains always permit recolouring; Heawood found the flaw but extracted the five-colour result.
  • Whitney's work (1930s): Irreducible configurations and the notion of unavoidable sets.
  • Appel–Haken reduction (1976): Reduced 4CT to checking a finite set of unavoidable configurations (about 1,936 cases).
Key idea: A reducible configuration cannot appear in a minimal counterexample to 4CT. An unavoidable set covers all possibilities in any minimal counterexample. If every configuration in an unavoidable set is reducible, 4CT holds.

3) The Appel–Haken proof (1976)

Kenneth Appel and Wolfgang Haken announced a proof in 1976, published in 1977. The argument:

  1. Assume a minimal counterexample exists (a planar graph requiring 5 colours, minimal number of vertices).
  2. Prove every such graph contains one of 1,936 unavoidable configurations.
  3. For each configuration, prove reducibility — it cannot occur in a minimal counterexample.
  4. Step (3) was verified by computer: approximately 1,200 hours of CPU time on an IBM 370-168.
Discharging method: Assign charges to vertices and faces; redistribute by local rules. Total charge is invariant; if every complete graph has positive charge, certain configurations are unavoidable. This is the combinatorial engine of the proof.
Human vs machine: Humans designed the unavoidable set and reducibility arguments; the computer checked thousands of case analyses that humans could not feasibly verify by hand.

4) Robertson–Seymour–Thomas and formal verification

A second proof (Robertson, Seymour, Thomas, 1997) used a smaller set of 633 unavoidable configurations and a quadratic-time algorithm to 4-colour planar graphs. Their proof was also computer-assisted.

Coq formalization

Gonthier (2008) formalized the 4CT in the Coq proof assistant, verifying the entire proof including combinatorics and computer-generated cases. This addressed concerns about programming errors in the original verification.

Modern status

The 4CT is accepted as proved. Formal verification in Coq and independent re-verification of reducibility checks have strengthened confidence beyond the original 1976 announcement.

5) The controversy: is it really a proof?

The 4CT sparked debate about the nature of mathematical proof:

Skeptical views

Some mathematicians (e.g. Erdős, at first) questioned whether a proof requiring computer verification of cases invisible to humans counts as a "proof" in the traditional sense. Errors in programs or hardware could invalidate the result.

Defensive views

The combinatorial reduction is human-verified; the computer checks finitary computations. Formal proof assistants (Coq) now provide machine-checkable certificates, analogous to trusting referees for long human proofs.

Philosophical question: Does mathematical truth require human surveyability? The 4CT controversy prefigured modern debates about automated theorem proving, proof assistants, and the role of computation in mathematics.
Fair assessment: The 4CT is not controversial among graph theorists today. The controversy was about epistemology (how we know), not about the result's correctness. Independent verification and formalization resolved practical doubts.

6) Extensions and related results

  • Four Colour Theorem for maps on the sphere is equivalent to the plane case.
  • Toroidal graphs may require 7 colours; \(\chi(G) \leq 7\) for graphs on the torus (Heawood).
  • Snark theorem (Robertson–Seymour–Thomas): Bridgeless planar cubic graphs are 3-edge-colourable iff they contain no Petersen subgraph obstruction.
  • List colouring conjecture for planar graphs: \(\chi_\ell(G) \leq 5\) (proved); \(\chi_\ell(G) \leq 4\) is the list-colouring version of 4CT (open in full generality, partial results exist).
Algorithmic consequence: 4-colouring planar graphs is in P. Linear-time algorithms exist (Robertson–Seymour–Thomas, later simplifications). This is unusual among NP-hard problems — planarity is a strong restriction.

7) Research connections

Graph minor theory

Robertson and Seymour's graph minor project (including the Graph Minor Theorem) grew from 4CT techniques. See Topology for connections to surface embeddings.

Computational proof culture

4CT pioneered computer-assisted proof. Modern successors include the Kepler conjecture (Hales), classification of finite simple groups verification, and Flyspeck/Lean formalizations.

Related: Combinatorics, P vs NP, Topology.
