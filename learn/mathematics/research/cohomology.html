1) de Rham cohomology

On a smooth manifold \(M\), the de Rham complex \((\Omega^*(M), d)\) consists of differential forms with exterior derivative \(d : \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M)\), satisfying \(d^2 = 0\). de Rham cohomology is \[ H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) = \frac{\ker(d : \Omega^k \to \Omega^{k+1})}{\mathrm{im}(d : \Omega^{k-1} \to \Omega^k)} = \frac{\{\text{closed } k\text{-forms}\}}{\{\text{exact } k\text{-forms}\}}. \]

de Rham's theorem: \(H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^k(M; \mathbb{R})\) (singular cohomology with real coefficients). Analytic forms compute topological invariants.
Poincaré lemma: On a contractible open set \(U\), every closed \(k\)-form is exact for \(k \geq 1\). Locally \(H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(U) = 0\) for \(k > 0\); cohomology measures global obstruction.
Manifold \(H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}\)
\(\mathbb{R}^n\) \(\mathbb{R}\) if \(k=0\), else \(0\)
\(S^n\) \(\mathbb{R}\) for \(k = 0, n\); else \(0\)
\(T^2 = S^1 \times S^1\) \(\mathbb{R}\) in degrees 0, 1, 2; \(H^1 \cong \mathbb{R}^2\)

Prerequisite: Differential Geometry, Topology, Integration.

2) Functoriality, Mayer–Vietoris, and Poincaré duality

Smooth maps \(f : M \to N\) induce pullback \(f^* : H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(N) \to H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\), making cohomology a contravariant functor. The Mayer–Vietoris sequence for \(M = U \cup V\) is a long exact sequence \[ \cdots \to H^k(M) \to H^k(U) \oplus H^k(V) \to H^k(U \cap V) \to H^{k+1}(M) \to \cdots \] enabling computation by covering decomposition.

Poincaré duality: For compact oriented \(n\)-manifold \(M\), cup product with the fundamental class gives an isomorphism \[ H^k(M) \xrightarrow{\;\smile [M]\;} H_{n-k}(M) \cong H^{n-k}(M)^*. \] Equivalently, \(H^k(M) \cong H^{n-k}(M)^*\) via integration pairing \(\int_M \alpha \wedge \beta\).

Künneth formula

\(H^*(M \times N) \cong H^*(M) \otimes H^*(N)\) (over a field). Computes cohomology of product manifolds from factors.

Lefschetz fixed-point

The Lefschetz number \(\Lambda_f = \sum (-1)^k \mathrm{Tr}(f^*|_{H^k})\) counts fixed points (with multiplicity) of \(f : M \to M\).

3) Čech cohomology

Given an open cover \(\mathcal{U} = \{U_i\}\) of \(M\), the Čech cochain complex \(\check{C}^*(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})\) for a sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) consists of collections of sections over intersections: \[ \check{C}^0 = \prod_i \mathcal{F}(U_i), \quad \check{C}^1 = \prod_{iČech cohomology \(\check{H}^k(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{F})\) measures failure of gluing local data to global sections.

Čech–de Rham comparison: For good covers (every finite intersection contractible), \(\check{H}^k(\mathcal{U}, \mathbb{R}) \cong H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)\). Čech cohomology is the bridge from combinatorics of covers to topology.
Sheaf dependence: Čech cohomology depends on the sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\). Constant sheaf \(\mathbb{R}\) gives topological cohomology; the sheaf of holomorphic functions on a non-algebraic complex manifold can have nontrivial \(H^1\).
Worked example: For \(S^1\) covered by two open arcs \(U, V\) with \(U \cap V\) disconnected, \(\check{H}^1(\mathcal{U}, \mathbb{R}) \cong \mathbb{R}\), detecting the hole.

4) Sheaf cohomology

A sheaf \(\mathcal{F}\) on a topological space \(X\) assigns to each open \(U\) a set (or group, ring) \(\mathcal{F}(U)\) of sections, with restriction maps satisfying gluing axioms. Sheaves encode local-to-global data: holomorphic functions, vector fields, differential forms, and more.

Sheaf cohomology \(H^k(X, \mathcal{F})\) is the derived functor of global sections: \[ H^k(X, \mathcal{F}) = R^k\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}). \] For an injective resolution \(0 \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{I}^0 \to \mathcal{I}^1 \to \cdots\), compute cohomology of the global section complex \(\Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^*)\).

Sheaf \(H^k\) meaning
Constant sheaf \(\mathbb{R}\) Ordinary cohomology \(H^k(X; \mathbb{R})\)
Structure sheaf \(\mathcal{O}_X\) (variety) Algebraic geometry cohomology; controls line bundles
Holomorphic tangent sheaf Deformation theory; \(H^1\) classifies deformations of complex structure

Leray spectral sequence

Computes \(H^*(X, \mathcal{F})\) from cohomology of a cover and cohomology of fibers — the main computational engine.

Derived categories

\(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) packages complexes of sheaves; central to Homological Mirror Symmetry.

5) The cup product and ring structure

The cup product \(\smile : H^k(X) \times H^\ell(X) \to H^{k+\ell}(X)\) gives cohomology a graded ring structure, richer than homology's coalgebra. For de Rham cohomology, the cup product is induced by wedge product of representative forms: \[ [\alpha] \smile [\beta] = [\alpha \wedge \beta]. \]

Properties: The cup product is associative, graded-commutative (\(\alpha \smile \beta = (-1)^{k\ell} \beta \smile \alpha\)), and natural under pullback. The cohomology ring \(H^*(X)\) encodes intersection data.
Intersection theory: For transverse submanifolds \(A, B\) of complementary dimensions, Poincaré duals satisfy \([A] \smile [B] = [A \cap B]\) (up to signs). Quantum cohomology in Symplectic Geometry deforms this product.
Sign conventions: Graded commutativity and orientation choices affect signs in cup products and Poincaré duality. Consistency is essential in index-theoretic computations.

6) Spectral sequences and advanced cohomology theories

Spectral sequences (Serre, Leray, Adams) compute cohomology in stages via successive approximations \(E_r^{p,q}\) converging to \(H^{p+q}\). They are indispensable for fibrations, group extensions, and stable homotopy.

Group cohomology

\(H^*(G; M)\) for a group \(G\) and \(G\)-module \(M\) classifies extensions and obstructions. Connects to Group Theory and Galois representations.

Étale cohomology

Grothendieck's cohomology for varieties over finite fields; Weil conjectures proved by Deligne. Bridge to Fermat's Last Theorem and arithmetic geometry.

Cohomological dimension

\(\mathrm{cd}(G)\) measures complexity of group cohomology; finite groups have \(\mathrm{cd} = 0\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\), while \(\mathbb{Z}\) has \(\mathrm{cd} = 1\).

de Rham–Hodge

On compact Kähler manifolds, harmonic forms represent cohomology classes; Hodge decomposition refines \(H^k\) into \((p,q)\)-types.

7) Research connections and frontier directions

Modern cohomology extends to motivic cohomology, perverse sheaves, \(p\)-adic étale cohomology (see Perfectoid Spaces), and Floer cohomology in symplectic topology.

Mirror symmetry: Cohomology rings (or derived categories) of mirror pairs are related by mirror maps. HMS predicts \(\mathbf{D}^b(X) \cong \mathbf{D}^b(X^\vee)\) rather than mere ring isomorphism.

Related genius-level topics: Homological Mirror Symmetry, Algebraic Topology, Perfectoid Spaces, Symplectic Geometry.
