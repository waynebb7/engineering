1) Mirror symmetry: from physics to mathematics

Mirror symmetry arose in string theory (Candelas et al., 1991): a pair of Calabi–Yau 3-folds \(M\) and \(W\) yield equivalent \(N=2\) supersymmetric \(\sigma\)-models despite different complex and Kähler structures. Cohomology counts match: \(h^{1,1}(M) = h^{2,1}(W)\) (Hodge numbers exchange).

The \(A\)-model on \(M\) computes Gromov–Witten invariants (rational curves, quantum cohomology). The \(B\)-model on \(W\) computes periods of holomorphic 3-forms and variation of Hodge structure. Classical mirror symmetry identifies these partition functions.

Example: The quintic threefold \(X \subset \mathbb{P}^4\) (degree 5) mirrors to a family of quintics in \(\mathbb{P}^4 / (\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z})\). Enumerative predictions (317,206,375 rational curves of degree 5) were confirmed via mirror formulas before rigorous curve counting existed.

Prerequisite: Symplectic Geometry, Cohomology, Algebraic Geometry.

2) Derived categories of coherent sheaves

For a variety \(X\), the bounded derived category \(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) has objects = bounded complexes of coherent sheaves, morphisms = morphisms in the derived category (localizing quasi-isomorphisms). It is the natural setting for cohomological algebra on singular or non-projective spaces.

Triangulated structure: \(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) is triangulated: every morphism extends to a distinguished triangle \(A \to B \to C \to A[1]\). Ext groups \(\mathrm{Ext}^i(A,B) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{D}^b}(A, B[i])\) encode extension data.
Construction Role in HMS
\(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) \(B\)-model category on the mirror \(W\)
Serre functor \(S\) \(S = [n] \otimes \omega_X[n]\); Calabi–Yau condition \(S \cong [n]\)
Stability (Bridgeland) Heart of a stability condition selects physical BPS states
Spherical objects Autoequivalences via spherical twists; mirror monodromy
Triangulated vs \(\infty\)-categorical: Kontsevich's modern formulation promotes triangulated categories to \(\mathcal{A}_\infty\)-categories (dg-categories) to capture higher homotopies lost in triangulated quotients.

3) Fukaya categories and Lagrangian submanifolds

The Fukaya category \(\mathcal{F}(M)\) of a symplectic manifold \(M\) (of real dimension \(2n\)) has:

  • Objects: compact Lagrangian submanifolds \(L\) (with brane structure: orientation, spin\(^c\) structure, bounding condition).
  • Morphisms: \(\mathrm{Hom}(L_0, L_1) = CF^*(L_0, L_1)\), the Floer complex generated by transverse intersection points.
  • Composition: defined by counting holomorphic strips (Floer product via J-holomorphic discs).
\(\mathcal{A}_\infty\) structure: Higher compositions \(m_k : L_0 \times \cdots \times L_{k-1} \to L_k\) satisfy Stasheff's \(A_\infty\) relations. Counting holomorphic polygons with \(k+1\) sides defines \(m_k\).

Wrapped Fukaya category

\(\mathcal{F}^w(M)\) allows non-compact Lagrangians with controlled behaviour at infinity (Liouville manifolds). Essential for toric mirrors and open string sectors.

Graded Lagrangians

A grading by \(\mathbb{Z}\) (or half-integer) fixes morphism degrees and ensures \(\mathcal{F}(M)\) is a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-graded \(A_\infty\)-category.

4) Kontsevich's homological mirror symmetry conjecture

Kontsevich (1994) proposed that mirror symmetry is an equivalence of triangulated (or \(A_\infty\)-) categories:

HMS Conjecture: If \(M\) and \(W\) are mirror Calabi–Yau manifolds, then \[ D^b\mathcal{F}(M) \simeq \mathbf{D}^b(W), \qquad D^b\mathcal{F}^w(M) \simeq \mathbf{D}^b_{\mathrm{dg}}(\mathrm{MF}(W)), \] where \(\mathrm{MF}(W)\) denotes matrix factorizations of the superpotential (Landau–Ginzburg \(B\)-model).

The equivalence should preserve the monoidal structure (tensor product of branes), Serre functor, and stability conditions. It vastly strengthens classical mirror symmetry (cohomology ring isomorphism) by matching all Ext groups and higher compositions.

Status: HMS is proved in several important cases (elliptic curves, K3 surfaces, toric varieties via Strominger–Yau–Zaslow, some hypersurfaces) but remains open in general for Calabi–Yau threefolds.

5) Strominger–Yau–Zaslow and the special Lagrangian picture

The SYZ conjecture (1996) posits that mirror Calabi–Yau 3-folds \(M\) and \(W\) are dual special Lagrangian \(T^3\)-fibrations with mirror fibres related by T-duality. HMS should arise from the category of fibres and monodromy.

Special Lagrangian: A Lagrangian submanifold \(L\) is special if \(\mathrm{Im}(e^{i\theta}\Omega|_L) = 0\) for a holomorphic 3-form \(\Omega\) and phase \(\theta\). Minimal volume in its homology class; central to calibrated geometry.

T-duality

On the mirror torus fibration, complex structure on one side corresponds to Kähler moduli on the other. SYZ explains the Hodge number exchange.

Gross–Siebert program

Reconstructs mirror pairs from tropical / log geometry via scattering diagrams on the base of the SYZ fibration.

7) Research connections and frontier directions

Stability conditions

Bridgeland stability on \(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) organizes wall-crossing of BPS invariants. Mirror stability conditions on \(\mathcal{F}(M)\) should correspond under HMS.

Non-commutative Hodge theory

Ext polynomials and categorical enumerative invariants generalize classical Hodge numbers; conjecturally match GW/open GW generating functions.

Homological projective duality

Kuznetsov's HPD generalizes HMS to pairs of subcategories of \(\mathbf{D}^b(X)\) linked by orthogonal complements with respect to a Lefschetz decomposition.

Arithmetic mirrors

Connections to Perfectoid Spaces and p-adic Hodge theory via periods; mirror symmetry over finite fields is emerging.

Related genius-level topics: Symplectic Geometry, Cohomology, Random Matrices, Algebraic Geometry.
