1) Rational vs irrational

All numbers on the number line belong to the set of real numbers. Real numbers split into two main types:

Rational numbers

Can be written as a fraction a/b where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0.

  • 3/4, −2, 0.75, 0.333…
  • Terminating or recurring decimals

Irrational numbers

Cannot be written as a fraction of two integers.

  • Non-terminating, non-recurring decimals
  • Examples: √2, π, √3
Key test: If a decimal goes on forever and never repeats a block of digits, it is irrational. If it repeats (or stops), it is rational.

2) √2 — the classic irrational

√2 is the length of the diagonal of a square with side length 1. The ancient Greeks proved it cannot be written as a fraction.

  • √2 ≈ 1.41421356… — the digits never terminate or repeat
  • On a calculator: 1.414213562… (rounded)
  • Exact form: √2 — see Radicals
Worked comparison: √2 lies between 1 and 2. Since 1.4² = 1.96 and 1.5² = 2.25, we know 1.4 < √2 < 1.5.
Common mistake: Rounding too early in multi-step problems. Keep √2 in surd form until the final answer unless asked for a decimal.

3) π and other famous irrationals

π (pi) is the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter. It appears throughout engineering — gears, pipes, orbits, and wave calculations.

Number Approximate value Exact form at GCSE
π 3.14159… Leave as π or use given value (e.g. π ≈ 3.14)
√2 1.414… √2
√3 1.732… √3
e (Higher) 2.718… Leave as e if given in the question
Engineering example: A circular disc of radius r has area πr². If r = 5 cm, exact area = 25π cm² ≈ 78.5 cm² (using π ≈ 3.14).

4) Recurring decimals

A recurring decimal has a digit or block of digits that repeats forever. Recurring decimals are always rational — they can be written as fractions.

Decimal Notation As a fraction
0.333… 0.3̇ 1/3
0.1666… 0.16̇ 1/6
0.142857142857… 0.1̇42857̇ 1/7

Converting to a fraction (Higher)

Let x = 0.3̇. Then 10x = 3.3̇, so 10x − x = 3, giving x = 1/3.

Not recurring?

0.101001000100001… never repeats a block — this pattern is irrational.

5) Placing irrationals on the number line

Irrational numbers have an exact position on the number line, even though we cannot write them as simple decimals. We locate them by bounding between nearby rationals.

Strategy: Find two rational numbers that squeeze the irrational.
√10: we know 3² = 9 and 4² = 16, so 3 < √10 < 4.
More tightly: 3.1² = 9.61 and 3.2² = 10.24, so 3.1 < √10 < 3.2.
  • π lies between 3 and 4 (closer to 3.14)
  • √5 lies between 2 and 3 (about 2.24)
  • Order irrationals by squaring (for positive roots) or using a calculator for estimates
Common mistake: Assuming √a + √b = √(a + b). Roots do not add that way — compare decimal estimates or simplify each surd separately.

6) Rounding and bounds

Because irrationals cannot be written exactly as decimals, we use rounding and bounds to work with them in practice. This connects to measurement in science and engineering.

Rounding

Use a given number of decimal places (d.p.) or significant figures (s.f.).

π ≈ 3.142 to 3 d.p.

√2 ≈ 1.41 to 3 s.f.

Bounds (error intervals)

If a length is 5 cm to the nearest cm, the true value lies in 4.5 ≤ L < 5.5.

Upper and lower bounds bracket the actual irrational or measured value.

GCSE link: When a question says “write your answer to 2 significant figures”, you are approximating an exact irrational value. Always keep more precision during working, then round at the end. See also Decimals for rounding rules.
