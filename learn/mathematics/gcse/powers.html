1) What is a power?

A power (or index) tells you how many times a number is multiplied by itself. We write 2³ (read as “two cubed”) to mean 2 × 2 × 2 = 8.

  • The base is the number being multiplied — here, 2
  • The index (or exponent) is the small raised number — here, 3
  • 5² means 5 × 5 = 25
Words you will see: power, index, exponent, squared (²), cubed (³). In GCSE exams, “index” and “power” mean the same thing.

2) Index laws — multiplying and dividing

When the base is the same, you can combine powers using index laws.

Multiplying powers

Add the indices. Keep the base.

aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ

Example: 2³ × 2⁴ = 2⁷ = 128

Dividing powers

Subtract the indices. Keep the base.

aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ

Example: 5⁶ ÷ 5² = 5⁴ = 625

Why it works: 2³ × 2⁴ = (2×2×2) × (2×2×2×2) — three 2s plus four 2s gives seven 2s, so 2⁷.
Common mistake: Adding bases instead of indices. 2³ × 3² cannot be simplified to a single power because the bases differ.

3) Power of a power

When you raise a power to another power, multiply the indices.

(aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ

Worked example: (3²)⁴ = 3²ˣ⁴ = 3⁸ = 6561
Think: (3²)⁴ = (3×3) × (3×3) × (3×3) × (3×3) — eight 3s multiplied together.

Combining laws

(2³)² × 2⁴ = 2⁶ × 2⁴ = 2¹⁰ = 1024

Brackets matter

2³² = 2⁹ but (2³)² = 2⁶. Always work inside brackets first.

4) Zero and negative indices

These rules extend the index laws and appear regularly on Foundation and Higher papers.

Rule Meaning Example
a⁰ = 1 (for a ≠ 0) Any non-zero number to the power zero equals 1 7⁰ = 1, (−3)⁰ = 1
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ A negative index means “one over” 2⁻³ = 1/2³ = 1/8
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ with fractions Flipping a fraction can change the sign of the index (2/3)⁻² = (3/2)² = 9/4
Common mistake: Writing 0⁰ = 1. At GCSE, 0⁰ is undefined — do not use the zero-index rule when the base is zero.

5) Fractional indices (Higher)

Fractional indices link powers to roots — a bridge to Radicals.

  • a^(1/2) = √a — the square root
  • a^(1/3) = ∛a — the cube root
  • a^(m/n) = ⁿ√(aᵐ) — root first, then power (or power first, then root)
Worked example: 8^(2/3) = (∛8)² = 2² = 4
Or: 8^(2/3) = ∛(8²) = ∛64 = 4

6) Standard form (scientific notation)

Standard form writes very large or very small numbers as a × 10ⁿ, where 1 ≤ a < 10 and n is an integer.

Ordinary number Standard form
3 400 000 3.4 × 10⁶
0.000 72 7.2 × 10⁻⁴
Speed of light ≈ 300 000 000 m/s 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s

Multiplying in standard form

Multiply the a values and add the powers of 10.

(2 × 10³) × (4 × 10⁵) = 8 × 10⁸

Dividing in standard form

Divide the a values and subtract the powers of 10.

(6 × 10⁷) ÷ (2 × 10⁴) = 3 × 10³

7) Engineering: area scales as a square

In engineering and physics, many quantities scale with a power of length. If you double a linear dimension, area does not double — it grows by the square.

Key idea: Area ∝ (length)²
A square of side L has area L². If L doubles to 2L, area becomes (2L)² = 4L² — four times as large.
  • A wire of diameter d has cross-sectional area π(d/2)² ∝ d² — doubling diameter quadruples area (and affects resistance inversely)
  • A solar panel of side s collects energy proportional to s²
  • Stress in a beam often involves L² or L³ depending on the cross-section
Exam tip: Volume scales as L³ (a cube). Do not confuse area (²) with volume (³) in scaling questions.
