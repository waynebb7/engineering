1) What is an equation?

An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal. It contains an equals sign (=) and usually includes an unknown — a letter such as x whose value you need to find.

  • x + 5 = 12 — find the value of x
  • 3n − 7 = 20 — find the value of n
  • 2a + 1 = a + 9 — the unknown appears on both sides
Key idea: To solve an equation means to find the value(s) of the unknown that make the statement true. At GCSE Foundation and Higher, you mainly solve linear equations — where the unknown has power 1 (no x²).

This builds on Variables and Orders of Operations (BIDMAS). You will use these skills again when working with Functions and graphs.

2) One-step equations

A one-step equation can be solved with a single inverse operation. Think: "What has been done to the unknown? Do the opposite to both sides."

Addition ↔ subtraction

x + 8 = 15

Subtract 8 from both sides: x = 7

y − 4 = 10

Add 4 to both sides: y = 14

Multiplication ↔ division

5x = 35

Divide both sides by 5: x = 7

n/3 = 6

Multiply both sides by 3: n = 18

Balance rule: Whatever you do to one side of the equation, you must do to the other side. The equals sign stays balanced.

3) Two-step equations

A two-step equation needs two inverse operations. Work in reverse order of BIDMAS: undo addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division (or the other way around depending on the equation).

Worked example: Solve 3x + 5 = 20
Step 1: Subtract 5 from both sides → 3x = 15
Step 2: Divide both sides by 3 → x = 5
Worked example: Solve 4n − 7 = 13
Step 1: Add 7 to both sides → 4n = 20
Step 2: Divide both sides by 4 → n = 5
Common mistake: Dividing only the first term. In 3x + 5 = 20, you cannot write x + 5 = 20/3. Remove the +5 before dividing by 3.

4) Unknown on both sides

When the unknown appears on both sides, collect like terms on one side first. This is common at Higher tier and appears on some Foundation papers.

Worked example: Solve 5x + 2 = 2x + 11
Subtract 2x from both sides → 3x + 2 = 11
Subtract 2 from both sides → 3x = 9
Divide by 3 → x = 3

Strategy

  1. Move all terms with the unknown to one side
  2. Move number terms to the other side
  3. Divide by the coefficient of the unknown

Check

LHS: 5(3) + 2 = 17

RHS: 2(3) + 11 = 17

Both sides match — solution is correct.

5) Equations with brackets

If an equation contains brackets, either expand them first or divide both sides by the number outside the bracket when the whole expression is multiplied.

Method A — expand: Solve 2(x + 3) = 14
Expand: 2x + 6 = 14
Subtract 6: 2x = 8 → x = 4
Method B — divide first: 3(n − 4) = 15
Divide both sides by 3: n − 4 = 5
Add 4: n = 9
Common mistake: Expanding incorrectly. 2(x + 3) means 2x + 6, not 2x + 3. Multiply every term inside the bracket by the number outside.

For equations like 3(2x − 1) = 4x + 5, expand both sides, then collect terms as in section 4.

6) Forming equations from words

GCSE questions often give a real-life situation. Translate the words into algebra, then solve.

Words Algebra
"Think of a number, double it, add 5" 2x + 5
"Consecutive integers" (one after another) x and x + 1
"Perimeter of a rectangle: length 3 more than width" Width w, length w + 3, perimeter 2(w + w + 3)
"Shared equally among n people" Divide total by n
Worked example: Three consecutive integers have a sum of 48. Find them.
Let the integers be x, x + 1, x + 2.
Equation: x + (x + 1) + (x + 2) = 48 → 3x + 3 = 48 → x = 15.
The integers are 15, 16, 17.

7) Checking your solution

Always substitute your answer back into the original equation. Both sides should give the same value. This catches sign errors and arithmetic slips — especially important in exam conditions.

How to check

  1. Write your solution clearly (e.g. x = 4)
  2. Replace every x in the original equation with that value
  3. Work out LHS and RHS separately
  4. Confirm they are equal

Example check

Equation: 2(x + 3) = 14, solution x = 4

LHS: 2(4 + 3) = 2 × 7 = 14

RHS: 14 ✓

Exam tip: If LHS ≠ RHS after substituting, retrace your steps. A wrong answer often means a sign error when moving terms across the equals sign.
