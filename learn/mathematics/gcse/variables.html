1) What is a variable?

A variable is a letter (or symbol) that stands for a number whose value can vary or is unknown. Algebra uses variables to write general rules and solve problems.

  • x, y, n are common variables
  • In 3x + 5, the letter x can represent any number
  • In engineering, V might mean voltage, I current, R resistance
Key idea: A variable is a placeholder. When you know its value, you can substitute and calculate.

2) Algebraic notation

Algebra has shorthand rules that you must read correctly in GCSE questions.

Notation Meaning Example
3x 3 × x (coefficient next to letter) 3 × 4 = 12 when x = 4
xy x × y Two variables multiplied
x² x × x See Powers
x/y x ÷ y A fraction with variables
2(x + 3) 2 × (x + 3) Brackets show grouping
Common mistake: Treating 3x as 34 if x = 4. Always multiply: 3 × 4 = 12.

3) Terms, coefficients, and constants

An expression is built from terms separated by + or − signs.

Example expression: 5x² − 3x + 7

Term

One part of an expression, separated by + or −.

Terms here: 5x², −3x, 7

Coefficient

The number multiplying a variable.

In 5x², the coefficient is 5. In −3x, it is −3.

Constant

A number on its own — no variable attached.

In 5x² − 3x + 7, the constant is 7.

Like terms

Same variable and same power: 3x and 5x are like terms; 3x and 3x² are not.

4) Substitution

Substitution means replacing each variable with a given number, then evaluating.

Worked example: Evaluate 2x² − 3x + 1 when x = 4.
2(4)² − 3(4) + 1 = 2 × 16 − 12 + 1 = 32 − 12 + 1 = 21

Negative values

When x = −2 in x²: (−2)² = 4.

Use brackets: −3x when x = −2 → −3 × (−2) = 6.

Two variables

If x = 3 and y = −1, evaluate 2x + 3y:

2(3) + 3(−1) = 6 − 3 = 3

Common mistake: Forgetting to square before multiplying. 2x² when x = 3 is 2 × 9 = 18, not 6² = 36.

5) Simplifying expressions

Collect like terms to write an expression in its simplest form.

Adding like terms: 4x + 3x = 7x
Subtracting: 8a − 2a + 5 = 6a + 5
Different variables: 3x + 2y cannot be simplified further.

Expanding brackets

3(x + 4) = 3x + 12

−2(3x − 1) = −6x + 2

Factorising (reverse)

Take out a common factor: 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)

Useful when solving Equations.

6) Formulae

A formula (plural: formulae) is an equation that links two or more variables in a rule. You rearrange or substitute into formulae to find unknown values.

Formula Variables Use
V = IR V voltage, I current, R resistance Ohm’s law — electrical engineering
E = mc² E energy, m mass, c speed of light Physics — energy and mass
A = πr² A area, r radius Circle area — exact answer uses π
v = u + at v, u velocities, a acceleration, t time Kinematics — motion problems
Worked example: Ohm’s law V = IR. Find V when I = 0.5 A and R = 12 Ω.
V = 0.5 × 12 = 6 V.

7) Rearranging formulae (changing the subject)

GCSE asks you to rearrange a formula so a different variable becomes the subject (stands alone on the left).

Example: Make r the subject of A = πr².
Divide both sides by π: A/π = r²
Square root both sides: r = √(A/π)

Step-by-step approach

  1. Identify the variable you want
  2. Undo operations in reverse order (SAMDEB: squares, additions, multiplications, divisions, etc.)
  3. Do the same operation to both sides

Another example

Make I the subject of V = IR:

I = V/R

Common mistake: Moving a term without changing its sign. When rearranging y = x − 5 to make x the subject: x = y + 5, not y − 5.
