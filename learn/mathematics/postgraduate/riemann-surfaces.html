1) Riemann surfaces as complex manifolds

A Riemann surface \(X\) is a connected Hausdorff topological space with an atlas of charts \(\{(U_\alpha, \varphi_\alpha)\}\) where \(\varphi_\alpha: U_\alpha \to \mathbb{C}\) are homeomorphisms and transition maps \(\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}\) are holomorphic.

Key idea: Locally every Riemann surface looks like an open subset of \(\mathbb{C}\), but globally it may curve — like a sphere or a torus with a complex structure.
Examples:
  • \(\mathbb{C}\) and the Riemann sphere \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}} = \mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}\)
  • Complex tori \(\mathbb{C}/\Lambda\) for a lattice \(\Lambda\)
  • Algebraic curves \(f(z,w)=0\) in \(\mathbb{C}^2\) — link to Algebraic Geometry

Prerequisite: Complex Numbers, Functions.

2) Holomorphic maps and differentials

A map \(f: X \to Y\) between Riemann surfaces is holomorphic if it is holomorphic in charts. At a point \(p\) where \(f'(p) \neq 0\), \(f\) is a local biholomorphism.

Meromorphic functions: A meromorphic function on \(X\) is holomorphic except at isolated poles. The field of meromorphic functions \(\mathcal{M}(X)\) encodes much of the geometry of \(X\).
Holomorphic 1-forms: Locally \( \omega = f(z)\,dz \). On compact Riemann surfaces, the space of holomorphic 1-forms has dimension equal to the genus \(g\).
Branch points: Multi-valued functions like \(\sqrt{z}\) require a Riemann surface cover to become single-valued holomorphic functions.

3) Uniformization theorem

The uniformization theorem (Koebe, Poincaré) classifies simply connected Riemann surfaces: each is biholomorphic to exactly one of \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\), \(\mathbb{C}\), or the unit disc \(\mathbb{D}\).

Corollary for compact surfaces: Every compact Riemann surface of genus \(g \geq 1\) is a quotient of \(\mathbb{D}\) (genus \(g \geq 2\)) or \(\mathbb{C}\) (genus \(g = 1\), elliptic curves) by a discrete group of automorphisms.
Genus 0: The only compact genus-0 Riemann surface is \(\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\), biholomorphic to any smooth conic in projective space.

4) Genus and the Euler characteristic

For a compact orientable surface, the genus \(g\) is the number of "handles." The Euler characteristic satisfies \(\chi = 2 - 2g\).

Triangulation formula: If a surface is triangulated with \(V\) vertices, \(E\) edges, and \(F\) faces, then \(\chi = V - E + F\).
Worked example: A torus (\(g=1\)) has \(\chi = 0\). A two-holed torus (\(g=2\)) has \(\chi = -2\).

Genus is a biholomorphism invariant for compact Riemann surfaces.

5) Riemann–Roch theorem

For a compact Riemann surface \(X\) of genus \(g\) and a divisor \(D = \sum n_P P\), the Riemann–Roch theorem relates dimensions of spaces of meromorphic functions with prescribed poles and zeros: \[ \ell(D) - \ell(K - D) = \deg(D) - g + 1 \] where \(K\) is a canonical divisor and \(\ell(D) = \dim \mathcal{L}(D)\).

Genus formula: Setting \(D = 0\) gives \(\ell(K) = g\): there are \(g\) independent holomorphic 1-forms.
Degree–genus relation for plane curves: A smooth plane curve of degree \(d\) has genus \(g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}\).
Scope: The full statement requires divisors, line bundles, and Serre duality — central to modern Algebraic Geometry.

6) Elliptic curves

An elliptic curve is a compact Riemann surface of genus 1, equivalently a complex torus \(E = \mathbb{C}/(\mathbb{Z} + \tau\mathbb{Z})\) with \(\Im(\tau) > 0\), or a smooth cubic in \(\mathbb{P}^2\).

Group law: Elliptic curves carry a natural abelian group structure: three collinear points sum to zero. This makes them central to number theory and cryptography.
j-invariant: Two elliptic curves are biholomorphic iff their j-invariants agree. The moduli space of elliptic curves is the modular curve \(Y(1) \cong \mathbb{C}\).

7) Covering spaces and monodromy

Holomorphic maps \(p: \widetilde{X} \to X\) of degree \(d\) are branched covers. The monodromy of a multi-valued function records how analytic continuation loops around branch points permute sheets.

Galois theory link

Covering spaces of compact Riemann surfaces correspond to subgroups of the fundamental group — an analytic analogue of Galois Theory.

Engineering

Branch cuts and Riemann sheets appear in inverse Laplace transforms, filter design, and complex potential theory.

Related: Laplace Transforms, Complex Numbers.
