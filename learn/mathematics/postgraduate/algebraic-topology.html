1) Homotopy and homotopy equivalence

Maps \(f, g : X \to Y\) are homotopic, written \(f \simeq g\), if there exists a continuous \(H : X \times [0,1] \to Y\) with \(H(x,0) = f(x)\) and \(H(x,1) = g(x)\). Homotopy is an equivalence relation on maps.

Spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) are homotopy equivalent (\(X \simeq Y\)) if there exist maps \(f : X \to Y\) and \(g : Y \to X\) with \(g \circ f \simeq \mathrm{id}_X\) and \(f \circ g \simeq \mathrm{id}_Y\).

Key idea: Homotopy equivalence is weaker than homeomorphism but preserves all "algebraic topology" invariants — \(\pi_1\), homology, cohomology.
Example: \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is contractible: \(\mathbb{R}^n \simeq \{*\}\). A coffee cup (genus 1) is homotopy equivalent to a torus but not to a sphere.

Prerequisite: Topology and Group Theory.

2) The fundamental group

A loop based at \(x_0\) is a continuous \(\gamma : [0,1] \to X\) with \(\gamma(0) = \gamma(1) = x_0\). Loops are equivalent up to based homotopy. Concatenation makes \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) a group.

Functoriality: A based map \(f : (X, x_0) \to (Y, y_0)\) induces \(f_* : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(Y, y_0)\). Homotopy equivalent spaces have isomorphic fundamental groups.
Space \(\pi_1\)
\(S^1\) (circle) \(\mathbb{Z}\) (winding number)
\(S^n\), \(n \geq 2\) \(0\) (simply connected)
\(T^2\) (torus) \(\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}\)
\(\mathbb{RP}^2\) \(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\)
Common mistake: \(\pi_1\) depends on the basepoint only up to conjugacy in path-connected spaces. For not-necessarily-simply-connected spaces, changing basepoint conjugates the group.

3) Covering spaces and the Galois correspondence

A covering map \(p : \tilde{X} \to X\) is a local homeomorphism: each point of \(X\) has a neighbourhood evenly covered by disjoint open sheets in \(\tilde{X}\).

Classification theorem: For \(X\) path-connected, locally path-connected, and semi-locally simply connected, connected coverings correspond to subgroups of \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\). The universal cover corresponds to the trivial subgroup.
Lifting property: A map \(f : Y \to X\) lifts to \(\tilde{f} : Y \to \tilde{X}\) (with \(p \circ \tilde{f} = f\)) iff \(f_*(\pi_1(Y)) \subseteq p_*(\pi_1(\tilde{X}))\).

Deck transformations

Automorphisms of \(\tilde{X}\) over \(X\) form a group isomorphic to \(\pi_1(X)/p_*(\pi_1(\tilde{X}))\) for the universal cover.

Riemann surfaces

Branched covers of \(\mathbb{C}\) or \(S^2\) arise in complex analysis. Monodromy encodes \(\pi_1\) via analytic continuation.

4) Singular homology

The singular \(n\)-simplex in \(X\) is a continuous map \(\sigma : \Delta^n \to X\). The chain group \(C_n(X)\) is the free abelian group on singular \(n\)-simplices. The boundary operator \(\partial : C_n \to C_{n-1}\) satisfies \(\partial^2 = 0\).

Homology groups: \[ H_n(X) = \ker \partial_n / \operatorname{im} \partial_{n+1} = Z_n(X) / B_n(X) \] \(H_n(X)\) counts \(n\)-dimensional "holes". \(H_0(X)\) counts path components.
Examples: \(H_n(S^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}\) for \(n = n\), else 0. \(H_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2\). \(H_2(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z}\).
Convention: Homology is computed with coefficients in \(\mathbb{Z}\) by default. \(H_n(X; \mathbb{Q})\) or \(H_n(X; \mathbb{F}_p)\) are obtained by tensoring or changing coefficients.

5) Exact sequences and Mayer–Vietoris

A sequence of groups \(\cdots \to A_{n+1} \to A_n \to A_{n-1} \to \cdots\) is exact at \(A_n\) if \(\operatorname{im} = \ker\) at that term. Short exact sequences \(0 \to A \to B \to C \to 0\) are fundamental computational tools.

Mayer–Vietoris sequence: For \(X = U \cup V\) with \(U, V\) open, \[ \cdots \to H_n(U \cap V) \to H_n(U) \oplus H_n(V) \to H_n(X) \to H_{n-1}(U \cap V) \to \cdots \] computes homology from pieces.
Long exact sequence of a pair: For \((X, A)\) with \(A \subseteq X\) a subspace, \[ \cdots \to H_n(A) \to H_n(X) \to H_n(X, A) \to H_{n-1}(A) \to \cdots \]

Exact sequences are the engine of algebraic topology: they propagate local information to global invariants.

6) Degree, fixed points, and higher homotopy

For \(f : S^n \to S^n\), the degree \(\deg(f) \in \mathbb{Z}\) counts how many times \(f\) wraps the sphere, signed by orientation. \(\deg\) is a homotopy invariant.

Brouwer fixed-point theorem: Every continuous map \(f : D^n \to D^n\) has a fixed point. Proof via degree or homology: the identity map \(S^{n-1} \to S^{n-1}\) has degree 1, but a fixed-point-free map would induce a retraction, impossible.
Higher homotopy groups: \(\pi_n(X, x_0)\) for \(n \geq 2\) are abelian. \(\pi_n(S^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}\). Computing \(\pi_n\) for \(n \geq 2\) is notoriously hard — most are unknown for simple spaces like \(S^2\).

Cohomology

\(H^n(X) = \operatorname{Hom}(H_n(X), \mathbb{Z})\) (universal coefficient theorem). Cup product gives ring structure. de Rham cohomology for smooth manifolds.

Characteristic classes

Chern, Stiefel–Whitney, and Pontryagin classes classify vector bundles. Obstructions to global sections and framings.

7) Applications and research outlook

Manifold classification

Homology distinguishes spheres from tori. Simply connected closed 4-manifolds are classified by intersection forms (Freedman). Smooth 4-manifolds remain mysterious.

Knot invariants

Knot complements \(S^3 \setminus K\) have fundamental group. Alexander, Jones, and HOMFLY polynomials arise from representations and categorification.

Topological data analysis

Persistent homology tracks birth/death of topological features across scales in data. Vietoris–Rips complexes approximate shape of point clouds.

Physics

Topological phases of matter, gauge bundles, and anomalies are classified by cohomology and K-theory. The hairy ball theorem forbids nowhere-zero vector fields on \(S^2\).

Related: Differential Geometry, Homotopy, Cohomology.
