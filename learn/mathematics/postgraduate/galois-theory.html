1) Field extensions and degrees

A field extension \(L/K\) is a field \(L\) containing a subfield isomorphic to \(K\). The degree \([L:K]\) is the dimension of \(L\) as a \(K\)-vector space.

Tower law: For \(K \subseteq M \subseteq L\), \([L:K] = [L:M][M:K]\).
Simple extensions: If \(\alpha\) is algebraic over \(K\) with minimal polynomial \(m_\alpha(x) \in K[x]\) of degree \(n\), then \(K(\alpha) \cong K[x]/(m_\alpha)\) and \([K(\alpha):K] = n\).

Prerequisite: Polynomials, Vector Spaces.

2) Splitting fields and normal extensions

The splitting field of \(f(x) \in K[x]\) is the smallest extension where \(f\) splits into linear factors. It is unique up to \(K\)-isomorphism.

Normal extensions: \(L/K\) is normal if every irreducible polynomial in \(K[x]\) with a root in \(L\) splits completely in \(L\). Splitting fields of finite families are normal and finite.
Separability: An extension is separable if minimal polynomials have no repeated roots (equivalently, formal derivative nonzero at roots). In characteristic 0, every algebraic extension is separable.
Galois extensions: Finite extensions that are both normal and separable — the setting for classical Galois theory.

3) Galois groups

The Galois group \(\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\) of a Galois extension consists of field automorphisms \(\sigma: L \to L\) fixing \(K\) pointwise. It is a group under composition.

Example: For \(L = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})\), \(\mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q}) = \{id, \sigma\}\) where \(\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = -\sqrt{2}\) — isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\).
Order: For a Galois extension, \(|\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)| = [L:K]\). The group acts on roots of polynomials and permutes them.
Fixed field: For a subgroup \(H \leq \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\), the fixed field \(L^H = \{x \in L : \sigma(x) = x\ \forall \sigma \in H\}\).

4) Fundamental theorem of Galois theory

For a Galois extension \(L/K\), there is a bijective order-reversing correspondence between intermediate fields \(K \subseteq E \subseteq L\) and subgroups \(H \leq \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\): \[ E \longleftrightarrow \mathrm{Gal}(L/E), \qquad H \longleftrightarrow L^H \]

Normal subgroups: \(E/K\) is normal iff \(\mathrm{Gal}(L/E) \trianglelefteq \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)\). Then \(\mathrm{Gal}(E/K) \cong \mathrm{Gal}(L/K) / \mathrm{Gal}(L/E)\).
Worked example: For the splitting field of \(x^3 - 2\) over \(\mathbb{Q}\), the Galois group is \(S_3\). Subgroups correspond to intermediate fields including \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2})\), \(\mathbb{Q}(\omega)\), and \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)\).

5) Solvability by radicals

A polynomial is solvable by radicals if its roots can be expressed using field operations and \(n\)th roots. Galois characterised this via the Galois group.

Solvability criterion: A polynomial over a field of characteristic 0 is solvable by radicals iff its Galois group is a solvable group (has a subnormal series with abelian quotients).
Abel–Ruffini / Galois: The general quintic is not solvable by radicals because \(S_5\) is not solvable. Degree \(\leq 4\) polynomials are always solvable by radicals.
Distinction: Some quintics are solvable by radicals (e.g. \(x^5 - 1\)); the theorem applies to the general polynomial of degree \(n\).

6) Finite fields and cyclotomic extensions

Every finite field has order \(p^n\) for a prime \(p\) and is unique up to isomorphism, denoted \(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}\) or \(\mathrm{GF}(p^n)\).

Construction: \(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}\) is the splitting field of \(x^{p^n} - x\) over \(\mathbb{F}_p\). The multiplicative group \(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}^*\) is cyclic of order \(p^n - 1\).
Cyclotomic extensions: \(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)\) where \(\zeta_n = e^{2\pi i/n}\) has Galois group \((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^*\). These extensions underpin constructibility and coding theory.

7) Constructibility and modern connections

Classical problems

Doubling the cube, trisecting the angle, and squaring the circle are impossible with ruler and compass — proved via degree \([ \mathbb{Q}(\alpha):\mathbb{Q} ]\) not a power of 2.

Modern mathematics

Galois theory connects to covering spaces on Riemann Surfaces, étale cohomology in Algebraic Geometry, and inverse Galois problems.
