1) The complex plane and analytic functions

A complex number \(z = x + iy\) lives in \(\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2\). A function \(f : D \subseteq \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}\) is analytic (holomorphic) on an open set \(D\) if it is complex differentiable at every point of \(D\): \[ f'(z) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z+h) - f(z)}{h} \] exists (as a complex limit).

Key idea: Holomorphic functions are extraordinarily rigid. Once differentiable on an open set, they are infinitely differentiable, equal to their Taylor series locally, and determined globally by values on any set with a limit point.

Prerequisite: Complex Numbers, Real Analysis, and Holomorphic Functions.

2) Cauchy–Riemann equations

Write \(f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y)\). If \(f\) is holomorphic, then \(u\) and \(v\) satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations: \[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}, \qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x} \]

Sufficient condition: If \(u, v\) have continuous first partial derivatives on \(D\) and satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations, then \(f = u + iv\) is holomorphic on \(D\).
Worked example: \(f(z) = z^2 = (x^2 - y^2) + i(2xy)\). Check: \(u_x = 2x = v_y\), \(u_y = -2y = -v_x\). Holomorphic everywhere.
Common mistake: Satisfying Cauchy–Riemann at a single point is insufficient. Holomorphy requires an open neighbourhood of differentiability and (for the sufficient condition) continuity of partial derivatives.

3) Cauchy's integral theorem and formula

For a simple closed contour \(\gamma\) in a domain \(D\) and \(f\) holomorphic in \(D\), \[ \oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 0 \] (Cauchy's integral theorem). If \(f\) is holomorphic inside and on \(\gamma\), then for \(z_0\) in the interior: \[ f(z_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z - z_0}\,dz \]

Cauchy estimates: If \(|f(z)| \leq M\) on \(|z - z_0| = R\), then \[ |f^{(n)}(z_0)| \leq \frac{n!\, M}{R^n} \] Holomorphic functions are smooth; derivatives grow at most factorially.

4) Laurent series and singularities

In an annulus \(r < |z - z_0| < R\), a holomorphic function has a Laurent series: \[ f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} a_n (z - z_0)^n \] Isolated singularities at \(z_0\) are classified as removable, poles, or essential.

Pole of order \(m\): Laurent series has finitely many negative powers; leading term \((z-z_0)^{-m}\). Essential singularity: infinitely many negative powers (Casorati–Weierstrass: image is dense near \(z_0\)).
Residue theorem: \[ \oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum \operatorname{Res}(f, z_k) \] where the sum is over poles \(z_k\) inside \(\gamma\). Primary tool for evaluating real integrals.

Computing residues

Simple pole: \(\operatorname{Res}(f, z_0) = \lim_{z \to z_0}(z-z_0)f(z)\). Pole order \(m\): use derivative formula or Laurent expansion.

Meromorphic functions

Holomorphic except for isolated poles on \(\mathbb{C}\) or the Riemann sphere. Rational functions are meromorphic on \(\mathbb{C}\).

5) Conformal mappings and the Riemann mapping theorem

A holomorphic function \(f\) with \(f'(z_0) \neq 0\) is conformal at \(z_0\): it preserves angles and orientation locally. Conformal maps solve Dirichlet problems and design airfoils (Joukowski transform).

Möbius transformations: \(T(z) = \dfrac{az + b}{cz + d}\) with \(ad - bc \neq 0\) map the Riemann sphere to itself conformally. Three points determine a unique Möbius map.
Riemann mapping theorem: Every simply connected proper subdomain of \(\mathbb{C}\) is conformally equivalent to the open unit disc \(\mathbb{D}\). The map is unique up to automorphisms of \(\mathbb{D}\).
Convention: Conformality requires \(f'(z) \neq 0\). At zeros of \(f'\), the map is not locally injective (folding occurs).

6) Harmonic functions and Dirichlet problems

If \(f = u + iv\) is holomorphic, then \(u\) and \(v\) are harmonic: \(\Delta u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0\). Conversely, on a simply connected domain, every harmonic \(u\) is the real part of some holomorphic \(f\).

Poisson integral formula: Solves the Dirichlet problem on the disc. For \(|z| < r\), \[ u(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} u(re^{i\theta})\, \operatorname{Re}\left(\frac{re^{i\theta} + z}{re^{i\theta} - z}\right) d\theta \]
Maximum principle (harmonic): A non-constant harmonic function on a bounded domain attains its maximum and minimum only on the boundary.

Harmonic analysis on \(\mathbb{C}\) connects to PDEs, potential theory, and fluid dynamics (velocity potential of irrotational flow).

7) Applications and research outlook

Signal processing

Analytic signals \(f + i\mathcal{H}f\) (Hilbert transform) encode amplitude and phase. Used in communications and seismic analysis.

Number theory

The Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) is meromorphic on \(\mathbb{C}\). The Riemann Hypothesis concerns zeros on \(\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2\).

Several complex variables

Holomorphic functions of several variables \(f(z_1, \ldots, z_n)\) have richer theory: Hartogs phenomena, domains of holomorphy, and pseudoconvexity.

Riemann surfaces

Multi-valued functions (e.g. \(\sqrt{z}\), \(\log z\)) live on branched covers. See Riemann Surfaces and Differential Geometry.

Related: Holomorphic Functions, Topology, Fourier Series.
