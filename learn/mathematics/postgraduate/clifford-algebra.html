1) Quadratic forms and the Clifford construction

Let \(V\) be a vector space over \(k\) with quadratic form \(q: V \to k\). The Clifford algebra \(\mathrm{Cl}(V, q)\) is the quotient of the tensor algebra \(T(V)\) by the two-sided ideal generated by: \[ v \otimes v - q(v) \cdot 1, \quad v \in V \] Equivalently, \(\mathrm{Cl}(V,q) = k \oplus V \oplus V^{\otimes 2} \oplus \cdots\) with relation \(v^2 = q(v)\).

Key idea: Clifford algebras linearise the quadratic relation \(v^2 = q(v)\), encoding inner products and orientations in a single associative algebra.
Examples:
  • \(\mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{R}^n, -\|\cdot\|^2) \cong \mathbb{C}l_n\) — complex Clifford algebra.
  • \(\mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{R}, x^2) \cong \mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}\) (split).
  • \(\mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{R}^2, -\|\cdot\|^2) \cong \mathbb{H}\) — the quaternions.

Prerequisite: Vector Spaces and Matrices.

2) Grading and geometric product

\(\mathrm{Cl}(V,q)\) decomposes as \(\bigoplus_{k=0}^{\dim V} \Lambda^k V\) when \(q\) is nondegenerate. The geometric product combines inner and outer (wedge) products: \[ ab = a \cdot b + a \wedge b \] for vectors \(a, b\) (with sign conventions depending on signature).

Dimension: \(\dim \mathrm{Cl}(V,q) = 2^{\dim V}\). Basis elements are \(e_{i_1}\cdots e_{i_k}\) for increasing multi-indices.
Convention: Signature matters. \(\mathrm{Cl}_{p,q}(\mathbb{R})\) uses \(e_i^2 = +1\) for \(p\) directions and \(e_i^2 = -1\) for \(q\) directions.

3) Pin and Spin groups

The Clifford group \(\Gamma(V,q)\) consists of invertible elements that conjugate \(V\) to itself. The Pin group \(\mathrm{Pin}(V,q)\) is its subgroup generated by unit vectors \(v\) with \(q(v) = \pm 1\).

Spin group: \(\mathrm{Spin}(V,q) = \mathrm{Pin}(V,q) \cap \mathrm{Cl}^{\mathrm{even}}(V,q)\), the double cover of \(\mathrm{SO}(V,q)\) when \(\dim V \geq 2\) and \(q\) is definite. \[ 1 \to \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \to \mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n) \to 1 \]
Worked example: \(\mathrm{Spin}(3) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)\), explaining the double-cover relationship in quantum mechanics for spin-\(\tfrac{1}{2}\) particles.

4) Spinors and representations

Spinors are elements of minimal left (or right) ideals in \(\mathrm{Cl}(V,q)\), or equivalently vectors in irreducible modules for \(\mathrm{Spin}(V,q)\). They transform under \(\mathrm{Spin}\), not \(\mathrm{SO}\), picking up a sign under \(2\pi\) rotation.

Key fact: A spinor requires \(720^\circ\) rotation to return to itself — the hallmark of spin-\(\tfrac{1}{2}\) representation theory.
Weyl, Dirac, Majorana: In Lorentzian signature, spinor types are classified by reality conditions on Clifford modules; Dirac spinors are complex, Majorana spinors are real where possible.

Related: Lie Algebras and Group Theory.

Periodicity: Bott periodicity gives \(\mathrm{Cl}_{p+8,q} \cong \mathrm{M}_{16}(\mathrm{Cl}_{p,q})\), classifying all real Clifford algebras.

6) Geometric algebra in physics and engineering

Geometric algebra (Clifford algebra applied to \(\mathbb{R}^{3,0}\) or \(\mathbb{R}^{3,1}\)) unifies scalars, vectors, bivectors (oriented areas), and trivectors (oriented volumes).

Rotations

Rotors \(R = e^{-\frac{\theta}{2}B}\) with bivector \(B\) implement rotations more compactly than matrices.

Electromagnetism

Maxwell's equations compress into a single equation \(\nabla F = J\) in spacetime algebra \(\mathrm{Cl}_{3,1}\).

Computer graphics

Rotor interpolation and conformal geometric algebra \(\mathrm{Cl}_{4,1}\) handle rigid and similarity transforms.

Quantum mechanics

Pauli and Dirac matrices are representations of Clifford algebras in low dimensions.

7) Advanced connections

Index theory

Dirac operators on spin manifolds use Clifford bundles; the Atiyah–Singer index theorem couples analysis and topology.

K-theory

Clifford module classifications appear in topological K-theory and Bott periodicity proofs.
