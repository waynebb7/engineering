1) Homotopy of maps

Let \(X, Y\) be topological spaces and \(f, g : X \to Y\) continuous maps. A homotopy between \(f\) and \(g\) is a continuous map \(H : X \times [0,1] \to Y\) such that \(H(x,0) = f(x)\) and \(H(x,1) = g(x)\) for all \(x \in X\). We write \(f \simeq g\) and say \(f\) and \(g\) are homotopic.

Key idea: Homotopy deforms one map into another continuously. It is an equivalence relation on \(\mathrm{Map}(X,Y)\). Homotopic maps induce the same maps on homotopy groups and homology.

Prerequisite: Topology and Homeomorphism.

2) Homotopy equivalence

Spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) are homotopy equivalent, written \(X \simeq Y\), if there exist maps \(f : X \to Y\) and \(g : Y \to X\) such that \(g \circ f \simeq \mathrm{id}_X\) and \(f \circ g \simeq \mathrm{id}_Y\). Such \(f\) is a homotopy equivalence.

Worked example: \(\mathbb{R}\) is homotopy equivalent to a point \(\{*\}\). Define \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \{*\}\), \(g(*) = 0\). Then \(g \circ f\) collapses \(\mathbb{R}\) to \(0\), homotopic to identity via \(H(x,t) = (1-t)x\).
Worked example: The circle \(S^1\) is not homotopy equivalent to a point — it has nontrivial fundamental group \(\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}\).
Hierarchy: Homeomorphic \(\Rightarrow\) homotopy equivalent, but not conversely. \(S^1 \times \mathbb{R}\) (cylinder) \(\simeq S^1\) but not homeomorphic to \(S^1\).

3) The fundamental group \(\pi_1\)

Fix a basepoint \(x_0 \in X\). The fundamental group \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) consists of homotopy classes of loops based at \(x_0\), with operation concatenation \([\gamma] \cdot [\delta] = [\gamma * \delta]\).

  • Identity: constant loop at \(x_0\)
  • Inverse: traverse loop in reverse
  • Group axioms follow from homotopy invariance of concatenation
Worked example: \(\pi_1(S^1, x_0) \cong \mathbb{Z}\). The winding number of a loop classifies homotopy classes. The map \(t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}\) generates the group.
Van Kampen theorem: If \(X = U \cup V\) with \(U, V, U \cap V\) open and path-connected, then \(\pi_1(X)\) is an amalgamated free product of \(\pi_1(U)\) and \(\pi_1(V)\) over \(\pi_1(U \cap V)\).

Related: Groupoids for the fundamental groupoid viewpoint.

4) Functoriality and homotopy invariance

A continuous map \(f : (X, x_0) \to (Y, y_0)\) induces a homomorphism \(f_* : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(Y, y_0)\) by \(f_*([\gamma]) = [f \circ \gamma]\). If \(f \simeq g\) (rel basepoint), then \(f_* = g_*\).

Worked example: The inclusion \(S^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}\) induces isomorphism on \(\pi_1\) — both have \(\mathbb{Z}\). The punctured plane deformation-retracts onto \(S^1\).
Retraction: If \(r : X \to A\) is a retraction and \(A\) is a deformation retract of \(X\), then \(i_* : \pi_1(A) \to \pi_1(X)\) is an isomorphism. Example: \(S^1\) is a retract of \(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}\).

5) Covering spaces and lifting

A covering map \(p : \tilde{X} \to X\) is a local homeomorphism with evenly covered neighbourhoods. The universal cover \(\tilde{X}\) is simply connected and classifies coverings via \(\pi_1(X)\).

Worked example: \(\mathbb{R} \to S^1\), \(t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}\), is the universal cover. Lifting loops to \(\mathbb{R}\) recovers the winding number as endpoint displacement.
Lifting criterion: A map \(f : Y \to X\) lifts to \(\tilde{X}\) iff \(f_*(\pi_1(Y))\) lies in the subgroup corresponding to the cover. This links algebra (\(\pi_1\) subgroups) to topology (covers).

6) Higher homotopy and obstruction theory

The \(n\)-th homotopy group \(\pi_n(X, x_0)\) generalises loops to maps \(S^n \to X\). \(\pi_n\) is abelian for \(n \geq 2\). The Hurewicz theorem relates \(\pi_n\) to homology \(H_n\) for \((n-1)\)-connected spaces.

Whitehead theorem

Between CW-complexes, a map inducing isomorphisms on all \(\pi_n\) is a homotopy equivalence (under mild connectedness hypotheses).

Fibrations

Serre fibrations yield long exact sequences of homotopy groups — the engine of spectral sequence computations in algebraic topology.

Related: Vector Calculus for de Rham cohomology analogues.

7) Applications and research directions

Robotics: Configuration spaces of robot arms are manifolds with nontrivial \(\pi_1\) — path planning must account for homotopy classes of trajectories.
Condensed matter: Topological phases are classified by homotopy groups of order parameters (e.g. \(\pi_3(S^2)\) for Hopf insulators).
Homotopy type theory: Identifies types with spaces and equality with paths — a foundational programme unifying logic, topology, and computer proof assistants.
Common mistake: Confusing homotopy of loops (elements of \(\pi_1\)) with homotopy of maps between spaces. The word "homotopy" applies at several categorical levels.
