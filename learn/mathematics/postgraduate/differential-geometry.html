1) Smooth manifolds and coordinate charts

An \(n\)-dimensional smooth manifold \(M\) is a Hausdorff, second-countable topological space locally homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}^n\), with a smooth atlas: a collection of charts \((U_\alpha, \varphi_\alpha)\) where \(\varphi_\alpha : U_\alpha \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is a homeomorphism and transition maps \(\varphi_\beta \circ \varphi_\alpha^{-1}\) are \(C^\infty\).

Examples: \(\mathbb{R}^n\), spheres \(S^n\), tori \(T^n\), projective spaces \(\mathbb{RP}^n\), Lie groups \(SO(n)\), \(SU(n)\).
Key idea: Manifolds are spaces that look locally like \(\mathbb{R}^n\) but may have global topology (curvature, holes, compactness) invisible in any single chart.

Prerequisite: Topology, Multivariable Calculus, and Smooth Manifolds.

2) Tangent spaces and the differential

The tangent space \(T_p M\) at \(p \in M\) is the vector space of derivations at \(p\): linear maps \(v : C^\infty(M) \to \mathbb{R}\) satisfying the Leibniz rule \(v(fg) = f(p)v(g) + g(p)v(f)\).

For a smooth map \(F : M \to N\), the differential \(dF_p : T_p M \to T_{F(p)} N\) is defined by \((dF_p(v))(f) = v(f \circ F)\).

Coordinate basis: In chart \((U, \varphi)\) with coordinates \(x^i\), \(\{\partial/\partial x^i|_p\}\) forms a basis of \(T_p M\).
Common mistake: Tangent vectors are not "arrows in ambient space" unless \(M\) is embedded in \(\mathbb{R}^N\). Intrinsically, they are derivations.

3) Vector fields, flows, and Lie brackets

A vector field \(X\) assigns \(X_p \in T_p M\) to each \(p\). Integral curves satisfy \(\dot{\gamma}(t) = X_{\gamma(t)}\). The flow \(\Phi_t^X\) of \(X\) gives a local diffeomorphism of \(M\).

Lie bracket: \([X, Y](f) = X(Yf) - Y(Xf)\). Vector fields form a Lie algebra under \([\cdot, \cdot]\). \([X,Y] = 0\) iff the flows commute (locally).
Frobenius theorem: A distribution (subbundle of \(TM\)) is integrable (tangent to submanifolds) iff it is closed under the Lie bracket.

Exterior derivative

\(d : \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M)\) satisfies \(d^2 = 0\). Stokes' theorem \(\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega\) generalizes the fundamental theorem of calculus.

de Rham cohomology

\(H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) = \ker d / \operatorname{im} d\) on closed forms. Links differential forms to topology via the de Rham theorem.

4) Riemannian metrics and connections

A Riemannian metric \(g\) is a smoothly varying inner product on each \(T_p M\). In coordinates, \(g = g_{ij}\,dx^i \otimes dx^j\) with positive-definite symmetric \(g_{ij}\).

Levi-Civita connection: The unique torsion-free connection \(\nabla\) compatible with \(g\). Christoffel symbols: \[ \Gamma^k_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} g^{k\ell}\left(\partial_i g_{j\ell} + \partial_j g_{i\ell} - \partial_\ell g_{ij}\right) \]
Parallel transport: A vector field \(V\) along a curve \(\gamma\) is parallel if \(\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}} V = 0\). Parallel transport depends on the metric and connection.
Convention: Einstein summation convention applies throughout. Lower indices denote components of covectors; upper indices denote vector components.

5) Curvature

The Riemann curvature tensor measures failure of second covariant derivatives to commute: \[ R(X,Y)Z = \nabla_X \nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y \nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z \]

Sectional curvature: For a 2-plane \(\sigma \subset T_p M\), \[ K(\sigma) = \frac{g(R(X,Y)Y, X)}{g(X,X)g(Y,Y) - g(X,Y)^2} \] where \(\{X,Y\}\) spans \(\sigma\).
Gauss–Bonnet theorem: For a compact oriented surface \(M\), \[ \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi \chi(M) \] linking curvature (local) to Euler characteristic (global topology).

Constant curvature

\(K = 1\) (sphere), \(K = 0\) (flat torus or plane), \(K = -1\) (hyperbolic plane). Models of non-Euclidean geometry.

Ricci and scalar curvature

Contractions of \(R\): Ric\((X,Y) = \operatorname{tr}(Z \mapsto R(Z,X)Y)\), \(S = \operatorname{tr}(\text{Ric})\). Central in Einstein's field equations.

6) Geodesics and the exponential map

A geodesic \(\gamma\) satisfies \(\nabla_{\dot{\gamma}} \dot{\gamma} = 0\) — the generalization of straight lines. Locally, geodesics minimize (or extremize) arc length.

Exponential map: \(\exp_p : T_p M \to M\) sends \(v\) to \(\gamma_v(1)\) where \(\gamma_v\) is the geodesic with \(\gamma_v(0) = p\), \(\dot{\gamma}_v(0) = v\). Locally a diffeomorphism near 0.
Hopf–Rinow theorem: On a complete Riemannian manifold, geodesics can be extended indefinitely, and any two points are joined by a minimizing geodesic.

Geodesic deviation encodes tidal forces in GR: nearby geodesics accelerate relative to each other via curvature.

7) Applications and research outlook

General relativity

Spacetime is a 4-dimensional Lorentzian manifold. Einstein's equation \(G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}\) relates curvature to stress-energy.

Gauge theory

Connections on principal bundles generalize \(\nabla\). Curvature is the field strength. Yang–Mills theory lives in this framework.

Complex geometry

Kähler manifolds combine complex, symplectic, and Riemannian structure. Links to Complex Analysis and Holomorphic Functions.

Geometric analysis

Ricci flow (Perelman), minimal surfaces, and harmonic maps use PDE methods on manifolds to prove topological results.

Related: Topology, Algebraic Topology, Tensors.
