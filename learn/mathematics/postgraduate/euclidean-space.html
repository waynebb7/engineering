1) Definition of Euclidean space \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Euclidean \(n\)-space \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the set of \(n\)-tuples \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)\) with the standard Euclidean metric: \[ d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2} \] The induced topology (open balls \(B_r(\mathbf{p}) = \{\mathbf{x} : \|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{p}\| < r\}\)) makes \(\mathbb{R}^n\) a complete metric space and a topological vector space.

Key idea: \(\mathbb{R}^n\) simultaneously carries algebraic (vector space), metric (distance), and topological (open sets) structure. Postgraduate work requires keeping these layers distinct while using them together.

Prerequisite: Vectors, Cartesian Coordinates.

2) Inner product, norm, and angles

The standard inner product on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is \[ \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i \] with \(\|\mathbf{x}\| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x} \rangle}\). The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality states \(|\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|\,\|\mathbf{y}\|\), with equality iff \(\mathbf{x}\) and \(\mathbf{y}\) are linearly dependent.

Angle: For nonzero \(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\), \[ \cos\theta = \frac{\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle}{\|\mathbf{x}\|\,\|\mathbf{y}\|} \] Vectors are orthogonal when \(\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = 0\).
Worked example: \(\mathbf{u} = (1,1,0)\), \(\mathbf{v} = (1,-1,1)\). \(\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0\), so they are orthogonal in \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

3) Orthogonality, projections, and orthonormal bases

A set \(\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}\) is orthonormal if \(\langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}\). The Gram–Schmidt process converts a linearly independent set into an orthonormal basis of its span.

Orthogonal projection: The projection of \(\mathbf{x}\) onto a subspace \(W\) with orthonormal basis \(\{\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_k\}\) is \[ \mathrm{proj}_W(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^k \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{u}_i \rangle \mathbf{u}_i \] This minimises \(\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{w}\|\) over \(\mathbf{w} \in W\).
Parseval's identity: If \(\{\mathbf{e}_i\}\) is an orthonormal basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\), then \(\|\mathbf{x}\|^2 = \sum_i \langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{e}_i \rangle^2\).

Related: Vector Spaces, Hilbert Spaces (infinite-dimensional analogue).

4) Linear isometries and the orthogonal group

A linear map \(T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is an isometry if \(\|T\mathbf{x}\| = \|\mathbf{x}\|\) for all \(\mathbf{x}\). Equivalently, \(T\) preserves the inner product. Such maps form the orthogonal group \(O(n)\).

Characterisation: \(T\) is an isometry iff its matrix \(\mathbf{Q}\) satisfies \(\mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}\). Determinant \(\det(\mathbf{Q}) = \pm 1\); the subgroup \(SO(n)\) has determinant \(+1\) (rotations).
Worked example: Rotation in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) by angle \(\theta\): \[ \mathbf{R}_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix} \in SO(2) \] Reflections and rotary reflections are also \(O(n)\) elements with \(\det = -1\).
Convention: \(O(n)\) acts on column vectors by left multiplication. Active vs passive transformations should be stated explicitly in physics applications.

5) Topology and completeness of \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Open sets in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) are unions of open balls. A sequence \(\{\mathbf{x}_k\}\) converges to \(\mathbf{L}\) if \(\|\mathbf{x}_k - \mathbf{L}\| \to 0\). \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is complete: every Cauchy sequence converges — the foundation for analysis on Euclidean space.

Heine–Borel: A subset of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is compact iff it is closed and bounded. This fails in infinite dimensions (unit ball non-compact in Hilbert space).
Connectedness: \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is path-connected and simply connected for \(n \geq 1\). Removing a point leaves \(\mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{\mathbf{p}\}\) homotopy equivalent to \(S^{n-1}\).

Related: Homeomorphism, Limits.

6) Applications in engineering and physics

Least squares

Overdetermined systems \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} \approx \mathbf{b}\) are solved by orthogonal projection onto \(\mathrm{Col}(\mathbf{A})\). Normal equations \(\mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}^T\mathbf{b}\) encode this geometry.

Rigid-body kinematics

SE(3) = semidirect product of \(SO(3)\) and translations describes rigid motions in \(\mathbb{R}^3\). Screw theory and robotics rely on this structure.

Signal processing

Fourier analysis on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) uses inner products on \(L^2\) spaces; discrete versions live on \(\mathbb{R}^N\) with dot products.

Optimisation

Gradient descent, convex sets, and separating hyperplanes are geometric statements in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the Euclidean metric (or weighted variants).

Related: Matrices, Optimization.

7) Generalisations beyond flat space

Euclidean space is the simply connected, complete, flat Riemannian manifold of dimension \(n\) (curvature zero). Replacing the metric or allowing curvature leads to:

  • Minkowski space \(\mathbb{R}^{3,1}\) — indefinite inner product for special relativity
  • Riemannian manifolds — locally modelled on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with a smoothly varying inner product
  • Normed spaces — distance without necessarily an inner product (e.g. \(\ell^1\), \(\ell^\infty\))
Research context: Euclidean buildings, crystallographic groups, and lattice packings connect \(\mathbb{R}^n\) geometry to number theory and materials science. Quantisation and wave mechanics use \(L^2(\mathbb{R}^n)\) as the canonical Hilbert space.
