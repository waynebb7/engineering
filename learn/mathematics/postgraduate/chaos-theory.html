Dynamical Systems (MSc) — Level: MSc · Domain: Applied

Open this topic on the prerequisite map to see upstream dependencies and study paths.

Builds on

  • Ordinary Differential Equations (BSc)

1) Deterministic chaos: definition and criteria

A dynamical system can be deterministic (evolution fixed by initial data) yet chaotic — long-term behaviour is unpredictable despite short-term determinism. Common criteria:

  • Sensitive dependence on initial conditions (SDIC): nearby trajectories diverge exponentially
  • Topological mixing: the system explores state space thoroughly
  • Dense periodic orbits: periodic behaviour is ubiquitous in the attractor
Key idea: Chaos is not randomness — it is irregularity arising from nonlinear deterministic evolution. Small measurement errors become macroscopic after finite time.

Prerequisite: Differential Equations, Nonlinear Dynamics.

2) Discrete maps: the logistic family

The logistic map \(x_{n+1} = r x_n(1 - x_n)\) on \([0,1]\) is a paradigm for period-doubling route to chaos. Fixed points satisfy \(x = rx(1-x)\); stability determined by \(|f'(x)| = |r(1-2x)|\).

Worked example: At \(r = 2\), the stable fixed point is \(x^* = 1 - 1/r = 1/2\). At \(r = 3.2\), a period-2 cycle appears. The Feigenbaum constant \(\delta \approx 4.669\) governs the ratio of successive bifurcation intervals.
Period-doubling cascade: As \(r\) increases through \(r_\infty \approx 3.5699\), the attractor becomes a Cantor-like set of non-periodic orbits — chaos on a subset of parameter values.
Convention: "Chaos" in the logistic map is often demonstrated at \(r = 4\) (fully chaotic on \([0,1]\) with invariant density) or in windows of periodic behaviour embedded in chaotic parameter ranges.

3) Lyapunov exponents

The Lyapunov exponent \(\lambda\) quantifies average exponential separation of trajectories: \[ \lambda = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \ln |f'(x_k)| \] For continuous flows \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\), tangent vectors \(\mathbf{v}(t)\) evolve by variational equation \(\dot{\mathbf{v}} = D\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\mathbf{v}\); \(\lambda\) is the long-time average growth rate of \(\|\mathbf{v}\|\).

Interpretation: \(\lambda > 0\) indicates SDIC (chaos); \(\lambda = 0\) marginal; \(\lambda < 0\) convergence. For the logistic map at \(r=4\), \(\lambda = \ln 2 > 0\).
Kaplan–Yorke dimension: For dissipative chaotic attractors, \(D_{KY} = j + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^j \lambda_i}{|\lambda_{j+1}|}\) where \(j\) is the largest index with \(\sum_{i=1}^j \lambda_i \geq 0\). Links chaos to Fractals.

4) Continuous systems: Lorenz and Rössler attractors

The Lorenz system (atmospheric convection model): \[ \dot{x} = \sigma(y - x), \quad \dot{y} = x(\rho - z) - y, \quad \dot{z} = xy - \beta z \] with classic parameters \(\sigma = 10\), \(\rho = 28\), \(\beta = 8/3\) exhibits a strange attractor.

Strange attractor: Trajectories are bounded, non-periodic, and approach a fractal set with zero volume but nonzero Lyapunov exponents. The Lorenz attractor has approximate dimension \(\approx 2.06\).
Rössler system: Simpler three-equation model with \(\dot{z} = 0.2 + z(x - 5.7)\) producing a banded strange attractor — useful for teaching and circuit analogues.

5) Bifurcations and routes to chaos

Bifurcations are qualitative changes in dynamics as a parameter varies:

Route Mechanism Example
Period-doubling Successive flip bifurcations Logistic map
Intermittency Laminar bursts between chaotic epochs Turbulent pipe flow onset
Quasiperiodicity breakup Torus destruction Driven nonlinear oscillators
Crisis Sudden attractor boundary jump Explosive bifurcations in maps
Poincaré map: For a periodic orbit in a flow, a transverse section \(\Sigma\) and return map \(P : \Sigma \to \Sigma\) reduce dimension. Chaotic flows often have chaotic Poincaré sections (horseshoes).

6) Symbolic dynamics and ergodic theory

The shift map on bi-infinite symbol sequences models chaotic maps via coding (e.g. logistic at \(r=4\) related to tent map and binary expansion). Topological entropy \(h_{\mathrm{top}}\) measures complexity of the orbit structure.

Ergodicity

An invariant measure \(\mu\) is ergodic if time averages equal space averages for \(\mu\)-almost all initial conditions — justification for statistical ensembles in chaotic systems.

Mixing

Stronger than ergodicity: correlations decay. Hyperbolic systems (Anosov, Axiom A) admit Sinai–Ruelle–Bowen measures.

Related: Statistical Mechanics, Measure Theory.

7) Control, prediction, and research outlook

Shadowing: True trajectories stay close to numerical approximations for hyperbolic systems — finite-precision simulation can be meaningful despite SDIC.
OGY method: Stabilising unstable periodic orbits within chaotic attractors via tiny parameter perturbations — applied in cardiology and laser control.
Research context: Quantum chaos (energy level statistics), spatiotemporal chaos (Kuramoto–Sivashinsky, Navier–Stokes turbulence), and machine learning of attractors continue active development.
Common mistake: Equating chaos with noise or requiring infinite precision. Chaotic systems can be low-dimensional, smooth, and dissipative with well-defined attractor geometry.
