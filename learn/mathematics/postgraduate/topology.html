1) Topological spaces and bases

A topology on a set \(X\) is a collection \(\mathcal{T}\) of subsets (called open sets) satisfying:

  • \(\varnothing, X \in \mathcal{T}\)
  • Arbitrary unions of sets in \(\mathcal{T}\) are in \(\mathcal{T}\)
  • Finite intersections of sets in \(\mathcal{T}\) are in \(\mathcal{T}\)

A basis \(\mathcal{B}\) generates \(\mathcal{T}\) if every open set is a union of basis elements. Every metric induces a topology via open balls; this is the metric topology.

Key idea: Topology studies properties preserved under continuous deformation — stretching without tearing or gluing. Homeomorphic spaces are "the same" topologically.

Prerequisite: Metric Spaces and Real Analysis.

2) Continuity and homeomorphisms

A function \(f : (X, \mathcal{T}_X) \to (Y, \mathcal{T}_Y)\) is continuous if \(f^{-1}(U)\) is open in \(X\) whenever \(U\) is open in \(Y\).

Equivalent conditions (for maps between topological spaces):
(a) Preimage of every open set is open.
(b) Preimage of every closed set is closed.
(c) For nets: \(x_\alpha \to x \Rightarrow f(x_\alpha) \to f(x)\) (when \(X\) is first-countable, sequences suffice).
Homeomorphism: A bijective continuous map with continuous inverse. Example: \((0,1) \cong \mathbb{R}\) via \(x \mapsto \tan(\pi(x - 1/2))\).
Common mistake: A bijective continuous map need not be a homeomorphism. The identity \([0,1) \to [0,1)\) with the subspace topology from \(\mathbb{R}\) is bijective and continuous but not a homeomorphism onto \([0,1]\).

3) Connectedness and path connectedness

\(X\) is connected if it is not the union of two disjoint non-empty open sets. Equivalently, the only subsets that are both open and closed are \(\varnothing\) and \(X\).

Path connected: For all \(x, y \in X\), there exists a continuous \(\gamma : [0,1] \to X\) with \(\gamma(0) = x\), \(\gamma(1) = y\). Path connected \(\Rightarrow\) connected.
Components: The connected components are maximal connected subspaces. They partition \(X\) and are closed.

4) Compactness

An open cover of \(K \subseteq X\) is a family \(\{U_\alpha\}\) of open sets with \(K \subseteq \bigcup U_\alpha\). \(K\) is compact if every open cover has a finite subcover.

Heine–Borel: In \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the standard topology, compact \(\Leftrightarrow\) closed and bounded.
Tychonoff theorem: Products of compact spaces are compact (in the product topology). Requires the Axiom of Choice in full generality.

Sequential compactness

In metric spaces, compactness equals sequential compactness. In general topological spaces, compactness is strictly stronger than sequential compactness.

Local compactness

Every point has a compact neighbourhood. \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is locally compact but not compact. One-point compactification adds a point at infinity.

5) Separation axioms (Tychonoff hierarchy)

Separation axioms control how well points and closed sets can be separated by open sets. They matter for metrizability, partitions of unity, and embedding theorems.

\(T_0\): For distinct \(x, y\), some open set contains one but not the other.
\(T_1\): Singletons are closed (finite sets are closed).
\(T_2\) (Hausdorff): Distinct points have disjoint open neighbourhoods.
Regular (\(T_3\)): Points can be separated from closed sets.
Normal (\(T_4\)): Disjoint closed sets have disjoint open neighbourhoods.
Convention: "Compact Hausdorff" is a standard hypothesis in analysis and algebraic topology. Compact subsets of Hausdorff spaces are closed.

Urysohn's lemma (in normal spaces) and Tietze extension theorem build continuous functions separating closed sets — essential for partitions of unity on manifolds.

6) Fundamental group and covering spaces (preview)

The fundamental group \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) consists of homotopy classes of loops based at \(x_0\), with concatenation as the group operation. It is a topological invariant: homeomorphic spaces have isomorphic \(\pi_1\).

Examples: \(\pi_1(\mathbb{R}^n) = 0\). \(\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}\) (winding number). \(\pi_1(T^2) \cong \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}\).
Covering space: A map \(p : \tilde{X} \to X\) that is locally a homeomorphism. The universal cover \(\tilde{X}\) is simply connected and classifies coverings via \(\pi_1(X)\).

Full treatment: Algebraic Topology, Homotopy.

7) Applications and research outlook

Manifolds

Locally Euclidean Hausdorff spaces. Smooth, PL, and topological manifolds underpin Differential Geometry and physics (spacetime models).

Knot theory

Knots are embeddings \(S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3\) up to ambient isotopy. Topological invariants (Jones polynomial, Khovanov homology) distinguish knots.

Algebraic topology

Homology and cohomology assign algebraic invariants to spaces. Functoriality makes topological problems tractable via algebra.

Data topology

Persistent homology extracts topological features (holes, components) from point-cloud data — applied in TDA (topological data analysis).

Related: Algebraic Topology, Metric Spaces, Group Theory.
