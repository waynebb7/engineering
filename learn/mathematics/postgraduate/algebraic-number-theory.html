1) Number fields and algebraic integers

A number field is a finite extension \(K/\mathbb{Q}\) of degree \(n = [K:\mathbb{Q}]\). An element \(\alpha \in K\) is an algebraic integer if it is a root of a monic polynomial with coefficients in \(\mathbb{Z}\). The ring of integers \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is the integral closure of \(\mathbb{Z}\) in \(K\).

Key idea: \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is a Dedekind domain — ideals factor uniquely into prime ideals even when \(\mathcal{O}_K\) is not a PID. This restores unique factorisation at the ideal level.
Worked example: For \(K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})\), \(\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]\). The element \(6\) factors as \(2 \cdot 3\) and as \((1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})\), illustrating failure of unique factorisation in the ring.

Prerequisite: Galois Theory and Elementary Algebra.

2) Discriminant and integral bases

For a basis \(\{\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n\}\) of \(K/\mathbb{Q}\), the discriminant is: \[ \Delta(\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n) = \det\bigl(\mathrm{Tr}(\omega_i\omega_j)\bigr) \] where \(\mathrm{Tr}\) is the field trace. The field discriminant \(d_K = \Delta(\mathcal{O}_K)\) is an invariant of \(K\).

Power basis: If \(\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\alpha]\) for \(\alpha\) with minimal polynomial \(f\), then \(d_K = \mathrm{Disc}(f)\) up to sign conventions.
Common mistake: Assuming \(\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[\alpha]\) for every \(\alpha\) generating \(K\). Non-monogenic fields require a non-power integral basis.

3) Factorisation of ideals

For a nonzero prime ideal \(\mathfrak{p} \subset \mathcal{O}_K\) and rational prime \(p\), the ideal \((p) = p\mathcal{O}_K\) decomposes as: \[ (p) = \mathfrak{p}_1^{e_1}\cdots\mathfrak{p}_g^{e_g} \] with \(\sum e_i f_i = n\), where \(f_i = [\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}_i : \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}]\).

Ramification: \(e_i > 1\) means \(\mathfrak{p}_i\) is ramified over \(p\). A prime \(p\) ramifies iff \(p \mid d_K\).
Worked example: In \(\mathbb{Q}(i)\), \(\mathcal{O}_K = \mathbb{Z}[i]\). \((2) = (1+i)^2\), \((5) = (2+i)(2-i)\), and inert/split behaviour depends on \(p \bmod 4\).

4) Class group and units

The ideal class group \(\mathrm{Cl}_K = \mathrm{Id}(\mathcal{O}_K)/\mathrm{Prin}(\mathcal{O}_K)\) measures failure of unique factorisation into elements. It is finite for every number field.

Dirichlet's unit theorem: The group of units \(\mathcal{O}_K^\times\) is finitely generated. With \(r_1\) real embeddings and \(r_2\) pairs of complex embeddings, \[ \mathcal{O}_K^\times \cong \mu_K \times \mathbb{Z}^{r_1 + r_2 - 1} \] where \(\mu_K\) is the finite group of roots of unity in \(K\).
Worked example: Units in \(\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{2}]\) are \(\pm(1+\sqrt{2})^n\). The fundamental unit generates the infinite part of \(\mathcal{O}_K^\times\).

5) Galois theory and splitting

If \(K/\mathbb{Q}\) is Galois with Galois group \(G\), then \(G\) acts on prime ideals lying over \(p\). The decomposition group \(D(\mathfrak{p})\) and inertia group \(I(\mathfrak{p})\) control local ramification.

Splitting types: A rational prime \(p\) splits completely in \(K\) if \((p)\) factors into \(n\) distinct prime ideals of norm \(p\). Chebotarev density links splitting behaviour to conjugacy classes in \(\mathrm{Gal}(K/\mathbb{Q})\).

Related: Galois Theory and Cryptography (class-group based crypto).

6) Zeta functions and L-functions

The Dedekind zeta function of \(K\) is: \[ \zeta_K(s) = \sum_{\mathfrak{a}} N(\mathfrak{a})^{-s} = \prod_{\mathfrak{p}} \left(1 - N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s}\right)^{-1} \] where the sum is over nonzero ideals and \(N(\mathfrak{p})\) is the norm.

Analytic continuation: \(\zeta_K(s)\) extends meromorphically to \(\mathbb{C}\) with a simple pole at \(s = 1\). The residue encodes class number and regulator data via the analytic class number formula.

Dirichlet L-functions

Characters modulo \(q\) produce L-functions whose zeros encode distribution of primes in arithmetic progressions.

Modern research

Langlands programme connects Galois representations, automorphic forms, and L-function analytic properties.

7) Applications

Diophantine equations

Class groups, heights, and local-global principles constrain integer solutions to curves and higher-dimensional varieties.

Cryptography

Ideal-class-group and isogeny-based schemes use hard problems in number fields and elliptic curves.

Algebraic geometry

Spec\((\mathcal{O}_K)\) bridges arithmetic and geometry; divisors and line bundles generalise ideals.

Computational methods

LLL reduction, class-group algorithms, and root-finding modulo primes power modern ANT software.
