1) Groups and the group axioms

A group \((G, \cdot)\) is a set \(G\) with a binary operation \(\cdot : G \times G \to G\) satisfying:

  • Closure: \(a \cdot b \in G\) for all \(a, b \in G\).
  • Associativity: \((ab)c = a(bc)\).
  • Identity: \(\exists\, e \in G\) with \(ea = ae = a\).
  • Inverses: for each \(a \in G\), \(\exists\, a^{-1} \in G\) with \(aa^{-1} = a^{-1}a = e\).

A group is abelian (or commutative) if \(ab = ba\) for all \(a, b \in G\). The order \(|G|\) is the cardinality of \(G\); finite group theory is central to combinatorics, cryptography, and crystallography.

Worked example: \((\mathbb{Z}, +)\) is an infinite abelian group. \((\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)\) is cyclic of order \(n\).
The nonzero rationals \((\mathbb{Q}^*, \times)\) form a non-abelian group only when considering matrices; \(\mathbb{Q}^*\) itself is abelian.
Key idea: Groups encode symmetry. Every geometric symmetry of a regular object corresponds to an element of a symmetry group (e.g. \(D_n\) for an \(n\)-gon).

Prerequisite: Elementary Algebra and Matrices.

2) Subgroups, cosets, and Lagrange's theorem

\(H \leq G\) means \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\): \(H \subseteq G\) is closed under the operation and inverses. A subgroup is normal, written \(H \trianglelefteq G\), if \(gHg^{-1} = H\) for all \(g \in G\).

For \(H \leq G\), the left cosets are \(gH = \{gh : h \in H\}\). Cosets partition \(G\), and all cosets of \(H\) have the same size \(|H|\).

Lagrange's theorem: If \(G\) is finite and \(H \leq G\), then \(|H|\) divides \(|G|\). Equivalently, \([G : H] = |G|/|H|\) is the index of \(H\).
Subgroup test Condition
One-step test Non-empty \(H\) and \(ab^{-1} \in H\) whenever \(a, b \in H\)
Normal subgroup \(gHg^{-1} \subseteq H\) for all \(g \in G\) (equivalently \(gH = Hg\))
Generated subgroup \(\langle S \rangle\) = all finite products of elements of \(S\) and their inverses
Common mistake: A subset closed under multiplication need not be a subgroup — closure under inverses is also required.

3) Homomorphisms and the isomorphism theorems

A group homomorphism \(\varphi : G \to K\) satisfies \(\varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b)\). The kernel \(\ker\varphi = \{g \in G : \varphi(g) = e_K\}\) is a normal subgroup of \(G\); the image \(\operatorname{im}\varphi \leq K\).

First isomorphism theorem: \[ G / \ker\varphi \cong \operatorname{im}\varphi \] where \(\cong\) denotes group isomorphism.
Second isomorphism theorem (Diamond theorem): For subgroups \(H \leq G\) and \(N \trianglelefteq G\): \[ HN \leq G, \quad H \cap N \trianglelefteq H, \quad H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N \]
Third isomorphism theorem: If \(N \trianglelefteq G\) and \(K \trianglelefteq G\) with \(N \subseteq K\), then \(K/N \trianglelefteq G/N\) and \((G/N)/(K/N) \cong G/K\).

Quotient groups \(G/N\) are defined only when \(N \trianglelefteq G\). Normality is the algebraic condition for factoring symmetry.

4) Symmetric groups and dihedral groups

The symmetric group \(S_n\) consists of all bijections \(\{1,\ldots,n\} \to \{1,\ldots,n\}\) under composition, with \(|S_n| = n!\). Every finite group embeds in some \(S_n\) (Cayley's theorem).

The dihedral group \(D_n = \langle r, s \mid r^n = s^2 = e,\, srs = r^{-1} \rangle\) is the symmetry group of a regular \(n\)-gon: \(r\) is rotation, \(s\) is reflection. \(|D_n| = 2n\).

Cycle notation: In \(S_4\), the permutation \((1\;2\;3)\) sends \(1 \mapsto 2\), \(2 \mapsto 3\), \(3 \mapsto 1\), fixing 4. Disjoint cycles commute.

Cyclic groups

If \(G = \langle g \rangle\) is generated by one element, then \(G \cong \mathbb{Z}\) (infinite) or \(G \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\) (order \(n\)).

Alternating group

\(A_n = \ker(\operatorname{sgn} : S_n \to \{\pm 1\})\) has order \(n!/2\). Simple for \(n \geq 5\).

Convention: Composition \((fg)(x) = f(g(x))\) (right-to-left) is standard in algebra texts; always check the author's convention.

5) Group actions and the orbit-stabilizer theorem

A group action of \(G\) on a set \(X\) is a map \(G \times X \to X\), \((g,x) \mapsto g \cdot x\), with \(e \cdot x = x\) and \((gh) \cdot x = g \cdot (h \cdot x)\).

The orbit of \(x\) is \(\mathcal{O}_x = \{g \cdot x : g \in G\}\). The stabilizer is \(\operatorname{Stab}_G(x) = \{g \in G : g \cdot x = x\} \leq G\).

Orbit-stabilizer theorem: \[ |\mathcal{O}_x| = [G : \operatorname{Stab}_G(x)] = |G| / |\operatorname{Stab}_G(x)| \] for finite \(G\). This is the primary counting tool in group theory.
Class equation: For \(G\) finite acting on itself by conjugation, \[ |G| = |Z(G)| + \sum [G : C_G(x_i)] \] where \(Z(G)\) is the centre and \(C_G(x)\) is the centralizer of \(x\).

Conjugacy classes partition \(G\). The class equation proves that groups of order \(p^2\) ( \(p\) prime) are abelian.

6) Sylow theorems and finite simple groups

For a finite group \(G\) and prime \(p\), a Sylow \(p\)-subgroup is a maximal \(p\)-subgroup. Sylow theory controls the structure of finite groups.

Sylow theorems (summary):
(1) Sylow \(p\)-subgroups exist and have order \(p^k\) where \(p^k \,\|\, |G|\) is maximal.
(2) All Sylow \(p\)-subgroups are conjugate.
(3) The number \(n_p\) of Sylow \(p\)-subgroups satisfies \(n_p \equiv 1 \pmod{p}\) and \(n_p \mid |G|\).
Worked example: A group of order 15 has \(n_3 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}\) and \(n_3 \mid 5\), so \(n_3 = 1\). Similarly \(n_5 = 1\). Hence \(G \cong \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/15\mathbb{Z}\).

Simple groups

\(G\) is simple if its only normal subgroups are \(\{e\}\) and \(G\). Building blocks of all finite groups via composition series.

Classification

The Classification of Finite Simple Groups (1980s–2000s) lists all finite simple groups: cyclic of prime order, alternating, Lie type, and 26 sporadic groups.

7) Research connections and applications

Galois theory bridge

Galois groups of field extensions classify solvability of polynomials by radicals. \(S_5\) is not solvable, explaining the quintic.

Representation theory

Homomorphisms \(G \to GL(V)\) linearize symmetry. Irreducible representations decompose physical state spaces in quantum mechanics.

Crystallography

Space groups in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) classify crystal structures. Point groups \(C_n, D_n, T, O, I\) describe molecular symmetry.

Cryptography

Elliptic curve groups over finite fields underpin modern public-key cryptography. Discrete logarithm hardness depends on group structure.

Related: Cryptography, Combinatorics, Algebraic Topology.
