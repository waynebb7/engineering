1) Boolean minimisation as combinatorial optimisation

Given a Boolean function \(f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}\), two-level minimisation seeks a disjunctive normal form (DNF) of minimum cost — fewest product terms and literals — equivalent to \(f\).

Cost functions: In logic synthesis, cost may count literals, product terms, or gate count. Exact minimisation is NP-hard in general, but efficient algorithms exist for small \(n\).
Cube notation: A minterm is a maximal product of literals. A product term (cube) covers all minterms obtained by setting don't-care literals. The set of minterms where \(f=1\) is the on-set.

Algebraic foundation: Boolean Algebra (Postgraduate). Undergraduate visual guide: Karnaugh Mapping (Logic & Digital Math).

2) Prime implicants and essential prime implicants

A prime implicant is a maximal cube that implies \(f\) (covers only on-set minterms, possibly plus don't-cares). An essential prime implicant (EPI) covers at least one minterm not covered by any other prime implicant.

Example: For \(f(A,B,C) = \sum m(1,3,5,7)\), the term \(A\) is a prime implicant covering minterms 1, 3, 5, 7. If minterm 1 is covered only by \(A\), then \(A\) is essential.
Key lemma: Every minimal DNF must include all essential prime implicants. Non-essential primes are selected by a covering step.
Don't-care conditions: Minterms in the don't-care set may be assigned 0 or 1 to enlarge cubes and reduce cost — a freedom not present in the on-set alone.

3) Quine–McCluskey algorithm

The Quine–McCluskey method systematically finds all prime implicants by iterative adjacent merging of minterms differing in exactly one variable.

Procedure:
Step 1: Group minterms by number of 1s.
Step 2: Merge pairs differing in one bit; record parent pairs.
Step 3: Repeat until no further merges.
Step 4: Unmerged terms are prime implicants.
Worked example: Minterms \(m(1)=001\), \(m(3)=011\), \(m(5)=101\) merge to \(--1\) (cube \(C\)). Continue merging to obtain the complete prime implicant table.

4) Petrick's method and set covering

After identifying prime implicants, selecting a minimum subset that covers all minterms is the set covering problem. Petrick's method solves it via logical absorption.

Covering table: Rows = minterms, columns = prime implicants. Entry 1 if the prime covers the minterm.
Petrick's formula: For each minterm \(m_i\), form the sum of primes covering \(m_i\): \(P_i = \sum_{j: \text{ covers } m_i} p_j\). The minimal covers are the products \(\prod_i P_i\) reduced by \(X + XY = X\) and \(X + X = X\).
Complexity: The number of prime implicants can grow exponentially in \(n\). Modern logic synthesis uses heuristic and SAT-based methods for large instances.

5) Karnaugh maps as Gray-code hypercubes

A Karnaugh map arranges minterms in a grid so that adjacent cells (including wrap-around) differ in one variable — a 2-D projection of an \(n\)-dimensional hypercube with Gray-code ordering.

Adjacency = merge: Grouping \(2^k\) adjacent 1s in the map corresponds to merging \(k\) variables to \(-\) (don't care) in the cube notation — exactly the Quine–McCluskey merge step.
Visual vs algorithmic: K-maps are effective for \(n \leq 6\). Beyond that, tabular and computer-aided methods dominate.

Step-by-step K-map tutorial: Karnaugh Mapping (Logic & Digital Math).

6) Multi-output and don't-care minimisation

For multiple outputs \(f_1, \ldots, f_m\), shared product terms reduce total cost. Don't-care sets \(d\) allow expansion of cubes: a prime may cover minterms from \(f \cup d\).

Multi-output Quine–McCluskey: Extend the prime implicant table across outputs; a product term may appear in several output functions if it covers the respective on-sets.
Engineering context: In PLA/FPGA synthesis, minimisation directly affects area, power, and delay. Exact two-level minimisation remains a benchmark for heuristic tools.
