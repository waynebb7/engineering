1) Counting principles and binomial coefficients

Combinatorics studies discrete structures: permutations, combinations, partitions, graphs, and designs. The addition principle: disjoint cases add. The multiplication principle: independent choices multiply.

Binomial theorem: \[ (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k, \quad \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} \]

2) Generating functions and recurrence relations

The ordinary generating function (OGF) of a sequence \((a_n)\) is \(A(x) = \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n x^n\). Convolution encodes combinatorial sums: if \(c_n = \sum_{k=0}^n a_k b_{n-k}\), then \(C(x) = A(x)B(x)\).

Exponential generating function (EGF): \(\hat{A}(x) = \sum a_n \dfrac{x^n}{n!}\). Products correspond to labelled structures (labelled enumeration).
Fibonacci example: \(F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}\), \(F_0 = 0\), \(F_1 = 1\). OGF: \[ F(x) = \frac{x}{1 - x - x^2} = \sum_{n \geq 0} F_n x^n \] Binet's formula follows from partial fractions.
Common mistake: OGFs and EGFs answer different enumeration problems. Use EGFs when objects carry labels that must be preserved under composition.

3) Graph theory fundamentals

A graph \(G = (V, E)\) has vertices \(V\) and edges \(E \subseteq V \times V\) (or 2-subsets for simple graphs). A tree on \(n\) vertices has exactly \(n-1\) edges and is acyclic and connected.

Euler's formula (planar graphs): \(v - e + f = 2\) for connected planar embeddings with \(f\) faces.

Extremal graph theory

Turán's theorem: the maximum edges in a \(K_{r+1}\)-free graph on \(n\) vertices is \(\left(1 - \frac{1}{r}\right)\frac{n^2}{2}\).

Spectral graph theory

Eigenvalues of the adjacency or Laplacian matrix encode connectivity, expansion, and random walk mixing times.

4) Ramsey theory

Ramsey theory asks: how large must a structure be before a desired pattern appears regardless of how it is coloured or arranged?

Ramsey's theorem: For all integers \(r, s, t \geq 2\), there exists \(R(r,s)\) such that any 2-colouring of edges of \(K_{R(r,s)}\) contains a red \(K_r\) or blue \(K_s\).
Known bounds: \(R(3,3) = 6\), \(R(4,4) = 18\), \(R(5,5)\) is unknown (between 43 and 48). Growth is explosive — Ramsey numbers are among the most difficult constants to pin down.
Exam tip: Ramsey proofs use induction on the number of colours and parameters. Constructive lower bounds use probabilistic method (Erdős) or explicit graphs.

5) Design theory and coding theory

A block design \((v, k, \lambda)\)-design is a \(v\)-set of points with blocks (subsets of size \(k\)) such that every pair of points lies in exactly \(\lambda\) blocks. Steiner systems \(S(t,k,v)\) generalize to \(t\)-subsets.

Fisher's inequality: In a nontrivial 2-design, the number of blocks \(b \geq v\).
Linear codes: A \([n,k,d]_q\) code is a \(k\)-dimensional subspace of \(\mathbb{F}_q^n\) with minimum Hamming distance \(d\). Designs and codes are dual objects — incidence matrices link combinatorics to Cryptography.

Error-correcting codes (Reed–Solomon, LDPC) underpin storage and communication; combinatorial design ensures balanced experimental layouts.

6) Enumerative combinatorics and group actions

Burnside's lemma (Cauchy–Frobenius): The number of orbits of a finite group \(G\) acting on a set \(X\) is \[ \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} |\operatorname{Fix}(g)| \]

Polya enumeration: Extends Burnside to weighted counting. The cycle index \(Z(G)\) of a permutation group encodes symmetry classes of coloured structures.
Catalan numbers: \(C_n = \dfrac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n}\) count Dyck paths, binary trees, non-crossing partitions, and many other objects — a hallmark of bijective combinatorics.

Probabilistic method

Erdős: if \(\mathbb{P}(\text{property holds}) > 0\) in a probability space of objects, such an object exists. Proves lower bounds without construction.

Asymptotic enumeration

Stirling's approximation \(n! \sim \sqrt{2\pi n}(n/e)^n\). Saddle-point methods for coefficients of generating functions.

7) Applications and research outlook

Algorithms and complexity

Combinatorial optimization (TSP, max-cut) bridges to Game Theory and operations research. Many counting problems are #P-complete.

Algebraic combinatorics

Symmetric functions, Young tableaux, and representation theory of \(S_n\). Schur polynomials enumerate representations of \(GL_n\).

Extremal combinatorics

Erdős–Ko–Rado theorem, Sunflower lemma, and hypergraph Ramsey theory. Additive combinatorics (sum-free sets, Szemerédi's theorem).

Topological combinatorics

Kneser conjecture proved via Borsuk–Ulam and \(\mathbb{Z}_2\)-index theory — combinatorics meets Topology.

Related: Group Theory, Topology, Statistics.
