1) Lie algebras: definitions and examples

A Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{g}\) over a field \(k\) is a vector space with a bilinear map \([\cdot, \cdot]: \mathfrak{g} \times \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}\) (the Lie bracket) satisfying: \[ [x,x] = 0,\quad [x,[y,z]] + [y,[z,x]] + [z,[x,y]] = 0 \quad \text{(Jacobi identity)} \]

Fundamental examples:
  • \(\mathfrak{gl}(n, k)\) — all \(n \times n\) matrices with \([A,B] = AB - BA\)
  • \(\mathfrak{sl}(n, k)\) — traceless matrices, subalgebra of \(\mathfrak{gl}(n)\)
  • \(\mathfrak{so}(n)\), \(\mathfrak{su}(n)\) — orthogonal and special unitary Lie algebras
  • The tangent space at the identity of a Lie group carries a natural Lie algebra structure

Prerequisite: Matrices, Vector Spaces.

2) Lie brackets and structure constants

In a basis \(\{e_i\}\) of \(\mathfrak{g}\), the bracket is determined by structure constants \(c_{ij}^k\): \[ [e_i, e_j] = \sum_k c_{ij}^k e_k \]

Derived series: \(\mathfrak{g}^{(0)} = \mathfrak{g}\), \(\mathfrak{g}^{(i+1)} = [\mathfrak{g}^{(i)}, \mathfrak{g}^{(i)}]\). \(\mathfrak{g}\) is solvable if \(\mathfrak{g}^{(n)} = 0\) for some \(n\).
Lower central series: \(\mathfrak{g}_0 = \mathfrak{g}\), \(\mathfrak{g}_{i+1} = [\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}_i]\). \(\mathfrak{g}\) is nilpotent if \(\mathfrak{g}_n = 0\) for some \(n\).
Engel's theorem: A Lie algebra of nilpotent matrices is nilpotent; all elements are ad-nilpotent iff the algebra is nilpotent.

3) Ideals, quotients, and semisimplicity

An ideal \(\mathfrak{h} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}\) satisfies \([\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{h}] \subseteq \mathfrak{h}\). Simple Lie algebras have no non-trivial ideals. A Lie algebra is semisimple if it is a direct sum of simple Lie algebras (no abelian ideals).

Levi decomposition: Every finite-dimensional Lie algebra over a field of characteristic 0 splits as a semidirect product of a semisimple and a solvable (radical) part.
Direct sum: \(\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{g}_1 \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathfrak{g}_k\) as Lie algebras iff \([\mathfrak{g}_i, \mathfrak{g}_j] = 0\) for \(i \neq j\).

4) Representations of Lie algebras

A representation is a Lie algebra homomorphism \(\rho: \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)\), equivalently an action of \(\mathfrak{g}\) on a vector space \(V\) satisfying \(\rho([x,y]) = \rho(x)\rho(y) - \rho(y)\rho(x)\).

Adjoint representation: \(\mathrm{ad}_x(y) = [x, y]\). The Killing form uses traces of \(\mathrm{ad}_x \circ \mathrm{ad}_y\).
Weyl's theorem: Every finite-dimensional representation of a semisimple Lie algebra over \(\mathbb{C}\) is completely reducible (direct sum of irreducibles).
Highest weight theory: Irreducible finite-dimensional representations of semisimple Lie algebras are classified by dominant integral weights.

5) Killing form and Cartan's criteria

The Killing form on \(\mathfrak{g}\) is the symmetric bilinear form \[ B(x,y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_x \circ \mathrm{ad}_y) \]

Cartan's criterion: A Lie algebra is semisimple iff its Killing form is non-degenerate. A semisimple Lie algebra is the direct sum of its simple ideals.
Compact real forms: For \(\mathfrak{g}\) complex semisimple, a compact real form exists with negative-definite Killing form — linking to \(\mathfrak{su}(n)\) and physics.
Characteristic 0: Cartan's criteria and classification require characteristic 0 (typically \(\mathbb{C}\) or \(\mathbb{R}\)).

6) Root systems and classification

A root system \(\Phi\) in a Euclidean space encodes the structure of semisimple Lie algebras via root space decomposition \(\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha \in \Phi} \mathfrak{g}_\alpha\).

Simple roots: Every root system has a basis of simple roots. Dynkin diagrams classify irreducible root systems: \(A_n, B_n, C_n, D_n, E_6, E_7, E_8, F_4, G_2\).
Classification: Simple complex Lie algebras correspond bijectively to connected Dynkin diagrams. For example, \(A_{n-1}\) corresponds to \(\mathfrak{sl}(n, \mathbb{C})\).
Serre relations: Generators and relations (Chevalley–Serre presentation) construct each simple Lie algebra from its Cartan matrix.

7) Applications in physics and engineering

Quantum mechanics

Angular momentum algebras \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\) govern spin and rotation. The Heisenberg algebra underlies quantum commutation relations.

Particle physics

The Standard Model gauge group \(SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)\) has Lie algebras \(\mathfrak{su}(3) \oplus \mathfrak{su}(2) \oplus \mathfrak{u}(1)\).

Robotics and control

The special Euclidean group \(SE(3)\) and its Lie algebra \(\mathfrak{se}(3)\) describe rigid-body motion — central to Control Theory.

Symmetry methods for ODEs

Lie's symmetry groups reduce order of differential equations via infinitesimal generators forming a Lie algebra.

Related: Matrices, Linear Transformations, Control Theory.
