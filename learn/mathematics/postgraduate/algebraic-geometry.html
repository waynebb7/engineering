Algebraic Geometry

Postgraduate guide. Varieties, the Zariski topology, Nullstellensatz, dimension, schemes, and connections to coding and cryptography.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Affine and projective varieties

An affine variety over an algebraically closed field \(k\) (e.g. \(\mathbb{C}\)) is the zero locus \(V(I) = \{x \in k^n : f(x) = 0\ \forall f \in I\}\) of an ideal \(I \subseteq k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\). A projective variety is defined similarly in \(\mathbb{P}^n(k)\).

Coordinate ring: For \(X = V(I)\), the ring \(k[X] = k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/I(X)\) where \(I(X)\) is the ideal of all polynomials vanishing on \(X\). Geometry and algebra are dual.
Examples:
  • Affine line \(\mathbb{A}^1\), plane conics, cubics (elliptic curves)
  • Projective line \(\mathbb{P}^1\), projective plane curves
  • Link to Riemann Surfaces for complex curves

Prerequisite: Polynomials, Galois Theory.

2) Zariski topology

On \(k^n\) or \(\mathbb{P}^n\), the Zariski topology declares closed sets to be algebraic sets \(V(I)\). Open sets are complements of such zero loci.

Properties: The Zariski topology is Noetherian (every descending chain of closed sets stabilises) and far coarser than the Euclidean topology — only "large" closed sets appear.
Irreducibility: A variety is irreducible if it cannot be written as a union of two proper closed subvarieties. Irreducible components correspond to minimal primes of the coordinate ring.
Generic points: The Zariski topology encodes algebraic rather than metric information — a foundation for scheme theory.

3) Hilbert's Nullstellensatz

The Nullstellensatz (over algebraically closed \(k\)) establishes the correspondence between geometry and algebra.

Weak Nullstellensatz: A proper ideal \(I \subsetneq k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) has a common zero in \(k^n\): \(V(I) \neq \emptyset\).
Strong Nullstellensatz: For an ideal \(I\), \[ I(V(I)) = \sqrt{I} \] the ideal of polynomials vanishing on \(V(I)\) equals the radical of \(I\).
Consequence: There is an inclusion-reversing bijection between radical ideals and affine varieties. Maximal ideals correspond to points.

4) Dimension and singularities

The Krull dimension of the coordinate ring equals the geometric dimension of the variety. A variety is smooth at a point if its local ring is regular; otherwise the point is a singularity.

Examples of singularities: The node \(y^2 = x^2(x+1)\) and cusp \(y^2 = x^3\) have singular points at the origin where the Jacobian matrix of defining equations has rank deficiency.
Bézout's theorem: Two plane curves of degrees \(d, e\) with no common component intersect in exactly \(de\) points counted with multiplicity (in \(\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{C})\)).
Genus of curves: Smooth projective curves of degree \(d\) have genus \(g = \frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}\) — see Riemann Surfaces.

5) Schemes: beyond varieties

Schemes (Grothendieck) generalise varieties by allowing nilpotent elements in structure sheaves and working over arbitrary commutative rings. An affine scheme is \(\mathrm{Spec}(R)\) for a ring \(R\).

Motivation: Schemes encode multiplicities (e.g. tangent directions via \(\mathrm{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^2)\)), arithmetic geometry (schemes over \(\mathbb{Z}\)), and moduli problems.
Structure sheaf: On a variety \(X\), the sheaf of regular functions \(\mathcal{O}_X\) assigns to each open set the ring of regular (locally rational) functions — the sheaf-theoretic coordinate ring.
Modern AG: Research-level algebraic geometry is largely scheme- and sheaf-theoretic, with cohomology (Hartshorne, Stacks Project) as the primary toolkit.

6) Intersection theory and cohomology

Intersection theory assigns intersection numbers to cycles on varieties. The Chow ring and cohomology (Čech, sheaf, étale) measure global properties.

Riemann–Roch: On curves, relates dimension of linear systems to genus and degree — a prototype for higher-dimensional Riemann–Roch and Grothendieck–Riemann–Roch.
Weil conjectures: Solved using étale cohomology, connecting point counts over finite fields to topological invariants — a triumph linking number theory and geometry.

7) Applications to coding, cryptography, and engineering

Algebraic coding theory

AG codes on curves over finite fields achieve good rate–distance trade-offs using divisors and Riemann–Roch spaces on Riemann Surfaces.

Elliptic-curve cryptography

Elliptic curves over \(\mathbb{F}_p\) are smooth projective genus-1 varieties. Group law and point counting use AG over finite fields.

Polynomial optimisation

Semidefinite relaxations and Gröbner bases (computational AG) solve polynomial systems in robotics and control.

Computer vision

Algebraic varieties model constraint manifolds; elimination theory solves multivariate polynomial systems.

Related: Galois Theory, Riemann Surfaces.
