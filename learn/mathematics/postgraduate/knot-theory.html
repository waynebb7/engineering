1) Knots, links, and Reidemeister moves

A knot is a smooth embedding \(K: S^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3\) (or \(S^3\)). A link is an embedding of a disjoint union of circles. Two knots are equivalent if related by ambient isotopy of \(\mathbb{R}^3\).

Reidemeister theorem: Knot equivalence can be detected combinatorially: two knot diagrams represent the same knot iff related by a finite sequence of Reidemeister moves I, II, and III.
Diagrammatic invariants: Quantities unchanged under Reidemeister moves are knot invariants — tools for proving knots are distinct (e.g. trefoil vs unknot).

Prerequisite: Geometry, Topology (if available).

2) Alexander polynomial

The Alexander polynomial \(\Delta_K(t) \in \mathbb{Z}[t^{\pm 1}]\) was the first significant knot invariant (1928). It arises from the Alexander module of the knot group or from a Seifert matrix.

Properties:
  • \(\Delta_K(1) = \pm 1\)
  • Symmetric: \(\Delta_K(t) = \Delta_K(t^{-1})\) (up to units)
  • \(\Delta_{\text{unknot}}(t) = 1\); \(\Delta_{\text{trefoil}}(t) = t - 1 + t^{-1}\)
Limitation: The Alexander polynomial fails to detect chirality and cannot distinguish many mutant knots. Stronger invariants were needed.
Alexander–Conway polynomial: A normalised version satisfying a skein relation, precursor to the Jones polynomial.

3) Jones and HOMFLY polynomials

The Jones polynomial \(V_K(t) \in \mathbb{Z}[t^{\pm 1/2}]\) (1984) and the HOMFLY-PT polynomial \(P_K(a, z) \in \mathbb{Z}[a^{\pm 1}, z^{\pm 1}]\) are stronger quantum invariants.

Skein relation (Jones): \[ t^{-1} V(L_+) - t V(L_-) = (t^{1/2} - t^{-1/2}) V(L_0) \] where \(L_+, L_-, L_0\) differ at one crossing.
Significance: Jones discovered his polynomial via von Neumann algebras; it launched quantum topology and connections to statistical mechanics and quantum field theory.
Trefoil: \(V_{\text{trefoil}}(t) = t^{-1} + t^{-3} - t^{-4}\), proving the trefoil is non-trivial (not equal to the unknot invariant 1).

4) Knot groups and the Wirtinger presentation

The knot group (fundamental group of the knot complement) \(\pi_1(S^3 \setminus K)\) is a complete invariant for prime knots in many cases.

Wirtinger presentation: Generators correspond to arcs in a diagram; relations come from crossings. For the trefoil: \(\langle a, b, c \mid aba^{-1} = c,\, bcb^{-1} = a,\, cac^{-1} = b \rangle\).
Abelianisation: The first homology of the infinite cyclic cover relates to the Alexander polynomial via \(\Delta_K(t)\).
Limitation: Non-isotopic knots can have isomorphic groups (mutants). Group theory alone does not always distinguish knots.

5) Seifert surfaces and genus

A Seifert surface is an oriented embedded surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) (or \(S^3\)) with boundary the knot. The genus \(g(K)\) is the minimum genus among all Seifert surfaces.

Seifert matrix: From a Seifert surface, the linking form yields a matrix whose determinant gives the Alexander polynomial: \(\Delta_K(t) = \det(tI - V + V^T)\).
Properties: \(g(K) = 0\) iff \(K\) is the unknot. The trefoil has genus 1. Genus is additive under connected sum for prime knots.

6) 3-manifolds and modern invariants

Knot complements are primary examples of 3-manifolds with torus boundary. Dehn surgery on knots produces closed 3-manifolds; the classification program uses geometric and quantum invariants.

Geometrisation: Most knot complements admit hyperbolic structures (Thurston). Hyperbolic volume is a topological invariant.
Khovanov homology: A categorification of the Jones polynomial — a bigraded homology theory whose Euler characteristic recovers \(V_K(t)\).

Physics

Chern–Simons theory and Witten's path integral interpret Jones polynomials as quantum observables.

DNA topology

Linking number, writhe, and topoisomerases in molecular biology use knot-theoretic quantities.

7) Engineering and applied topology

Knot theory intersects material science (polymers, tangled fibres), robotics (path planning with entanglement), and data analysis (persistent homology detecting loop structures).

Linking number: For two closed curves, the Gauss linking integral gives an integer invariant measuring entanglement — used in electromagnetism and fluid vortices.

Related: Geometry, Statistics (topological data analysis).
