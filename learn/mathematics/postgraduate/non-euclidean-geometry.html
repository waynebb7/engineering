1) Euclid's fifth postulate and its failure

Euclidean geometry rests on five postulates. The parallel postulate states: through a point not on a given line, there exists exactly one line parallel to the given line. Non-Euclidean geometry replaces this with alternatives:

  • Hyperbolic: there are infinitely many parallels through the external point
  • Elliptic (spherical): there are no parallels — all great circles meet

Both are consistent mathematical systems. The discovery that no geometry is a priori "true" of physical space was central to 19th-century mathematics and underpins general relativity.

Key idea: Parallelism is a property of the metric and curvature of space, not a universal logical necessity.

Prerequisite: Euclidean Space and Geometry.

2) Gaussian curvature and the Gauss–Bonnet theorem

For a smooth surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), the Gaussian curvature \(K\) measures intrinsic bending. Surfaces with constant \(K\) model the three geometries:

Curvature Geometry Standard model
\(K = 0\) Euclidean (flat) \(\mathbb{R}^2\)
\(K = -1\) Hyperbolic Poincaré disk, upper half-plane
\(K = +1\) Elliptic (spherical) \(S^2\) (unit sphere)
Gauss–Bonnet: For a compact orientable surface \(M\), \[ \int_M K\,dA = 2\pi\chi(M) \] where \(\chi\) is the Euler characteristic. A torus (\(\chi = 0\)) cannot have constant positive curvature; a sphere (\(\chi = 2\)) cannot be flat.

3) Hyperbolic geometry: Poincaré disk model

The Poincaré disk \(\mathbb{D} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : |z| < 1\}\) carries the metric

\[ ds^2 = \frac{4|dz|^2}{(1 - |z|^2)^2} \]

Geodesics are diameters and arcs of circles orthogonal to the boundary \(\partial\mathbb{D}\). The hyperbolic distance between \(0\) and \(r \in (0,1)\) on the real axis is \(d(0,r) = 2\operatorname{artanh}(r)\).

Worked example: The angle defect of a hyperbolic triangle with angles \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) satisfies \[ \alpha + \beta + \gamma = \pi - A \] where \(A > 0\) is the area. Triangles have angle sum strictly less than \(\pi\).
Möbius isometries: Orientation-preserving isometries of \(\mathbb{D}\) are Möbius transformations \(z \mapsto \dfrac{az + b}{\bar{b}z + \bar{a}}\) with \(|a|^2 - |b|^2 = 1\). The group is \(\mathrm{PSU}(1,1)\).

Related: Complex Functions for Möbius maps.

4) Hyperbolic geometry: upper half-plane and Lorentz model

The upper half-plane \(\mathbb{H} = \{z : \Im z > 0\}\) has metric \(ds^2 = |dz|^2 / (\Im z)^2\). Geodesics are vertical lines and semicircles centred on the real axis. It is isometric to \(\mathbb{D}\) via the Cayley transform \(z \mapsto (z-i)/(z+i)\).

Lorentz (hyperboloid) model: The future sheet of the hyperboloid \(\{x \in \mathbb{R}^{2,1} : x_0^2 - x_1^2 - x_2^2 = 1,\, x_0 > 0\}\) with the induced metric from Minkowski space \(\mathbb{R}^{2,1}\) is a model of the hyperbolic plane. Isometries are restrictions of Lorentz group \(\mathrm{SO}^+(2,1)\) actions.
Convention: Curvature sign conventions vary. Here \(K = -1\) for standard hyperbolic models with sectional curvature \(-1\).

5) Elliptic and spherical geometry

On the unit sphere \(S^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^3\), geodesics are great circles. Antipodal points are identified in elliptic geometry \(\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{R})\): the real projective plane as a geometry where every pair of lines meets exactly once.

Worked example: A spherical triangle with angles \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) on \(S^2\) has area \(A = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi\) (excess over \(\pi\)). A triangle with three right angles exists on \(S^2\) (octant of the sphere).
Compact positively curved spaces: Compact surfaces admitting constant positive curvature are exactly \(S^2\) and \(\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{R})\). This follows from Gauss–Bonnet and classification of spherical space forms.

6) Applications in physics and topology

General relativity

Spacetime curvature is Riemannian (or Lorentzian) geometry, not Euclidean. Mass-energy curves geodesics; local Minkowski patches are approximately flat.

Cosmology

Spatial slices may be hyperbolic, flat, or spherical depending on mean density and the cosmological constant (Friedmann–Lemaître models).

3-manifold topology

Thurston's geometrisation: most 3-manifolds carry hyperbolic structure. The figure-eight knot complement admits a complete finite-volume hyperbolic metric.

Teichmüller theory

Moduli of hyperbolic structures on surfaces; connected to complex analysis, dynamics, and string theory.

Related: General Relativity, Cosmology.

7) Comparison and research outlook

All three constant-curvature geometries are homogeneous: isometry groups act transitively. In dimension 2, complete simply connected spaces are unique up to isometry: \(\mathbb{R}^2\), \(\mathbb{H}^2\), \(S^2\). In higher dimensions, hyperbolic space \(\mathbb{H}^n\) and spherical space \(S^n\) generalise naturally; Euclidean \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is the flat case.

Cartan–Hadamard: Complete, simply connected manifolds with non-positive sectional curvature are diffeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and their universal cover is negatively curved — a bridge between non-Euclidean geometry and Riemannian geometry.
Common mistake: Confusing models (disk, half-plane, hyperboloid) with the abstract geometry. They are isometric — theorems proved in one model transfer to all.
