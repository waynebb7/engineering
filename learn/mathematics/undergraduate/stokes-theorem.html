Stokes' Theorem

Undergraduate guide. Statement of Stokes' theorem, orientation, relation to Green's theorem, and applications.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Statement of Stokes' theorem

Stokes' theorem relates the circulation of a vector field around a closed curve to the flux of curl through any surface bounded by that curve.

\[ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} \]

Here \(\partial S\) is the boundary curve of an oriented surface \(S\), with orientation induced by that of \(S\). \(\mathbf{F}\) must be continuously differentiable on a neighbourhood of \(S\).

Key idea: The line integral depends only on the boundary curve, not on which surface \(S\) you choose (as long as \(\mathbf{F}\) is defined throughout). This is because \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\).

Requires Vector Calculus — curl, line integrals, and surface integrals.

2) Curl and circulation

The circulation of \(\mathbf{F}\) around a closed curve \(C\) is \(\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}\). It measures the net tangential component of \(\mathbf{F}\) around the loop — how much \(\mathbf{F}\) "swirls" around \(C\).

The curl \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) gives the local circulation density. Stokes' theorem says: total circulation around a boundary equals the total normal flux of curl through the interior surface.

Worked example (infinitesimal interpretation): For a small flat disc of area \(A\) with unit normal \(\mathbf{n}\), centred at a point, Stokes' theorem gives: \[ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} \approx (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n}\, A \] so \((\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot \mathbf{n}\) is circulation per unit area.
Irrotational fields: If \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\) on a simply connected region, then \(\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0\) for every closed curve — \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative.

3) Orientation and the right-hand rule

Orientation is essential. For a surface \(S\) with unit normal \(\mathbf{n}\), the boundary \(\partial S\) is oriented by the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right hand in the direction of traversal along \(\partial S\); your thumb points in the direction of \(\mathbf{n}\).

Consistent orientation

Reversing the direction along \(\partial S\) negates the line integral. Reversing \(\mathbf{n}\) (flipping the surface) negates the surface integral. Both must match.

Parametrised surfaces

For \(\mathbf{r}(u,v)\), the normal \(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v\) determines orientation. Check that it agrees with the right-hand rule on the boundary.

Common mistake: Using an outward normal on a surface that is not closed, or orienting \(\partial S\) opposite to the induced orientation — the theorem then picks up a sign error.

4) Relation to Green's theorem

Green's theorem in the plane is the 2D special case of Stokes' theorem. For a vector field \(\mathbf{F} = P\mathbf{i} + Q\mathbf{j}\) and region \(D\) with positively oriented boundary \(C\): \[ \oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA \]

In 2D, \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left(\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)\mathbf{k}\), and the surface integral reduces to the area integral over \(D\). Green's theorem is Stokes' theorem for a flat surface in the \(xy\)-plane.

Worked example: Evaluate \(\oint_C (y\,dx - x\,dy)\) where \(C\) is the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\), counter-clockwise.
\(P = y\), \(Q = -x\). \(\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y} = -1 - 1 = -2\).
Integral \(= \iint_D (-2)\,dA = -2\pi\).

Green's theorem also has a divergence (normal) form, linking to the Divergence Theorem in 2D.

5) Relation to the divergence theorem

Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem (Gauss's theorem) are complementary:

  • Stokes: circulation on \(\partial S\) ↔ \((\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\) flux through \(S\)
  • Divergence: flux through closed \(\partial V\) ↔ \((\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\) integral over \(V\)

Together with the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals (gradient fields), they form the generalised Stokes' theorem in differential forms: \(\int_{\partial\Omega} \omega = \int_\Omega d\omega\).

Unified view: In each case, the integral of a "derivative" (exterior derivative) over a region equals the integral of the original quantity over the boundary. Dimension 1: FTC; 2: Green; 3: Stokes and Divergence.
Dimension Region Boundary Theorem
1 Interval \([a,b]\) Points \(a, b\) Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
2 Planar region \(D\) Curve \(C\) Green's / Stokes'
3 Surface \(S\) Curve \(\partial S\) Stokes'
3 Solid \(V\) Closed surface \(\partial V\) Divergence

6) Applications

Fluid mechanics

Circulation \(\oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) around a wing profile relates to lift (Kutta–Joukowski). Vorticity \(\boldsymbol{\omega} = \nabla \times \mathbf{v}\) is the local rotation of fluid elements.

Electromagnetism

Faraday's law: \(\oint_C \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\dfrac{d}{dt}\iint_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{S}\). Ampère's law (with displacement current): \(\oint_C \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}\).

Computing work efficiently: When \(\mathbf{F}\) has complicated components but simple curl, evaluate \(\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}\) over a convenient surface (often a flat disc or portion of a plane) instead of parameterising the boundary curve directly.

In conservative fields (\(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}=\mathbf{0}\)), Stokes' theorem immediately gives zero circulation around any closed curve bounding a surface in the domain.

7) Worked example — hemisphere

Evaluate \(\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\) where \(\mathbf{F} = y\mathbf{i} - x\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}\) and \(C\) is the circle \(x^2 + y^2 = 1\), \(z = 0\), oriented counter-clockwise when viewed from above.

Solution via Stokes: Compute \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\): \[ \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\ \partial/\partial x & \partial/\partial y & \partial/\partial z \\ y & -x & z \end{vmatrix} = -2\mathbf{k} \] Take \(S\) as the upper unit disc (\(x^2+y^2 \leq 1\), \(z = 0\)) with upward normal \(\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{k}\): \[ \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D (-2\mathbf{k})\cdot\mathbf{k}\,dA = -2\pi \] So \(\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -2\pi\).
Check orientation: Counter-clockwise in the \(xy\)-plane with upward normal satisfies the right-hand rule.
