1) Taylor and Maclaurin series

If \(f\) has derivatives of all orders at \(x = a\), the Taylor series of \(f\) about \(a\) is the power series:

Taylor series about \(x = a\): \[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x - a)^n \] When \(a = 0\), the expansion is called a Maclaurin series.
Worked example: Maclaurin series for \(f(x) = e^x\).
\(f^{(n)}(x) = e^x\), so \(f^{(n)}(0) = 1\) for all \(n\).
\(e^x = \displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots\)

Taylor series extend Series and Differentiation to approximate functions locally by polynomials.

2) Common Maclaurin expansions

These standard results should be memorised for second- and third-year engineering mathematics. Each converges within the stated interval.

Function Maclaurin series Valid for
\(e^x\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^n}{n!}\) all \(x\)
\(\sin x\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}\) all \(x\)
\(\cos x\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}\) all \(x\)
\(\ln(1+x)\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^{n+1} x^n}{n}\) \(-1 < x \leq 1\)
\((1+x)^\alpha\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{n} x^n\) \(|x| < 1\)
\(\dfrac{1}{1-x}\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n\) \(|x| < 1\)
Common mistake: Using a binomial or logarithmic series outside its radius of convergence. \(\ln(1+x)\) diverges at \(x = -1\) and the geometric series fails for \(|x| \geq 1\).

3) Taylor's theorem and the remainder

A Taylor polynomial of degree \(n\) approximates \(f\) near \(x = a\). The remainder \(R_n(x)\) measures the error:

Taylor polynomial of order \(n\): \[ P_n(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k, \qquad f(x) = P_n(x) + R_n(x) \] Lagrange remainder: \[ R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1} \] for some \(c\) between \(a\) and \(x\).
Worked example: For \(f(x) = e^x\) near \(x = 0\) with \(n = 2\):
\(P_2(x) = 1 + x + \dfrac{x^2}{2}\).
At \(x = 0.1\): \(R_2(0.1) = \dfrac{e^c}{6}(0.1)^3\) for some \(c \in (0, 0.1)\).
Since \(e^c < e^{0.1}\), \(|R_2| < \dfrac{e^{0.1}}{6000} \approx 0.00018\).

4) Approximations and error bounds

Engineers truncate Taylor series to obtain fast approximations. The remainder gives a rigorous bound on the truncation error.

Small-angle approximations

\(\sin x \approx x - \dfrac{x^3}{6}\) for small \(x\) (radians).

\(\cos x \approx 1 - \dfrac{x^2}{2}\) for small \(x\).

Linearisation

\(f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)\) — first-order Taylor, used in sensitivity analysis and linear control.

Worked example: Approximate \(\sqrt{1.02}\) using the binomial series.
\(\sqrt{1+x} = (1+x)^{1/2} \approx 1 + \dfrac{x}{2} - \dfrac{x^2}{8}\) with \(x = 0.02\).
\(= 1 + 0.01 - 0.00005 = 1.00995\) (true value \(\approx 1.00995\)).
Exam tip: Always state the order of the approximation and, where required, bound the remainder using the maximum of \(|f^{(n+1)}(c)|\) on the interval.

5) Using series to evaluate limits and integrals

Taylor expansions simplify indeterminate limits and allow term-by-term integration of power series within the radius of convergence.

Worked example (limit): Evaluate \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{e^x - 1 - x}{x^2}\).
Using \(e^x = 1 + x + \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \dfrac{x^3}{6} + \cdots\):
Numerator \(= \dfrac{x^2}{2} + \dfrac{x^3}{6} + \cdots\), so the limit \(= \dfrac{1}{2}\).
Worked example (integral): Approximate \(\displaystyle\int_0^{0.5} e^{-x^2}\,dx\).
\(e^{-x^2} = 1 - x^2 + \dfrac{x^4}{2} - \cdots\). Integrating term by term:
\(\approx \left[x - \dfrac{x^3}{3} + \dfrac{x^5}{10}\right]_0^{0.5} \approx 0.4648\).

Term-by-term differentiation and integration are justified inside the radius of convergence. See Inverse Chain Rule for direct integration methods.

6) Engineering applications

Taylor series underpin linearisation of nonlinear systems, finite-difference approximations, and numerical solvers used throughout engineering analysis.

Small-signal analysis (electronics)

Transistor and diode characteristics are linearised about a DC operating point using the first Taylor term, yielding small-signal gain and impedance models.

Numerical methods

Runge–Kutta and Newton–Raphson methods are built from truncated Taylor expansions. Truncation error analysis uses the remainder term directly.

Worked example: The period of a simple pendulum of length \(L\) for small angle \(\theta\) is \(T \approx 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\), obtained by expanding \(\sin\theta \approx \theta\) in the equation of motion \(\ddot{\theta} + (g/L)\sin\theta = 0\).

7) Radius of convergence

Every power series \(\sum c_n (x-a)^n\) has a radius of convergence \(R\) such that the series converges for \(|x - a| < R\) and diverges for \(|x - a| > R\).

Ratio test: If \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right| = L\), then \(R = \dfrac{1}{L}\) (with \(R = \infty\) if \(L = 0\)).
Worked example: Find the radius of convergence of \(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n}\).
\(\left|\dfrac{c_{n+1}}{c_n}\right| = \dfrac{n}{n+1} \to 1\), so \(R = 1\).
At \(x = 1\) the series is the harmonic series (diverges); at \(x = -1\) it is alternating (converges). See Convergence for endpoint analysis.
