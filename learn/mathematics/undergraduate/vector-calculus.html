1) Vector fields and the del operator

A vector field assigns a vector to each point in space. In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = P\,\mathbf{i} + Q\,\mathbf{j} + R\,\mathbf{k}\) might represent fluid velocity, electric field, or force. The del operator \(\nabla\) combines partial differentiation with vector algebra:

\[ \nabla = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\mathbf{k} \]

Three fundamental operations act on scalar and vector fields:

Operation Input Output Formula (\(\mathbb{R}^3\))
Gradient \(\nabla f\) Scalar \(f\) Vector \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial z}\mathbf{k}\)
Divergence \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\) Vector \(\mathbf{F}\) Scalar \(\dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \dfrac{\partial R}{\partial z}\)
Curl \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) Vector \(\mathbf{F}\) Vector Determinant of \(\mathbf{i},\mathbf{j},\mathbf{k};\,\partial/\partial x,\partial/\partial y,\partial/\partial z;\,P,Q,R\)

This extends Vectors, Differentiation, and Multivariable Calculus.

2) The gradient and directional derivatives

For a scalar field \(f(x,y,z)\), the gradient \(\nabla f\) points in the direction of steepest increase of \(f\), with magnitude equal to that maximum rate of change.

The directional derivative of \(f\) in the direction of a unit vector \(\mathbf{u}\) is: \[ D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} \]

Worked example: For \(f(x,y) = x^2 + xy + y^2\), find \(\nabla f\) at \((1,2)\).
\(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + y = 4\), \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x + 2y = 5\).
So \(\nabla f(1,2) = 4\mathbf{i} + 5\mathbf{j}\). The steepest ascent direction at \((1,2)\) is along this vector.
Level surfaces: \(\nabla f\) is normal to the level surface \(f(x,y,z) = c\). This is used in optimisation (Lagrange multipliers) and in interpreting potential fields in physics.

3) Divergence and curl — physical meaning

Divergence

\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\) measures the outflow per unit volume at a point. Positive divergence: source; negative: sink; zero: incompressible flow.

Example: \(\mathbf{F} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}\) has \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 3\) everywhere.

Curl

\(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) measures local rotation (circulation density). Non-zero curl indicates swirling; irrotational fields have \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\).

Example: \(\mathbf{F} = -y\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j}\) (rotation) has \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 2\mathbf{k}\).

Key identities (assuming sufficient smoothness): \[ \nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}, \qquad \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0 \]
Common mistake: Treating \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\) or \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\) as dot or cross products with a vector \(\mathbf{F}\) plugged in after \(\nabla\) — always apply the partial derivatives to the components of \(\mathbf{F}\).

4) Line integrals

A line integral integrates a function or vector field along a curve \(C\). For a vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) and curve from \(A\) to \(B\): \[ \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C P\,dx + Q\,dy + R\,dz \] This measures the work done by \(\mathbf{F}\) along \(C\).

If \(C\) is parameterised by \(\mathbf{r}(t)\), \(a \leq t \leq b\): \[ \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)\,dt \]

Worked example: Evaluate \(\int_C (y\,dx + x\,dy)\) where \(C\) is the line from \((0,0)\) to \((1,2)\).
Parameterise: \(x = t\), \(y = 2t\), \(0 \leq t \leq 1\). Then \(dx = dt\), \(dy = 2\,dt\).
Integral \(= \int_0^1 (2t + 2t)\,dt = \int_0^1 4t\,dt = 2\).

The value depends on the path unless \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative (section 6).

5) Surface integrals

A surface integral integrates over a surface \(S\). For a scalar function \(g\): \[ \iint_S g\,dS \] For a vector field, the flux through \(S\) is: \[ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}\,dS \] where \(\mathbf{n}\) is the unit normal (orientation matters).

For a surface \(z = g(x,y)\) over region \(D\) in the \(xy\)-plane, with upward normal: \[ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \left(-P\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x} - Q\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y} + R\right) dx\,dy \]

Worked example: Flux of \(\mathbf{F} = z\mathbf{k}\) through the unit disc \(x^2 + y^2 \leq 1\), \(z = 0\), with upward normal.
\(\mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} = z = 0\) on the surface, so flux \(= 0\).

Surface integrals connect to Divergence Theorem and Stokes' Theorem.

6) Conservative fields and potentials

A vector field \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative if \(\mathbf{F} = \nabla \phi\) for some scalar potential \(\phi\). Equivalently (on simply connected domains):

  • \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}\)
  • \(\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}\) is independent of path
  • \(\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0\) for every closed curve \(C\)

When \(\mathbf{F}\) is conservative, the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals gives: \[ \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \phi(B) - \phi(A) \]

Worked example: Show \(\mathbf{F} = 2x\mathbf{i} + 2y\mathbf{j}\) is conservative and find \(\phi\).
\(\dfrac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} = 2x \Rightarrow \phi = x^2 + h(y)\). Then \(\dfrac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} = h'(y) = 2y\), so \(\phi = x^2 + y^2 + C\). Work from \((0,0)\) to \((3,4)\): \(\phi(3,4) - \phi(0,0) = 25\).
Exam tip: A field can have zero curl on a domain with a hole (e.g. \(\mathbf{F} = \dfrac{-y\mathbf{i} + x\mathbf{j}}{x^2+y^2}\)) yet fail to be conservative globally — check the domain is simply connected.

7) Integral theorems — connecting the pieces

Vector calculus unifies line, surface, and volume integrals through three major theorems (studied in detail on linked pages):

Theorem Relates Statement (sketch)
Green's theorem (2D) Line integral ↔ area integral \(\oint_C P\,dx + Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA\)
Stokes' theorem Circulation ↔ curl flux \(\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}\)
Divergence theorem Flux ↔ divergence volume integral \(\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV\)
Key idea: Gradient fields have zero curl; curl fields have zero divergence. These theorems are the multidimensional generalisations of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
