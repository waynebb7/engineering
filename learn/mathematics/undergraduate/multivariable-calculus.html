1) Functions of several variables

A scalar field \(f(x, y, z)\) assigns a real number to each point in space. Engineering quantities such as temperature, pressure, and electric potential are naturally modelled as functions of position.

Notation: \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\). For \(n = 2\), the graph is a surface \(z = f(x,y)\). Level curves (or surfaces) are sets where \(f = \text{constant}\).
Worked example: For \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\), level curves \(f = c\) are circles centred at the origin. The surface is a paraboloid opening upward.

This extends Differentiation and Functions to higher dimensions, leading to Triple Integrals and Vector Calculus.

2) Partial derivatives

The partial derivative with respect to \(x\) is found by differentiating with respect to \(x\) while treating all other variables as constants:

Definitions: \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h, y, z) - f(x,y,z)}{h}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \text{ defined similarly.} \]
Worked example: For \(f(x,y) = x^3 y^2 + 2xy\):
\(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2 y^2 + 2y\), \quad \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x^3 y + 2x\).
Function \(\partial f/\partial x\) \(\partial f/\partial y\)
\(x^2 + y^2\) \(2x\) \(2y\)
\(e^{xy}\) \(ye^{xy}\) \(xe^{xy}\)
\(\sin(xy)\) \(y\cos(xy)\) \(x\cos(xy)\)
Common mistake: Forgetting which variables are held constant. In \(\partial f/\partial y\), treat \(x\) (and \(z\)) as constants throughout.

3) The gradient vector

The gradient \(\nabla f\) collects all first partial derivatives into a vector pointing in the direction of steepest ascent:

Gradient: \[ \nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right) \] Magnitude \(|\nabla f|\) gives the maximum rate of change; direction is perpendicular to level surfaces.
Worked example: For \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\), \(\nabla f = (2x, 2y)\).
At \((1, 2)\): \(\nabla f = (2, 4)\), \(|\nabla f| = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5}\).
Steepest ascent direction: unit vector \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1, 2)\).

The gradient is central to Optimization and appears in Vector Calculus as \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}\) and \(\nabla \times \mathbf{F}\).

4) Directional derivatives

The directional derivative of \(f\) in the direction of a unit vector \(\mathbf{u} = (u_1, u_2, u_3)\) measures the rate of change of \(f\) along \(\mathbf{u}\):

Directional derivative: \[ D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = u_1\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + u_2\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} + u_3\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \] Maximum value \(= |\nabla f|\) when \(\mathbf{u}\) is parallel to \(\nabla f\).
Worked example: For \(f(x,y) = xy\) at \((2, 1)\) in direction \(\mathbf{u} = (1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})\):
\(\nabla f = (y, x) = (1, 2)\) at \((2,1)\).
\(D_{\mathbf{u}} f = (1, 2) \cdot (1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2}) = 3/\sqrt{2}\).

Physical interpretation

Temperature gradient gives heat flux direction; potential gradient gives force direction.

Zero directional derivative

\(D_{\mathbf{u}} f = 0\) when \(\mathbf{u}\) is tangent to a level curve (perpendicular to \(\nabla f\)).

5) Chain rule for several variables

When variables depend on a parameter (or on other variables), the multivariable chain rule combines partial derivatives.

Case 1: \(z = f(x, y)\), \(x = x(t)\), \(y = y(t)\): \[ \frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} \] Case 2: \(z = f(x,y)\), \(x = x(s,t)\), \(y = y(s,t)\): \[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t} \]
Worked example: Let \(z = x^2 y\), \(x = t^2\), \(y = t^3\). Find \(dz/dt\).
\(\dfrac{dz}{dt} = 2xy \cdot 2t + x^2 \cdot 3t^2 = 4t^5 + 3t^6\).
(Check: \(z = t^7\), so \(dz/dt = 7t^6\) — agrees.)
Exam tip: Draw a tree diagram showing dependencies before writing the chain rule. Each path from the dependent variable to the independent one contributes a product of partial derivatives.

6) Critical points and classification

A critical point of \(f(x,y)\) occurs where \(\nabla f = \mathbf{0}\) or where partial derivatives fail to exist. The Hessian matrix classifies stationary points:

Hessian test: Let \(D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2\) at a critical point.
\(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} > 0\) → local minimum.
\(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\) → local maximum.
\(D < 0\) → saddle point.
\(D = 0\) → test inconclusive.
Worked example: Classify critical points of \(f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2\).
\(\nabla f = (2x, -2y) = (0,0)\) at origin. \(f_{xx} = 2\), \(f_{yy} = -2\), \(f_{xy} = 0\).
\(D = 2(-2) - 0 = -4 < 0\) → saddle point at \((0,0)\).

7) Engineering applications

Multivariable calculus is essential for modelling fields, optimising designs, and analysing sensitivity in systems with many parameters.

Heat conduction

Fourier's law: \(\mathbf{q} = -k\nabla T\). Heat flows opposite to the temperature gradient, with magnitude proportional to \(|\nabla T|\).

Structural optimisation

Minimising mass subject to stress constraints uses \(\nabla f = \mathbf{0}\) and Lagrange multipliers (see Optimization).

Worked example: A temperature field \(T(x,y) = 100 - x^2 - 2y^2\) (°C) on a plate.
\(\nabla T = (-2x, -4y)\). At \((1, 2)\): \(\nabla T = (-2, -8)\), so heat flows toward increasing \(x\) and \(y\) (toward cooler regions). Maximum rate of change: \(|\nabla T| = \sqrt{68}\) °C/unit.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Find \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) for \(f(x,y) = e^{x^2 y}\).
  2. Compute \(\nabla f\) for \(f(x,y,z) = xyz\) at \((1, 2, 3)\).
  3. Find the directional derivative of \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2\) at \((3, 4)\) in direction \(\mathbf{u} = (3/5, 4/5)\).
  4. If \(z = \ln(xy)\), \(x = st\), \(y = s/t\), find \(\partial z/\partial s\).
  5. Find and classify critical points of \(f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - 2x - 4y + 5\).
  6. Explain why \(\nabla f\) is perpendicular to level curves of \(f\).
  7. State the chain rule for \(z = f(x,y)\) where \(x\) and \(y\) depend on \(t\).
