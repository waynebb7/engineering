Eigenvalues (Electrical Engineering)

Undergraduate guide. Stability, state-space poles, circuit modes, and control-system eigenvalue analysis.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Eigenvalues and the characteristic equation

For a square matrix \(\mathbf{A}\), a scalar \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue if there exists a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) such that: \[ \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \] Eigenvalues are found by solving the characteristic equation: \[ \det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0 \]

Key idea: In electrical engineering, eigenvalues of a system matrix are the poles of the transfer function. Their real and imaginary parts determine stability, damping, and oscillation frequency.
Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -4 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\), \(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda\mathbf{I}) = \lambda^2 + 3\lambda + 4 = 0\).
Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = \dfrac{-3 \pm j\sqrt{7}}{2}\) (complex conjugate pair — underdamped response).

Prerequisite: Matrices and Eigenvalues.

2) State-space models and stability

Linear time-invariant (LTI) systems are often written in state-space form: \[ \dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}(t) + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{u}(t), \qquad \mathbf{y}(t) = \mathbf{C}\mathbf{x}(t) + \mathbf{D}\mathbf{u}(t) \] where \(\mathbf{x}\) holds state variables (voltages, currents, positions, etc.) and \(\mathbf{u}\) is the input.

Stability criterion (continuous time): The unforced system \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}\) is asymptotically stable if every eigenvalue of \(\mathbf{A}\) has negative real part (\(\Re(\lambda_i) < 0\)).

Stable

All poles in the left half of the complex plane. Disturbances decay to zero.

Unstable

Any pole with \(\Re(\lambda) > 0\) causes unbounded growth. Marginal stability when poles lie on the imaginary axis.

Exam tip: Stability depends only on the eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{A}\), not on \(\mathbf{B}\), \(\mathbf{C}\), or \(\mathbf{D}\).

3) RLC circuit modes and eigenvalues

A series RLC circuit with state variables charge \(q\) and current \(i\) gives: \[ \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -\dfrac{1}{LC} & -\dfrac{R}{L} \end{bmatrix} \] The characteristic equation is \(\lambda^2 + \dfrac{R}{L}\lambda + \dfrac{1}{LC} = 0\).

Condition Eigenvalues Response
\(R^2 < 4L/C\) Complex conjugate pair Underdamped oscillation
\(R^2 = 4L/C\) Repeated real root Critically damped
\(R^2 > 4L/C\) Two distinct real roots Overdamped
Worked example: \(R = 10\,\Omega\), \(L = 0.1\,\text{H}\), \(C = 100\,\mu\text{F}\).
\(\dfrac{R}{L} = 100\), \(\dfrac{1}{LC} = 10^5\). Discriminant \(= 10^4 - 4 \times 10^5 < 0\) → underdamped.
Natural frequency \(\omega_n = 1/\sqrt{LC} = 316\,\text{rad/s}\); damping ratio \(\zeta = \dfrac{R}{2}\sqrt{\dfrac{C}{L}} \approx 0.16\).

4) Control systems and pole placement

In feedback control, the closed-loop eigenvalues (closed-loop poles) determine transient response: speed of settling, overshoot, and oscillation. Engineers design controllers to place eigenvalues in desired regions.

State feedback: With control law \(\mathbf{u} = -\mathbf{K}\mathbf{x}\), the closed-loop matrix is \(\mathbf{A}_{\text{cl}} = \mathbf{A} - \mathbf{B}\mathbf{K}\). Choosing \(\mathbf{K}\) shifts eigenvalues.
Design targets:
Fast settling → eigenvalues further left (more negative real parts).
Low overshoot → avoid eigenvalues too close to the imaginary axis.
Adequate damping → complex poles with sufficient real part.

The imaginary part of a complex eigenvalue pair gives the damped natural frequency: \(\omega_d = |\Im(\lambda)|\).

5) Power systems and small-signal stability

Power grids are modelled by nonlinear differential-algebraic equations. For small disturbances around an operating point, the system is linearised to obtain a Jacobian matrix \(\mathbf{J}\): \[ \Delta\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{J}\,\Delta\mathbf{x} \] Eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{J}\) are electromechanical modes — they reveal whether oscillations between generators and loads will grow or decay.

Interpretation:
Eigenvalues near \(j0.5\)–\(j3\,\text{rad/s}\): low-frequency inter-area oscillations.
Negative real parts → oscillations damp out (desirable).
Positive real parts → growing oscillations, risk of blackout.
Common mistake: Confusing eigenvalues of the open-loop plant with closed-loop eigenvalues after feedback. Always identify which matrix you are analysing.

6) Modal analysis and circuit natural frequencies

Each eigenvalue \(\lambda_i\) corresponds to a mode — a pattern of state evolution. The full response is a superposition of modes \(e^{\lambda_i t}\) weighted by initial conditions and eigenvectors.

Diagonalisation (when possible): If \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{\Lambda}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) with \(\mathbf{\Lambda} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)\), then \(\mathbf{z} = \mathbf{P}^{-1}\mathbf{x}\) decouples: \[ \dot{z}_i = \lambda_i z_i \quad \Rightarrow \quad z_i(t) = z_i(0)\,e^{\lambda_i t} \]
Filter and resonator design: LC tanks, coupled resonators, and transmission-line resonances all have natural frequencies determined by eigenvalue problems. Avoid placing operating frequencies near unstable or lightly damped modes.

See also Eigenvectors (Electrical Engineering) for mode shapes.

7) Computing eigenvalues in practice

For \(2 \times 2\) matrices, solve the quadratic characteristic polynomial directly. For larger systems, use numerical tools (MATLAB eig, Python numpy.linalg.eig, or calculator software).

Trace and determinant

For \(2 \times 2\) matrix: \(\lambda^2 - (\text{tr}\,\mathbf{A})\lambda + \det\mathbf{A} = 0\).

\(\text{tr}\,\mathbf{A} = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2\), \(\det\mathbf{A} = \lambda_1\lambda_2\).

Complex conjugate pairs

Real \(\mathbf{A}\) → complex eigenvalues occur in conjugate pairs. \(\Re(\lambda)\) sets decay rate; \(\Im(\lambda)\) sets oscillation frequency.

Worked example: \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 5 \\ -1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}\).
\(\text{tr} = -4\), \(\det = 4 + 5 = 9\). So \(\lambda^2 + 4\lambda + 9 = 0\).
\(\lambda = -2 \pm j\sqrt{5}\). Real part \(-2\) → stable; imaginary part \(\sqrt{5}\) → oscillation at \(\sqrt{5}\,\text{rad/s}\).
