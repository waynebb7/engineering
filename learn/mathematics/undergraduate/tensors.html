1) Tensors and index notation

A tensor is a mathematical object that describes physical quantities in a way that is independent of the coordinate system, yet can be represented by components in any chosen basis. The rank (or order) of a tensor is the number of indices needed to label its components.

In index notation, components are written with subscripts (covariant) or superscripts (contravariant). For example, a rank-2 tensor \(T\) has components \(T_{ij}\) where \(i, j\) run over the dimension of the space (often \(1, 2, 3\) in physics and engineering).

Einstein summation convention: When an index appears twice (once up, once down, or both as subscripts in Cartesian settings), summation over that index is implied: \[ a_i b_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i \]

This builds on Vectors and Matrices. Tensors generalise both to higher rank and coordinate-free settings.

2) Rank-0 tensors: scalars

A rank-0 tensor has no free index — it is a single number, a scalar. Scalars are invariant under coordinate transformations: their value does not change when you rotate or translate the coordinate system.

Examples:
  • Temperature at a point: \(T = 300\,\text{K}\)
  • Mass: \(m = 5\,\text{kg}\)
  • Trace of a matrix (in Cartesian coordinates): \(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = A_{ii}\)
Worked example: Under a rotation of coordinates, the scalar \(s = x^2 + y^2\) in 2D remains the same value (it equals \(r^2\) in polar coordinates). Rank-0 tensors need no index subscript.

3) Rank-1 tensors: vectors

A rank-1 tensor has one free index. In \(\mathbb{R}^n\), this is a vector with components \(v_i\) (or \(v^i\)).

Under a change of basis with transformation matrix \(R\), vector components transform as: \[ v'_i = R_{ij}\, v_j \quad \text{(covariant)} \qquad \text{or} \qquad v'^i = R^i{}_j\, v^j \quad \text{(contravariant)} \]

Worked example: The displacement vector \(\mathbf{d} = (3, 4)\) has magnitude \(\|\mathbf{d}\| = \sqrt{d_i d_i} = 5\). The magnitude is a rank-0 tensor built from a rank-1 tensor via the dot product (contraction).
Common mistake: Treating a single row or column of a matrix as a vector without checking transformation rules. A vector transforms differently from a covector (dual vector).

4) Rank-2 tensors: matrices and beyond

A rank-2 tensor has two free indices: components \(T_{ij}\). An \(n \times n\) matrix is the most familiar rank-2 tensor in Cartesian coordinates.

Important rank-2 tensors include:

  • Identity: \(\delta_{ij}\) (Kronecker delta) — 1 on the diagonal, 0 elsewhere
  • Metric: \(g_{ij}\) — defines lengths and angles (in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), often \(\delta_{ij}\))
  • Stress and strain in continuum mechanics
  • Inertia tensor in rigid-body dynamics
Contraction: Setting two indices equal and summing reduces rank by 2: \[ T_{ij} \to T_{ii} = T_{11} + T_{22} + T_{33} \quad \text{(trace)} \]
Worked example: For \[ T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T_{11} = 2,\; T_{12} = 1,\; T_{21} = 3,\; T_{22} = 4 \] The trace is \(T_{ii} = 2 + 4 = 6\).

5) Index notation in practice

Index notation makes multilinear operations compact. Key rules:

Expression Meaning
\(a_i b_i\) Dot product \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}\)
\(C_{ij} = A_{ik} B_{kj}\) Matrix product \(\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{B}\)
\(\delta_{ij}\) Identity: \(\delta_{ij} v_j = v_i\)
\(\epsilon_{ijk}\) Levi-Civita symbol (cross product in 3D)
Worked example: Matrix-vector product in index form: \[ y_i = A_{ij} x_j \] For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, \(y_1 = A_{11}x_1 + A_{12}x_2\) and \(y_2 = A_{21}x_1 + A_{22}x_2\).

Free indices must match on both sides of an equation. Dummy (summed) indices can be renamed without changing the meaning.

6) Transformations of tensor components

A tensor is defined by how its components transform under a change of coordinates. For a rotation matrix \(Q\) (orthogonal, \(\det Q = 1\)):

  • Rank 0: \(s' = s\)
  • Rank 1: \(v'_i = Q_{ij} v_j\)
  • Rank 2: \(T'_{ij} = Q_{ik} Q_{jl} T_{kl}\)

Each index picks up one factor of the transformation matrix. This ensures that physical laws written in tensor form are coordinate-independent.

Worked example: If \(\mathbf{T}\) represents stress and the coordinate system rotates by \(Q\), the new components are \(T'_{ij} = Q_{ik} Q_{jl} T_{kl}\). The physical stress state is unchanged; only the numbers in the matrix depend on the axes chosen.
Exam tip: Symmetry matters. Many physical rank-2 tensors are symmetric: \(T_{ij} = T_{ji}\). This reduces independent components (6 in 3D instead of 9).

7) The stress tensor in engineering

In solid mechanics, the Cauchy stress tensor \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\) is a symmetric rank-2 tensor. Component \(\sigma_{ij}\) is the force per unit area in direction \(i\) acting on a face whose normal is in direction \(j\).

Traction vector: On a surface with unit normal \(\mathbf{n}\): \[ t_i = \sigma_{ij} n_j \] This gives the stress vector (force per area) acting on that surface.
Worked example: For a uniaxial tension \(\sigma_{11} = 100\,\text{MPa}\) with all other components zero, a face normal to the \(x\)-axis (\(n_1 = 1\)) has traction \(t_1 = 100\,\text{MPa}\). A face normal to \(y\) (\(n_2 = 1\)) has \(t_1 = \sigma_{12} = 0\) — no shear on that face in this case.

Principal stresses are the eigenvalues of \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}\); principal directions are the eigenvectors. See Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors for the mathematical tools.
