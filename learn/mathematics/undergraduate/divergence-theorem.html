Divergence Theorem

Undergraduate guide. Gauss's theorem, flux integrals through closed surfaces, and physical interpretation.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Gauss's theorem — statement

The Divergence Theorem (also called Gauss's theorem) relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the integral of its divergence over the enclosed volume.

\[ \oiint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})\, dV \]

Here \(V\) is a solid region bounded by a piecewise smooth closed surface \(S\), with \(\mathbf{n}\) the outward unit normal. \(\mathbf{F}\) must be continuously differentiable on a neighbourhood of \(V\).

Key idea: Total outward flux through the boundary equals the sum of all sources and sinks (positive and negative divergence) inside the volume.

Builds on Vector Calculus — divergence, flux integrals, and Triple Integrals.

2) Divergence and flux

The divergence of \(\mathbf{F} = P\mathbf{i} + Q\mathbf{j} + R\mathbf{k}\) is: \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z} \] At a point, it measures the net outflow per unit volume — how much the field spreads out locally.

The flux through a surface \(S\) is: \[ \Phi = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n}\, dS = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} \] For a closed surface, outward flux counts what leaves the volume minus what enters.

Infinitesimal form: For a small volume \(\Delta V\) shrinking to a point, \[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \lim_{\Delta V \to 0} \frac{1}{\Delta V}\oiint_{\partial(\Delta V)} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} \]
Common mistake: Applying the divergence theorem when \(S\) is not closed, or using an inward normal without adjusting the sign.

3) Flux integrals through surfaces

To compute \(\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}\) directly, parametrise each piece of \(S\) or use symmetry. For a surface \(z = g(x,y)\) with outward normal (on a graph surface): \[ d\mathbf{S} = \pm\left(-\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} - \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \mathbf{k}\right) dx\,dy \] Choose the sign so \(\mathbf{n}\) points outward from \(V\).

Worked example (direct flux): Flux of \(\mathbf{F} = z\mathbf{k}\) through the unit sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=1\).
On the sphere, \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n} = z\) (outward normal). By symmetry, \(\oiint_S z\,dS = 0\) (equal positive and negative \(z\) contributions).
When to use Gauss: If \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\) is constant or simple inside \(V\), the volume integral \(\iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV\) is often far easier than integrating over a curved surface.

4) Physical interpretation

Fluid flow

If \(\mathbf{F}\) is velocity \(\mathbf{v}\), outward flux is the volume flow rate leaving \(V\) per second. \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} > 0\): fluid expanding (source); \(< 0\): compressing (sink). Incompressible flow: \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0\).

Electromagnetism

Gauss's law: \(\oiint_S \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \dfrac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}\). Electric charge is a source of \(\mathbf{E}\): \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0\).

Conservation laws: If \(\mathbf{F}\) represents flux of a conserved quantity (mass, energy) and no sources exist inside \(V\), then \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0\) and total outward flux is zero — what leaves must equal what enters.

Heat flux \(\mathbf{q} = -k\nabla T\) satisfies \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{q} = -\rho c\,\partial T/\partial t\) in transient heat conduction.

5) Relation to the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

In one dimension, the FTC connects values at endpoints to an integral of the derivative: \[ f(b) - f(a) = \int_a^b f'(x)\,dx \] Writing \(\mathbf{F} = f(x)\mathbf{i}\) on \([a,b]\), the "flux" out of the interval is \(f(b) - f(a)\) and \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = f'(x)\). Gauss's theorem is the 3D generalisation.

2D divergence (normal) form of Green's theorem: \[ \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,ds = \iint_D (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dA \] where \(C\) is the positively oriented boundary of \(D\). This is Gauss's theorem in the plane.

The pattern \(\displaystyle\int_{\partial\Omega} = \int_\Omega \text{(derivative)}\) unifies FTC, Green, Stokes, and Divergence theorems.

6) Relation to Stokes' theorem

Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem address different geometries:

  • Stokes: open surface \(S\) with boundary \(\partial S\) — relates line integral to \(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\)
  • Divergence: closed surface \(S = \partial V\) with no boundary — relates flux to \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\)

A key identity linking them: \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0\) (curl fields have no divergence) and \(\nabla\times(\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}\) (gradient fields have no curl).

Choosing a theorem: Closed surface flux → Divergence. Circulation around a loop bounding a surface → Stokes. Conservative field / potential difference → Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals.

7) Worked example — unit ball

Find the outward flux of \(\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k}\) through the sphere \(x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1\).

Solution via Gauss: \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3\).
\[ \oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V 3\,dV = 3 \cdot \text{Vol}(V) = 3 \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi = 4\pi \]
Alternative check: On the unit sphere, outward \(\mathbf{n} = x\mathbf{i}+y\mathbf{j}+z\mathbf{k}\), so \(\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n} = x^2+y^2+z^2 = 1\) and flux \(= \oiint_S 1\,dS = 4\pi\) (surface area of unit sphere).
Exam tip: Verify \(\mathbf{F}\) is defined throughout \(V\) (no singularities inside). If \(\mathbf{F}\) blows up at the origin, exclude a small ball and take a limit.
