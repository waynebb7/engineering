Data Plots

KS2 to KS3 guide. How to choose, draw, and read bar charts, pictograms, line graphs, pie charts, and scatter graphs — and spot misleading graphs.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Why do we plot data?

Data plots (graphs and charts) turn numbers into pictures so patterns are easier to see. They help you compare categories, show change over time, and spot relationships between variables.

Key idea: Choose the graph type that matches your data and the question you want to answer. A good graph makes the message clear at a glance.

Examples: favourite sports in a class (categories), temperature through a day (time), height vs shoe size (two measurements).

2) Bar charts and pictograms

Bar charts

Best for comparing discrete categories (separate groups).

  • Bars must be the same width
  • Equal gaps between bars
  • Label axes and include units
  • Use a clear title

Pictograms

Use symbols or pictures to represent counts.

  • State what one symbol represents (e.g. 1 icon = 2 pupils)
  • Half symbols can show odd values
  • Good for younger learners; less precise than bar charts
Worked example: Books read by four pupils: Ali 3, Beth 5, Chen 2, Dana 4. Bar chart: four bars with heights 3, 5, 2, 4 on a “Number of books” axis.

3) Line graphs — change over time

A line graph joins points with straight segments to show how a value changes, usually over time. Common in science for temperature, distance, or growth measurements.

How to draw one:
1. Put time (or the independent variable) on the x-axis
2. Put the measured quantity on the y-axis
3. Plot crosses or dots at each (time, value) pair
4. Join with straight lines in order

Reading a line graph

  • Steeper line = faster change
  • Flat section = no change
  • Going up = increase; going down = decrease

Interpolation

Reading a value between plotted points (within the data range).

Example: estimate temperature at 2:30 pm from hourly readings.

4) Pie charts — parts of a whole

A pie chart shows how a total is split into fractions or percentages. The whole circle is 100% or 360°.

Worked example: 40 pupils chose lunch options: Pizza 10, Pasta 16, Salad 8, Other 6.
Pizza = 10/40 = 25% → sector angle = 25% of 360° = 90°
Pasta = 16/40 = 40% → 144°

When to use

  • Showing proportions of one total
  • Comparing share of a whole
  • Small number of categories (roughly 4–6)

When not to use

  • Many tiny slices (hard to read)
  • Comparing totals across different groups (use bar chart)
  • Showing change over time (use line graph)
Common mistake: Drawing pie charts without checking that all categories sum to the total. Always verify: fractions should add to 1 (or percentages to 100%).

5) Scatter graphs and correlation (KS3)

A scatter graph plots pairs of values without joining the points. It shows whether two variables might be related.

Types of correlation

  • Positive: as one increases, the other tends to increase
  • Negative: as one increases, the other tends to decrease
  • None: no clear pattern

Important vocabulary

  • Outlier: a point far from the trend
  • Line of best fit: a straight line through the middle of the cloud (KS3/GCSE)
KS3 note: Correlation does not prove causation. Ice cream sales and sunburn may both rise in summer — one does not necessarily cause the other.

6) Mean, median, mode, and range

Before plotting, you often summarise data with averages and spread.

Measure What it tells you Example data: 3, 5, 5, 7, 10
Mean Total ÷ number of values (3+5+5+7+10) ÷ 5 = 6
Median Middle value when ordered 5
Mode Most frequent value 5
Range Largest − smallest 10 − 3 = 7
Common mistake: Using the mean when there is an extreme outlier. The median is often better for skewed data (e.g. house prices).

7) Misleading graphs — read critically

Truncated y-axis

Starting the y-axis at 50 instead of 0 can exaggerate small differences between bars.

Missing labels

No units, no title, or unclear categories make graphs impossible to interpret fairly.

Good practice: Always check the scale, axis labels, sample size, and source before drawing conclusions from a graph in the news or online.
