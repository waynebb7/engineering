1) What is multiplication?

Multiplication finds the total when you have several equal groups of the same size.

  • Written with the times sign: × (or sometimes ·)
  • 4 × 3 = 12 means 4 groups of 3, or 3 added four times: 3 + 3 + 3 + 3
  • The numbers multiplied are called factors; the answer is the product
Words for multiplication: times, multiply, product, groups of, lots of, double, triple.

2) Arrays and equal groups

An array is a rectangle of dots in rows and columns. It shows multiplication visually.

Rows and columns

3 rows of 4 dots → 3 × 4 = 12. Turning the array shows 4 × 3 = 12 too.

Repeated addition

5 × 6 means 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6 = 30.

3) Times tables and commutativity

Learning times tables up to 12 × 12 saves time in almost every maths topic.

  • Commutative: 7 × 8 = 8 × 7 — order does not change the product
  • Identity: 6 × 1 = 6
  • Zero: 6 × 0 = 0
Tip: If you know 7 × 8 = 56, you also know 56 ÷ 7 = 8.

4) Multiplying by 10, 100, and 1000

Multiplying whole numbers by 10, 100, or 1000 shifts digits to the left — each zero adds a place value shift.

Calculation Result Rule
34 × 10 340 Digits move one place left
34 × 100 3400 Digits move two places left
5.2 × 10 52 Decimal point moves one place right
Common mistake: Adding zeros without thinking about place value — 3.5 × 10 is 35, not 3.50.

5) Grid method (partitioning)

Split numbers into tens and units, multiply separately, then add partial products.

Worked example: 23 × 4
20 × 4 = 80
3 × 4 = 12
80 + 12 = 92

At KS3 this idea extends to multiplying two-digit numbers: 23 × 45 uses four smaller products in a grid.

6) Short multiplication

Multiply by the units digit first, carrying as needed, then multiply by tens (and higher place values).

Worked example: 47 × 6
Units: 7 × 6 = 42 → write 2, carry 4
Tens: 4 × 6 + 4 = 28 → answer 282

7) Word problems

Clue words: each, every, per, altogether (with equal groups), area (length × width).

Worked example: A box holds 8 eggs. How many eggs in 15 boxes?
15 × 8 = 120 eggs.
