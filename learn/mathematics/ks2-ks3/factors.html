1) Factors and multiples — what is the difference?

Factors

A factor is a whole number that divides exactly into another number with no remainder.

Factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

Because 12 ÷ 3 = 4 exactly.

Multiples

A multiple is the result of multiplying a number by a whole number.

Multiples of 5: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, …

Because 5 × 4 = 20.

Key idea: Factors are usually smaller (or equal) to the number. Multiples are usually larger (or equal). 3 is a factor of 12; 12 is a multiple of 3.

2) Finding all factors of a number

Work in factor pairs that multiply to give the target number. Stop when pairs start to repeat.

Worked example — factors of 24:
1 × 24, 2 × 12, 3 × 8, 4 × 6
Factors: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24

Systematic method

  1. Test division by 1, 2, 3, 4, …
  2. When you find a pair, write both numbers
  3. Stop when the first number exceeds the second in a pair

Factor bugs (KS2 activity)

Draw “legs” in pairs from a body number. Each pair shows two factors that multiply together.

Useful for visual learners in primary and early KS3.

3) Prime numbers and prime factorisation

A prime number has exactly two factors: 1 and itself. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, …

1 is not prime. 2 is the only even prime number.

Prime factorisation: Write a number as a product of prime factors.
60 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 2² × 3 × 5
Use a factor tree or repeated division by primes.
Common mistake: Calling 1 a prime number, or forgetting that 2 is prime because it is even.

4) Highest Common Factor (HCF)

The HCF (also called GCD) is the largest factor that two or more numbers share.

Listing method

Factors of 18: 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18

Factors of 24: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24

Common factors: 1, 2, 3, 6 → HCF = 6

Prime factor method (KS3)

18 = 2 × 3²

24 = 2³ × 3

Take lowest powers of shared primes: 2 × 3 = 6

Uses: Simplifying fractions (18/24 → 3/4), sharing items equally into the largest equal groups, and solving ratio problems.

5) Lowest Common Multiple (LCM)

The LCM is the smallest number that is a multiple of each number in the list.

Worked example — LCM of 4 and 6:
Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, …
Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, …
LCM = 12

Prime factor method

4 = 2², 6 = 2 × 3

Take highest powers: 2² × 3 = 12

Uses

  • Finding common denominators for fractions
  • Events that repeat on different cycles (e.g. buses every 4 and 6 minutes)
  • Timing problems in KS3

6) Square numbers and cube numbers

A square number is the result of multiplying a whole number by itself. A cube number is a whole number multiplied by itself three times.

n Square n² Cube n³
1 1 1
2 4 8
3 9 27
4 16 64
5 25 125
KS3 link: Square numbers appear in area (side × side) and in indices. Cube numbers link to volume.
