1) What is a fraction?

A fraction shows part of a whole. It tells you how many equal parts you have out of how many equal parts make the whole.

In the fraction 3/4, the numerator (top number) is 3 and the denominator (bottom number) is 4. This means 3 out of 4 equal parts.

Key idea: The denominator tells you how many equal pieces the whole is split into. The numerator tells you how many of those pieces you are counting.

Real-life examples: half a pizza (1/2), three quarters of an hour (3/4), two fifths of a bar of chocolate (2/5).

2) Types of fractions you will meet

Proper fractions

Numerator is smaller than denominator. Value is less than 1.

  • 1/3, 5/8, 7/10

Improper fractions

Numerator is equal to or larger than denominator. Value is 1 or more.

  • 5/4, 9/5, 12/6

Mixed numbers

A whole number plus a proper fraction.

  • 1 1/2 means one whole and one half
  • 2 3/4 means two wholes and three quarters
Convert: 7/4 = 1 3/4 because 4 quarters make 1 whole, with 3 quarters left over.

3) Equivalent fractions

Two fractions are equivalent if they represent the same amount, even though they look different.

Rule: Multiply or divide the top and bottom by the same number (except zero).
Example: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8

Simplifying (cancelling down)

Find the highest common factor of numerator and denominator, then divide both.

12/18 → divide top and bottom by 6 → 2/3

Why it matters

You need equivalent fractions to add or subtract when denominators differ, and to compare fractions fairly.

Common mistake: Adding the top numbers and bottom numbers separately. 1/2 + 1/3 is not 2/5. You must use a common denominator first.

4) Comparing and ordering fractions

When denominators are the same, compare numerators. The larger numerator means the larger fraction.

Compare Method Result
3/8 and 5/8 Same denominator — compare tops 3/8 < 5/8
2/3 and 3/5 Common denominator 15 → 10/15 and 9/15 2/3 > 3/5
1/4 and 1/2 Halves are bigger pieces than quarters 1/4 < 1/2
KS3 tip: On a number line, fractions further to the right are greater. Place 0, 1/2, and 1 first, then fit others between.

5) Adding and subtracting fractions

Same denominator

Add or subtract the numerators. Keep the denominator the same.

2/7 + 3/7 = 5/7

5/6 − 1/6 = 4/6 = 2/3 (simplify)

Different denominators

  1. Find a common denominator (often the LCM)
  2. Convert both fractions
  3. Add or subtract numerators
  4. Simplify if possible
Worked example: 1/3 + 1/4
Common denominator 12 → 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12

6) Multiplying and dividing fractions

Multiply

Multiply tops together, multiply bottoms together, then simplify.

2/3 × 3/5 = 6/15 = 2/5

“Of” often means multiply: 1/2 of 20 = 1/2 × 20 = 10

Divide

Keep the first fraction. Flip the second fraction (find its reciprocal). Then multiply.

2/3 ÷ 4/5 = 2/3 × 5/4 = 10/12 = 5/6

Common mistake: Forgetting to flip when dividing. Always use “Keep, Change, Flip” for division.

7) Fractions, decimals, and percentages

Fractions, decimals, and percentages are different ways to write the same value.

Fraction Decimal Percentage
1/2 0.5 50%
1/4 0.25 25%
3/5 0.6 60%
KS3 link: To convert a fraction to a decimal, divide numerator by denominator. 3/8 = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375
