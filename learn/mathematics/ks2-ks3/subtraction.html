1) What is subtraction?

Subtraction finds how much is left after taking away, or the difference between two amounts.

  • Written with the minus sign: −
  • 9 − 4 = 5 can mean “9 take away 4” or “the difference between 9 and 4”
Inverse: Subtraction undoes addition. If 5 + 4 = 9, then 9 − 4 = 5.

2) Mental subtraction strategies

Counting back

12 − 3: start at 12, count back 3 → 11, 10, 9.

Counting up (difference)

42 − 37: from 37 to 40 is +3, from 40 to 42 is +2 → difference 5.

Partitioning: 52 − 28 = 52 − 20 − 8 = 32 − 8 = 24.

3) Column subtraction

Line up by place value. Subtract column by column from right to left.

If the top digit is smaller, exchange (borrow) 1 from the next column to the left.

Worked example: 503 − 278
Units: exchange from tens → 13 − 8 = 5
Tens: after exchange, 9 − 7 = 2
Hundreds: 4 − 2 = 2 → answer 225

4) Zeros in column subtraction

When there is a zero in the top number, you may need to exchange from further left.

Example

400 − 156: exchange from hundreds through tens to units before subtracting.

Check

Use addition: 225 + 278 = 503 confirms the subtraction answer.

Common mistake: Swapping digits when the top is smaller (writing 7 − 3 instead of exchanging). Always exchange first.

5) Subtraction and negative results

At KS2 you usually subtract a smaller number from a larger one. At KS3 you meet cases like 3 − 7, which need negative numbers.

Example: Temperature drops from 4 °C to −2 °C. The change is −2 − 4 = −6 degrees (a drop of 6 °C).

6) Word problems

Clue words: left, remaining, difference, how many more, decrease, less.

Worked example: A rope is 2.4 m long. 0.85 m is cut off. How much remains?
2.4 − 0.85 = 1.55 m
“How many more?” means find the difference, not always “bigger minus smaller” in words — set up the subtraction carefully from the story.
