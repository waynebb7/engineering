Basic Geometry

KS2 to KS3 guide. Shapes, angles, perimeter, area, and simple 3D solids.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Points, lines, and shapes

Geometry is the maths of shape, size, position, and space.

  • A point shows a location (no size)
  • A line continues forever in both directions; a line segment has two end points
  • 2D shapes are flat; 3D solids have length, width, and height
Key vocabulary: vertex (corner), edge, face, parallel, perpendicular.

2) Types of angles

Angle sizes

  • Acute: less than 90°
  • Right: exactly 90°
  • Obtuse: between 90° and 180°
  • Reflex: between 180° and 360°

Measuring

Use a protractor. Place the centre on the vertex and read where the second ray crosses the scale.

Common mistake: Reading the wrong scale on a protractor (inner vs outer). Check whether the angle is acute or obtuse first.

3) Triangles and quadrilaterals

Triangles by sides: equilateral (3 equal), isosceles (2 equal), scalene (none equal).

Quadrilaterals include squares, rectangles, parallelograms, rhombuses, trapeziums, and kites.

Fact: Angles in any triangle add to 180°. Angles in any quadrilateral add to 360°.

4) Perimeter

Perimeter is the total distance around the outside of a shape.

Worked example: Rectangle 8 cm by 5 cm
Perimeter = 8 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 26 cm
Or 2 × (8 + 5) = 26 cm

For irregular shapes, add every side length.

5) Area of rectangles and triangles

Rectangle

Area = length × width
Example: 8 × 5 = 40 cm²

Triangle

Area = ½ × base × height
Example: base 10 cm, height 6 cm → ½ × 10 × 6 = 30 cm²

Units: Area uses square units (cm², m²). Do not mix cm and m in one calculation.

6) Circles (intro)

A circle has every point the same distance from the centre.

  • Radius (r): centre to edge
  • Diameter (d): across through centre — d = 2r
  • Circumference: distance around — at KS3 use C = πd or C = 2πr
KS3 note: π is about 3.14 (or use the π button on a calculator). Area of a circle: A = πr².

7) 3D shapes and nets

Common 3D solids: cube, cuboid, cylinder, sphere, cone, pyramid, prism.

  • Volume measures space inside (cm³, m³)
  • A net is a flat pattern that folds into a 3D shape
Worked example: Cuboid 4 cm × 3 cm × 2 cm → volume = 4 × 3 × 2 = 24 cm³.
