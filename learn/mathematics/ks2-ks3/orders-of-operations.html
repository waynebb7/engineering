1) Why order matters

In maths, the same symbols can give different answers depending on the order you work in. Without a shared rule, 2 + 3 × 4 could be read as 20 or 14.

Key idea: The order of operations is an agreement so everyone gets the same correct answer. In the UK this is often taught as BIDMAS (or BODMAS).

Correct: 2 + 3 × 4 = 2 + 12 = 14 — multiply first, then add.

2) BIDMAS — what each letter means

Letter Operation Example
B Brackets (work inside brackets first) 3 × (2 + 4) = 3 × 6 = 18
I Indices (powers and roots) 2 + 3² = 2 + 9 = 11
D and M Division and Multiplication (left to right) 12 ÷ 3 × 2 = 4 × 2 = 8
A and S Addition and Subtraction (left to right) 10 − 4 + 2 = 6 + 2 = 8
BODMAS note: Some schools say “Orders” instead of “Indices” — it means the same thing (powers, squares, cubes).

3) Brackets come first

Anything inside brackets (or parentheses) must be calculated before operations outside. Nested brackets are worked from the innermost pair outward.

Single brackets

5 × (7 − 2) = 5 × 5 = 25

(4 + 6) ÷ 2 = 10 ÷ 2 = 5

Nested brackets

2 × (3 + (4 − 1))

Inner: 4 − 1 = 3

Middle: 3 + 3 = 6

Outer: 2 × 6 = 12

Common mistake: Ignoring brackets and working left to right from the start. Always simplify inside brackets before multiplying or dividing outside.

4) Indices (powers) before × and ÷

Indices means powers (and square roots at KS3). Evaluate these before multiplication and division.

Worked example: 4 + 2³ × 3
Step 1 — indices: 2³ = 8
Step 2 — multiply: 8 × 3 = 24
Step 3 — add: 4 + 24 = 28

At KS3 you may also see square roots: √16 + 3 × 2 = 4 + 6 = 10

5) Multiplication, division, addition, subtraction

× and ÷ have equal priority

Work from left to right when only × and ÷ appear.

24 ÷ 6 × 2 = 4 × 2 = 8

Not 24 ÷ 12 = 2

+ and − have equal priority

Work from left to right when only + and − appear.

15 − 6 + 4 = 9 + 4 = 13

Not 15 − 10 = 5

Full example: 20 − 3 × 2² + 8 ÷ 4
Indices: 2² = 4 → 20 − 3 × 4 + 8 ÷ 4
× and ÷: 3 × 4 = 12, 8 ÷ 4 = 2 → 20 − 12 + 2
+ and −: 20 − 12 = 8, 8 + 2 = 10

6) Calculators and negative numbers

Scientific calculators follow order of operations automatically, but you must enter brackets correctly for fractions and grouped terms.

Calculator tips

  • Use bracket keys for grouped calculations
  • Check whether your calculator uses − for negative or for subtract
  • Use the fraction template if available

With negatives

−3² on many calculators means −(3²) = −9

For (−3)² = 9, use brackets around the negative number.

Common mistake: Typing 2 + 3 × 4 into a basic four-function calculator left-to-right gives 20. A scientific calculator gives 14. Know which type you are using.
