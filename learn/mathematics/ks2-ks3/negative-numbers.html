Negative Numbers

KS2 to KS3 guide. Numbers below zero, how to read them on a number line, and how to add, subtract, multiply, and divide with negatives.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) What are negative numbers?

Negative numbers are numbers less than zero. They are written with a minus sign, such as −3, −12, or −0.5.

On a number line, positive numbers are to the right of zero and negative numbers are to the left. The further left you go, the smaller the number.

Key idea: Negative numbers represent values below a reference point — temperature below freezing, debt, floors below ground level, or scores below par.

Example: If the temperature is −4°C, it is 4 degrees below zero. −4°C is colder than −1°C.

2) Reading and comparing negative numbers

Greater and less

  • −2 is greater than −7 (less cold, less debt)
  • −10 is less than −3
  • Any negative number is less than zero
  • Any positive number is greater than any negative number

Real contexts

  • Temperature: −5°C vs 2°C
  • Lift floors: Ground = 0, basement = −1
  • Bank balance: −£20 means you owe £20
  • Sea level: depth 30 m = −30 m
Memory aid: On a number line, “more positive” means further right; “more negative” means further left.

3) Adding and subtracting with negatives

Adding a negative number is the same as subtracting a positive number.

Calculation Think of it as Answer
5 + (−3) 5 − 3 2
−4 + 7 Start at −4, move 7 right 3
−2 − 5 −2 + (−5), move 5 left from −2 −7
−3 − (−6) Subtracting a negative = adding 3
Rule: Subtracting a negative number means adding the positive version. a − (−b) = a + b

4) Multiplying and dividing negatives

Multiplication rules

  • Positive × positive = positive
  • Negative × negative = positive
  • Positive × negative = negative
  • Negative × positive = negative

(−4) × 3 = −12

(−5) × (−2) = 10

Division rules

The same sign rules apply to division.

(−20) ÷ 4 = −5

(−24) ÷ (−6) = 4

KS3 note: If there is an even number of negative signs in a multiplication, the answer is positive. An odd number of negatives gives a negative answer.
Common mistake: Thinking −3² equals 9. Without brackets, it means −(3²) = −9. Only (−3)² = 9.

5) Absolute value (distance from zero)

The absolute value of a number is its distance from zero, always written as a positive value. It is written with vertical bars: |−6| = 6 and |4| = 4.

Example: The temperature change from −3°C to 2°C is a rise of 5 degrees. On a number line, the distance from −3 to 2 is 5.

Absolute value is useful in KS3 when working with distance, error, or symmetry on graphs.

6) Worked examples across contexts

Temperature

Morning: −2°C. Afternoon: 5°C.

Change = 5 − (−2) = 7°C rise.

Money

Balance −£15. You pay in £20.

New balance = −15 + 20 = £5 (in credit).

Elevation

A submarine at −80 m rises 35 m.

New depth = −80 + 35 = −45 m.

Number line moves

Start at −4, subtract 6, then add 10.

−4 − 6 + 10 = 0
