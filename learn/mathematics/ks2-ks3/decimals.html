1) What is a decimal?

A decimal uses a decimal point to show parts of a whole number.

  • 3.5 means 3 wholes and 5 tenths
  • 0.25 means 25 hundredths — the same as the fraction ¼
  • Each place to the right is ten times smaller: tenths, hundredths, thousandths …
Key idea: The decimal point separates whole numbers from fractional parts.

2) Place value in decimals

Thousands Hundreds Tens Units . Tenths Hundredths
0 0 0 4 . 7 2

4.72 = 4 + 0.7 + 0.02

Trailing zeros: 3.5 and 3.50 have the same value — the extra zero shows precision in money (£3.50).

3) Comparing and ordering decimals

Compare place value from left to right, just like whole numbers.

Example

0.8 > 0.75 because 8 tenths is more than 7 tenths.

Padding

Write 0.6 as 0.60 to compare fairly with 0.58.

Common mistake: Thinking 0.19 is bigger than 0.2 because 19 > 2. Compare tenths first: 0.2 = 0.20.

4) Rounding decimals

Look at the digit to the right of the place you are rounding to:

  • 5 or more → round up
  • 4 or less → round down
Worked example: Round 3.847 to 1 decimal place → look at hundredths (4) → answer 3.8
To 2 decimal places → look at thousandths (7) → answer 3.85

5) Adding and subtracting decimals

Line up decimal points vertically. Add zeros as placeholders if needed.

Worked example: 5.3 + 2.47 = 7.77
Write as 5.30 + 2.47.

Subtraction uses the same alignment: 4.2 − 1.85 = 2.35.

6) Multiplying and dividing decimals

Multiply: ignore decimals at first, multiply whole numbers, then count total decimal places in both factors.

Example: 0.3 × 0.4 = 0.12 (3 × 4 = 12; two decimal places total).

Divide: move the decimal point in both numbers to make the divisor a whole number, then divide.

Common mistake: Misplacing the decimal point after multiplication — always check whether your answer should be bigger or smaller than the factors.

7) Decimals, fractions, and percentages

Decimals connect to other ways of writing parts of a whole:

  • 0.5 = ½ = 50%
  • 0.25 = ¼ = 25%
  • 0.1 = ¹⁄₁₀ = 10%
KS3 note: Converting between forms is essential for science, finance, and statistics.
