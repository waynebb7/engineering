Irrational Pattern Functions

Exploratory guide. How irrational numbers create quasiperiodic patterns — and what is real mathematics behind this non-standard label.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) About this topic (read first)

Irrational pattern functions is not a standard term in university mathematics textbooks. This catalog section flags topics that may be speculative, AI-generated labels, or informal names for ideas that exist elsewhere under proper terminology.

Critical reading: Treat this page as a bridge from the informal label to established mathematics: quasiperiodic functions, Beatty sequences, Kronecker's theorem, and irrational rotations on the circle.

Related standard topics: Irrational Numbers, Trigonometry, Fractals.

2) Irrational numbers and repeating patterns

An irrational number cannot be written as a fraction \(p/q\) of integers. Its decimal expansion never settles into a repeating block. That lack of periodicity in digits does not mean "no pattern at all" — many constructions using irrationals produce highly structured, non-repeating patterns.

Example: The sequence \(\lfloor n\sqrt{2} \rfloor \bmod 2\) (parity of integer parts of multiples of \(\sqrt{2}\)) looks irregular but is governed by deep Diophantine approximation theory.

3) Beatty sequences (a precise pattern from irrationals)

If \(\alpha > 1\) is irrational, the Beatty sequence is \(B_\alpha = \{\lfloor n\alpha \rfloor : n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\}\).

Rayleigh–Beatty theorem: If \(\alpha, \beta > 1\) are irrationals with \(\frac{1}{\alpha} + \frac{1}{\beta} = 1\), then the Beatty sequences \(B_\alpha\) and \(B_\beta\) partition the positive integers (every positive integer appears in exactly one sequence).

Example

\(\alpha = \sqrt{2}\): sequence begins 1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8, 10, 11, …

Partner sequence

\(\beta = 2 + \sqrt{2}\) fills the gaps — together they cover all \(\mathbb{Z}^+\).

4) Quasiperiodic functions (the function viewpoint)

A function \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is quasiperiodic if it is a sum (or composition) of periodic functions with incommensurate frequencies — periods whose ratios are irrational.

Classic example: \[ f(x) = \sin(x) + \sin(\sqrt{2}\,x) \] is not periodic (no \(T \neq 0\) with \(f(x+T)=f(x)\) for all \(x\)), yet it is built from periodic parts. Such functions appear in phyllotaxis (plant leaf arrangements) and quasicrystal models.

Informal link to this catalog label: "Irrational pattern functions" likely refers to functions whose structure is driven by irrational frequencies or slopes, producing non-repeating but ordered behaviour.

5) Irrational rotation on the circle

Map \(n \mapsto \{n\alpha\}\) (fractional part of \(n\alpha\)) for irrational \(\alpha\) distributes points on \([0,1)\) uniformly — a consequence of equidistribution theory (Weyl's theorem).

Graphing \(\{n\alpha\}\) against \(n\) produces a "staircase with irrational slope" whose gaps encode \(\alpha\). Computer musicians use similar ideas for rhythmic patterns that never exactly repeat.

6) Engineering and signal contexts

Where patterns matter

  • Frequency synthesis with incommensurate tones
  • Quasicrystal diffraction patterns
  • Low-discrepancy sequences (Halton, Sobol) in Monte Carlo simulation

What to avoid

  • Claiming irrationals "encode hidden messages" in noise without evidence
  • Confusing non-repeating with random
  • Using non-standard terms in formal reports
