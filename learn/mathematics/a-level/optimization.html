1) What is optimization?

Optimization means finding the best value of a variable — often the largest or smallest value that a quantity can take. In A-Level mathematics this usually means finding a maximum or minimum of a function.

  • Maximize profit for a given production model
  • Minimize material used in a design
  • Find the peak height of a projectile
  • Locate the turning point of a cost function
Key idea: At a local maximum or minimum of a smooth function, the gradient is zero: \( f'(x) = 0 \). You then use the second derivative or a sign table to classify the turning point.

This builds on Differentiation and appears again in Calculus (Real World Problems).

2) Stationary points and the second derivative

A stationary point occurs where \( f'(x) = 0 \). To decide whether it is a maximum, minimum, or point of inflection, examine \( f''(x) \) at that point.

Condition at \( x = a \) Type of stationary point
\( f'(a) = 0 \) and \( f''(a) < 0 \) Local maximum
\( f'(a) = 0 \) and \( f''(a) > 0 \) Local minimum
\( f'(a) = 0 \) and \( f''(a) = 0 \) Inconclusive — use a sign table for \( f'(x) \)
Worked example: Find and classify the turning points of \( f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 1 \).
\( f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x-1)(x-3) \) → stationary points at \( x = 1 \) and \( x = 3 \).
\( f''(x) = 6x - 12 \): at \( x = 1 \), \( f'' = -6 \) (maximum); at \( x = 3 \), \( f'' = 6 \) (minimum).

3) Optimising on a closed interval

When the domain is restricted, e.g. \( 0 \le x \le 10 \), the global maximum or minimum may occur at a stationary point inside the interval or at an endpoint.

  1. Find stationary points in the interval
  2. Evaluate \( f(x) \) at each stationary point and at both endpoints
  3. Compare values — the largest is the maximum, the smallest is the minimum
Worked example: For \( f(x) = 12x - x^2 \) on \( 0 \le x \le 10 \):
\( f'(x) = 12 - 2x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 6 \).
\( f(0) = 0 \), \( f(6) = 36 \), \( f(10) = 20 \). Maximum value is 36 at \( x = 6 \).
Common mistake: Assuming a turning point always gives the answer. On a restricted domain, endpoints can produce better (or worse) values than any interior stationary point.

4) Modelling problems — setting up the function

Optimisation questions in exams rarely give \( f(x) \) directly. You must translate words or a diagram into a single-variable function, then differentiate.

Strategy

  1. Draw a diagram and label variables
  2. Write a constraint (e.g. fixed perimeter, fixed volume)
  3. Express the quantity to optimise in one variable
  4. Differentiate, solve \( f'(x) = 0 \), check validity

Example setup

A rectangle has perimeter 40 cm. Let width \( = w \), length \( = 20 - w \).

Area: \( A(w) = w(20 - w) = 20w - w^2 \)

\( A'(w) = 20 - 2w = 0 \Rightarrow w = 10 \) → square of side 10 cm, max area 100 cm².

5) Introduction to constraints

A constraint is a condition that limits the variables. At A-Level you usually eliminate extra variables using the constraint before differentiating. At university, Lagrange multipliers handle multiple variables with constraints — but the A-Level approach is substitution.

Worked example: An open-top box is made from a 30 cm × 30 cm sheet by cutting squares of side \( x \) from each corner and folding up the sides.
Base: \( (30 - 2x) \times (30 - 2x) \), height: \( x \). Volume: \[ V(x) = x(30 - 2x)^2, \quad 0 < x < 15 \] Expanding and differentiating gives maximum volume at \( x = 5 \) cm, \( V = 2000 \) cm³.

Always check that your solution satisfies physical constraints: lengths must be positive, angles in valid ranges, and variables must stay within the stated domain.

6) Engineering and science applications

Optimization appears throughout engineering whenever you want the best trade-off between competing quantities.

Field What is optimised? Method
Structural design Minimum weight for given strength Model stress as function of dimensions; minimise
Projectile motion Maximum range or height Differentiate \( R(\theta) \) or \( h(t) \)
Manufacturing Minimum cost Cost function with production volume constraint
Electrical circuits Maximum power transfer Optimise load resistance (see Power Calculations)
Projectile range: For launch speed \( u \) and angle \( \theta \), range on level ground is \( R = \frac{u^2 \sin 2\theta}{g} \). Maximum range occurs when \( \sin 2\theta = 1 \), i.e. \( \theta = 45° \).

7) Exam technique and checking

Always show

  • Clear definition of variables and units
  • The function being optimised
  • Differentiation and solution of \( f'(x) = 0 \)
  • Second derivative test or comparison of values
  • Answer in context with units

Sanity checks

  • Does the answer lie in the valid domain?
  • Is a maximum physically sensible (positive area, etc.)?
  • Substitute back into the original expression
Exam tip: If the question asks for the minimum amount of material, you may need to minimise surface area subject to a fixed volume — the algebra is longer, but the method is identical.
