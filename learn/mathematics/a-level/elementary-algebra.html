1) Expanding single brackets

At A-Level you extend Equations and Variables by manipulating expressions before solving. Start by multiplying every term inside a bracket by the term outside — the distributive law \(a(b + c) = ab + ac\).

Positive multiplier

3(x + 4) = 3x + 12

5(2a − 1) = 10a − 5

Negative multiplier

−2(x + 3) = −2x − 6

−(4y − 7) = −4y + 7

Worked example: Expand 4(2x − 5) + 3(x + 1)
8x − 20 + 3x + 3 = 11x − 17
Common mistake: Forgetting to multiply the second term inside the bracket. 2(x + 5) is 2x + 10, not 2x + 5.
Key idea: Expanding and factorising are inverse processes. Check factorisation by expanding your answer.

2) Expanding double brackets

To expand \((x + a)(x + b)\), multiply each term in the first bracket by each term in the second. A useful pattern: \((x + a)(x + b) = x^2 + (a+b)x + ab\).

Worked example: Expand (x + 3)(x + 5)
\(x \cdot x + x \cdot 5 + 3 \cdot x + 3 \cdot 5 = x^2 + 8x + 15\)
Special products:
Difference of two squares: \((x + a)(x − a) = x^2 − a^2\)
Perfect square: \((x + a)^2 = x^2 + 2ax + a^2\)

Grid / area method

Place terms along top and side of a grid; add the four products inside.

Check

For (x + 2)(x − 2), expect no middle term: x² − 4.

3) Factorising — common factor

Always look for a highest common factor (HCF) first. Factorising means writing an expression as a product; the HCF goes outside the bracket.

Worked example: Factorise 6x² + 9x
HCF is 3x → 3x(2x + 3)
Difference of two squares: Factorise 4x² − 25
(2x)² − 5² = (2x + 5)(2x − 5)
Common mistake: Stopping too early. 2x² + 4x factorises to 2x(x + 2), not 2(x² + 2x) — take out the full HCF.

4) Factorising quadratics (a = 1)

For x² + bx + c, find two numbers that multiply to c and add to b.

Expression Numbers needed Factorised form
x² + 7x + 12 3 and 4 (product 12, sum 7) (x + 3)(x + 4)
x² − 5x + 6 −2 and −3 (product 6, sum −5) (x − 2)(x − 3)
x² + x − 12 4 and −3 (product −12, sum 1) (x + 4)(x − 3)
Why it works: \((x + p)(x + q) = x^2 + (p+q)x + pq\). Matching coefficients gives the number search.

5) Factorising quadratics (a ≠ 1)

When the coefficient of \(x^2\) is not 1, use splitting the middle term or the ac method: for \(ax^2 + bx + c\), find two numbers multiplying to \(ac\) and adding to \(b\).

Worked example: Factorise 2x² + 7x + 3
\(ac = 6\). Numbers 6 and 1 (sum 7): rewrite as 2x² + 6x + x + 3
Group: 2x(x + 3) + 1(x + 3) = (2x + 1)(x + 3)

Strategy

  1. Check for HCF first
  2. Find two numbers from \(ac\) and \(b\)
  3. Split the middle term and group in pairs
  4. Factorise each pair; common bracket remains

Link to solving

If \((2x+1)(x+3)=0\), then \(x = -\tfrac{1}{2}\) or \(x = -3\) (zero product rule).

6) Algebraic fractions — introduction

An algebraic fraction has polynomials in the numerator and/or denominator. Simplify by factorising and cancelling common factors — never cancel terms that are added.

Worked example: Simplify \(\dfrac{x^2 − 9}{x^2 + 5x + 6}\)
Numerator: \((x+3)(x−3)\). Denominator: \((x+2)(x+3)\).
Cancel \((x+3)\): \(\dfrac{x−3}{x+2}\), where \(x \ne −3, −2\).
Common mistake: Cancelling incorrectly. In \(\dfrac{x+2}{x+5}\), you cannot cancel the +2 and +5 — they are not factors.

Adding and subtracting algebraic fractions requires a common denominator — the same idea as Fractions, but with algebraic expressions.

7) Completing the square — preview

Any quadratic \(x^2 + bx + c\) can be written as \((x + p)^2 + q\). This reveals the turning point of a parabola and is essential for solving and graphing at A-Level.

Method for \(x^2 + bx\): Halve \(b\), square it, add and subtract that value.
Example: \(x^2 + 6x + 1 = (x+3)^2 − 9 + 1 = (x+3)^2 − 8\)

Vertex form

\(a(x+p)^2 + q\) has minimum (or maximum) at \((−p, q)\) when sketched.

When \(a \ne 1\)

Factor out \(a\) first: \(2x^2 + 8x + 3 = 2(x^2 + 4x) + 3\), then complete inside the bracket.

Completing the square connects to Slope (parabola shape) and later Calculus (optimisation).
