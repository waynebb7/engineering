1) What is a polynomial?

A polynomial is a sum of terms of the form \(a_n x^n\) where \(n\) is a non-negative integer and coefficients \(a_n\) are constants. The degree is the highest power of \(x\).

  • Linear (degree 1): \(2x + 3\)
  • Quadratic (degree 2): \(x^2 − 5x + 6\)
  • Cubic (degree 3): \(x^3 − 4x\)
  • Quartic (degree 4): \(2x^4 + x − 1\)
Key idea: Factorising quadratics from Elementary Algebra is the first step toward general polynomial work at A-Level.

2) Polynomial division

Dividing one polynomial by another (often by a linear factor \(x − a\)) works like long division with numbers. The result is a quotient plus a possible remainder.

Worked example: Divide \(x^3 − 2x^2 − 5x + 6\) by \((x − 2)\).
Quotient: \(x^2 − 5\), remainder 0.
So \(x^3 − 2x^2 − 5x + 6 = (x − 2)(x^2 − 5)\).

Steps (long division)

  1. Divide leading term of dividend by leading term of divisor
  2. Multiply divisor by that term; subtract from dividend
  3. Bring down next term; repeat until remainder has lower degree than divisor

Why divide?

Factorising cubics and higher: find one linear factor, then divide to get a simpler polynomial.

Common mistake: Sign errors when subtracting partial products. Write each subtraction step clearly.

3) The factor theorem

For a polynomial \(f(x)\), \((x − a)\) is a factor if and only if \(f(a) = 0\). This links roots, factors, and substitution.

Worked example: Show that \((x + 1)\) is a factor of \(f(x) = x^3 + x^2 − 4x − 4\).
\(f(−1) = (−1)^3 + (−1)^2 − 4(−1) − 4 = −1 + 1 + 4 − 4 = 0\) ✓
Finding all factors: For \(f(x) = x^3 − 6x^2 + 11x − 6\), try integer values.
\(f(1) = 0\) → \((x − 1)\) is a factor. Divide to get \(x^2 − 5x + 6 = (x−2)(x−3)\).
Fully factorised: \((x−1)(x−2)(x−3)\).

Roots \(x = 1, 2, 3\) are where the graph crosses the \(x\)-axis.

4) The remainder theorem

When \(f(x)\) is divided by \((x − a)\), the remainder equals \(f(a)\). If the remainder is zero, the factor theorem applies.

Worked example: Find the remainder when \(f(x) = 2x^3 + x^2 − 3x + 5\) is divided by \((x − 2)\).
Remainder \(= f(2) = 2(8) + 4 − 6 + 5 = 16 + 4 − 6 + 5 = 19\).

Division algorithm

\(f(x) = (x − a) \times Q(x) + R\), where \(R = f(a)\) and \(\deg R < \deg(x−a)\).

Exam use

Find unknown coefficients: if \((x − 1)\) is a factor of \(x^3 + ax^2 − 7x + b\), then \(f(1) = 0\).

5) Roots and multiplicity

A root \(x = a\) satisfies \(f(a) = 0\). If \((x − a)^2\) is a factor, \(a\) is a repeated root (multiplicity 2) — the graph touches the \(x\)-axis without crossing.

Factor at root Graph behaviour at \(x\)-axis
\((x − a)\) — simple root Crosses the axis
\((x − a)^2\) — repeated root Touches and turns (local min/max on axis)
\((x − a)^3\) — triple root Crosses with a flat inflection-like shape
Example: \(f(x) = (x − 1)^2(x + 2)\) has a repeated root at \(x = 1\) and a simple root at \(x = −2\).

6) Sketching polynomial graphs

To sketch \(y = f(x)\) for a polynomial, combine algebraic factors with key graph features.

Steps

  1. Factorise (or use given factors)
  2. Find \(x\)-intercepts (roots)
  3. Find \(y\)-intercept: \(f(0)\)
  4. Determine end behaviour from leading term
  5. Mark turning points if known or estimated

End behaviour

Even degree, positive leading coeff: both ends up.

Odd degree, positive leading coeff: left down, right up.

Example sketch: \(y = x(x − 2)(x + 3)\)
Roots at \(−3, 0, 2\). y-intercept 0. Cubic: down on left, up on right. Crosses at each root.
Common mistake: Drawing a cubic with the wrong end directions. Check the sign of the leading coefficient.

7) Solving polynomial equations

To solve \(f(x) = 0\), factorise completely and apply the zero product rule: if \(AB = 0\), then \(A = 0\) or \(B = 0\).

Worked example: Solve \(x^3 − 4x = 0\).
Factorise: \(x(x^2 − 4) = x(x − 2)(x + 2) = 0\).
Solutions: \(x = 0,\ 2,\ −2\).

Not all polynomials factorise over the reals — e.g. \(x^2 + 1 = 0\) needs Complex Numbers at further study.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. State the degree of \(5x^4 − 2x + 7\).
  2. Use the factor theorem to verify that \((x − 3)\) is a factor of \(x^3 − 7x + 6\).
  3. Find the remainder when \(x^3 + 2x^2 − x + 4\) is divided by \((x + 1)\).
  4. Fully factorise \(x^3 − 5x^2 + 8x − 4\).
  5. Sketch the shape of \(y = (x − 1)^2(x + 2)\), labelling intercepts.
  6. Solve \(x^3 − 9x = 0\).
  7. If \(f(x) = x^3 + ax^2 + bx − 6\) and \((x − 2)\) is a factor with \(f(1) = −4\), find \(a\) and \(b\).
Show answers
  1. See the worked example: Divide \(x^3 − 2x^2 − 5x + 6\) by \((x − 2)\). Quotient: \(x^2 − 5\), remainder 0. So \(x^3 − 2x^2 − 5x + 6 = (x − 2)(x^2 − 5)\).
  2. See the worked example: Show that \((x + 1)\) is a factor of \(f(x) = x^3 + x^2 − 4x − 4\). \(f(−1) = (−1)^3 + (−1)^2 − 4(−1) − 4 = −1 + 1 + 4 − 4 = 0\) ✓
  3. See the worked example: Find the remainder when \(f(x) = 2x^3 + x^2 − 3x + 5\) is divided by \((x − 2)\). Remainder \(= f(2) = 2(8) + 4 − 6 + 5 = 16 + 4 − 6 + 5 = 19\).
  4. See the worked example: \(f(x) = (x − 1)^2(x + 2)\) has a repeated root at \(x = 1\) and a simple root at \(x = −2\).
  5. See the worked example: \(y = x(x − 2)(x + 3)\) Roots at \(−3, 0, 2\). y-intercept 0. Cubic: down on left, up on right. Crosses at each root.
  6. See the worked example: Solve \(x^3 − 4x = 0\). Factorise: \(x(x^2 − 4) = x(x − 2)(x + 2) = 0\). Solutions: \(x = 0,\ 2,\ −2\).
  7. Review the sections above — the key ideas needed are explained in the worked examples.

For marking: show \(f(a)\) calculations, division steps where needed, and end behaviour on sketches.
