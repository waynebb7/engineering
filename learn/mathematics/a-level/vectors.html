1) What is a vector?

A vector has both magnitude (size) and direction. A scalar has magnitude only. Vectors model displacement, velocity, force, and many engineering quantities.

  • Displacement: 5 m north-east — vector
  • Speed: 60 km h\(^{-1}\) — scalar (no direction specified)
  • Velocity: 60 km h\(^{-1}\) due east — vector
Key idea: Two vectors are equal if they have the same magnitude and direction, regardless of where they are drawn. \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{CD}\) are equal if \(ABDC\) is a parallelogram.

Vectors extend work on coordinates from Cartesian Coordinates and connect to Matrices and mechanics.

2) Column vector notation

In 2D and 3D, vectors are written as column vectors giving components relative to unit basis vectors \(\mathbf{i}\), \(\mathbf{j}\) (and \(\mathbf{k}\) in 3D):

\[ \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j}, \qquad \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = x\mathbf{i} + y\mathbf{j} + z\mathbf{k} \]

Addition

Add corresponding components:

\(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\)

Scalar multiplication

Multiply each component by the scalar:

\(3\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ -12 \end{pmatrix}\)

Geometric meaning: \(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\) is the diagonal of the parallelogram formed by \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) (triangle rule). \(k\mathbf{a}\) scales the length by \(|k|\) and reverses direction if \(k < 0\).

3) Magnitude

The magnitude (or modulus) of a vector is its length, found using Pythagoras:

\[ \left|\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\right| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \left|\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix}\right| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} \]
Worked example: Find \(|\mathbf{a}|\) where \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -4 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5\).

A unit vector has magnitude 1. To find the unit vector in the direction of \(\mathbf{a}\): \(\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}\).

Common mistake: Forgetting to square negative components. \((-4)^2 = 16\), not \(-16\).

4) The dot product (scalar product)

The dot product of two vectors produces a scalar:

\[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta \] where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors. In components: \[ \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 \quad \text{(2D)}, \qquad \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3 \quad \text{(3D)} \]

Finding angles

\(\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|}\)

Perpendicular test

\(\mathbf{a} \perp \mathbf{b} \Leftrightarrow \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0\)

Worked example: \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 1(3) + 2(-1) = 1\). \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{5}\), \(|\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{10}\).
\(\cos\theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{50}} = \frac{1}{5\sqrt{2}}\).

5) Position vectors

The position vector of a point \(P\) relative to the origin \(O\) is \(\overrightarrow{OP} = \mathbf{p}\). The vector from \(A\) to \(B\) is:

\[ \overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a} \] where \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) are position vectors of \(A\) and \(B\).
Worked example: Points \(A\) and \(B\) have position vectors \(\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix}\). Midpoint of \(AB\): \(\frac{\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}}{2} = \begin{pmatrix} 3.5 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}\).

In ratio problems, if \(P\) divides \(AB\) in ratio \(m : n\), then \(\overrightarrow{OP} = \frac{n\mathbf{a} + m\mathbf{b}}{m + n}\).

6) Lines in 2D

A line through point \(A\) with position vector \(\mathbf{a}\), parallel to direction vector \(\mathbf{d}\), has vector equation:

\[ \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d} \] where \(t\) is a scalar parameter and \(\mathbf{r}\) is the position vector of any point on the line.
Worked example: Line through \((1, 2)\) with direction \(\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\):
\(\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\).
Cartesian form: \(x = 1 + 3t\), \(y = 2 - t\). Eliminating \(t\): \(x + 3y = 7\).

To test if two lines intersect, equate their vector equations and solve for parameters. Parallel lines have direction vectors that are scalar multiples of each other.

7) Lines in 3D

In 3D, the vector equation has the same form \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{d}\), but \(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{d}, \mathbf{r} \in \mathbb{R}^3\):

\[ \mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} d_1 \\ d_2 \\ d_3 \end{pmatrix} \]

Cartesian form

If no component of \(\mathbf{d}\) is zero:

\(\frac{x - a_1}{d_1} = \frac{y - a_2}{d_2} = \frac{z - a_3}{d_3}\)

Skew lines

In 3D, two lines may neither intersect nor be parallel — they are skew. Shortest distance between skew lines uses the cross product (Further Maths).

Worked example: Line through \((1, 0, -2)\) parallel to \(\mathbf{i} + 2\mathbf{j} - \mathbf{k}\):
\(\mathbf{r} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} + t\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}\).

See also Vectors in Physics for force and velocity applications.
