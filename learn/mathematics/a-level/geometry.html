1) Geometry at A-Level

A-Level geometry combines proof, measurement, and coordinate methods. You extend Basic Geometry with circle theorems, similarity ratios, and 3D mensuration.

  • Circle theorems — angle relationships in circles
  • Similarity — shapes with equal angles and proportional sides
  • Pythagoras — right-angled triangles in 2D and 3D
  • Area and volume — standard formulae and composite solids
  • Vectors — directed quantities for position and proof
Key idea: Always draw a clear diagram, mark known angles and lengths, and state the theorem or property you use.

2) Pythagoras' theorem

In a right-angled triangle with legs \(a\), \(b\) and hypotenuse \(c\): \[a^2 + b^2 = c^2\]

Worked example: Find the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 5 cm and 12 cm.
\(c^2 = 25 + 144 = 169\) → \(c = 13\) cm.

Finding a shorter side

\(a^2 = c^2 − b^2\). Example: \(c = 10\), \(b = 6\) → \(a = 8\).

3D use

Space diagonal of a cuboid: \(d = \sqrt{l^2 + w^2 + h^2}\).

Common mistake: Applying Pythagoras without a right angle. Confirm the triangle is right-angled first.

Pythagoras links directly to Trigonometry (SOHCAHTOA) and distance in Cartesian Coordinates.

3) Similarity

Two shapes are similar if corresponding angles are equal and corresponding sides are in the same ratio (scale factor \(k\)).

Length ratio: If scale factor is \(k\), lengths multiply by \(k\).
Area ratio: Areas multiply by \(k^2\).
Volume ratio: Volumes multiply by \(k^3\).
Worked example: Two similar triangles have sides 6 cm and 9 cm on corresponding edges. Scale factor \(k = \tfrac{3}{2}\).
If the smaller area is 20 cm², larger area \(= 20 \times \left(\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 = 45\) cm².
Test for triangle similarity Condition
AA (angle–angle) Two pairs of equal angles (third pair automatically equal)
SSS proportional All three pairs of sides in the same ratio
SAS proportional Two pairs of sides proportional with included angle equal

4) Circle theorems

These angle facts apply to circles and are used in proof and problem-solving. \(O\) denotes the centre throughout.

Theorem Statement
Angle at centre Angle at centre \(= 2 \times\) angle at circumference (same arc)
Angle in semicircle Angle in a semicircle is \(90°\)
Angles in same segment Angles subtended by the same chord at the circumference are equal
Cyclic quadrilateral Opposite angles sum to \(180°\)
Tangent and radius Radius to point of tangency is perpendicular to tangent
Alternate segment Angle between tangent and chord equals angle in alternate segment
Worked example: Angle at circumference on arc AB is \(38°\). Angle at centre on the same arc is \(76°\).
Exam tip: Quote the theorem name in proofs — e.g. "angle at centre is twice angle at circumference".

5) Area formulae

2D shapes

  • Triangle: \(\tfrac{1}{2}bh\) or \(\tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C\)
  • Trapezium: \(\tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h\)
  • Circle: \(\pi r^2\)
  • Sector: \(\tfrac{\theta}{360}\pi r^2\)

Units

Area uses square units (cm², m²). Convert consistently before calculating.

Composite shapes: split into standard parts or subtract unwanted regions.

Worked example: A sector has radius 6 cm and angle \(120°\).
Area \(= \tfrac{120}{360} \times \pi \times 36 = \tfrac{1}{3} \times 36\pi = 12\pi\) cm².

The formula \(\tfrac{1}{2}ab\sin C\) is developed fully in Trigonometry.

6) Volume and surface area

Know standard 3D formulae and apply scale factors from similarity for enlarged or reduced solids.

Solid Volume Surface area (key parts)
Prism cross-section area \(\times\) length sum of face areas
Cylinder \(\pi r^2 h\) \(2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh\)
Cone \(\tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h\) \(\pi r^2 + \pi r l\) (slant \(l\))
Sphere \(\tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\) \(4\pi r^2\)
Worked example: Similar cones with heights 4 cm and 10 cm. Volume scale factor \(= \left(\tfrac{10}{4}\right)^3 = \left(\tfrac{5}{2}\right)^3 = \tfrac{125}{8}\).

7) Vectors in geometry — preview

A vector has magnitude and direction. Position vectors express points relative to an origin; vector addition follows parallelogram or triangle rules.

Notation

\(\vec{AB}\) or \(\mathbf{a}\) — column form \(\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\).

Magnitude: \(|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\).

Geometric uses

Prove parallel lines: \(\mathbf{a} = k\mathbf{b}\).

Midpoint of AB: \(\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b})\).

Example: If \(\overrightarrow{OA} = \mathbf{a}\) and \(\overrightarrow{OB} = \mathbf{b}\), then \(\overrightarrow{AB} = \mathbf{b} − \mathbf{a}\).

Full vector methods are covered in Vectors and used in mechanics and Slope (direction of lines).

  6. A sphere has radius 5 cm. Find its volume in terms of \(\pi\).
