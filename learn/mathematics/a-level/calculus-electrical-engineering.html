1) Why calculus in electrical engineering?

Voltage and current in real circuits are often functions of time. Capacitors and inductors respond to rates of change; AC supplies oscillate sinusoidally. Calculus is essential for analysing these dynamic behaviours.

Key idea: Differentiation links voltage and current for reactive components. Integration accumulates charge, flux, and energy over time.

Prerequisite: Differentiation and Integration. Related: Calculus (Power Calculations).

2) AC circuits and sinusoidal quantities

Alternating voltage and current vary sinusoidally: \[ v(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t + \phi_v), \qquad i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t + \phi_i) \] where \( \omega = 2\pi f \) is angular frequency (rad s⁻¹) and \( f \) is frequency in hertz.

Period and frequency

Period \( T = 1/f \). One complete cycle in time \( T \).

Differentiating: \( \dfrac{dv}{dt} = \omega V_m \cos(\omega t + \phi) \).

Phase difference

\( \phi = \phi_v - \phi_i \) determines whether power is delivered or stored.

In-phase (\( \phi = 0 \)): maximum power transfer to load.

Engineering note: UK mains supply: \( f = 50 \) Hz, so \( \omega = 100\pi \) rad s⁻¹. Peak voltage 325 V corresponds to RMS \( V_{\text{rms}} = V_m / \sqrt{2} \approx 230 \) V.

3) Capacitors — differentiation and integration

A capacitor stores charge. Current is the rate of change of charge (and voltage): \[ i_C = C\,\frac{dv_C}{dt} \] Equivalently, voltage is the integral of current: \[ v_C(t) = \frac{1}{C}\int i_C\,dt + v_0 \]

Charging through a resistor (RC circuit): When a capacitor charges toward supply voltage \( V_s \): \[ v_C(t) = V_s\left(1 - e^{-t/RC}\right) \] The time constant \( \tau = RC \) is the time to reach ~63% of final voltage.

Stored energy: \( E_C = \tfrac{1}{2}CV^2 \). For AC, a capacitor causes current to lead voltage by 90°.

4) Inductors — differentiation and integration

An inductor opposes changes in current. The voltage across it is: \[ v_L = L\,\frac{di_L}{dt} \] Current is the integral of voltage: \[ i_L(t) = \frac{1}{L}\int v_L\,dt + i_0 \]

Current growth (RL circuit): When an inductor is connected to a DC supply through a resistor: \[ i_L(t) = \frac{V_s}{R}\left(1 - e^{-tR/L}\right) \] Time constant \( \tau = L/R \).

Stored energy: \( E_L = \tfrac{1}{2}LI^2 \). For AC, an inductor causes current to lag voltage by 90°.

5) RLC transients and second-order circuits

Combining resistors, inductors, and capacitors gives second-order differential equations. For a series RLC circuit driven by voltage \( V(t) \): \[ L\,\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\,\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{i}{C} = \frac{dV}{dt} \]

Condition Response type Behaviour
Underdamped Oscillatory decay Ringing before settling — e.g. motor drives, filters
Critically damped Fastest non-oscillatory Ideal for rapid settling without overshoot
Overdamped Slow exponential No oscillation, slower approach to steady state
A-Level scope: You may be asked to interpret solutions or set up the equation. Full solution methods (characteristic equations) appear in further maths and university courses.

6) Integrals for energy and power

Instantaneous power is \( p(t) = v(t)\,i(t) \). Total energy transferred over an interval: \[ E = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} v(t)\,i(t)\,dt = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} p(t)\,dt \]

Average power over one period \( T \): \[ \bar{P} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T v(t)\,i(t)\,dt \]

Worked example: A resistor carries \( i(t) = 2\sin(\omega t) \) A and has \( R = 10 \) Ω. Then \( v = 20\sin(\omega t) \) V and \( p = 200\sin^2(\omega t) \) W. Average over a cycle: \( \bar{P} = 100 \) W (using \( \overline{\sin^2} = 1/2 \)).

See Calculus (Power Calculations) for detailed AC power analysis and AC Power Calculator for numeric work.

7) PID control and engineering applications

Feedback controllers combine proportional, integral, and derivative terms: \[ u(t) = K_p\,e(t) + K_i\int e(t)\,dt + K_d\,\frac{de(t)}{dt} \]

  • P — responds to current error
  • I — eliminates steady-state error (integration accumulates past error)
  • D — anticipates future error (rate of change)
Applications: Motor speed control, temperature regulation, voltage stabilisers, and aircraft autopilot systems all rely on calculus-based control loops.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Write the relationship between capacitor current and voltage. If \( v_C = 5\sin(100\pi t) \) V and \( C = 10 \) μF, find \( i_C \).
  2. An inductor \( L = 0.5 \) H carries current \( i = 4(1 - e^{-2t}) \) A. Find \( v_L \) at \( t = 0 \) and as \( t \to \infty \).
  3. State the time constant for an RC circuit with \( R = 10 \) kΩ and \( C = 100 \) μF.
  4. Write the second-order ODE for a series RLC circuit in terms of current \( i \).
  5. Calculate the energy stored in a 1000 μF capacitor charged to 12 V.
  6. Explain why average power in a purely reactive (L or C only) AC circuit is zero.
Show answers
  1. \( i_C = C\,\dfrac{dv_C}{dt} \) (and equivalently \( v_C = \dfrac{1}{C}\int i_C\,dt + v_0 \)). Here \( v_C = 5\sin(100\pi t) \) V and \( C = 10\,\mu\text{F} = 10 \times 10^{-6} \) F, so \( \dfrac{dv_C}{dt} = 500\pi\cos(100\pi t) \) V s\(^{-1}\). Thus \( i_C = 10 \times 10^{-6} \times 500\pi\cos(100\pi t) = 5\pi \times 10^{-3}\cos(100\pi t) \) A \( \approx 15.7\cos(100\pi t) \) mA.
  2. \( v_L = L\,\dfrac{di}{dt} \). With \( i = 4(1 - e^{-2t}) \) A, \( \dfrac{di}{dt} = 8e^{-2t} \) A s\(^{-1}\), so \( v_L = 0.5 \times 8e^{-2t} = 4e^{-2t} \) V. At \( t = 0 \): \( v_L = 4 \) V. As \( t \to \infty \): \( e^{-2t} \to 0 \), so \( v_L \to 0 \) V (current settles at 4 A with no further change).
  3. Time constant \( \tau = RC \). \( R = 10\,\text{k}\Omega = 10^4\,\Omega \), \( C = 100\,\mu\text{F} = 10^{-4} \) F, so \( \tau = 10^4 \times 10^{-4} = 1 \) s.
  4. For a series RLC circuit driven by voltage \( V(t) \), Kirchhoff's law gives the second-order ODE in current \( i \): \( L\,\dfrac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\,\dfrac{di}{dt} + \dfrac{i}{C} = \dfrac{dV}{dt} \).
  5. Stored energy \( E_C = \tfrac{1}{2}CV^2 \). \( C = 1000\,\mu\text{F} = 10^{-3} \) F, \( V = 12 \) V: \( E_C = \tfrac{1}{2} \times 10^{-3} \times 12^2 = 0.072 \) J \( = 72 \) mJ.
  6. In a purely reactive (L or C only) AC circuit, voltage and current are 90° out of phase, so \( p(t) = v(t)i(t) \) is alternately positive and negative with equal magnitude each half-cycle. Over one period, \( \bar{P} = \dfrac{1}{T}\int_0^T p(t)\,dt = 0 \): energy oscillates between source and field rather than being dissipated as average real power.

For marking: show differentiation or integration steps, include units (V, A, J, W), and state time constants where relevant.
