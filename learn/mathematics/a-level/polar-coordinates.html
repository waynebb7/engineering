1) Polar coordinates \((r, \theta)\)

In polar coordinates, a point is described by its distance \(r\) from the pole (origin) and the angle \(\theta\) measured anticlockwise from the initial line (positive \(x\)-axis).

We write the point as \((r, \theta)\), where \(r \geq 0\) in standard convention and \(\theta\) is in radians unless degrees are specified.

Key idea: Polar coordinates are natural for curves with circular or rotational symmetry — circles, spirals, cardioids, and rose curves are often simpler in polar form.
Example: The point with Cartesian coordinates \((3, 4)\) has \(r = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5\) and \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\dfrac{4}{3}\right)\).

This topic links to Trigonometry and Complex Numbers (modulus and argument).

2) Converting between polar and Cartesian

From the right-angled triangle formed by \(r\), \(\theta\), and the Cartesian axes: \[ x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta \] \[ r^2 = x^2 + y^2, \quad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x} \]

Polar → Cartesian example: \((r, \theta) = \left(4, \dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)\).
\(x = 4\cos\dfrac{\pi}{3} = 2\), \(y = 4\sin\dfrac{\pi}{3} = 2\sqrt{3}\).
Cartesian point: \((2, 2\sqrt{3})\).
Cartesian → Polar example: \((-1, 1)\).
\(r = \sqrt{2}\). Point is in the second quadrant, so \(\theta = \dfrac{3\pi}{4}\).
Polar form: \(\left(\sqrt{2}, \dfrac{3\pi}{4}\right)\).
Negative \(r\): \((-r, \theta)\) is equivalent to \((r, \theta + \pi)\). A-Level questions usually use \(r \geq 0\), but be aware of this convention when sketching.

3) Common polar curves

A polar curve has an equation relating \(r\) and \(\theta\), written as \(r = f(\theta)\).

Curve Polar equation
Circle through pole, diameter along initial line \(r = 2a\cos\theta\)
Circle centred at pole, radius \(a\) \(r = a\)
Cardioid \(r = a(1 + \cos\theta)\)
Rose curve (4 petals) \(r = a\cos 2\theta\)
Archimedean spiral \(r = a\theta\)
Line through pole \(\theta = \alpha\) (constant)
Converting to Cartesian: For \(r = 2a\cos\theta\), multiply by \(r\):
\(r^2 = 2ar\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2 + y^2 = 2ax\) — a circle.

4) Sketching polar curves

To sketch \(r = f(\theta)\), create a table of values: choose key angles (often multiples of \(\dfrac{\pi}{6}\) or \(\dfrac{\pi}{4}\)), calculate \(r\), and plot points in polar form.

Worked example: Sketch \(r = 1 + \cos\theta\) (cardioid).
\(\theta = 0\): \(r = 2\); \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\): \(r = 1\); \(\theta = \pi\): \(r = 0\); \(\theta = \dfrac{3\pi}{2}\): \(r = 1\).
The curve is heart-shaped, with a cusp at the pole when \(\theta = \pi\).

Symmetry checks

  • \(f(-\theta) = f(\theta)\) → symmetric about initial line
  • \(f(\pi - \theta) = f(\theta)\) → symmetric about \(\theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2}\)
  • \(f(\theta + \pi) = f(\theta)\) → half-turn symmetry

Maximum and minimum \(r\)

Find where \(r\) is largest and smallest to locate the curve's extent from the pole.

5) Area enclosed by a polar curve

The area enclosed by \(r = f(\theta)\) from \(\theta = \alpha\) to \(\theta = \beta\) is: \[ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2\,d\theta \]

Why this formula? The area of a sector of radius \(r\) and angle \(d\theta\) is approximately \(\dfrac{1}{2}r^2\,d\theta\). Integrating sums these infinitesimal sectors.
Worked example: Area enclosed by \(r = 2\) (a circle of radius 2) for \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\).
\(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi} 4\,d\theta = \dfrac{1}{2}[4\theta]_0^{2\pi} = 4\pi\).
Common mistake: Forgetting the factor \(\dfrac{1}{2}\) or squaring \(r\) incorrectly. Always write \(r^2\) inside the integral.

6) Area between two polar curves

When two curves \(r = f(\theta)\) (outer) and \(r = g(\theta)\) (inner) enclose a region between \(\theta = \alpha\) and \(\theta = \beta\): \[ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \big( f(\theta)^2 - g(\theta)^2 \big)\,d\theta \]

Worked example: Area inside \(r = 2\) and outside \(r = 1 + \cos\theta\) for \(-\dfrac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta \leq \dfrac{\pi}{2}\).
Outer minus inner: \(A = \dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \big(4 - (1+\cos\theta)^2\big)\,d\theta\). Expand and integrate term by term.

Find intersection angles by solving \(f(\theta) = g(\theta)\) to determine correct limits of integration.

7) Tangents to polar curves

The angle \(\psi\) between the radius vector and the tangent at a point on \(r = f(\theta)\) satisfies: \[ \tan\psi = \frac{r}{dr/d\theta} \] The angle between the tangent and the initial line is \(\theta + \psi\).

Worked example: For \(r = a\theta\) (spiral), \(\dfrac{dr}{d\theta} = a\).
\(\tan\psi = \dfrac{a\theta}{a} = \theta\), so \(\psi = \tan^{-1}(\theta)\).

Tangents at the pole (\(r = 0\)) occur when \(r = 0\) for some \(\theta\); the tangent line passes through the pole at angle \(\theta\) (or \(\theta + \pi\)).
