Matrices

A-Level guide. Matrix arithmetic, determinants, inverses, transformations, and solving simultaneous equations.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Matrix notation and structure

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers arranged in rows and columns. An \(m \times n\) matrix has \(m\) rows and \(n\) columns.

Example — a \(2 \times 2\) matrix: \[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \] The entry in row \(i\), column \(j\) is denoted \(a_{ij}\).

Key idea: Matrices compactly store linear relationships. They are used to represent transformations of the plane, systems of equations, and data tables in applied mathematics.

This topic connects to Vectors and Linear Transformations.

2) Addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication

Matrices of the same order can be added and subtracted by adding or subtracting corresponding entries.

Example: \[ \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & -1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 0 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \]

Scalar multiplication multiplies every entry by the scalar: \[ 3\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 4 & 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 12 & 15 \end{pmatrix} \]

Common mistake: You cannot add matrices of different sizes. A \(2 \times 2\) matrix cannot be added to a \(2 \times 3\) matrix.

3) Matrix multiplication

To multiply \(A_{m \times n}\) by \(B_{n \times p}\), the inner dimensions must match (\(n\)). The result is an \(m \times p\) matrix.

2 × 2 example: \[ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot5+2\cdot7 & 1\cdot6+2\cdot8 \\ 3\cdot5+4\cdot7 & 3\cdot6+4\cdot8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix} \]
Important: Matrix multiplication is not commutative in general: \(AB \neq BA\). Always check the order when multiplying.

The identity matrix \(I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\) satisfies \(AI = IA = A\) for any compatible \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(A\).

4) Determinants of 2 × 2 matrices

For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\), the determinant is: \[ \det(A) = |A| = ad - bc \]

Worked example: \[ \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = (3)(4) - (2)(1) = 10 \]

Geometric meaning

\(|\det(A)|\) is the area scale factor of the transformation represented by \(A\) (with sign indicating orientation reversal).

When \(\det = 0\)

The matrix is singular (no inverse). The transformation squashes area to zero.

5) Inverse of a 2 × 2 matrix

If \(\det(A) \neq 0\), the inverse \(A^{-1}\) satisfies \(AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I\). For \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\): \[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix} \]

Worked example: Find the inverse of \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\det = (2)(3) - (1)(5) = 1\).
\(A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).
Common mistake: Swapping the wrong entries or forgetting to divide by the determinant. Swap \(a\) and \(d\), negate \(b\) and \(c\), then divide by \(\det(A)\).

6) Matrices and transformations

A \(2 \times 2\) matrix can transform a point \(\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\) by multiplication: \(\begin{pmatrix} x' \\ y' \end{pmatrix} = A\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\).

Transformation Matrix
Reflection in the \(x\)-axis \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\)
Enlargement scale factor \(k\), centre origin \(\begin{pmatrix} k & 0 \\ 0 & k \end{pmatrix}\)
Rotation \(90°\) anticlockwise about origin \(\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\)
Shear parallel to \(x\)-axis, factor \(k\) \(\begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\)
Successive transformations: Apply matrices in order — if \(T_1\) then \(T_2\), the combined matrix is \(T_2 T_1\) (applied right to left on the column vector).

7) Solving simultaneous equations

A pair of linear equations can be written as a matrix equation: \[ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} e \\ f \end{pmatrix} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad AX = B \]

Solution method: If \(A^{-1}\) exists, multiply both sides on the left by \(A^{-1}\): \[ X = A^{-1}B \]
Worked example: Solve \(2x + y = 7\), \(5x + 3y = 16\).
\(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\), \(B = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 16 \end{pmatrix}\).
From section 5: \(A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).
\(\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 7 \\ 16 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).
Solution: \(x = 5\), \(y = 1\).

If \(\det(A) = 0\), the equations are either inconsistent (no solution) or dependent (infinitely many solutions).
