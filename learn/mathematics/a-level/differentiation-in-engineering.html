Differentiation in Engineering

A-Level guide. Rates of change in electrical, mechanical, and aircraft engineering — from circuits to flight dynamics.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Differentiation as rate of change

In engineering, differentiation answers: how fast is this quantity changing? The derivative \( \dfrac{dy}{dx} \) or \( \dfrac{dy}{dt} \) gives the instantaneous rate of change of \( y \) with respect to \( x \) or time \( t \).

Key idea: Position → velocity → acceleration is the classic chain: \( v = \dfrac{ds}{dt} \), \( a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \). In circuits, voltage and current rates govern capacitor and inductor behaviour.

Prerequisite: Differentiation. Related: Integration in Engineering reverses these relationships.

2) Electrical engineering — reactive components

Capacitors and inductors are defined by derivatives of voltage and current:

\[ v_L = L\,\frac{di}{dt}, \qquad i_C = C\,\frac{dv}{dt} \]

Capacitor

Current flows when voltage changes. Constant DC voltage → no current through an ideal capacitor.

High-pass filters use \( i = C\,dv/dt \) to pass rapid voltage changes.

Inductor

Voltage appears when current changes. Constant DC current → no voltage across an ideal inductor.

Back-EMF in motors: \( v = L\,di/dt \) opposes sudden current changes.

Worked example: Current through a 50 mH inductor changes as \( i = 0.1t^2 \) A. At \( t = 2 \) s: \( di/dt = 0.2t = 0.4 \) A s⁻¹, so \( v_L = 0.05 \times 0.4 = 0.02 \) V.

3) Electrical engineering — AC, power, and control

  • AC signals: Differentiating \( v = V_m\sin(\omega t) \) gives \( dv/dt = \omega V_m\cos(\omega t) \) — phase shifts by 90°
  • Power rate: \( P = dE/dt \). Instantaneous power \( p(t) = v(t)\,i(t) \)
  • PID control: Derivative term \( K_d\,\dfrac{de}{dt} \) responds to how fast error is changing
  • Fault detection: Rapid \( di/dt \) triggers protective relays in power systems
PID controller: \[ u(t) = K_p\,e(t) + K_i\int e(t)\,dt + K_d\,\frac{de(t)}{dt} \] The derivative term dampens overshoot and improves stability in motor drives and voltage regulators.

See Calculus (Electrical Engineering) and Control Theory for deeper coverage.

4) Mechanical engineering — motion and forces

Mechanical systems use the same position-velocity-acceleration relationships: \[ v = \frac{ds}{dt}, \qquad a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2} \]

Application Derivative used
Linear motion of a piston \( v = ds/dt \), \( a = dv/dt \)
Rotational motion \( \omega = d\theta/dt \), \( \alpha = d\omega/dt \)
Strain in a beam \( \varepsilon = du/dx \) (displacement gradient)
Spring-mass-damper \( m\,\ddot{x} + c\,\dot{x} + kx = F(t) \)
Newton's second law: \( F = ma = m\,\dfrac{dv}{dt} \). Force equals mass times rate of change of velocity — the foundation of dynamic mechanical analysis.

5) Aircraft engineering — flight dynamics

Aircraft motion is described by derivatives of position, orientation, and aerodynamic coefficients:

\[ v = \frac{dh}{dt} \text{ (rate of climb)}, \qquad \omega = \frac{d\theta}{dt} \text{ (angular rate)} \]

  • Lift and drag vs angle of attack: \( \dfrac{dC_L}{d\alpha} \), \( \dfrac{dC_D}{d\alpha} \) — slope of aerodynamic curves near operating point
  • Vertical Speed Indicator (VSI): measures \( dh/dt \) from static port pressure rate
  • Gyroscopes: output angular rate \( d\theta/dt \) for attitude reference
  • Engine spool rate: \( d(\text{RPM})/dt \) affects thrust response and fuel flow
Stall warning: As angle of attack \( \alpha \) increases, \( C_L \) rises until \( dC_L/d\alpha \) drops sharply near stall. Fly-by-wire systems monitor rates of change to prevent departure from controlled flight.

6) Structural dynamics and vibration

Vibrating structures — wings, turbine blades, fuselage panels — satisfy differential equations involving second derivatives: \[ m\,\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + c\,\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = F(t) \]

Electrical analogue

RLC circuit: \( L\,\ddot{i} + R\,\dot{i} + i/C = V(t)/L \)

Same mathematical form — mechanical and electrical systems are analogous.

Frequency analysis

Natural frequency depends on \( k/m \). Damping from \( c \) controls oscillation decay.

Differentiation appears when converting between displacement, velocity, and acceleration sensors.

7) Numerical differentiation in practice

Real sensors provide discrete data, not continuous functions. Engineers approximate derivatives numerically: \[ \frac{dy}{dt} \approx \frac{y(t + \Delta t) - y(t)}{\Delta t} \]

  • Embedded systems compute velocity from GPS position updates
  • Accelerometers integrate to velocity; differentiation of velocity gives acceleration (noisy — filtering required)
  • Simulation tools (MATLAB, Simulink) solve ODEs using numerical methods
Practical note: Differentiation amplifies noise. Integration smooths data; differentiation sharpens it. Sensor fusion (e.g. Kalman filters) combines both for accurate state estimation in aircraft navigation.
