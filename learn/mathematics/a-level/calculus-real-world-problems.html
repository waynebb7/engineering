Calculus (Real World Problems)

A-Level guide. Modelling motion, growth and decay, and optimisation in bridges, projectiles, and population dynamics.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Modelling with calculus

Real-world problems often involve quantities that change continuously. Calculus provides the language: differentiation for rates of change, integration for accumulation over time or space.

Key idea: If \( s(t) \) is displacement, then velocity \( v = \dfrac{ds}{dt} \) and acceleration \( a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \). Integration reverses these relationships.

Builds on Differentiation and Integration. See also Optimization for max/min problems.

2) Motion — kinematics

Under constant acceleration \( a \), starting from initial velocity \( u \) and displacement \( s_0 \):

\[ v(t) = u + at, \qquad s(t) = s_0 + ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Worked example: A vehicle decelerates from 25 m s⁻¹ at \( a = -5 \) m s⁻². Time to stop: \( 0 = 25 - 5t \Rightarrow t = 5 \) s. Distance: \( s = 25(5) + \tfrac{1}{2}(-5)(25) = 62.5 \) m.

Differentiation path

Given \( s(t) \), find \( v \) and \( a \) by differentiating.

Integration path

Given \( a(t) \), integrate to get \( v \), then integrate again for \( s \).

3) Projectile motion

A projectile launched with speed \( u \) at angle \( \theta \) above horizontal, neglecting air resistance, has horizontal and vertical components under gravity \( g \):

\[ x(t) = u\cos\theta \cdot t, \qquad y(t) = u\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2 \]

Maximum height: At the top, vertical velocity is zero. From \( v_y = u\sin\theta - gt = 0 \), time to peak: \( t = \dfrac{u\sin\theta}{g} \). Maximum height: \[ H = \frac{u^2\sin^2\theta}{2g} \]
Range on level ground: \[ R = \frac{u^2\sin 2\theta}{g} \] Maximum range when \( \theta = 45° \) (for launch and landing at the same height).
Assumption: These formulae ignore air resistance. Real aircraft and long-range projectiles need more complex models.

4) Exponential growth and decay

Many natural processes change at a rate proportional to the current amount: \[ \frac{dN}{dt} = kN \] Solution: \( N(t) = N_0 e^{kt} \). Growth if \( k > 0 \), decay if \( k < 0 \).

Model Equation Example
Population growth \( P(t) = P_0 e^{rt} \) Bacterial culture doubling
Radioactive decay \( N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t} \) Half-life: \( t_{1/2} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \)
Newton cooling \( T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{env}})e^{-kt} \) Hot component cooling in air
Capacitor discharge \( V(t) = V_0 e^{-t/RC} \) See Calculus (Electrical Engineering)

5) Structural modelling — bridges and beams

Engineers use calculus to find bending moments, shear forces, and deflections in structures. For a simply supported beam of length \( L \) with uniform load \( w \) per unit length:

\[ M(x) = \frac{w}{2}x(L - x) \]

The maximum bending moment occurs at mid-span (\( x = L/2 \)): \( M_{\max} = \dfrac{wL^2}{8} \). Differentiating \( M(x) \) and setting to zero confirms this turning point.

Deflection: Beam deflection \( y(x) \) involves integrating the bending moment twice: \[ EI\,\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = M(x) \] where \( E \) is Young's modulus and \( I \) is the second moment of area.

Suspension bridge cables follow a catenary or parabolic shape under load — optimisation minimises material while meeting strength constraints.

6) Optimisation applications

Real-world optimisation combines modelling with differentiation. Common A-Level and engineering examples:

  • Minimum material: Surface area of a cylinder for fixed volume \( V = \pi r^2 h \)
  • Maximum profit: Revenue minus cost as a function of production quantity
  • Shortest time / path: Minimise a time function subject to constraints
  • Population carrying capacity: Logistic model \( \dfrac{dP}{dt} = rP(1 - P/K) \)
Logistic growth: Population grows quickly at first, then slows as it approaches carrying capacity \( K \). Unlike pure exponential growth, it models limited resources realistically.

7) Setting up and validating models

Modelling steps

  1. Identify variables and units
  2. State assumptions (constant \( g \), no friction, etc.)
  3. Write the differential relationship or function
  4. Solve using calculus techniques
  5. Interpret results and check units

Validation

  • Does the answer have sensible magnitude?
  • Do limiting cases work (e.g. \( t = 0 \))?
  • Compare with data or simulation
Exam tip: Always define symbols at the start. Examiners award marks for correct setup even if later algebra goes wrong.
