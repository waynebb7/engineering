1) Why Quine–McCluskey?

The Quine–McCluskey (Q-M) method is a systematic tabular procedure for minimising Boolean functions. Unlike Karnaugh maps, it scales to many variables and multiple outputs without relying on spatial grouping.

  • Guarantees finding all prime implicants
  • Suitable for computer implementation and homework verification
  • Two phases: (1) generate prime implicants, (2) select a minimal cover
When to use it: More than four variables, many don't cares, or when you need a provably minimal SOP cover.

2) Minterm table and combining

Start by listing all minterms (and don't cares treated as 1s) grouped by the number of 1-bits in their binary code.

Example: F = Σ(0, 1, 2, 8, 9, 10, 11)

Group (# of 1s)MintermBinary (ABCD)
0m₀0000
1m₁, m₂, m₈0001, 0010, 1000
2m₉, m₁₀1001, 1010
3m₁₁1011

Combine terms that differ in exactly one bit; replace the differing bit with − (dash). Repeat until no further combinations are possible. Uncombined terms are prime implicants.

Example combine: m₀(0000) + m₁(0001) → 000− which is A'B'C'.

3) Prime implicants

A prime implicant is a product term that cannot be merged further — it covers a maximal group of minterms. The Q-M combine table produces every prime implicant for the function.

  • Implicant: any product term that covers some 1s of F
  • Prime implicant: an implicant that is not contained in a larger implicant
  • Essential prime implicant: a prime implicant that is the only cover for at least one minterm

Essential prime implicants must appear in every valid minimal cover — always include them first.

Notation: A dash − in a combined term means that variable is eliminated (can be 0 or 1).

4) Prime implicant (PI) chart

The PI chart (coverage table) lists prime implicants as rows and minterms as columns. Mark an × where a prime implicant covers a minterm.

From the chart you can:

  1. Identify essential prime implicants (sole × in a column)
  2. Cross off minterms they cover
  3. Resolve remaining choices for a minimal cover

A column with a single × identifies an essential prime implicant — include it and remove covered columns.

5) Selecting a minimal cover

After essential prime implicants are chosen, some minterms may remain uncovered. The remaining problem is a set-cover question: pick the fewest prime implicants that cover all remaining 1s.

  • Try inspection on small charts: branch on choices and compare total term count
  • Multiple minimal covers may tie — compare literal count if gate cost differs
  • Don't cares expand prime implicants but are not required to be covered unless needed for minimisation
Important: A minimal cover uses the fewest prime implicants. Fewer literals may require secondary optimisation.

6) Petrick method (introduction)

When the PI chart has no essential prime implicants left, the Petrick method solves the cover problem algebraically.

  1. For each remaining minterm mj, form a sum of the prime implicants that cover it: (Pa + Pb + …)
  2. Multiply these sums together (logical AND of ORs): (Pa + Pb)(Pc + Pd)…
  3. Expand using distributive law: (A + B)(A + C) = A + BC
  4. Choose the product term with the fewest literals (fewest prime implicants)

Example fragment: If m₃ is covered by P₁ or P₂, and m₇ by P₂ or P₃:

(P₁ + P₂)(P₂ + P₃) = P₂ + P₁P₃

Minimal cover: P₂ alone if it covers all remaining minterms; otherwise P₁ and P₃.

Role of Petrick: Removes guesswork when several prime implicants tie. Often taught as the formal finish after Q-M tabulation.

7) Worked example (outline)

Minimise F = Σ(0, 1, 2, 5, 6, 7) with three variables A, B, C.

Step 1 — Combine:

  • m₀ + m₁ → A'B'
  • m₀ + m₂ → A'C'
  • m₅ + m₇ → BC
  • m₂ + m₆ → BC'
  • m₁ + m₅ → B'C (if in set)
  • m₃ + m₇ → further merges as applicable

Step 2 — PI chart: Mark coverage; find essentials.

Step 3 — Result: Typical minimal SOP: F = A'B' + BC + A'C' (verify against your combine table).

Cross-check with a 3-variable K-map to confirm the same minimum.
