1) Why quantum metrology?

Quantum metrology exploits quantum resources — superposition, entanglement, and squeezing — to measure physical quantities with precision beyond classical limits. Sensors using quantum effects already outperform classical instruments in timing, magnetometry, and gravimetry.

  • Atomic clocks: optical lattice clocks reach \(10^{-18}\) fractional frequency uncertainty.
  • Gravitational wave detection: LIGO uses squeezed vacuum to beat the shot-noise limit.
  • NV centres in diamond: nanoscale magnetometry at room temperature.
  • Quantum-enhanced imaging: sub-shot-noise phase microscopy.
Key idea: The precision of any measurement is bounded by quantum mechanics. Quantum strategies saturate tighter bounds than classical ones by using entanglement across probe particles.

Prerequisites: Uncertainty Principle, Superposition and Measurement.

2) Parameter estimation framework

A parameter \(\phi\) is encoded in a probe state via unitary \(U(\phi) = e^{-i\phi H}\). Repeated measurements yield an estimator \(\hat{\phi}\) with variance \(\text{Var}(\hat{\phi})\). The quantum Cramér–Rao bound sets the ultimate limit:

\(\text{Var}(\hat{\phi}) \geq \frac{1}{m\,F_Q(\rho_\phi)}\)

where \(m\) is the number of measurements and \(F_Q\) is the quantum Fisher information. Maximising \(F_Q\) over probe states and measurements defines optimal metrological protocols.

Signal-to-noise: For phase estimation with \(N\) probe particles, the quantum Fisher information scales as \(F_Q \sim N^2\) for maximally entangled (NOON) states — the origin of Heisenberg scaling.

3) Standard quantum limit vs Heisenberg scaling

Probe statePrecision scalingName
\(N\) independent particles \(\Delta\phi \sim 1/\sqrt{N}\) Standard quantum limit (SQL)
\(N\)-particle entangled (NOON, GHZ) \(\Delta\phi \sim 1/N\) Heisenberg scaling (HL)
Squeezed vacuum interferometry Beats SQL in one quadrature Sub-shot-noise (not full HL)

The NOON state \(|N,0\rangle + |0,N\rangle\) achieves Heisenberg-limited phase sensitivity but is fragile against particle loss. Squeezed states offer a practical compromise: reduced noise in the measured quadrature at the cost of increased noise in the conjugate quadrature.

4) Ramsey interferometry

Ramsey spectroscopy measures atomic transition frequencies — the basis of atomic clocks. A superposition \(|+\rangle = (|0\rangle + |1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) evolves for time \(T\) under the parameter to be estimated (e.g. magnetic field \(B\)):

\(|\psi(T)\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(|0\rangle + e^{-i\omega T}|1\rangle\bigr)\)

A second \(\pi/2\) pulse maps accumulated phase to measurable population. Precision improves with interrogation time \(T\) but is limited by decoherence (\(T_2\)) and the linewidth of the transition.

Clock stability: Fractional frequency uncertainty scales as \(\sigma_y(\tau) \sim 1/(\omega_0 T \sqrt{N \tau})\) for \(N\) atoms, interrogation time \(T\), and averaging time \(\tau\).

Related: Quantum Control and Calibration — Ramsey in qubit calibration.

5) Squeezed states in LIGO

Gravitational wave detectors are Michelson interferometers where shot noise (photon counting statistics) limits sensitivity at high frequencies. Injecting squeezed vacuum into the dark port reduces fluctuations in the phase quadrature:

\(\Delta X_{\text{squeezed}} < \Delta X_{\text{vacuum}} = \sqrt{\hbar/2}, \qquad \Delta P_{\text{squeezed}} > \Delta P_{\text{vacuum}}\)

LIGO's squeezed-light source (frequency-dependent squeezing) improved sensitivity by ~3 dB, increasing the detection rate of merger events. This is the most prominent real-world application of quantum metrology.

Squeezing parameter: \(S^2 = e^{-2r}\) where \(r\) is the squeezing factor. 10 dB squeezing (\(r \approx 1.15\)) reduces phase noise by a factor of \(\sim 3\).

6) Solid-state quantum sensors

NV centres (diamond)

  • Electron spin sensitive to magnetic fields
  • Optically read out via fluorescence
  • Single-spin sensitivity: \(\sim\) nT/√Hz
  • Applications: MRI at nanoscale, current mapping

Other platforms

  • SQUIDs: femtotesla magnetometry
  • Atom interferometers: gravimetry, inertial nav
  • Rydberg atoms: microwave field sensing
  • Quantum dots: thermometry, electric fields

NV magnetometry uses Ramsey or Hahn-echo sequences on the spin-1 ground state, with sensitivity \(\eta \propto 1/(C\sqrt{T_2 \cdot t})\) where \(C\) is contrast and \(t\) is integration time.

7) Decoherence and the ultimate limit

In practice, environmental noise degrades metrological advantage. For \(N\) particles with dephasing time \(T_2\), the effective precision scales as:

\(\Delta\phi \sim \begin{cases} 1/\sqrt{N} & \text{(independent probes, SQL)} \\ 1/N^{3/4} & \text{(with decoherence, realistic HL)} \end{cases}\)

Adaptive and error-corrected metrology uses quantum error correction to recover Heisenberg scaling in the presence of noise — an active research area connecting metrology to Quantum Error Correction.

Common mistake: Entanglement alone does not guarantee advantage — the measurement must be compatible with the probe state, and loss channels can erase all quantum gain.

8) Applications and engineering impact

  • GNSS-independent navigation: quantum accelerometers and gravimeters for dead reckoning.
  • Medical imaging: ultra-low-field MRI enhanced by atomic magnetometers.
  • Fundamental physics: test of general relativity, dark matter searches, variation of fundamental constants.
  • Network synchronisation: optical clock networks for finance, telecommunications, and science.
Near-term value: Quantum sensing is widely considered closer to commercial deployment than fault-tolerant quantum computing — lower qubit counts, less error correction needed, operating at room temperature in many platforms.
