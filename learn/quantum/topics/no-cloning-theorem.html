1) What is the no-cloning theorem?

The no-cloning theorem (Wootters & Zurek, 1982; Dieks, 1982) states that there is no unitary operation that takes an unknown quantum state \(|\psi\rangle\) and produces two identical copies: \[ |\psi\rangle \otimes |0\rangle \;\xrightarrow{?}\; |\psi\rangle \otimes |\psi\rangle \] for all \(|\psi\rangle\). This is not a technological limitation — it follows from the linear structure of quantum mechanics.

Key idea: Classical bits can be copied freely; qubits cannot. This single fact shapes quantum teleportation, quantum cryptography, and error correction.

Prerequisite: Qubits and the Bloch Sphere, Hilbert Spaces.

2) The cloning machine we would need

A cloning machine is a unitary \(U\) acting on a qubit plus an ancilla \(|0\rangle\): \[ U\bigl(|\psi\rangle \otimes |0\rangle\bigr) = |\psi\rangle \otimes |\psi\rangle \] We demand this hold for every state \(|\psi\rangle\), not just a fixed basis.

Linearity of \(U\) then requires that for any superposition \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\): \[ U\bigl((\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle) \otimes |0\rangle\bigr) = \alpha|0\rangle \otimes |0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \otimes |1\rangle \]

But the machine also claims: \[ U\bigl((\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle) \otimes |0\rangle\bigr) = (\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle) \otimes (\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle) \] These two right-hand sides are equal only in trivial cases — a contradiction.

3) Proof for two non-orthogonal states

Take two non-orthogonal states \(|\psi\rangle\) and \(|\phi\rangle\) with \(\langle\psi|\phi\rangle \neq 0\). If cloning worked: \[ \langle\psi|\phi\rangle = \langle\psi|\phi\rangle^2 \] because inner products are preserved under unitary evolution on both copies.

Since \(|\langle\psi|\phi\rangle| \leq 1\), the only solutions are \(\langle\psi|\phi\rangle \in \{0, 1\}\). Non-orthogonal states have \(0 < |\langle\psi|\phi\rangle| < 1\), so cloning fails.

Orthogonal states can be cloned — but only if you already know which basis they belong to. A universal cloner for unknown states is impossible.

4) Approximate and probabilistic cloning

Approximate cloning (Bužek & Hillery, 1996) produces copies with fidelity \(F < 1\). The optimal symmetric cloner achieves \(F = \frac{5}{6}\) for qubits — never perfect.

Probabilistic cloning succeeds with some probability \(p < 1\) and outputs perfect copies when it succeeds. You cannot make \(p = 1\) for a universal machine.

Broadcasting: You can sometimes distribute partial classical information about a state, but never create two full independent copies of an unknown pure state.

5) Consequences for quantum information

  • QKD security: An eavesdropper cannot copy photons and keep one — any intercept disturbs the state.
  • Teleportation: Moving quantum information requires destroying the original (see Quantum Teleportation).
  • Error correction: You cannot back up a qubit before measurement; redundancy must be built into encoded logical qubits.
  • Classical simulation: You cannot maintain a hidden copy of a quantum state to cheat at quantum protocols.
Common mistake: The theorem forbids copying unknown states. Preparing many qubits in a known state \(|+\rangle\) is fine — you are not cloning; you are manufacturing.

6) Connection to entanglement and measurement

Cloning would let you measure one copy and keep another — effectively performing non-destructive measurement on arbitrary unknown states. The no-cloning theorem is consistent with the measurement postulate: measurement collapses the state.

Entanglement also resists local copying. If Alice and Bob share \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\), neither party can clone their half without accessing the joint state.

Related: Entanglement and Bell Inequalities (when created), Density Matrices for mixed-state generalisations.

7) What you can do instead

Quantum teleportation

Transfer \(|\psi\rangle\) using entanglement and classical bits — original is destroyed.

State tomography

Many copies of identical prepared states let you estimate \(\rho\) statistically — not cloning an unknown single specimen.

Quantum repeaters and error correction use entanglement swapping and syndrome extraction rather than copying arbitrary qubits.
