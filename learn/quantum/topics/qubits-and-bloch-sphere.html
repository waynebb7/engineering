1) What is a qubit?

A qubit (quantum bit) is a two-level quantum system. Its state is a unit vector in \(\mathbb{C}^2\): \[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle, \quad |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \] where \(|0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\) and \(|1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}\) are computational basis states.

Key idea: Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can exist in a superposition — both amplitudes are nonzero until measured.

Prerequisite: Complex Numbers, Vectors, Dirac Notation.

2) Global phase and physical states

States \(|\psi\rangle\) and \(e^{i\gamma}|\psi\rangle\) are physically identical — global phase is unobservable. The set of distinct pure qubit states is the projective space \(\mathbb{C}P^1\), parameterised by two real angles.

General form with \(\alpha = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}\) and \(\beta = e^{i\phi}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\): \[ |\psi\rangle = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}|0\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}|1\rangle \]

Common mistake: \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are complex amplitudes, not probabilities. Probabilities are \(|\alpha|^2\) and \(|\beta|^2\).

3) The Bloch sphere

Every pure qubit state maps to a point on the unit Bloch sphere: \[ |\psi\rangle \mapsto \vec{r} = (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\; \sin\theta\sin\phi,\; \cos\theta) \]

  • North pole \((0,0,1)\): \(|0\rangle\)
  • South pole \((0,0,-1)\): \(|1\rangle\)
  • Equator: equal superpositions, e.g. \(|+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle)\) at \((1,0,0)\)
  • \(|-\rangle\) at \((-1,0,0)\); \(|{+i}\rangle\) at \((0,1,0)\)
Density matrix form: \(\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi| = \frac{1}{2}(I + \vec{r}\cdot\vec{\sigma})\) where \(\vec{\sigma} = (X, Y, Z)\) are Pauli matrices.

5) Measurement in the computational basis

Measuring \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) in the \(\{|0\rangle, |1\rangle\}\) basis yields:

  • Outcome 0 with probability \(P(0) = |\alpha|^2\)
  • Outcome 1 with probability \(P(1) = |\beta|^2\)

Post-measurement state: \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\) respectively (normalised).

Expectation value: \(\langle Z \rangle = |\alpha|^2 - |\beta|^2 = r_z\) — the Bloch \(z\)-coordinate equals the spin expectation along \(Z\).

Full treatment: Measurement in Quantum Computing.

6) Physical implementations

Superconducting

Two lowest energy levels of a Josephson junction circuit; \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) are persistent current states.

Trapped ions

Hyperfine or optical clock states of an ion; excellent coherence times.

More platforms: Superconducting Qubits, Trapped-Ion Systems, Photonic Computing.

7) Qubit vs classical bit — information capacity

Reading one qubit gives at most one classical bit of information (one outcome from \(\{0,1\}\)). However, during computation, \(n\) qubits carry \(2^n\) complex amplitudes — enabling quantum parallelism in superposition (not cloning).

Nuance: You cannot access all \(2^n\) amplitudes in one measurement. Quantum advantage comes from interference and entanglement, not from storing exponentially many classical bits.
