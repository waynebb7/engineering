1) Why photons?

Photonic quantum computing encodes qubits in optical modes — polarisation, path (dual-rail), time-bin, or Fock number states. Photons interact weakly with the environment, enabling operation at room temperature with minimal decoherence.

  • Natural interface to fibre-optic networks and telecommunications infrastructure.
  • Clock rate set by detection and feed-forward electronics (GHz repetition possible).
  • Challenge: photons do not interact with each other — entangling gates require measurement or nonlinear optics.
  • Leading approaches: KLM linear optics, cluster-state generation, Gaussian boson sampling (GBS).
Key idea: Photons are excellent flying qubits for communication; making them compute requires clever use of interference, post-selection, or weak nonlinearities.

Prerequisites: Optics and Photonics, Qubits and the Bloch Sphere.

2) Dual-rail qubit encoding

In the dual-rail encoding, one photon in two spatial modes defines a qubit:

\(|0\rangle \equiv |1,0\rangle, \qquad |1\rangle \equiv |0,1\rangle\)

Single-qubit gates are implemented by unitary \(2\times 2\) transformations on the mode amplitudes — realised physically as beam splitters and phase shifters. A 50:50 beam splitter acts as a Hadamard on dual-rail:

\(H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}\)

Common mistake: Dual-rail requires exactly one photon in the two rails. Vacuum \(|0,0\rangle\) and two-photon \(|1,1\rangle\) components are error states and must be post-selected or suppressed.

3) Fock states and boson sampling

In Fock encoding, the qubit is the presence or absence of a photon in a mode: \(|0\rangle = |0\rangle_{\text{Fock}}\), \(|1\rangle = |1\rangle_{\text{Fock}}\). Multi-photon interference through a linear optical network \(U\) samples output configurations with probabilities given by the permanent of submatrices of \(U\):

\(P(\mathbf{n}_{\text{out}} | \mathbf{n}_{\text{in}}) \propto \bigl|\text{Perm}(U_{\mathbf{n}_{\text{out}},\mathbf{n}_{\text{in}}})\bigr|^2\)

Boson sampling (Aaronson–Arkhipov) uses this to demonstrate quantum computational advantage: sampling from this distribution is hard for classical computers under standard complexity assumptions.

Gaussian boson sampling (GBS): Uses squeezed states and linear optics; the hafnian replaces the permanent. Xanadu and others demonstrated GBS advantage with up to 216 modes.

4) KLM gates and measurement-induced nonlinearity

Knill, Laflamme, and Milburn (KLM) showed that linear optics plus post-selected measurement suffices for universal quantum computation. The controlled-NOT gate uses ancilla photons and parity checks; success probability is boosted via gate teleportation chains.

A key building block is the controlled-Z (CZ) gate via Hong–Ou–Mandel (HOM) interference: two indistinguishable photons incident on a 50:50 beam splitter bunch together, imparting a \(\pi\) phase when both inputs are \(|1\rangle\).

Resource cost: Original KLM schemes require many ancilla photons per gate. Fusion-based and cluster-state approaches reduce overhead by pre-generating entangled resource states.

Related: Quantum Gates, Entanglement and Bell Inequalities.

5) Cluster-state and measurement-based models

In the one-way quantum computer, a large entangled cluster state is prepared, then single-qubit measurements drive the computation. For photonics, cluster states are generated from pulsed lasers and interferometric fusion of smaller entangled graphs.

  • Raussendorf–Briegel cluster: 2D lattice of entangled qubits; measurements implement universal computation.
  • Fusion gates: type-I and type-II photonic fusion operations merge graph states.
  • Feed-forward: measurement outcomes determine adaptive basis rotations — requires fast classical electronics (< 10 ns latency for GHz clocks).

PsiQuantum and other companies pursue fault-tolerant photonic architectures based on cluster states with GKP (Gottesman–Kitaev–Preskill) error-corrected qubits.

Programmable meshes of Mach–Zehnder interferometers (Reck or Clements decomposition) implement arbitrary unitaries \(U \in U(N)\) on \(N\) modes. Phase shifters are tuned via thermo-optic or electro-optic control.

Loss is critical: Every dB of optical loss reduces success probability exponentially in post-selected schemes. Low-loss waveguides (< 0.1 dB/cm) and high-efficiency detectors (> 90%) are essential.

7) Photon sources and detectors

Single-photon sources: spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC), quantum dots in cavities, colour centres (e.g. SiV in diamond), and heralded sources where one photon of a pair triggers detection of the other.

Detectors: superconducting nanowire single-photon detectors (SNSPDs) offer > 90% efficiency and < 100 ps jitter at cryogenic temperatures; avalanche photodiodes (APDs) work at room temperature with lower efficiency.

Source metrics

  • Indistinguishability: Hong–Ou–Mandel visibility
  • Brightness: pairs per pump pulse

Detector metrics

  • Efficiency \(\eta\)
  • Dark count rate
  • Timing jitter

8) Advantages, limits, and applications

Strengths

  • Room-temperature operation
  • Natural QKD and networking
  • Massive parallelism in sampling tasks
  • Low crosstalk in spatial modes

Challenges

  • Probabilistic gates without error correction
  • Photon loss and detector inefficiency
  • Large resource overhead for fault tolerance
  • Feed-forward latency at scale

Near-term applications: boson sampling demonstrations, quantum machine learning with GBS, and networked quantum computing linking matter qubits via photonic buses.
