1) What NISQ means

NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum), coined by John Preskill, describes processors with \(50\)–\(10{,}000+\) qubits that are not yet fault-tolerant. Gates have error rates \(\sim 10^{-3}\)–\(10^{-2}\); coherence times limit circuit depth before outcomes are dominated by noise.

  • Noisy: every operation is approximate
  • Intermediate scale: beyond classical simulation of full state vectors for some sizes
  • Pre-fault-tolerance: no full Quantum Error Correction yet
Key idea: NISQ is an engineering era, not an algorithm. Success depends on shallow circuits, problem structure, and error mitigation — not asymptotic proofs alone.

3) NISQ-friendly algorithms

Algorithms with modest depth and hybrid classical loops:

  • VQE: molecular ground-state energies
  • QAOA: combinatorial optimisation
  • Short-time dynamics: Trotterised Hamiltonian evolution
  • Randomised benchmarking circuits: hardware characterisation, not applications
Not NISQ-ready at scale: Shor's algorithm and deep QFT circuits need Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing.

4) Error mitigation (not correction)

Error mitigation post-processes noisy results without full QEC:

  • Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE): run at amplified noise, extrapolate to zero
  • Probabilistic error cancellation: quasi-probability decomposition of noise
  • Readout correction: calibrate measurement confusion matrices
  • Symmetry verification: discard unphysical shots

Mitigation improves estimates at the cost of extra circuit runs; it does not provide exponential suppression like Surface Codes below threshold.

5) Quantum utility and supremacy debates

Quantum supremacy (random circuit sampling) demonstrated classical intractability of some NISQ tasks — not necessarily useful applications. Quantum utility asks whether NISQ devices beat classical methods on practical problems (chemistry, optimisation, ML).

Current consensus: Demonstrations exist for specialised benchmarks; broad commercial advantage on NISQ remains contested and problem-dependent. Classical algorithms also improve in parallel.

Algorithm landscape: Quantum Algorithms Overview.

6) Circuit depth and barren plateaus

Variational training on NISQ suffers from:

  • Barren plateaus: gradients vanish exponentially with qubit count in random ansätze
  • Noise-induced barren plateaus: depolarising noise further flattens trainability
  • Depth–noise trade-off: more layers → more gate errors before convergence

\[ \mathrm{Var}\left[\frac{\partial E}{\partial \theta_i}\right] \sim \exp(-\alpha n) \]

Ansatz design and problem-informed initialisation are active research areas for VQE and QAOA.

7) Path from NISQ to FTQC

  1. Improve \(p\): better materials, control, and calibration
  2. Logical qubit demos: break-even and below-threshold logical error scaling
  3. Code integration: embed algorithms in logical layers
  4. Factories: magic states and distillation at scale
Timeline: Industry roadmaps target useful logical qubits this decade; large-scale FTQC may follow in the 2030s — highly uncertain.
