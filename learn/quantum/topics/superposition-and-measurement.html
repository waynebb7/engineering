1) Superposition principle

If \(|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle\) are valid quantum states, then any linear combination

\[|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\psi_1\rangle + \beta|\psi_2\rangle\]

is also a valid state (provided it is normalised: \(|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1\)). The system does not have to be "in one state or the other" before measurement — it can occupy a coherent superposition.

Foundation: This linearity is the bedrock of quantum computing — qubits are superpositions of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). See Wave–Particle Duality for the interference origin of superposition.

2) The Born rule

Measurement outcomes are probabilistic. If a system is in \(|\psi\rangle\) and you measure observable with eigenstates \(|a_n\rangle\), the probability of outcome \(a_n\) is:

\[P(a_n) = |\langle a_n | \psi \rangle|^2\]

Expectation value: \(\langle A \rangle = \langle\psi|A|\psi\rangle\). The Born rule connects abstract amplitudes to laboratory statistics.

Bra–ket notation is explained in Dirac Notation.

3) Measurement as projection

Ideal projective measurement collapses the state onto an eigenstate of the measured observable. After measuring \(a_n\):

\[|\psi\rangle \to \frac{P_n|\psi\rangle}{\|P_n|\psi\rangle\|} = |a_n\rangle\]

where \(P_n = |a_n\rangle\langle a_n|\) is the projector. Subsequent measurements of the same observable yield \(a_n\) with certainty — the state has been updated.

Common mistake: Assuming collapse is a physical "instantaneous process" in all interpretations. The postulate describes state update upon measurement; interpretations differ on ontology.

4) Qubit example: spin along \(z\)

A spin-\(\tfrac{1}{2}\) particle can be in:

\[|\psi\rangle = \alpha|{\uparrow z}\rangle + \beta|{\downarrow z}\rangle\]

Measuring \(S_z\) yields \(+\hbar/2\) with probability \(|\alpha|^2\) and \(-\hbar/2\) with probability \(|\beta|^2\). After \(+\hbar/2\) outcome, the state is \(|{\uparrow z}\rangle\).

Pauli matrices formalise spin operators — see Spin and Pauli Matrices.

5) Non-commuting observables

If \([A,B] \neq 0\), a state cannot simultaneously be an eigenstate of both. Measuring \(B\) after \(A\) generally disturbs the \(A\) statistics of a re-measurement — measurement order matters.

The Uncertainty Principle quantifies this: \(\Delta A\,\Delta B \geq \tfrac{1}{2}|\langle[A,B]\rangle|\).

Compatible observables

Commuting operators share eigenbases; joint measurement is possible without mutual disturbance.

Incompatible observables

Position and momentum; \(S_x\) and \(S_z\) — no common eigenstates for both.

6) POVM and generalised measurement

Real detectors often implement positive operator-valued measures (POVMs) — not every measurement is a sharp projection. POVM elements \(\{E_m\}\) satisfy \(E_m \geq 0\), \(\sum_m E_m = I\), with \(P(m) = \langle\psi|E_m|\psi\rangle\).

Mixed-state measurement and open systems connect to Density Matrices and Quantum Channels.

7) Decoherence and the classical limit

Superpositions of macroscopically distinct states (e.g. Schrödinger's cat) rapidly lose phase coherence through entanglement with the environment — a process called decoherence. It explains why classical objects appear to have definite properties without solving the measurement problem fully.

Entanglement link: Environment-induced decoherence is entanglement in disguise — Entanglement and Bell Inequalities.
