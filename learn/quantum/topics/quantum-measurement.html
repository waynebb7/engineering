1) The measurement postulate

Measuring observable \(M\) with spectral decomposition \(M = \sum_m m\, P_m\) on state \(|\psi\rangle\):

  • Outcome \(m\) with probability \(P(m) = \langle\psi|P_m|\psi\rangle\) (Born rule)
  • Post-measurement state: \(\frac{P_m|\psi\rangle}{\|P_m|\psi\rangle\|}\) (projective / Lüders)
Key idea: Measurement is probabilistic and generally disturbs the state — unlike classical readout.

Foundations: Postulates of Quantum Mechanics, Superposition and Measurement.

2) Computational basis measurement

The standard readout in quantum computing measures the Pauli \(Z\) observable: \[ Z = |0\rangle\langle 0| - |1\rangle\langle 1| \] For \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\): \[ P(0) = |\alpha|^2,\quad P(1) = |\beta|^2 \]

In circuits, the meter symbol on a wire performs this measurement and stores a classical bit.

Bloch view: \(Z\) measurement distinguishes northern (\(|0\rangle\)) from southern (\(|1\rangle\)) hemispheres.

3) Measuring in other bases

To measure in basis \(\{|+\rangle, |-\rangle\}\), apply \(H\) then measure in \(Z\): \[ H|\psi\rangle \to \text{measure } Z \to \text{outcome } \pm 1 \]

General recipe: rotate the desired eigenbasis to computational basis with unitary \(U\), measure, interpret.

Basis matters: Measuring \(|\psi\rangle = |+\rangle\) in \(Z\) gives 0 or 1 with 50% probability each — not deterministic.

4) Observables and expectation values

The expectation value of observable \(M\): \[ \langle M \rangle = \langle\psi|M|\psi\rangle = \sum_m m\, P(m) \]

For Pauli operators on the Bloch sphere: \[ \langle X \rangle = r_x,\quad \langle Y \rangle = r_y,\quad \langle Z \rangle = r_z \]

Quantum algorithms: Many algorithms estimate \(\langle\psi|H|\psi\rangle\) for a Hamiltonian \(H\) via repeated measurement — basis of VQE and QAOA.

5) Multi-qubit measurement

Measuring all qubits projects onto \(|x_1 x_2 \cdots x_n\rangle\) with probability \(|\langle x_1\cdots x_n|\psi\rangle|^2\).

Partial measurement: Measuring qubit 1 of an entangled pair collapses the joint state. For \(|\Phi^+\rangle\), measuring qubit 1 as 0 projects to \(|00\rangle\).

Joint vs marginal: Marginal probabilities need not factor for entangled states. See Multi-Qubit Systems.

6) POVMs and generalised measurement

A POVM (positive operator-valued measure) \(\{E_m\}\) satisfies \(\sum_m E_m = I\), \(E_m \geq 0\), with \(P(m) = \langle\psi|E_m|\psi\rangle\).

POVMs are more general than projective measurements — they model realistic detectors with non-unit efficiency, loss, and readout errors.

Hardware: Superconducting qubits use dispersive readout; photonic systems use photon detection. Both implement imperfect POVMs, not ideal projectors.

7) Measurement in algorithms

  • Deutsch–Jozsa: Single final measurement determines if \(f\) is constant or balanced.
  • Grover: Measure after \(O(\sqrt{N})\) iterations to find the marked item.
  • Shor: Measure the period-register in the computational basis; continued fractions extract \(r\).
  • Teleportation: Bell measurement on Alice's qubits; classical outcomes steer Bob's correction.

Most algorithms require repeated runs (shots) to estimate outcome statistics.
