1) What is quantum teleportation?

Quantum teleportation (Bennett et al., 1993) transfers the quantum state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\) from Alice to Bob using:

  • One pair of qubits in a Bell state (shared entanglement)
  • Two classical bits of communication
  • Local quantum operations only
Key idea: Teleportation moves quantum information, not matter. It requires the no-cloning theorem — Alice's qubit is destroyed in the process.

Prerequisite: Qubits, Quantum Gates, Entanglement.

2) Setup: three qubits

Qubit 1 (Alice): unknown state \(|\psi\rangle_1 = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\).
Qubits 2 & 3 (Alice & Bob): shared Bell state \[ |\Phi^+\rangle_{23} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle) \]

Initial joint state: \[ |\Psi\rangle = |\psi\rangle_1 \otimes |\Phi^+\rangle_{23} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\alpha|0\rangle|00\rangle + \beta|1\rangle|11\rangle\bigr) \]

Resources: 1 ebit (entanglement bit) + 2 cbits (classical bits). No quantum channel needed after entanglement is distributed.

3) Bell measurement on Alice's pair

Alice applies CNOT\(_{1,2}\) then \(H_1\), and measures qubits 1 and 2 in the computational basis, obtaining outcomes \(m_1, m_2 \in \{0,1\}\).

The four Bell states form a measurement basis. Rewriting \(|\Psi\rangle\) in the Bell basis of qubits 1–2: \[ |\Psi\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{m_1,m_2} |\beta_{m_1 m_2}\rangle_{12} \otimes X^{m_2} Z^{m_1}|\psi\rangle_3 \] where \(|\beta_{00}\rangle = |\Phi^+\rangle\), etc.

After measurement: Qubits 1 and 2 collapse to \(|\beta_{m_1 m_2}\rangle\). Bob's qubit 3 holds \(X^{m_2} Z^{m_1}|\psi\rangle\) — the original state with Pauli corrections applied.

4) Classical correction at Bob

Alice sends \((m_1, m_2)\) to Bob over a classical channel. Bob applies:

  • \(Z^{m_1}\) if \(m_1 = 1\)
  • \(X^{m_2}\) if \(m_2 = 1\)

Result: Bob's qubit is in state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle\).

Gate reference: Pauli matrices from Quantum Gates — \(X = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}\), \(Z = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\).

5) Circuit diagram (conceptual)

In circuit notation (time flows left to right):

  • Wire 1: \(|\psi\rangle\) → CNOT target → \(H\) → measure → \(m_1\)
  • Wire 2: Bell pair with wire 3 → CNOT control → measure → \(m_2\)
  • Wire 3: at Bob → \(Z^{m_1}\) → \(X^{m_2}\) → \(|\psi\rangle\)

Full circuit conventions: Quantum Circuits. Multi-qubit gates: Multi-Qubit Systems.

No-communication theorem: Before Alice sends \((m_1,m_2)\), Bob's reduced state is the maximally mixed state \(\frac{I}{2}\) — he has zero information about \(|\psi\rangle\).

6) Why this is not faster-than-light

Entanglement alone cannot transmit information. Bob needs the classical bits, which travel at most at the speed of light. The protocol consumes one Bell pair per teleportation.

Teleportation is a primitive in quantum networks and distributed quantum computing, enabling qubit routing between processors.

Resource accounting: Teleporting one qubit costs 1 ebit + 2 cbits. This is optimal for faithful transfer of an arbitrary unknown qubit.

7) Experimental realisations and variants

  • Photonic: Polarisation-entangled photons; first demonstrations in the late 1990s.
  • Ion traps: Deterministic Bell measurements with high fidelity.
  • Continuous-variable: Teleportation of coherent states in optical modes.
  • Port-based teleportation: Reduces correction to local isometries at the cost of more entanglement.

Related: Quantum Channels, Trapped-Ion Systems.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. What three resources does the standard qubit teleportation protocol consume?
  2. Write the initial state \(|\psi\rangle \otimes |\Phi^+\rangle\) explicitly for \(\alpha = 1/\sqrt{2}, \beta = 1/\sqrt{2}\).
  3. Why does Bob apply \(X^{m_2} Z^{m_1}\) after receiving classical bits?
  4. What is Bob's reduced state before he receives \((m_1, m_2)\)?
  5. Explain why teleportation does not violate relativity.
  6. How does teleportation relate to the no-cloning theorem?
Show answers
  1. One shared Bell pair (1 ebit), two classical bits of communication, and local quantum operations (Bell measurement at Alice, Pauli corrections at Bob).
  2. With \(\alpha = \beta = 1/\sqrt{2}\): \(|\psi\rangle \otimes |\Phi^+\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(|0\rangle|00\rangle + |0\rangle|11\rangle + |1\rangle|00\rangle + |1\rangle|11\rangle\bigr) = \frac{1}{2}\bigl(|000\rangle + |011\rangle + |100\rangle + |111\rangle\bigr)\).
  3. Bell measurement leaves Bob's qubit in \(X^{m_2} Z^{m_1}|\psi\rangle\). Classical bits \((m_1, m_2)\) tell Bob which Pauli corrections undo the random \(Z^{m_1} X^{m_2}\) rotation to recover \(|\psi\rangle\).
  4. Before receiving \((m_1, m_2)\), Bob's reduced state is the maximally mixed state \(\rho = \frac{I}{2}\)—he has zero information about \(|\psi\rangle\).
  5. Entanglement alone sends no information. Bob needs classical bits, which travel at most at speed \(c\). The protocol consumes one Bell pair per teleportation but does not enable faster-than-light signalling.
  6. The no-cloning theorem forbids copying an unknown qubit. Teleportation destroys Alice's original qubit while faithfully transferring its state to Bob using entanglement plus classical communication.
