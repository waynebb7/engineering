1) Why the QFT matters

The quantum Fourier transform (QFT) is the quantum analogue of the discrete Fourier transform (DFT). Classically, the FFT computes Fourier coefficients in \(O(N\log N)\) time for \(N = 2^n\) samples. Quantumly, the QFT acts as a unitary on an \(n\)-qubit register in \(O(n^2)\) gates — exponentially faster than naive classical DFT on the full \(2^n\) amplitudes.

  • Core subroutine in Shor's Factoring Algorithm
  • Foundation of quantum phase estimation
  • Inverse QFT (IQFT) extracts hidden periodicity from superposition
Key idea: The QFT does not output classical Fourier coefficients you can read directly — it prepares a quantum state whose measurement statistics encode the phases. Classical post-processing often follows.

Prerequisites: Quantum Gates, Multi-Qubit Systems, Complex Numbers.

2) Definition on computational basis

For \(N = 2^n\), the QFT maps basis state \(|j\rangle\) to:

\[ \mathrm{QFT}_N |j\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} e^{2\pi i j k / N} |k\rangle \]

Equivalently, in binary \(j = j_1 j_2 \cdots j_n\) (MSB first), the QFT can be written as a product of single-qubit Hadamards and controlled phase rotations.

Unitarity

\(\mathrm{QFT}_N^\dagger \mathrm{QFT}_N = I\). The inverse QFT uses negative phases \(e^{-2\pi i jk/N}\).

Classical link

Replacing amplitudes with classical data and measuring everything gives the same phases as a DFT — but that classical route costs \(O(N\log N)\) or worse.

3) Circuit construction

The standard \(n\)-qubit QFT circuit uses:

  • Hadamard \(H\) on each qubit
  • Controlled phase \(R_m\) on target when control is 1, where \(R_m = \mathrm{diag}(1, e^{2\pi i / 2^m})\)
  • Qubits processed in a specific order; swap gates often reverse bit order at the end
Gate count: Roughly \(\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\) controlled phases plus \(n\) Hadamards and \(O(n)\) swaps — polynomial in \(n\), not exponential in \(N = 2^n\).

See Quantum Circuits for how controlled-\(U\) gates compose in larger algorithms.

4) Quantum phase estimation

Given a unitary \(U\) with eigenstate \(|\psi\rangle\) and eigenvalue \(e^{2\pi i \phi}\), phase estimation approximates \(\phi\) using controlled powers of \(U\) and an inverse QFT on an ancilla register.

\[ |\psi\rangle \mapsto e^{2\pi i \phi} |\psi\rangle, \qquad \text{estimate } \phi \text{ to } t \text{ bits with } t \text{ ancilla qubits.} \]

Accuracy scales as \(O(1/2^t)\) in \(\phi\); success probability can be boosted with extra ancillas or repeated runs. Phase estimation is how Shor's algorithm extracts the period of \(f(x) = a^x \bmod N\).

Common mistake: Assuming phase estimation works without preparing an approximate eigenstate. If the input is not in the eigenstate subspace, the readout mixes multiple phases.

5) Period finding and Shor's connection

Shor's algorithm creates a superposition \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{Q}}\sum_x |x\rangle |a^x \bmod N\rangle\), then measures the second register to obtain a periodic sequence in the first. The inverse QFT on the first register concentrates probability on values related to the period \(r\).

Continued fractions: After measuring an approximate phase \(c/2^t\), classical post-processing recovers \(r\) via continued-fraction expansion — the quantum step supplies \(c\); the classical step is cheap.

Full algorithm walkthrough: Shor's Factoring Algorithm. Compare with Grover's Search (quadratic, not exponential speedup).

6) Implementation challenges

  • Controlled rotations: Arbitrary precision phases need decomposition into native gate set
  • Depth: QFT depth is \(O(n^2)\) — costly on NISQ hardware
  • Approximate QFT: Truncate small-angle phases to reduce depth at accuracy cost
  • Measurement: Only bit strings are obtained; many runs may be needed for reliable period extraction
Engineering note: On superconducting platforms, two-qubit gate fidelity and coherence time limit how large an QFT register can be before error dominates.

7) Worked sketch: 3-qubit QFT

For \(n=3\), input \(|j_1 j_2 j_3\rangle\). Apply \(H\) on qubit 1, then controlled \(R_2, R_3\) from qubits 2, 3; repeat pattern on qubit 2 with \(R_2\) from qubit 3; \(H\) on qubit 3; swap qubits 1 and 3.

Output amplitudes peak at \(k\) values where \(k/8 \approx j/8\) (mod 1) in the phase sense — interference from controlled phases implements the \(e^{2\pi i jk/8}\) kernel.

Check: \(\mathrm{QFT}_8 |1\rangle\) produces equal magnitudes on all \(|k\rangle\) with phases \(2\pi k/8\) — a uniform superposition with structured relative phases.
