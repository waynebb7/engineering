1) What makes an algorithm quantum?

A quantum algorithm is a sequence of unitary operations and measurements on qubits that solves a computational problem. Advantage appears when quantum interference, entanglement, or phase estimation reduce resources (time, queries, samples) below classical baselines for the same task.

  • Input: classical data encoded into quantum states (or oracle access)
  • Computation: gates from Quantum Gates composed in Quantum Circuits
  • Output: classical bit strings from Measurement
Key idea: Speedup is problem-specific. There is no single quantum program that accelerates all classical workloads.

2) Oracle and query models

Many algorithms are analysed with a black-box oracle \(O_f\) that implements \(f:\{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}\) or phase oracles \(O_f|x\rangle = (-1)^{f(x)}|x\rangle\). Query complexity counts oracle calls.

Oracles abstract problem structure; real algorithms replace oracles with explicit circuits (e.g. modular arithmetic in Shor's algorithm).

3) Complexity class BQP

BQP (bounded-error quantum polynomial time) is the class of problems solvable by quantum computers in polynomial time with error \(\leq 1/3\). Known containments:

\[ \mathrm{P} \subseteq \mathrm{BQP} \subseteq \mathrm{PSPACE} \]

Whether \(\mathrm{BQP} \supsetneq \mathrm{P}\) is open in full generality, but concrete problems (integer factoring, discrete log) have quantum polynomial algorithms believed super-polynomial classically.

Common mistake: Confusing BQP with "all NP problems." Factoring is in NP∩coNP; NP-complete problems are not known to be in BQP.

4) Exponential-speedup algorithms

Algorithms with exponential separation (under standard assumptions):

  • Shor's algorithm: factoring and discrete logarithm via Quantum Fourier Transform and period finding
  • Simon: finds hidden XOR masks; inspired Shor
  • Quantum phase estimation: eigenvalue phases for unitaries
  • HHL (simplified): linear systems under strict data-access assumptions
Requirement: These need long coherent circuits and fault tolerance at scale — not yet practical for cryptographically large instances on NISQ devices.

5) Quadratic and polynomial speedups

  • Grover: unstructured search — \(O(\sqrt{N})\)
  • Amplitude amplification: generalises Grover for broader success criteria
  • Quantum walk algorithms: some graph problems gain polynomial factors
  • Hamiltonian simulation: simulating quantum dynamics with precision \(\epsilon\) in poly\((\log(1/\epsilon), n)\) time under sparsity assumptions

Quadratic speedups still matter for large search spaces but may be overwhelmed by constant overheads on hardware.

6) Variational and NISQ-era algorithms

Near-term algorithms trade proven asymptotic advantage for shallow circuits and hybrid loops:

  • VQE: ground-state energies
  • QAOA: combinatorial optimisation
  • Quantum machine learning ansätze: heuristic feature maps — advantage debated
Status: These are engineering workflows with problem-dependent utility, not proven exponential speedups like Shor.

7) Choosing an algorithm: decision guide

Problem structure

Periodicity → QFT/Shor; unstructured search → Grover; ground energy → VQE; graph cut → QAOA; dynamics → Hamiltonian simulation.

Hardware era

NISQ: low depth, error mitigation; fault-tolerant era: magic states, error correction, millions of physical qubits.

Also consider data encoding cost: loading classical vectors into quantum states can erase theoretical speedups if not structured (e.g. QRAM assumptions).
