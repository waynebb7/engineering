Quantum Approximate Optimization (QAOA)

Quantum guide. QAOA encodes combinatorial problems into cost Hamiltonians and uses alternating mixer layers to search for high-quality solutions on shallow NISQ circuits.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) Combinatorial optimisation on qubits

Many NP-hard problems — MaxCut, portfolio optimisation, scheduling — can be written as minimising a cost function \(C(z)\) over bit strings \(z \in \{0,1\}^n\). QAOA maps this to finding low-energy states of a cost Hamiltonian \(H_C\) diagonal in the computational basis:

\[ H_C |z\rangle = C(z) |z\rangle, \qquad \min_z C(z) \Leftrightarrow \min \langle H_C \rangle \]

Key idea: QAOA is a variational algorithm like VQE, but the Hamiltonian encodes a combinatorial objective and the ansatz is the structured QAOA circuit rather than chemistry-motivated UCC.

2) The QAOA circuit

Start from uniform superposition \(|+\rangle^{\otimes n}\). Apply \(p\) layers of:

  • Cost unitary: \(U_C(\gamma_\ell) = e^{-i \gamma_\ell H_C}\)
  • Mixer unitary: \(U_M(\beta_\ell) = e^{-i \beta_\ell H_M}\), typically \(H_M = \sum_j X_j\)

\[ |\boldsymbol{\gamma}, \boldsymbol{\beta}\rangle = \prod_{\ell=1}^{p} e^{-i \beta_\ell H_M} e^{-i \gamma_\ell H_C} |+\rangle^{\otimes n} \]

Parameters \((\boldsymbol{\gamma}, \boldsymbol{\beta})\) are optimised classically to minimise \(\langle H_C \rangle\). Final measurement in the \(Z\) basis yields a candidate bit string \(z\).

Circuit patterns: Quantum Circuits, Quantum Gates.

3) MaxCut example

For an undirected graph \(G = (V,E)\), MaxCut partitions vertices to maximise edges between partitions. Assign \(z_i \in \{0,1\}\) per vertex; cut value:

\[ C(z) = \sum_{(i,j) \in E} \frac{1 - z_i z_j}{2} \]

This maps to \(H_C = \sum_{(i,j) \in E} \frac{1}{2}(I - Z_i Z_j)\) (up to constants). Each edge term is implementable with \(R_{ZZ}\) interactions on connected qubits.

Depth vs \(p\): Larger \(p\) can improve approximation ratios but increases circuit depth — a central trade-off on NISQ hardware.

4) Approximation guarantees and limits

For MaxCut at \(p=1\), QAOA achieves a known approximation ratio on 3-regular graphs. As \(p \to \infty\), QAOA can reach the optimum if parameters are chosen perfectly — but optimising parameters is hard classically.

  • \(p=1\): analytically tractable on some graph families
  • Large \(p\): approaches adiabatic quantum computation under certain limits
  • No proven polynomial speedup over classical heuristics for general instances yet
Common mistake: Assuming QAOA always beats classical solvers. On many benchmarks, classical metaheuristics still win unless problem structure favours the quantum ansatz.

5) Mixer Hamiltonians and constraints

Standard mixer \(H_M = \sum_j X_j\) preserves uniform superposition over all bit strings — suited to unconstrained problems. Constrained QAOA replaces the mixer with one that preserves feasible subspaces (e.g. fixed Hamming weight for cardinality constraints).

XY mixer

Used when preserving Hamming weight; encodes one-hot constraints in assignment problems.

Penalty methods

Add penalty terms to \(H_C\) for violated constraints; simpler but may flatten energy landscape.

6) Implementation on hardware

  • Problem mapping: embed graph on qubit connectivity (minor embedding on superconducting chips)
  • Parameter optimisation: warm-start from classical solutions or smaller \(p\)
  • Shot budgets: estimate \(\langle H_C \rangle\) with grouped Pauli measurements
  • Error mitigation: ZNE and readout correction before trusting cut values
Engineering: Native \(ZZ\) or \(XX\) interactions reduce depth compared to decomposing into many CNOTs — platform choice matters for QAOA viability.

7) Relation to adiabatic computing

Adiabatic quantum computation evolves from \(H_M\) to \(H_C\) slowly. QAOA with large \(p\) and tuned \((\boldsymbol{\gamma}, \boldsymbol{\beta})\) can mimic this path in discrete Trotterised steps. The adiabatic theorem motivates why low-energy states of \(H_C\) may be reachable, but finite \(p\) and noise break ideal adiabaticity.
