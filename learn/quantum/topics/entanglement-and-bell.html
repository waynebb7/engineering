1) Separable vs entangled states

A pure state of two subsystems \(A\) and \(B\) is separable (product) if \(|\psi\rangle_{AB} = |\phi\rangle_A \otimes |\chi\rangle_B\). Otherwise it is entangled — joint information is not stored in either subsystem alone.

Example (Bell state): \(|\Phi^+\rangle = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle + |11\rangle)\) — measuring \(A\) instantly fixes the statistics of \(B\) even when spatially separated (no signalling of information).

Tensor products: Dirac Notation; spin singlet: Spin and Pauli Matrices.

2) EPR and local realism

Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (1935) argued that if quantum mechanics is complete, it must be non-local or incomplete. Local realism assumes: (i) measurement outcomes are predetermined by hidden variables \(\lambda\); (ii) settings on \(A\) cannot affect outcomes on \(B\) (locality).

Entangled predictions violate intuitions built on classical factorisation of joint probabilities.

3) Bell's theorem

John Bell (1964) showed any local hidden-variable theory satisfies an inequality constraining correlations. For spin measurements on entangled pairs, the CHSH inequality (Clauser–Horne–Shimony–Holt) states:

\[|S| = |E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| \leq 2\]

where \(E(a,b)\) is the correlation of outcomes when Alice measures along \(a\) and Bob along \(b\). Quantum mechanics predicts \(|S| \leq 2\sqrt{2}\) (Tsirelson bound) — violations up to \(2\sqrt{2}\).

4) Quantum prediction for singlet

For the singlet \(|\Psi^-\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|01\rangle - |10\rangle)\), measuring spins along directions \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\):

\[E(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) = -\cos\theta_{ab}\]

Choosing appropriate angles (e.g. \(a=0°, a'=45°, b=22.5°, b'=67.5°\)) gives \(S = 2\sqrt{2}\), maximally violating CHSH.

Measurement: Outcomes are random individually but jointly correlated — see Superposition and Measurement.

5) Experimental tests

Aspect (1980s) and later loophole-free experiments with photons, ions, and superconducting qubits confirm Bell violations. Key experimental challenges: locality (space-like separation), detection efficiency (fair sampling), and freedom-of-choice (setting independence).

  • Photon polarisation entanglement from SPDC sources
  • Time-bin and frequency-bin encodings in fibre QKD
  • Superconducting qubit Bell tests on chip

6) Entanglement as a resource

Entanglement enables protocols impossible classically:

  • Quantum teleportation — transfer unknown states using shared entanglement + classical bits
  • Superdense coding — send two classical bits per qubit with shared ebit
  • Device-independent QKD — security from Bell violation alone
  • Error correction — entanglement structure of stabiliser codes

Mixed and noisy entanglement: Density Matrices, Quantum Channels.

7) No signalling theorem

Although Bell correlations are non-local in the sense of violating hidden-variable locality, they cannot transmit controllable information faster than light. Marginal probabilities at \(B\) are independent of Alice's measurement choice: \(\mathrm{Tr}_A(\rho_{AB}) = \rho_B\).

Common mistake: "Entanglement = faster-than-light communication." Correlations are stronger than classical, but outcome bits remain random without a classical side channel.
