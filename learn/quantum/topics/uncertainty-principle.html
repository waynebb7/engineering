1) What uncertainty means in QM

The uncertainty \(\Delta A\) of observable \(A\) in state \(|\psi\rangle\) is the standard deviation of measurement outcomes:

\[(\Delta A)^2 = \langle A^2\rangle - \langle A\rangle^2\]

It is not merely experimental imprecision — for incompatible observables, no quantum state can make both uncertainties arbitrarily small simultaneously.

Not a measurement flaw alone: Uncertainty is a property of the state, though measurement can disturb subsequent statistics — see Superposition and Measurement.

2) Robertson–Schrödinger relation

For any two Hermitian operators \(A\) and \(B\):

\[\Delta A\,\Delta B \geq \frac{1}{2}\left|\langle[A,B]\rangle\right|\]

The commutator \([A,B] = AB - BA\) quantifies incompatibility. When \([A,B] = 0\), a common eigenbasis exists and both can have zero uncertainty simultaneously.

3) Position–momentum uncertainty

The canonical commutation relation \([\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar\) gives the famous Heisenberg form:

\[\Delta x\,\Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}\]

A localised wave packet (small \(\Delta x\)) requires many momentum components (large \(\Delta p\)) — the wave origin of uncertainty from Wave–Particle Duality.

Gaussian minimum: A Gaussian wave packet saturates \(\Delta x\,\Delta p = \hbar/2\).

4) Energy–time uncertainty

A widely used (but subtly interpreted) relation:

\[\Delta E\,\Delta t \gtrsim \frac{\hbar}{2}\]

\(\Delta t\) is not an operator in standard QM; interpretations include: lifetime of a resonance (width \(\Gamma \sim \hbar/\tau\)), or time to distinguish energy levels by evolution. Short-lived states have broad energy distributions.

Enables Quantum Tunneling through barriers with ill-defined energy.

5) Spin uncertainty example

Pauli matrices satisfy \([\sigma_x,\sigma_z] = 2i\sigma_y\). For spin along distinct axes:

\[\Delta S_x\,\Delta S_z \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}|\langle S_y\rangle|\]

In eigenstate \(|{\uparrow z}\rangle\), \(\langle S_y\rangle = 0\) so the bound is trivially satisfied, but \(\Delta S_x = \hbar/2\) and \(\Delta S_z = 0\) — you cannot prepare a state with both \(\Delta S_x\) and \(\Delta S_z\) arbitrarily small unless \(\langle S_y\rangle \neq 0\).

Details: Spin and Pauli Matrices.

6) Harmonic oscillator ground state

The ground state \(|0\rangle\) of \(\hat{H} = \hat{p}^2/2m + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\hat{x}^2\) has \(\Delta x = \sqrt{\hbar/2m\omega}\) and \(\Delta p = \sqrt{\hbar m\omega/2}\), giving \(\Delta x\,\Delta p = \hbar/2\). Zero-point energy \(E_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\hbar\omega\) follows from non-zero \(\Delta x\) even at minimum energy.

7) Engineering implications

  • Quantum metrology: shot-noise limits and squeezed states trade one uncertainty for another
  • Electron microscopes: higher momentum (shorter \(\lambda\)) can localize better but increase energy spread
  • Superconducting qubits: charge–phase uncertainty constrains device parameters
  • Security proofs: QKD protocols exploit measurement disturbance tied to non-commutation
