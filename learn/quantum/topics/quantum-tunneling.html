1) Classical vs quantum barriers

Classically, a particle with energy \(E\) cannot enter a region where potential \(V(x) > E\) — kinetic energy would be negative. Quantum mechanically, the wave function can be non-zero inside and beyond the barrier, allowing non-zero transmission probability.

Wave origin: Tunnelling is a direct consequence of the wave nature of matter — Wave–Particle Duality.

2) Rectangular barrier: qualitative picture

Consider a barrier \(V_0\) for \(0 < x < a\), particle energy \(E < V_0\). Inside the barrier the solution is evanescent (decaying exponential), not zero:

\[\psi(x) \propto e^{-\kappa x}, \quad \kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}}{\hbar}\]

Matching boundary conditions at \(x=0\) and \(x=a\) yields reflected and transmitted amplitudes. Transmission \(T\) can be exponentially small but non-zero.

3) WKB approximation

For slowly varying barriers, the Wentzel–Kramers–Brillouin (WKB) method gives:

\[T \approx \exp\!\left(-2\int_{x_1}^{x_2}\kappa(x)\,dx\right)\]

where \(x_1, x_2\) are classical turning points (\(E = V(x)\)). Wider or higher barriers suppress \(T\) exponentially — explains extreme sensitivity of tunnelling current to barrier width in STM.

4) Resonant tunnelling

Double-barrier structures (quantum wells) can exhibit resonances when bound-state energies in the well align with incident energy — transmission approaches unity despite individual barriers being classically opaque.

RTD devices

Resonant tunnelling diodes exploit sharp peaks in \(T(E)\) for high-speed switching.

Alpha decay

Nuclear \(\alpha\) particles tunnel through the Coulomb barrier — Gamow factor.

5) Scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM)

A sharp tip held nanometres above a conductive sample measures tunnelling current \(I \propto T \propto e^{-2\kappa d}\). Adjusting tip height maps surface topography at atomic resolution. Variants include scanning tunnelling spectroscopy (STS) for local density of states.

Engineering note: Vacuum barrier height \(\sim\) few eV; \(\kappa \sim 1\,\mathrm{nm}^{-1}\), so current changes by orders of magnitude per ångström of tip motion.

6) Josephson junctions and superconductivity

Cooper pairs tunnel between superconductors separated by a thin insulator (Josephson effect). Critical current \(I_c\) and phase dynamics underpin SQUID magnetometers and superconducting qubits. Tunnelling here is paired-electron transport, not single-particle STM.

Energy–time considerations: Uncertainty Principle.

7) Tunnelling time and interpretation

How long does tunnelling take? Definitions include phase time, dwell time, and Larmor clock — experiments with ultracold atoms and attosecond pulses continue to refine answers. Tunnelling does not require violating relativity; group delays can be sub-femtosecond without superluminal signalling.

Common mistake: Equating tunnelling with "the particle goes through the forbidden region at a classical speed." The process is non-classical; trajectory language is misleading.
