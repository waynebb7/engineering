Wave–Particle Duality

Quantum guide. Light and matter exhibit both wave-like interference and particle-like localisation — the starting point for quantum theory.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) The classical puzzle

In classical physics, waves and particles are distinct: waves spread and interfere; particles have definite positions and follow trajectories. Quantum experiments show that both descriptions are needed for light and matter, depending on what is measured.

  • Wave behaviour: interference, diffraction, superposition of amplitudes
  • Particle behaviour: discrete detection events, quantised energy and momentum
  • Neither picture alone captures the full quantum story
Key idea: Duality is not "sometimes a wave, sometimes a particle" in a naive sense — it is that quantum objects are described by a wave function whose squared magnitude gives detection probabilities. See Superposition and Measurement.

2) Light: photons and interference

Young's double-slit experiment shows bright and dark fringes — classic wave interference. Yet when intensity is low enough, light arrives as individual photon clicks on a detector, one at a time.

Remarkable fact: Even when photons pass one at a time, the fringe pattern builds up — each photon interferes with itself. The wave aspect governs probabilities; the particle aspect is what you observe at a screen.

The wavelength of light is \(\lambda = h/p\) (de Broglie relation), linking wave and particle momentum.

3) Matter waves: de Broglie hypothesis

Louis de Broglie proposed that any particle with momentum \(p\) has an associated wavelength:

\[\lambda = \frac{h}{p}\]

where \(h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm{J\,s}\) is Planck's constant. Electrons, neutrons, and atoms have been diffracted through crystals — direct evidence that matter is wave-like.

Electron diffraction

Accelerated electrons produce ring patterns on thin films — same physics as X-ray crystallography but with massive particles.

Scale note

Everyday objects have \(\lambda\) so small that wave effects are unobservable; quantum behaviour dominates at atomic scales.

4) The wave function

Quantum mechanics replaces classical trajectories with a wave function \(\psi(x,t)\) (or \(\psi(\mathbf{r},t)\) in 3D). For a single particle:

  • \(\psi\) is a complex-valued amplitude, not a directly measurable field
  • Probability density: \(P(x,t) = |\psi(x,t)|^2\)
  • Superposition: if \(\psi = c_1\psi_1 + c_2\psi_2\), interference terms appear in \(|\psi|^2\)
Notation: State vectors in Dirac Notation generalise \(\psi\) to abstract Hilbert-space kets \(|\psi\rangle\).

5) Complementarity and which-path information

Bohr's complementarity principle: experiments can reveal wave-like or particle-like aspects, but not both with arbitrary precision in the same setup.

If you determine which slit a photon passed through (which-path information), interference fringes disappear. Erasing that information can restore fringes — a striking demonstration of how measurement shapes observed behaviour.

Common mistake: Thinking the wave function is a physical wave in space like a water wave. It is a probability amplitude; the "wave" in duality refers to interference of amplitudes, not a literal medium.

6) Energy quantisation connection

Planck's hypothesis \(E = h\nu\) and Einstein's photon \(E = hf\) tie particle energy to wave frequency. Bound states (e.g. electrons in atoms) have discrete energies because wave functions must fit standing-wave conditions — particle quantisation emerges from wave boundary conditions.

Example: A particle in a 1D infinite well of width \(L\) has energies \(E_n = \dfrac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2}\) — integer \(n\) from wave nodes at the walls.

Formal rules: Postulates of Quantum Mechanics.

7) Engineering and modern applications

  • Electron microscopy: shorter \(\lambda\) than visible light → higher resolution
  • Quantum tunnelling devices: wave nature allows barrier penetration — Quantum Tunneling
  • Atom interferometry: precision gravimeters and inertial sensors
  • Photonics: design waveguides and cavities using wave interference
