1) The factoring problem

Given composite integer \(N\), find non-trivial factors. Classically, the best known algorithms (general number field sieve) run in sub-exponential time. Shor (1994) solves factoring in \(O((\log N)^3)\) quantum gates — polynomial in the bit length.

Key idea: Factoring reduces to period finding (order finding) in modular arithmetic, which quantum phase estimation solves efficiently.

Prerequisite: Quantum Circuits, Cryptography.

2) Reduction: factoring to order finding

Pick random \(a < N\) with \(\gcd(a, N) = 1\). The order \(r\) is the smallest positive integer with: \[ a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N} \]

If \(r\) is even and \(a^{r/2} \not\equiv -1 \pmod{N}\), then: \[ \gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N) \] gives non-trivial factors of \(N\) with high probability.

Lucky case: If \(\gcd(a, N) > 1\) initially, you already found a factor — no quantum step needed.

3) Quantum period finding

The function \(f(x) = a^x \bmod N\) is periodic with period \(r\). Create superposition over \(x\): \[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{Q}}\sum_{x=0}^{Q-1}|x\rangle|a^x \bmod N\rangle \] Measuring the second register collapses to states where \(x\) differs by multiples of \(r\).

Apply the inverse Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) to the first register and measure to extract frequency information revealing \(r\).

QFT: Quantum Fourier Transform — \(O(n^2)\) gates for \(n\) qubits.

4) The Quantum Fourier Transform

The QFT on \(n\) qubits maps basis state \(|j\rangle\) to: \[ \mathrm{QFT}|j\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_{k=0}^{2^n-1} e^{2\pi i jk / 2^n}|k\rangle \]

Measuring the output gives \(k\) with probability related to the period \(r\): \[ \frac{k}{2^n} \approx \frac{c}{r} \quad \text{for integer } c \]

Continued fractions: Classical post-processing extracts \(r\) from the measured fraction \(k/2^n\).

5) Shor's circuit overview

  1. Choose \(Q = 2^q\) with \(N^2 \leq Q < 2N^2\) (first register size)
  2. Prepare \(|0\rangle^{\otimes q}|1\rangle^{\otimes t}\) where \(t = \lceil\log_2 N\rceil\)
  3. Apply \(H^{\otimes q}\) to first register
  4. Controlled modular exponentiation: \(|x\rangle|1\rangle \to |x\rangle|a^x \bmod N\rangle\)
  5. Apply \(\mathrm{QFT}^{-1}\) to first register
  6. Measure first register; continued fractions yield candidate \(r\)
  7. Classical post-processing: compute \(\gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N)\)

Modular arithmetic circuits: largest resource cost in fault-tolerant implementations.

6) Complexity and resources

Gate complexity: \(O((\log N)^3)\) for the full algorithm with efficient modular exponentiation.

Qubits: Roughly \(2\log N\) for the main registers; fault-tolerant implementations need millions of physical qubits for cryptographically relevant \(N\) (2048-bit RSA).

Not yet practical: No quantum computer has factored RSA-scale integers. Record demonstrations factor small numbers (e.g. 21 = 3 × 7).

Fault-Tolerant Computing, Post-Quantum Cryptography.

7) Impact on cryptography

RSA and Diffie–Hellman security relies on the hardness of factoring and discrete logarithms. Shor breaks both in polynomial quantum time.

  • RSA: Public key \(N = pq\) — factoring \(N\) recovers private key.
  • Discrete log: Shor generalises to \(\mathbb{Z}_p^*\) and elliptic curve groups.
  • Response: NIST post-quantum standards (CRYSTALS-Kyber, Dilithium) use lattice problems.
Symmetric crypto: Grover halves effective key length — AES-256 remains safe; AES-128 may not.
