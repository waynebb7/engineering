1) Feynman's original insight

Richard Feynman (1982) argued that quantum systems are hard to simulate classically because an \(N\)-particle wavefunction lives in a \(2^N\)-dimensional Hilbert space. A quantum computer, being quantum itself, can efficiently simulate other quantum systems — the original motivation for quantum computing.

  • Classical cost scales exponentially with particle number for exact simulation.
  • Quantum simulation cost scales polynomially in \(N\) for many physically relevant Hamiltonians.
  • Applications: molecular electronic structure, materials, nuclear physics, lattice gauge theories.
  • Near-term focus: small molecules (H2, LiH, BeH2) on NISQ devices.
Key idea: Encode the simulated system's state on qubits, evolve under a programmed Hamiltonian, and measure observables — chemistry, condensed matter, and high-energy physics on a chip.

Prerequisites: Postulates of Quantum Mechanics, Quantum Circuits.

2) Hamiltonian simulation problem

Given a Hamiltonian \(H = \sum_{j=1}^{L} h_j\) (sum of local terms) and evolution time \(t\), the goal is to implement the unitary \(U = e^{-iHt}\) on qubits. The Trotter–Suzuki formula approximates:

\(e^{-iHt} \approx \Bigl[\prod_{j} e^{-i h_j t/r}\Bigr]^r + O(t^2/r)\)

Higher-order Trotter formulas reduce error per step. The number of Trotter steps \(r\) needed for precision \(\epsilon\) depends on commutator norms \(\|[h_j, h_k]\|\).

Gate complexity: Simulating \(H\) with \(L\) local terms to precision \(\epsilon\) typically requires \(O(L \cdot t \cdot \text{polylog}(1/\epsilon))\) gates — efficient when \(L\) is polynomial in system size.

4) Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)

VQE finds ground-state energies of molecular Hamiltonians \(H\) using a hybrid quantum–classical loop. A parametrized ansatz state \(|\psi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\rangle = U(\boldsymbol{\theta})|0\rangle\) prepares trial states; the energy expectation

\(E(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \langle\psi(\boldsymbol{\theta})|H|\psi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\rangle\)

is measured and minimised classically. Common ansätze: unitary coupled cluster (UCC), hardware-efficient layered circuits.

Worked example (H2): The 4-qubit STO-3G Hamiltonian has ground-state energy \(\sim -1.137\) Ha at equilibrium bond length. VQE on superconducting hardware has achieved chemical accuracy (\(\sim 1.6\) mHa) for small molecules.

Related: Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE).

5) Lattice models and condensed matter

Quantum simulators target lattice Hamiltonians central to condensed matter physics:

  • Transverse-field Ising model: \(H = -J\sum_{\langle i,j\rangle} Z_i Z_j - h\sum_i X_i\) — quantum phase transitions, simplest testbed.
  • Hubbard model: \(H = -t\sum_{\langle i,j\rangle,\sigma} c_{i\sigma}^\dagger c_{j\sigma} + U\sum_i n_{i\uparrow}n_{i\downarrow}\) — high-temperature superconductivity, Mott insulators.
  • Heisenberg model: magnetic ordering and spin liquids.

Analog simulators (trapped ions, Rydberg atom arrays, superconducting qubit lattices) directly engineer interaction graphs, while digital simulators use Trotterised gate sequences.

Related: Trapped-Ion Quantum Systems, Condensed Matter Theory.

6) QAOA for combinatorial optimisation

The Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm encodes combinatorial problems in a cost Hamiltonian \(H_C\) and applies alternating evolution under \(H_C\) and a mixer \(H_M\):

\(|\gamma,\boldsymbol{\beta}\rangle = \prod_{p=1}^{P} e^{-i\beta_p H_M}\,e^{-i\gamma_p H_C}\,|+\rangle^{\otimes n}\)

Parameters \(\gamma_p, \beta_p\) are optimised classically. QAOA applies to MaxCut, portfolio optimisation, and scheduling — overlapping with simulation when cost Hamiltonians are Ising models.

Related: QAOA.

7) Measurement and error mitigation

Molecular observables require measuring Pauli strings \(P\) in the Hamiltonian decomposition \(H = \sum_P c_P P\). Each term is measured by rotating into the measurement basis and sampling.

  • Grouped measurements: commuting Pauli terms measured simultaneously reduce shot count.
  • Zero-noise extrapolation (ZNE): run at amplified noise levels and extrapolate to zero.
  • Probabilistic error cancellation (PEC): quasi-probability decomposition of noise channels.
  • Symmetry verification: enforce particle number, spin, and point-group symmetries.
NISQ reality: Without error correction, simulation depth is limited. Chemical accuracy for industrially relevant molecules (e.g. catalysts, drug candidates) likely requires fault-tolerant hardware.

Related: NISQ Era Computing, Quantum Error Correction.

8) Classical benchmarks and advantage

Classical methods

  • Density functional theory (DFT)
  • Coupled cluster (CCSD(T)) — gold standard
  • Tensor network methods (DMRG, PEPS)
  • Quantum Monte Carlo

Quantum advantage targets

  • FeMoco nitrogenase active site
  • High-Tc cuprate phase diagram
  • Lattice gauge theory (QCD at density)
  • Catalysis reaction pathways

Quantum advantage in simulation means solving problems where classical methods require exponential resources. No definitive demonstration exists yet, but the problem class is widely regarded as the most credible path to useful quantum computation.
