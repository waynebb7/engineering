Eigenvectors

Undergraduate guide. Finding eigenvectors, eigenspaces, diagonalisation, and geometric meaning.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Definition of eigenvectors

A non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of an \(n \times n\) matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) if there exists a scalar \(\lambda\) (the eigenvalue) such that: \[ \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \] The vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is only scaled by \(\mathbf{A}\), not rotated or sheared — its direction is invariant under the linear map.

Key idea: Eigenvectors are the "natural axes" of a linear transformation. Any vector not in an eigenspace is both stretched and redirected.

Eigenvalues are found from the characteristic equation — see Eigenvalues. This page focuses on the vectors themselves and their geometry.

2) Finding eigenvectors

For each eigenvalue \(\lambda\), solve the homogeneous system: \[ (\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \] by row reduction. Non-zero solutions are eigenvectors (any non-zero scalar multiple is also an eigenvector).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\), eigenvalues \(\lambda = 2\) and \(\lambda = 5\).

For \(\lambda = 2\): \((\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) gives \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\), so \(2x + y = 0\). Eigenvector: \(\mathbf{v} = t(-1, 2)^T\), e.g. \((-1, 2)^T\).

For \(\lambda = 5\): \(\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) gives \(-x + y = 0\). Eigenvector: \((1, 1)^T\).
Common mistake: Forgetting to exclude \(\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). The zero vector is never an eigenvector by definition.

3) Eigenspaces

The eigenspace \(E_\lambda\) for eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is the set of all eigenvectors together with \(\mathbf{0}\): \[ E_\lambda = \ker(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = \{\mathbf{v} : \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\} \] \(E_\lambda\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) (or \(\mathbb{C}^n\)). Its dimension is the geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda\).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\):
  • \(E_1 = \text{span}\{(1,0,0)^T, (0,1,0)^T\}\) — the \(xy\)-plane, dimension 2
  • \(E_2 = \text{span}\{(0,0,1)^T\}\) — the \(z\)-axis, dimension 1

Eigenvectors from different eigenspaces are automatically linearly independent.

4) Geometric meaning

A linear map \(T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2\) with matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) acts on each eigenvector by stretching or reversing it by factor \(|\lambda|\) (and reversing direction if \(\lambda < 0\)).

Stretching

If \(\lambda = 3\), the eigenvector triples in length but keeps its line.

Rotation (no real eigenvectors)

90° rotation has no real eigenvectors — no line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) is preserved.

Worked example: Projection onto the \(x\)-axis: \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\). Eigenvalues \(1\) and \(0\). Eigenvector \((1,0)^T\) for \(\lambda = 1\) stays fixed; \((0,1)^T\) for \(\lambda = 0\) is collapsed to the origin.

In 3D, eigenvectors of symmetric matrices (e.g. inertia or stress tensors) give principal axes — see Tensors.

5) Diagonalisation procedure

If \(\mathbf{A}\) has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors, form: \[ \mathbf{P} = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \mathbf{v}_2 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_n], \quad \mathbf{D} = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n) \] Then \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\), so \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\).

Worked example: Using \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) from section 2: \[ \mathbf{P} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{D} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \] Verify: \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\). Columns of \(\mathbf{P}\) are eigenvectors; \(\mathbf{D}\) lists matching eigenvalues on the diagonal.
Exam tip: Order eigenvalues in \(\mathbf{D}\) to match the column order of eigenvectors in \(\mathbf{P}\).

6) Powers and repeated application

When \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) with diagonal \(\mathbf{D}\): \[ \mathbf{A}^k = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}^k\mathbf{P}^{-1} \] and \(\mathbf{D}^k = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^k, \ldots, \lambda_n^k)\). This simplifies computing powers, matrix exponentials \(e^{\mathbf{A}t}\), and long-term behaviour of dynamical systems.

Worked example: For the diagonalisable matrix above, \(\mathbf{A}^2 = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}^2\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) with \(\mathbf{D}^2 = \text{diag}(4, 25)\). Repeated application scales each eigenvector direction by \(\lambda_i^k\) — the dominant eigenvalue (largest \(|\lambda|\)) governs long-term growth.

When diagonalisation fails, use Jordan Normal Form and generalised eigenvectors.

7) Orthogonal diagonalisation

For a real symmetric matrix \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^T\), choose eigenvectors that are mutually orthogonal. Normalise to unit length and set \(\mathbf{Q}\) with these columns: \[ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{Q}^T, \quad \mathbf{Q}^T\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I} \] This is the spectral decomposition.

Worked example: \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) has eigenvectors \(\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,-1)^T\) (\(\lambda = 1\)) and \(\mathbf{v}_2 = (1,1)^T\) (\(\lambda = 3\)). These are orthogonal. Unit vectors: \(\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)^T\), \(\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)^T\).

Orthogonal eigenvectors arise in quadratic forms, principal component analysis, and normal modes — the mathematical foundation is general; engineering uses are in a separate page.
