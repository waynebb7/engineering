Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture

Research guide. Elliptic curves, L-functions, ranks and torsion, and the Clay Millennium Problem connecting arithmetic to analytic invariants.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Elliptic curves over \(\mathbb{Q}\)

An elliptic curve over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is a smooth projective curve of genus 1 with a specified rational point (the identity), given by a Weierstrass equation \[ E : y^2 = x^3 + ax + b, \quad a, b \in \mathbb{Q}, \quad \Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27b^2) \neq 0. \] The set of rational points \(E(\mathbb{Q})\) forms a finitely generated abelian group (Mordell–Weil theorem): \[ E(\mathbb{Q}) \cong E(\mathbb{Q})_{\mathrm{tors}} \oplus \mathbb{Z}^r, \] where \(r = \mathrm{rank}(E)\) is the Mordell–Weil rank and \(E(\mathbb{Q})_{\mathrm{tors}}\) is the finite torsion subgroup.

Worked example: For \(E : y^2 = x^3 - x\), \(E(\mathbb{Q})_{\mathrm{tors}} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}\) and \(r = 0\). For \(y^2 = x^3 - 2x\), the rank is 1 with generator \((-1, 1)\).

Prerequisite: Algebraic Number Theory, Algebraic Geometry, and Complex Analysis.

2) The L-function of an elliptic curve

For an elliptic curve \(E/\mathbb{Q}\) with good reduction outside a finite set of primes \(S\), define the local Euler factors: \[ a_p = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p) \quad \text{(good } p \text{)}, \] and appropriate polynomials \(L_p(s)\) at bad primes. The Hasse–Weil L-function is \[ L(E, s) = \prod_{p} L_p(p^{-s})^{-1}, \] convergent for \(\operatorname{Re}(s) > \tfrac{3}{2}\) by the Hasse bound \(|a_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}\).

Modularity (Wiles et al.): Every elliptic curve over \(\mathbb{Q}\) is modular: \(L(E, s)\) equals the L-function of a weight-2 newform \(f\) of level \(N\). This implies analytic continuation to \(\mathbb{C}\) and a functional equation relating \(s\) and \(2 - s\).

The root number \(w_E = \pm 1\) in the functional equation detects the sign of the leading Taylor coefficient at \(s = 1\).

3) Statement of the BSD Conjecture

Let \(r = \mathrm{rank}(E)\), \(L(E, s)\) the completed L-function \(\Lambda(E, s)\), and \(\Omega_E\) the real period. Define the regulator \(R_E\) (determinant of the Néron–Tate height pairing on a \(\mathbb{Z}\)-basis of the free part), Tamagawa numbers \(c_p\), and Sha \(\Sha(E)\) (conjecturally finite).

Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer Conjecture: \[ \operatorname{ord}_{s=1} L(E, s) = r, \] and the leading coefficient satisfies \[ \lim_{s \to 1} \frac{L(E, s)}{(s-1)^r} = \frac{\Omega_E \cdot R_E \cdot \prod c_p \cdot |\Sha|}{|E(\mathbb{Q})_{\mathrm{tors}}|^2} \cdot \#\text{(conjectural terms)}. \]

Informally: the analytic rank (order of vanishing of \(L(E,s)\) at \(s = 1\)) equals the algebraic rank (Mordell–Weil rank). The full formula predicts the leading Taylor coefficient in terms of arithmetic invariants.

4) Weak BSD and strong BSD

Weak BSD

\(\operatorname{ord}_{s=1} L(E, s) = r\). This is the rank equality alone. Known in many cases via Gross–Zagier, Kolyvagin (rank 0 and 1), and subsequent work on Heegner points.

Strong BSD

The full leading-coefficient formula including \(\Sha\), regulator, and Tamagawa products. Open in general; implies weak BSD.

Gross–Zagier formula: For modular \(E\) and a suitable Heegner point \(P\), the height \(\hat{h}(P)\) equals the derivative \(L'(E, 1)\) (up to explicit factors) when \(r = 1\). This was the first rigorous bridge between analytic and algebraic ranks.

Rank 0: \(L(E, 1) \neq 0 \Rightarrow r = 0\) Kolyvagin (1988)

5) Heegner points and Iwasawa theory

Heegner points are special points on \(E\) defined over imaginary quadratic fields, constructed from CM theory. The Gross–Zagier formula and Kolyvagin's Euler system relate their heights to L-function derivatives.

Iwasawa theory studies the growth of Selmer groups in \(\mathbb{Z}_p\)-extensions. The main conjecture of Iwasawa theory for elliptic curves (proved in many cases by Skinner–Urban, Wan, and others) relates \(p\)-adic L-functions to Selmer groups and implies BSD \(p\)-adic statements.

Key idea: Euler systems (Heegner points, Kato's elements) provide algebraic input that matches L-function data. Proving BSD for all ranks requires extending these constructions or finding new ones.
Sha ambiguity: The Tate–Shafarevich group \(\Sha(E)\) measures failure of local-to-global principle for principal homogeneous spaces. Its finiteness is itself conjectural and is a barrier to proving strong BSD.

6) Computational verification and status

BSD has been verified numerically for thousands of curves:

  • Cremona's tables (all curves of conductor \(\leq 500{,}000\)).
  • Rank equality checked where analytic rank can be computed.
  • Leading coefficients match predicted formulas when \(\Sha\) is trivial and regulators are computable.
Status (2025): Weak BSD is known for rank 0 and 1 (under standard conjectures on Sha in some formulations). Ranks \(\geq 2\) remain open. The BSD Conjecture is a Clay Millennium Prize Problem with a \$1M bounty.

Cryptography link

Elliptic curves over finite fields underpin Cryptography (ECDSA, ECDH). BSD over \(\mathbb{Q}\) does not directly affect discrete log hardness, but the same curves are studied.

Rational points

BSD predicts when \(E(\mathbb{Q})\) is infinite: iff \(L(E, s)\) vanishes at \(s = 1\). Finding generators remains computationally hard for high rank.

7) Research connections

Langlands program

Modularity of elliptic curves is a case of the Langlands correspondence. L-functions connect to Automorphic Forms.

Riemann Hypothesis analogy

Zeros of L-functions and the Riemann Hypothesis share machinery with BSD. Generalized RH influences error terms in rank computations.

Related: Algebraic Number Theory, Millennium Prize Problems, Cryptography.
