Game Theory

1) Normal-form games and best responses

A normal-form game has players \(N = \{1, \ldots, n\}\), strategy sets \(S_i\), and payoff functions \(u_i : S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n \to \mathbb{R}\). Player \(i\) chooses \(s_i \in S_i\) simultaneously.

Best response: \(BR_i(s_{-i}) = \arg\max_{s_i \in S_i} u_i(s_i, s_{-i})\), where \(s_{-i}\) denotes opponents' strategy profile.
Prisoner's dilemma (2-player): Payoff matrix (row player, column player): \[ \begin{array}{c|cc} & C & D \\ \hline C & (3,3) & (0,5) \\ D & (5,0) & (1,1) \end{array} \] Mutual defection \((D,D)\) is the unique Nash equilibrium, though \((C,C)\) Pareto-dominates it.

Prerequisite: Optimization, Statistics (for mixed strategies), and Combinatorics.

2) Dominance and Nash equilibrium

Strategy \(s_i\) is strictly dominated if \(\exists\, s_i'\) with \(u_i(s_i', s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i})\) for all \(s_{-i}\). Iterated elimination of dominated strategies (IESDS) refines predictions.

Nash equilibrium: A profile \(s^* = (s_1^*, \ldots, s_n^*)\) where each \(s_i^* \in BR_i(s_{-i}^*)\). No player can unilaterally improve payoff.
Nash existence (finite games): Every finite game has at least one mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium (Nash, 1950). Pure-strategy equilibria need not exist (matching pennies).
Common mistake: Nash equilibrium does not imply Pareto optimality. Equilibria can be collectively suboptimal (Prisoner's Dilemma, tragedy of the commons).

3) Mixed strategies and von Neumann's minimax theorem

A mixed strategy for player \(i\) is a probability distribution \(\sigma_i \in \Delta(S_i)\). Expected payoff: \[ u_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}) = \sum_{s \in S} \left(\prod_j \sigma_j(s_j)\right) u_i(s) \]

Two-player zero-sum: \(u_1(s) + u_2(s) = 0\) for all \(s\). von Neumann's minimax theorem: \(\max_{\sigma_1} \min_{\sigma_2} u_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = \min_{\sigma_2} \max_{\sigma_1} u_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = v\) (value of the game).
Matching pennies: No pure NE. Mixed NE: both play H/T with probability \(1/2\), expected payoff 0.

4) Extensive-form games and subgame perfection

An extensive-form game is a game tree: nodes, information sets, actions, payoffs at terminal nodes. Perfect recall means players remember their past actions and what they knew.

Subgame perfect equilibrium (SPE): A strategy profile that is a Nash equilibrium in every subgame. Refines Nash by ruling out non-credible threats.
Backward induction: Solve finite perfect-information games from terminal nodes backward. In the ultimatum game, the subgame-perfect offer is the minimum acceptable split.

Information sets

Nodes in the same information set are indistinguishable to the player moving there. Imperfect information models simultaneous moves and hidden actions.

Bayesian games

Players have private types \(\theta_i\) drawn from a known distribution. Bayesian Nash equilibrium conditions strategies on beliefs about opponents' types.

5) Cooperative games and the Shapley value

In cooperative game theory, coalitions \(S \subseteq N\) form binding agreements. A characteristic function \(v : 2^N \to \mathbb{R}\) gives the value coalition \(S\) can guarantee.

Shapley value: Unique allocation satisfying efficiency, symmetry, dummy, and additivity: \[ \phi_i(v) = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|N|-|S|-1)!}{|N|!}\left[v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S)\right] \]
Core: Allocations \(x\) with \(\sum_i x_i = v(N)\) and \(\sum_{i \in S} x_i \geq v(S)\) for all \(S\). The core may be empty; Shapley value always exists.
Convention: Cooperative solutions (Shapley, core) assume binding contracts. Non-cooperative theory models how agreements are enforced (or not) via strategies.

6) Evolutionary game theory and replicator dynamics

Evolutionary game theory models populations of strategies. Payoffs are fitness; successful strategies spread. A strategy is ESS (evolutionarily stable strategy) if it resists invasion by mutants.

Replicator equation: For population shares \(x_i\) of strategies \(i\), \[ \dot{x}_i = x_i\left(u_i(x) - \bar{u}(x)\right) \] where \(\bar{u}(x) = \sum_j x_j u_j(x)\) is average fitness. Nash equilibria are fixed points.
Hawk–Dove game: ESS can be a mixed strategy when fighting costs exceed resource value. Explains territorial behaviour in biology and escalation in economics.

Links to dynamical systems and Chaos Theory when strategy populations exhibit complex trajectories.

7) Applications and research outlook

Mechanism design

Design rules so that selfish players' equilibria achieve social goals. Auction theory (Vickrey, VCG), matching markets (Gale–Shapley), and incentive compatibility.

Algorithmic game theory

Complexity of computing Nash equilibria (PPAD-complete). Price of anarchy measures efficiency loss at equilibrium in networks.

Cryptography and security

Multi-party computation and Cryptography use game-theoretic models for rational adversaries and protocol incentives.

Repeated games

Folk theorems: in infinitely repeated games with discounting, many payoff profiles are sustainable as equilibrium outcomes via threat strategies.

Related: Combinatorics, Optimization, Statistics.
