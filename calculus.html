Calculus

A-Level guide. Overview of differential and integral calculus — rate of change, accumulation, and links to limits, differentiation, and integration.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) What is calculus?

Calculus is the branch of mathematics that studies change and accumulation. It splits into two main ideas:

  • Differential calculus — how fast a quantity changes (slopes, rates, gradients)
  • Integral calculus — how quantities build up over time or space (areas, totals, volumes)
Key idea: Differentiation and integration are inverse processes. One asks "how fast?", the other asks "how much in total?" — connected by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Calculus builds on Functions, Limits, and algebra. It is essential for A-Level Maths, Further Maths, and engineering degrees.

2) Rate of change — differentiation

The derivative of a function measures its instantaneous rate of change. Geometrically, it is the gradient of the tangent to the curve.

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \]

Interpretations

  • Gradient of \(y = f(x)\) at a point
  • Velocity if \(s(t)\) is displacement
  • Marginal cost or revenue in economics

Notation

\(f'(x)\), \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), \(\frac{d}{dx}(f(x))\), and \(\dot{x}\) (with respect to time) all mean derivative.

Full techniques: Differentiation and Differentiation in Engineering.

3) Accumulation — integration

Integration reverses differentiation. It finds the total accumulated quantity and, geometrically, the area under a curve.

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \text{signed area under } y = f(x) \text{ from } x = a \text{ to } x = b \]

Interpretations

  • Area under a velocity–time graph gives displacement
  • Total charge from current over time
  • Probability from a density function

Notation

\(\int f(x)\,dx\) is an indefinite integral (family of antiderivatives). \(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) is a definite integral (a number).

Full techniques: Integration and Integration in Engineering.

4) The role of limits

Both differentiation and integration are defined using limits. Without limits, "instantaneous rate" and "area under a curve" would be undefined.

Differentiation: The derivative is the limit of average gradients as the interval shrinks to zero.
Integration: The definite integral is the limit of Riemann sums as rectangles become infinitely thin.

Standard limits such as \(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\) underpin trig derivatives. See Limits for full treatment.

Common mistake: Treating \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) as a fraction and cancelling \(dx\) without understanding it as limit notation. The notation is suggestive but the meaning is precise.

5) The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The two branches of calculus are linked by a central theorem. If \(F'(x) = f(x)\), then:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a) \]

This means: to evaluate a definite integral, find any antiderivative \(F\), substitute the limits, and subtract. Differentiation "undoes" integration and vice versa.

Worked example: Evaluate \(\int_0^2 3x^2\,dx\).
Antiderivative: \(F(x) = x^3\). So \(\int_0^2 3x^2\,dx = F(2) - F(0) = 8 - 0 = 8\).

7) Standard results to know

At A-Level you learn differentiation and integration rules for common functions:

Differentiation

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x) = \cos x\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x) = -\sin x\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}(e^x) = e^x\)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}(\ln x) = \frac{1}{x}\)

Integration

  • \(\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C\) (\(n \neq -1\))
  • \(\int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C\)
  • \(\int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C\)
  • \(\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C\)
  • \(\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C\)
Chain, product, and quotient rules extend these for composite and combined functions — covered in Differentiation.
