Topological Quantum Computing

Quantum guide. Anyons, braiding, non-Abelian statistics, Majorana zero modes, the toric code, and why topological protection promises hardware-level fault tolerance for quantum information.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) The topological promise

Topological quantum computing stores and processes quantum information in global, non-local properties of a physical system that are robust against local perturbations. Errors from small environmental disturbances do not easily flip topological quantum numbers.

  • Information encoded in topological phases of matter, not individual microscopic states.
  • Logical operations performed by braiding anyonic excitations around each other.
  • Fault tolerance at the hardware level — potentially reducing error-correction overhead.
  • Leading candidate: Majorana zero modes in topological superconductors.
Key idea: Local noise cannot continuously deform a topological quantum number; only global changes (e.g. creating quasiparticle pairs) cause errors — and these are exponentially suppressed at low temperature.

Prerequisites: Quantum Error Correction, Topology.

2) Anyons and exchange statistics

In two dimensions, particles can obey fractional statistics. Exchanging two anyons \(\psi_i\) and \(\psi_j\) multiplies the wavefunction by a phase (Abelian anyons) or transforms it via a unitary matrix (non-Abelian anyons):

\(\psi_i \psi_j = e^{i\theta}\,\psi_j \psi_i \quad\text{(Abelian)}, \qquad \psi_i \psi_j = R_{ij}\,\psi_j \psi_i \quad\text{(non-Abelian)}\)

Braiding — adiabatically exchanging anyons along paths that wind around each other — implements unitary gates on the degenerate ground-state manifold. For universal computation, non-Abelian anyons with sufficiently rich fusion rules are needed.

Fibonacci anyons: The \(\tau\) anyon theory supports universal TQC with braiding alone (no magic-state distillation needed), but physical realisation remains elusive.

3) Toric code — the canonical model

Kitaev's toric code is a exactly solvable lattice model on a torus with four-spin interactions. Ground states are \(+1\) eigenstates of stabiliser operators \(A_v\) (vertex) and \(B_p\) (plaquette):

\(A_v = \prod_{e \ni v} \sigma_e^z, \qquad B_p = \prod_{e \in p} \sigma_e^x\)

Excitations are anyons: \(e\)-particles (vertex violations) and \(m\)-particles (plaquette violations). An \(e\) braiding around an \(m\) acquires a phase of \(-1\) — the Abelian anyon statistics of \(\mathbb{Z}_2\).

Limitation: Abelian anyons (toric code) do not support universal braiding alone. Magic-state injection or code deformation is needed for a universal gate set.

Related: Surface Codes, Stabilizer Codes.

4) Majorana zero modes

A Majorana zero mode (MZM) is a zero-energy excitation at the boundary of a topological superconductor. Each MZM \(\gamma\) is a fermionic operator satisfying \(\gamma = \gamma^\dagger\) and \(\{\gamma_i, \gamma_j\} = 2\delta_{ij}\).

A pair of spatially separated MZMs \(\gamma_1, \gamma_2\) defines a Majorana qubit via the occupation of a non-local fermionic mode:

\(f^\dagger = \tfrac{1}{2}(\gamma_1 + i\gamma_2), \qquad |0\rangle_f \equiv \text{even parity}, \; |1\rangle_f \equiv \text{odd parity}\)

Braiding MZMs (moving vortices in a p+ip superconductor) implements protected Clifford gates. Microsoft and academic groups pursue MZM platforms in hybrid semiconductor–superconductor nanowires.

Experimental status: Zero-bias conductance peaks consistent with MZMs have been reported in InAs/Al nanowires, but definitive non-Abelian braiding statistics remain unconfirmed as of 2025.

5) Ising anyons and the Majorana chain

The Kitaev chain is a 1D model of p-wave superconductivity:

\(H = -t\sum_n c_n^\dagger c_{n+1} - \Delta\sum_n c_n^\dagger c_{n+1}^\dagger + \text{h.c.} + \mu\sum_n c_n^\dagger c_n\)

In the topological phase (\(|\mu| < 2t\)), zero-energy Majorana modes appear at each end. Vortices in 2D p+ip superconductors carry Ising anyons (\(\mathbb{Z}_2\) topological order) with non-Abelian braiding within a projective representation.

Ising anyons are not universal for braiding alone; T-gates require magic-state distillation or additional operations.

6) Topological vs active error correction

AspectTopological protectionStabiliser codes (surface code)
Error mechanism Exponentially suppress local errors Detect and correct via syndrome measurement
Overhead Potentially lower (hardware protected) ~1000 physical qubits per logical qubit
Gate implementation Braiding (slow, limited set) Microwave/laser gates (fast, full set)
Maturity Experimental, no logical qubit yet Logical qubits demonstrated on multiple platforms

Many researchers view topological and stabiliser approaches as complementary: topological phases may eventually provide the physical qubits that stabiliser codes operate on.

7) Experimental platforms

Majorana wires

  • InAs, InSb nanowires with Al superconductor
  • Epitaxial full-shell nanowires
  • Topological insulator – superconductor heterostructures

Fractional quantum Hall

  • \(\nu = 5/2\) state — non-Abelian anyons proposed
  • Interferometry to detect braiding statistics
  • Ultra-low temperatures and strong magnetic fields

Quantum annealers and topological materials research (e.g. Majorana quantised conductance) feed into the long-term TQC roadmap but are distinct from NISQ processors used today.

8) Challenges and outlook

  • Verification: Distinguishing true MZMs from trivial Andreev bound states requires interferometry and braiding experiments.
  • Universality: Most known topological phases require supplemental gates for universality.
  • Timescales: Braiding operations may be slow (milliseconds) compared to superconducting gates (nanoseconds).
  • Scalability: Networking topological qubits in 2D layouts with controlled braiding paths is a major engineering challenge.
Long-term bet: If non-Abelian anyons are confirmed and braiding is demonstrated, topological quantum computing could dramatically reduce the overhead for fault-tolerant quantum computation.
