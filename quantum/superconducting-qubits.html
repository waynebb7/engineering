Superconducting Qubits

Quantum guide. Josephson junctions, transmon and flux qubits, circuit QED readout, and the engineering path from NISQ processors to fault-tolerant superconducting arrays.

← Quantum Subjects Catalog

1) Why superconducting circuits?

Superconducting qubits encode quantum information in the collective charge or flux states of macroscopic circuits cooled to millikelvin temperatures. They are fabricated with lithographic techniques borrowed from CMOS, wired on-chip with microwave resonators, and controlled with room-temperature electronics.

  • Two-level systems arise from nonlinear inductance of Josephson junctions (JJ).
  • Typical coherence times: \(T_1 \sim 50\text{–}200\,\mu\text{s}\), \(T_2 \sim 30\text{–}100\,\mu\text{s}\) on leading transmon devices.
  • Gate times \(\sim 10\text{–}50\,\text{ns}\) — fast relative to many other platforms.
  • IBM, Google, Rigetti, and many labs use transmon-based processors with 50–1000+ qubits.
Key idea: A JJ replaces the linear inductor in an LC oscillator with a nonlinear element whose energy spectrum is anharmonic — only the lowest two levels are used as the qubit.

Prerequisites: Qubits and the Bloch Sphere, Complex Numbers.

2) Josephson junction physics

A Josephson junction is a weak link between two superconductors. Its phase difference \(\phi\) across the junction obeys the Josephson relations:

\(I = I_c \sin\phi, \qquad V = \frac{\Phi_0}{2\pi}\frac{d\phi}{dt}\)

where \(I_c\) is the critical current and \(\Phi_0 = h/2e \approx 2.07 \times 10^{-15}\,\text{Wb}\) is the superconducting flux quantum. The junction energy is

\(U_J = -E_J \cos\phi\)

with \(E_J = I_c \Phi_0 / 2\pi\). Parasitic capacitance \(C_J\) across the junction gives a charging energy \(E_C = e^2 / 2C_J\) when Cooper pairs tunnel.

Dimensionless ratio: \(E_J / E_C\) determines whether the device is charge-sensitive (small \(E_J/E_C\)) or phase-dominated (large \(E_J/E_C\)). The transmon operates in the latter regime.

3) Transmon qubit design

The transmon is a Cooper-pair box shunted by a large capacitor so that charge dispersion is exponentially suppressed. Its Hamiltonian in the charge basis is approximately:

\(H \approx 4E_C n^2 - E_J \cos\phi\)

Energy levels \(|0\rangle, |1\rangle\) are separated by transition frequency \(\omega_{01}/2\pi \sim 4\text{–}6\,\text{GHz}\). Anharmonicity \(\alpha = \omega_{12} - \omega_{01}\) is negative (\(\sim -200\) to \(-300\,\text{MHz}\)), suppressing leakage to \(|2\rangle\) during single-qubit drives.

Other designs: flux qubits (tunable via SQUID loop), phase qubits, and gatemon variants for semiconductor hybrid integration.

4) Qubit–resonator coupling (circuit QED)

Transmons are coupled capacitively to microwave readout resonators at \(\omega_r/2\pi \sim 6\text{–}8\,\text{GHz}\). In the dispersive regime (\(|\Delta| = |\omega_{01} - \omega_r| \gg g\)), the Jaynes–Cummings Hamiltonian reduces to:

\(H/\hbar = \omega_r a^\dagger a + \frac{\omega_{01}}{2}\sigma_z + \chi\, a^\dagger a\,\sigma_z\)

The dispersive shift \(\chi = g^2/\Delta\) encodes qubit state in the resonator frequency — the basis of dispersive readout. A probe pulse reflects with phase depending on \(\sigma_z\).

Worked idea: If \(\chi/2\pi \approx 1\,\text{MHz}\) and the resonator linewidth is \(\kappa/2\pi \approx 2\,\text{MHz}\), integrating \(\sim 1\,\mu\text{s}\) of reflected signal yields single-shot fidelity above 98%.

5) Single- and two-qubit gates

Single-qubit gates are implemented with microwave pulses resonant at \(\omega_{01}\), shaped to realise rotations on the Bloch sphere: \(R_x(\theta)\), \(R_y(\theta)\), \(R_z(\phi)\).

Two-qubit gates typically use capacitive or inductive coupling between neighbouring transmons. The iSWAP and CZ gates are common native entangling operations; cross-resonance drives on fixed-frequency qubits produce effective CNOT gates.

Gate fidelity targets

  • Single-qubit: > 99.9%
  • Two-qubit: > 99%
  • Readout: > 98%

Calibration needs

  • Frequency drift compensation
  • Pulse amplitude and phase tuning
  • Crosstalk cancellation

See Quantum Gates and Quantum Control and Calibration.

6) Cryogenic stack and wiring

Superconducting processors operate inside a dilution refrigerator at base temperature \(T \sim 10\text{–}20\,\text{mK}\). Thermal photons and Johnson noise from cables must be attenuated at each temperature stage.

  • MXL (mixing chamber): qubit chip mounted here.
  • Attenuators: typically 0 dB at output, 10+20+30 dB on input lines per stage.
  • Low-pass filters: infrared blocking at the chip (e.g. Eccosorb filters).
  • Amplifiers: Josephson parametric amplifiers (JPAs) or TWPA at 4 K for readout chain.
Engineering constraint: Each coax line adds heat load. Scaling to thousands of qubits requires multiplexed readout and on-chip control electronics — active research areas.

7) Noise and decoherence

Qubit errors arise from \(T_1\) (energy relaxation) and \(T_2\) (dephasing). Dominant mechanisms include dielectric loss in junction materials, quasiparticle tunnelling, flux noise, and stray two-level systems (TLS) in oxides.

The Lindblad master equation for a single qubit with relaxation rate \(\Gamma_1 = 1/T_1\) and pure dephasing rate \(\Gamma_\phi\) gives:

\(T_2^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{2}\Gamma_1 + \Gamma_\phi\)

Material science matters: TiN, tantalum, and aluminium junctions on high-resistivity silicon substrates have pushed \(T_1\) past 100 μs. Surface participation ratio of electric fields in lossy dielectrics is a key design metric.

Related: Quantum Channels and Decoherence, NISQ Era Computing.

8) Scaling toward fault tolerance

Surface-code architectures require a 2D grid of data and ancilla qubits with stabilizer measurements every \(\sim 1\,\mu\text{s}\). Superconducting platforms target:

  • Logical error rate \(\ll 10^{-12}\) per gate for useful algorithms.
  • Physical error rate \(\sim 10^{-3}\) today — needs ~1000:1 overhead per logical qubit initially.
  • Fast syndrome extraction exploiting native CZ gates and mid-circuit measurement.

Advantages

  • Mature microfabrication
  • Fast gates and readout
  • Strong industry investment

Challenges

  • Frequency collisions in large arrays
  • Cryogenic I/O bottleneck
  • TLS and quasiparticle bursts

Error correction: Surface Codes, Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computing.
