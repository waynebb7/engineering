Triple Integrals

Undergraduate guide. Volumes, cylindrical and spherical coordinates, the Jacobian, and engineering applications.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Triple integrals and volume

A triple integral over a region \(E\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) integrates a scalar function \(f(x,y,z)\) throughout a solid volume. When \(f = 1\), the integral gives the volume of \(E\).

Definition: \[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \lim_{\|\Delta\|\to 0} \sum_{i,j,k} f(\xi_{ijk})\,\Delta V_{ijk} \] In Cartesian coordinates, \(dV = dx\,dy\,dz\).
Worked example: Volume of the unit cube \([0,1]^3\):
\(\displaystyle\iiint_E 1\,dV = \int_0^1\int_0^1\int_0^1 dz\,dy\,dx = 1\).

Triple integrals extend Integration and build on Multivariable Calculus and Vectors.

2) Iterated integrals and bounds

Fubini's theorem allows evaluation as an iterated integral. The key skill is determining correct limits from the geometry of the region.

Method:
Step 1: Sketch the region \(E\) in 3D.
Step 2: Project onto a coordinate plane to find outer limits.
Step 3: For each fixed outer variable, find inner bounds where the region enters and exits.
Step 4: Integrate from innermost to outermost variable.
Worked example: Volume under \(z = 4 - x - y\) over \(0 \leq x \leq 2\), \(0 \leq y \leq 2\):
\(V = \displaystyle\int_0^2\int_0^2\int_0^{4-x-y} dz\,dy\,dx = \int_0^2\int_0^2 (4-x-y)\,dy\,dx = \dfrac{16}{3}\).
Common mistake: Using the same limits for all three integrals regardless of geometry. The \(z\)-limits often depend on \(x\) and \(y\).

3) Cylindrical coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates \((r, \theta, z)\) are natural for regions with circular symmetry about the \(z\)-axis (cylinders, pipes, shafts).

Transformation: \(x = r\cos\theta\), \(y = r\sin\theta\), \(z = z\).
Volume element: \(dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\).
Coordinate Range (typical) Role
\(r\) \(0 \leq r \leq R\) Radial distance from \(z\)-axis
\(\theta\) \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) Azimuthal angle
\(z\) Problem-dependent Height
Worked example: Volume of a cylinder of radius \(R\), height \(h\):
\(V = \displaystyle\int_0^h\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz = h \cdot 2\pi \cdot \dfrac{R^2}{2} = \pi R^2 h\).

See also Polar Coordinates for the 2D analogue.

4) Spherical coordinates

Spherical coordinates \((\rho, \phi, \theta)\) suit regions with symmetry about a point (spheres, balls, radar coverage zones).

Transformation: \(x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\), \(y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\), \(z = \rho\cos\phi\).
Volume element: \(dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\).
Coordinate Range (typical) Role
\(\rho\) \(0 \leq \rho \leq R\) Distance from origin
\(\phi\) \(0 \leq \phi \leq \pi\) Colatitude from \(z\)-axis
\(\theta\) \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) Azimuthal angle in \(xy\)-plane
Worked example: Volume of a sphere of radius \(R\):
\(V = \displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^R \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = \dfrac{4\pi R^3}{3}\).
Convention alert: Physics texts sometimes swap \(\phi\) and \(\theta\). Always check the definition used in your module.

5) The Jacobian determinant

When changing variables from \((u,v,w)\) to \((x,y,z)\), the volume element transforms via the Jacobian determinant:

Change of variables: \[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \iiint_{E'} f(x(u,v,w), y(u,v,w), z(u,v,w)) \left|\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)}\right| du\,dv\,dw \] The Jacobian is the determinant of the matrix of partial derivatives.
Worked example: For cylindrical coordinates, the Jacobian is: \[ \frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(r,\theta,z)} = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = r \] Hence \(dV = r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz\).

The Jacobian generalises the \(u\)-substitution factor from Inverse Chain Rule to three dimensions.

6) Mass, centre of mass, and moments

Triple integrals compute physical quantities by integrating density over a solid body.

Quantity Formula (density \(\rho(x,y,z)\))
Mass \(M = \displaystyle\iiint_E \rho\,dV\)
Centre of mass (\(x\)-coordinate) \(\bar{x} = \dfrac{1}{M}\displaystyle\iiint_E x\rho\,dV\)
Moment of inertia (about \(z\)-axis) \(I_z = \displaystyle\iiint_E (x^2 + y^2)\rho\,dV\)
Worked example: A solid hemisphere of radius \(R\) with uniform density \(\rho_0\).
By symmetry, \(\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0\). Using spherical coordinates:
\(\bar{z} = \dfrac{3}{2R^3}\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^R \rho\cos\phi \cdot \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta = \dfrac{3R}{8}\).

7) Engineering applications

Triple integrals are used wherever engineers need total quantities distributed over a volume: mass, charge, heat content, and radiation dose.

Fluid reservoir capacity

Irregular tank shapes require numerical or coordinate-based triple integration to compute volume and fill levels.

Gravitational and electromagnetic fields

Total flux or potential from a volumetric source distribution is a triple integral of density over the source region.

Worked example: Heat stored in a solid with temperature \(T(x,y,z)\) and density \(\rho\): \(Q = \iiint_E c\,\rho\,T\,dV\), where \(c\) is specific heat capacity. Choosing cylindrical coordinates for a pipe simplifies the integral when \(T\) depends only on \(r\) and \(z\).
Exam tip: Choose coordinates that match the symmetry of the region. A sphere in Cartesian coordinates is far harder than in spherical coordinates.
