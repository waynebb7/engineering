Convergence

Undergraduate guide. Sequences and series, convergence tests, and absolute versus conditional convergence.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Sequences and limits

A sequence \((a_n)\) converges to limit \(L\) if terms approach \(L\) as \(n \to \infty\): \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L \] We write \(a_n \to L\) or \((a_n) \to L\).

Key idea: Convergence of a sequence is the foundation for series convergence. A series cannot converge unless its terms \(a_n \to 0\) (necessary condition).
Worked example: \(a_n = \dfrac{3n + 1}{n + 2} \to 3\) as \(n \to \infty\) (divide numerator and denominator by \(n\)).
Common mistake: \(a_n \to 0\) is necessary but not sufficient for \(\sum a_n\) to converge. Example: harmonic series terms \(\dfrac{1}{n} \to 0\) but the series diverges.

Related: Limits and Functions.

2) Infinite series

An infinite series is the formal sum \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n\). The partial sums \(S_N = \sum_{n=1}^{N} a_n\) form a sequence. The series converges if \((S_N) \to S\) for some finite \(S\); otherwise it diverges.

Geometric series: For \(|r| < 1\): \[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1 - r} \] This is the benchmark for comparison tests and appears in power series, Z-transforms, and feedback loops.
Harmonic series: \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{n}\) diverges (slowly). The alternating harmonic series \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\) converges to \(\ln 2\).

3) Comparison tests

To test \(\sum a_n\), compare term sizes with a series whose behaviour is known.

Direct comparison

If \(0 \leq a_n \leq b_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) converges, then \(\sum a_n\) converges.

If \(0 \leq b_n \leq a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) diverges, then \(\sum a_n\) diverges.

Limit comparison

If \(a_n, b_n > 0\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{a_n}{b_n} = L\) with \(0 < L < \infty\), then \(\sum a_n\) and \(\sum b_n\) behave the same.

Worked example: Test \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{n^2 + 1}\).
Compare with \(\sum \dfrac{1}{n^2}\) (convergent \(p\)-series, \(p = 2\)).
\(\dfrac{a_n}{b_n} = \dfrac{n^2}{n^2+1} \to 1\). Limit comparison → converges.
Exam tip: Choose a comparison series of the same "degree": for rational terms in \(n\), use \(\dfrac{1}{n^p}\) with appropriate \(p\).

4) Ratio and root tests

These tests are especially effective for series involving factorials, powers, and exponentials.

Ratio test: Let \(L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|\).
\(L < 1\) → converges absolutely.
\(L > 1\) → diverges.
\(L = 1\) → inconclusive.
Root test: Let \(L = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}\). Same conclusions as ratio test.
Worked example: \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{n!}{3^n}\).
\(\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \dfrac{(n+1)!/3^{n+1}}{n!/3^n} = \dfrac{n+1}{3} \to \infty\).
Ratio test → diverges.

For \(\sum \dfrac{x^n}{n!}\) (exponential series), the ratio test gives \(L = |x|\); converges for all \(x\).

5) Integral test

If \(f(x)\) is positive, continuous, and decreasing for \(x \geq 1\), and \(a_n = f(n)\), then: \[ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \text{ and } \int_1^{\infty} f(x)\,dx \] either both converge or both diverge.

\(p\)-series: \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{n^p}\) converges if \(p > 1\), diverges if \(p \leq 1\).
Proof via integral test with \(f(x) = x^{-p}\).
Worked example: Test \(\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{n\ln n}\).
\(\int_2^{\infty} \dfrac{dx}{x\ln x} = \lim_{b\to\infty}[\ln(\ln x)]_2^b = \infty\).
Integral diverges → series diverges.

The integral test also provides error bounds: the tail \(\sum_{n=N+1}^{\infty} a_n\) is bounded by \(\int_N^{\infty} f(x)\,dx\).

6) Absolute versus conditional convergence

A series \(\sum a_n\) converges absolutely if \(\sum |a_n|\) converges. It converges conditionally if \(\sum a_n\) converges but \(\sum |a_n|\) diverges.

Key facts:
Absolute convergence \(\Rightarrow\) convergence (always).
Absolute convergence allows free reordering of terms (Riemann rearrangement theorem does not apply).
Conditional convergence is fragile — rearranging terms can change the sum.
Worked example: \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\) (alternating harmonic).
Converges by alternating series test. But \(\sum \dfrac{1}{n}\) diverges → conditionally convergent.
Series \(\sum a_n\) \(\sum |a_n|\) Type
\(\sum 1/n^2\) Converges Converges Absolute
\(\sum (-1)^{n+1}/n\) Converges Diverges Conditional
\(\sum 1/n\) Diverges Diverges —

7) Alternating series and test strategy

An alternating series has the form \(\sum (-1)^{n+1} b_n\) with \(b_n > 0\). The alternating series test: if \(b_n\) is decreasing and \(b_n \to 0\), the series converges.

Choosing a test — decision guide:
Terms involve \(n!\) or \(r^n\) → ratio or root test.
Rational in \(n\) → limit comparison with \(p\)-series.
Form \(f(n)\) with smooth \(f(x)\) → integral test.
Alternating signs → check absolute convergence first; else alternating series test.
Engineering link: Convergence of Fourier series, Taylor series, and iterative numerical methods all rely on these tests. Truncation error analysis uses comparison and integral bounds.

See Fourier Series for convergence at discontinuities (Dirichlet conditions).

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. State the condition on \(a_n\) necessary for \(\sum a_n\) to converge.
  2. Does \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{n^{3/2}}\) converge? Which test do you use?
  3. Apply the ratio test to \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{2^n}{n!}\).
  4. Is \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}\) absolutely or conditionally convergent?
  5. Find the sum of \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{3^n}\).
  6. Use limit comparison to test \(\sum \dfrac{n+1}{n^3 + 2}\).
  7. Why can reordering terms change the sum of a conditionally convergent series?

For marking: name the test, show key calculations, and state convergence or divergence clearly.
