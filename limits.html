Limits

A-Level guide. The limit concept, one-sided limits, standard results, and an introduction to continuity.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) What is a limit?

The limit describes the value a function approaches as the input gets close to some number. We write:

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \] means: as \(x\) gets arbitrarily close to \(a\) (without necessarily equalling \(a\)), \(f(x)\) gets arbitrarily close to \(L\).

Limits make precise ideas such as "instantaneous gradient" and "area under a curve" that appear in Calculus. The value \(f(a)\) may differ from the limit, or may not exist at all.

Example: \(\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 7\). As \(x\) approaches 2, \(3x + 1\) approaches 7. Here the limit equals the function value: \(f(2) = 7\).

2) Limit notation and existence

For a limit to exist at \(x = a\), the function must approach the same value from both sides.

\[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L \text{ and } \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \]

When limits fail

  • Left and right limits differ (jump discontinuity)
  • Function increases or decreases without bound (infinite limit)
  • Function oscillates without settling (e.g. \(\sin\frac{1}{x}\) near 0)

Limit laws

If \(\lim f(x) = L\) and \(\lim g(x) = M\), then \(\lim[f(x) \pm g(x)] = L \pm M\), \(\lim[f(x)g(x)] = LM\), and \(\lim\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}\) if \(M \neq 0\).

3) One-sided limits

A one-sided limit restricts the approach to one direction:

  • \(\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)\) — approach from the left (\(x < a\))
  • \(\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\) — approach from the right (\(x > a\))
Worked example: For \(f(x) = \frac{|x|}{x}\) (sign function for \(x \neq 0\)):
\(\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{|x|}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} 1 = 1\).
\(\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{|x|}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0^-} (-1) = -1\).
Left and right limits differ, so \(\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) does not exist.
Worked example: For \(f(x) = \begin{cases} x + 1 & x < 2 \\ 5 & x = 2 \\ 7 - x & x > 2 \end{cases}\):
Left limit: \(\lim_{x \to 2^-} (x + 1) = 3\). Right limit: \(\lim_{x \to 2^+} (7 - x) = 5\). Limit DNE at \(x = 2\).

4) Standard results

These limits are used repeatedly in A-Level calculus. You should know them or be able to derive them.

Limit Value Notes
\(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x}\)\(1\)Requires \(x\) in radians
\(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{1 - \cos x}{x}\)\(0\)Used for \(\frac{d}{dx}(\cos x)\)
\(\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{1}{x^n}\) (\(n > 0\))\(0\)Reciprocal powers vanish
\(\lim_{x \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x\)\(e\)Definition of \(e\)
\(\lim_{x \to 0}(1 + x)^{1/x}\)\(e\)Alternative form
\(\lim_{x \to a} x^n\)\(a^n\)Polynomials are continuous
Using \(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1\): To differentiate \(\sin x\), consider \(\frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin x}{h}\) and apply the limit — the standard result is essential.

5) Evaluating limits

Common techniques for limits that initially give indeterminate forms such as \(\frac{0}{0}\):

  1. Direct substitution — if \(f(a)\) is defined and continuous, \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\)
  2. Factorise and cancel — removes common factors causing \(\frac{0}{0}\)
  3. Rationalise — multiply by conjugate for square-root expressions
  4. Standard results — especially for trig and exponential limits
Worked example: Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 3}\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}\).
Factorise: \(\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3\) for \(x \neq 3\).
Limit: \(\lim_{x \to 3}(x + 3) = 6\).
Worked example: Evaluate \(\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin 3x}{x}\).
Rewrite: \(\frac{\sin 3x}{x} = 3 \cdot \frac{\sin 3x}{3x} \to 3 \times 1 = 3\).

6) Limits at infinity

Limits as \(x \to \infty\) or \(x \to -\infty\) describe end behaviour of functions — important for sketching curves and comparing growth rates.

Polynomials: Highest-degree term dominates. \(\lim_{x \to \infty}(3x^2 - 5x + 1) = \infty\) (behaves like \(3x^2\)).
Rational functions: Compare degrees of numerator and denominator.
\(\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{2x^2 + 1}{x^2 - 4} = 2\) (same degree: ratio of leading coefficients).
\(\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{3x}{x^2 + 1} = 0\) (degree of denominator greater).
Common mistake: Confusing \(\lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{1}{x} = 0\) with \(\frac{1}{\infty}\) being "zero". Infinity is not a number; limits describe approach, not substitution.

7) Continuity — an introduction

A function \(f\) is continuous at \(x = a\) if all three conditions hold:

  1. \(f(a)\) is defined
  2. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists
  3. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\)
Intuition: You can draw the graph without lifting your pen. Polynomials, \(\sin x\), \(\cos x\), and \(e^x\) are continuous everywhere on their domains.

Types of discontinuity

  • Removable: limit exists but \(\neq f(a)\) or \(f(a)\) undefined
  • Jump: one-sided limits exist but differ
  • Infinite: function blows up to \(\pm\infty\)

Why it matters

Continuous functions on an interval take every value between their minimum and maximum (Intermediate Value Theorem). Differentiation requires continuity (though continuity alone does not guarantee differentiability).
