Jordan Normal Form

Undergraduate guide. When diagonalisation fails, Jordan blocks, and an introduction to computing JNF.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Review: diagonalisation

A square matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) is diagonalisable if there exists an invertible matrix \(\mathbf{P}\) and diagonal matrix \(\mathbf{D}\) such that: \[ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{-1} \quad \text{equivalently} \quad \mathbf{P}^{-1}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{D} \] The columns of \(\mathbf{P}\) are eigenvectors; the diagonal entries of \(\mathbf{D}\) are eigenvalues.

Diagonalisation conditions: \(\mathbf{A}\) is \(n \times n\) and diagonalisable if and only if:
  • \(\mathbf{A}\) has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors, or
  • For each eigenvalue, algebraic multiplicity equals geometric multiplicity

See Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors for the diagonalisation procedure when it succeeds.

2) When diagonalisation fails

Diagonalisation fails when there are not enough eigenvectors. This happens when some eigenvalue \(\lambda\) has geometric multiplicity \(<\) algebraic multiplicity.

Worked example: Consider \[ \mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \] Characteristic polynomial: \((\lambda - 2)^2 = 0\), so \(\lambda = 2\) with algebraic multiplicity 2. Eigenvectors: \((\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) gives only \(\mathbf{v} = t(1,0)^T\). Geometric multiplicity \(= 1 < 2\). Not diagonalisable over \(\mathbb{C}\) (or \(\mathbb{R}\)).
Key point: Defective matrices (not diagonalisable) still have a canonical form — the Jordan normal form — which is "almost diagonal."

3) Generalised eigenvectors

When ordinary eigenvectors run out, we use generalised eigenvectors. A vector \(\mathbf{v}\) is a generalised eigenvector of rank \(k\) for eigenvalue \(\lambda\) if: \[ (\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})^k \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{but} \quad (\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})^{k-1} \mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0} \] An ordinary eigenvector has rank 1: \((\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,0)^T\) is an eigenvector. Solve \((\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1\): \[ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \Rightarrow y = 1,\; x \text{ free} \] Take \(\mathbf{v}_2 = (0,1)^T\), a generalised eigenvector of rank 2.

A Jordan chain is a sequence \(\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\) where \((\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{0}\) and \((\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v}_j = \mathbf{v}_{j-1}\) for \(j > 1\).

4) Jordan blocks

A Jordan block \(J_k(\lambda)\) of size \(k\) for eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is: \[ J_k(\lambda) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix} \] \(J_1(\lambda) = (\lambda)\) is a \(1 \times 1\) block. Superdiagonal entries are 1; all other off-diagonal entries are 0.

Properties:
  • \(\lambda\) appears \(k\) times on the diagonal (algebraic multiplicity contribution \(k\))
  • Only one eigenvector per block (geometric multiplicity contribution 1)
  • \(J_k(\lambda) = \lambda \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{N}\) where \(\mathbf{N}^k = \mathbf{0}\) (nilpotent part)
Example: \(J_2(2) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) — exactly the non-diagonalisable matrix from section 2.

5) Jordan normal form (JNF)

Over \(\mathbb{C}\), every \(n \times n\) matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) is similar to a block-diagonal matrix \(\mathbf{J}\) in Jordan normal form: \[ \mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} J_{k_1}(\lambda_1) & & \\ & J_{k_2}(\lambda_2) & \\ & & \ddots \end{pmatrix} \] so \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{J}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) for some invertible \(\mathbf{P}\).

The Jordan blocks are uniquely determined (up to ordering) by \(\mathbf{A}\). The eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\) are the diagonal entries; block sizes encode how eigenvectors and generalised eigenvectors fit together.

Geometric vs algebraic multiplicity: For each \(\lambda\), the number of Jordan blocks with that eigenvalue equals the geometric multiplicity. The sum of block sizes equals the algebraic multiplicity.

6) Computing JNF: an introduction

A systematic procedure (over \(\mathbb{C}\)):

  1. Find eigenvalues from the characteristic polynomial \(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0\).
  2. For each \(\lambda\), find \(\dim \ker(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\) (geometric multiplicity).
  3. Compare with algebraic multiplicity to determine Jordan block sizes.
  4. Construct Jordan chains to build generalised eigenvectors.
  5. Form \(\mathbf{P}\) from these vectors; \(\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{P}^{-1}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P}\).
Block size rule: If algebraic multiplicity \(= a\) and geometric multiplicity \(= g\) for eigenvalue \(\lambda\), there are \(g\) Jordan blocks, and their sizes sum to \(a\). The largest block size is at most \(a - g + 1\).
Exam tip: For small matrices, often guess block structure from the characteristic polynomial and verify by finding chains. Full algorithms (primary decomposition) are taught in advanced courses.

7) Worked example: \(3 \times 3\) matrix

Find the JNF of \[ \mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \]

Solution:
Characteristic polynomial: \((\lambda - 3)^3\). Single eigenvalue \(\lambda = 3\), algebraic multiplicity 3.
\(\mathbf{A} - 3\mathbf{I} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), rank 1, so \(\dim \ker = 2\). Geometric multiplicity \(= 2\).
Two Jordan blocks for \(\lambda = 3\), sizes summing to 3: one \(J_2(3)\) and one \(J_1(3)\): \[ \mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \] In this case \(\mathbf{A}\) is already in Jordan form.

Powers and matrix functions: \(\mathbf{A}^k = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{J}^k\mathbf{P}^{-1}\). Jordan blocks make \(\mathbf{J}^k\) easier to compute than for general defective matrices.
