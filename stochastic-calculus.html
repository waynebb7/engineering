Stochastic Calculus

Postgraduate guide. Brownian motion, filtrations, the Itô integral, Itô's lemma, stochastic differential equations, and financial applications.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Probability space and filtrations

Stochastic calculus is built on a filtered probability space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, (\mathcal{F}_t)_{t \geq 0}, \mathbb{P})\), where \(\mathcal{F}_t\) records all information available up to time \(t\). A process \((X_t)\) is adapted if \(X_t\) is \(\mathcal{F}_t\)-measurable for each \(t\).

Key idea: Non-anticipating integrands are essential. You cannot use future values of Brownian motion when defining the stochastic integral — only the past and present filtration \(\mathcal{F}_t\) is allowed.

A process is a martingale if \(\mathbb{E}[X_t \mid \mathcal{F}_s] = X_s\) for \(s \leq t\). Martingales encode fair-game dynamics and underpin risk-neutral pricing.

Prerequisite: Measure Theory and Probability Theory.

2) Brownian motion

A standard Brownian motion (Wiener process) \(W_t\) satisfies:

  • \(W_0 = 0\) almost surely
  • Independent increments: \(W_t - W_s\) independent of \(\mathcal{F}_s\) for \(t > s\)
  • \(W_t - W_s \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t-s)\)
  • Continuous sample paths almost surely
Quadratic variation: For partitions with mesh \(\to 0\), \[ [W]_t = \lim \sum (W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i})^2 = t \] This is the fundamental departure from classical calculus: \(dW_t^2 = dt\) in Itô sense.
Worked example: \(\mathbb{E}[W_t] = 0\), \(\mathrm{Var}(W_t) = t\), and \(\mathbb{E}[W_t W_s] = \min(t,s)\) for \(t, s \geq 0\).

3) The Itô integral

For an adapted process \(H_t\) with \(\mathbb{E}\int_0^T H_t^2\,dt < \infty\), the Itô integral is defined as the \(L^2\) limit of simple-process integrals: \[ \int_0^T H_t\,dW_t = \lim \sum H_{t_i}(W_{t_{i+1}} - W_{t_i}) \]

Itô isometry: \[ \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\int_0^T H_t\,dW_t\right)^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\int_0^T H_t^2\,dt \]
Properties: The Itô integral is a martingale (zero mean), and \(\int_0^t dW_s = W_t\). Unlike Riemann–Stieltjes, Brownian paths have unbounded variation, so classical Stieltjes integration fails.
Common mistake: Treating \(dW_t\) like \(dt\) in algebraic manipulations. Quadratic terms in \(dW_t\) contribute at first order.

4) Itô's lemma

If \(X_t\) follows \(dX_t = \mu_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t\) and \(f(t,x)\) is twice continuously differentiable, then: \[ df(t, X_t) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mu_t\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right)dt + \sigma_t\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,dW_t \]

Worked example: For \(f(x) = x^2\) and \(X_t = W_t\): \(d(W_t^2) = 2W_t\,dW_t + dt\). Integrating gives \(W_T^2 = 2\int_0^T W_t\,dW_t + T\).
Multidimensional form: For vector processes, include cross-terms via \(\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} a_{ij}\partial_{x_i x_j} f\) where \(d\langle X^i, X^j\rangle_t = a_{ij}\,dt\).

Related: Differentiation (chain rule analogue) and Integration.

5) Stochastic differential equations

An SDE has the form: \[ dX_t = b(t, X_t)\,dt + \sigma(t, X_t)\,dW_t, \quad X_0 = x_0 \] Existence and uniqueness require Lipschitz and linear-growth conditions on \(b\) and \(\sigma\).

Geometric Brownian motion: \[ dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t \quad\Rightarrow\quad S_t = S_0\exp\!\left(\left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W_t\right) \]
Ornstein–Uhlenbeck: \(dX_t = -\theta X_t\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t\) is mean-reverting; \(X_t\) is Gaussian with stationary variance \(\sigma^2/(2\theta)\) when \(\theta > 0\).

Strong vs weak solutions

Strong solutions are pathwise unique given the same Brownian path. Weak solutions allow different driving noise with matching law.

Feynman–Kac

Links SDEs to parabolic PDEs: expectations of payoffs solve backward Kolmogorov equations with terminal/boundary data.

6) Girsanov theorem and risk-neutral pricing

Girsanov's theorem changes probability measure by a density involving stochastic exponentials. Under an equivalent martingale measure \(\mathbb{Q}\), discounted asset prices become martingales.

Black–Scholes setup: Under \(\mathbb{Q}\), \(dS_t = r S_t\,dt + \sigma S_t\,dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\). A European call with payoff \((S_T - K)^+\) has price \[ C_0 = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[(S_T - K)^+] = S_0\Phi(d_1) - Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_2) \] with standard \(d_1, d_2\) formulas.
Exam tip: Distinguish physical measure \(\mathbb{P}\) (drift \(\mu\)) from risk-neutral \(\mathbb{Q}\) (drift \(r\)). Option pricing uses \(\mathbb{Q}\).

7) Applications and extensions

Mathematical finance

Derivatives pricing, hedging portfolios, interest-rate models (Vasicek, HJM), and volatility surfaces.

Engineering and physics

Langevin equations, Kalman filtering, stochastic control, and noise-driven dynamical systems.

Multidimensional calculus

Correlated Brownian motions, matrix \(\sigma\), and Itô calculus on \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Jump processes

Poisson jumps extend diffusion models; Lévy processes generalise Brownian motion.

Related: Partial Differential Equations, Control Theory.
