Forces (Basics)

KS2 to KS3 guide. What forces are, how they work, and how to describe them clearly.

Back to Physics Subjects Catalog

1) What is a force?

A force is a push or a pull that can change an object’s motion or shape. Forces can:

  • Start something moving
  • Stop something moving
  • Speed up or slow down an object
  • Change direction (make it turn)
  • Change shape (squash, stretch, bend)
Key idea: If nothing changes, forces might still exist, but they could be balanced.

2) Forces are vectors (they have size and direction)

Size (magnitude)

How strong the push or pull is. Measured in newtons, written as N.

Example: A force of 10 N is stronger than 2 N.

Direction

Forces always act in a direction. For example: up, down, left, right, forward, backward.

Forces are often shown using an arrow. Longer arrow means a bigger force.

Example: Pushing a shopping trolley has a direction (forward). Gravity acts downward.

3) Contact forces and non-contact forces

Contact forces

These forces happen when objects are touching.

  • Push / pull (applied force)
  • Friction (rubbing force that resists sliding)
  • Air resistance (drag)
  • Water resistance (drag in water)
  • Tension (pulling force in a rope or string)
  • Normal contact force (support force from a surface)

Non-contact forces

These forces act even when objects are not touching.

  • Gravity (attraction between masses)
  • Magnetism (forces between magnets or magnetic materials)
  • Electrostatic force (forces between electric charges)
KS3 note: Gravity acts on all masses. Magnetism and electrostatics only affect certain materials or charges.

4) Balanced and unbalanced forces

Often more than one force acts on an object at the same time. The important question is: Do the forces cancel out?

Balanced forces

  • Forces are equal in size and opposite in direction
  • The resultant force (overall force) is 0 N
  • Motion does not change (object stays still or continues at constant speed in a straight line)
Example: A book resting on a table. Gravity pulls down, the table pushes up.

Unbalanced forces

  • Forces do not cancel out
  • The resultant force is not zero
  • The object’s motion changes (speed or direction changes)
Example: A kicked football speeds up because the kick force is larger than friction for a short time.
Common misunderstanding: “If something is moving, there must be a force pushing it forward.” In reality, an object can keep moving without a forward push if forces are balanced (very low friction).

5) Resultant force (overall force)

The resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all the forces together.

Simple rule (straight line): Resultant force = (forces in one direction) minus (forces in the opposite direction).
Situation Forces Resultant force What happens
Pushing a box Push = 20 N forward, friction = 5 N backward 20 - 5 = 15 N forward Box speeds up (accelerates) forward
Car cruising at steady speed Engine force forward equals air resistance + friction backward 0 N Speed stays constant
Tug of war (equal teams) Pull left equals pull right 0 N Rope stays still (or moves steadily if already moving)

6) Gravity, weight, and mass (important vocabulary)

At KS2 and KS3, confusion often happens between mass and weight.

Mass

  • How much “stuff” (matter) an object has
  • Measured in kilograms kg
  • Mass is the same on Earth, Moon, and in space

Weight

  • The force of gravity pulling on a mass
  • Measured in newtons N
  • Weight changes depending on gravity (Earth vs Moon)
KS3 equation: Weight = mass × gravitational field strength
Written as: W = m × g
On Earth, g is about 9.8 N/kg (often rounded to 10 N/kg for easier calculations).

7) Friction and air resistance (drag)

Friction is a force that resists motion between surfaces that touch. Air resistance is a type of friction caused by air pushing against a moving object.

Friction is useful

  • Walking (shoes grip the ground)
  • Brakes on bicycles and cars
  • Holding objects without slipping

Friction can be a problem

  • Wears out machine parts
  • Wastes energy as heat
  • Slows moving objects down
KS3 note: Drag increases when speed increases. That is why cycling fast becomes much harder.

8) Simple experiments (safe and classroom-friendly)

Experiment A: friction test

  • Slide the same object across different surfaces (table, cloth, carpet)
  • Observe how quickly it stops
  • Conclusion: rougher surfaces usually cause more friction

Experiment B: balanced forces

  • Hang a small weight from a string
  • It stays still because forces balance: weight downward, tension upward
  • Draw a simple force diagram (two arrows, same size)
Safety: Avoid heavy weights, sharp edges, and overstretched elastic. Use classroom-safe objects only.

9) Quick knowledge check

  1. What is a force?
  2. Name two contact forces.
  3. Name one non-contact force.
  4. If the resultant force is 0 N, what can be said about motion?
  5. What is the difference between mass and weight?
  6. Why do parachutes slow people down?

For marking: correct answers should reference direction, balanced/unbalanced forces, and correct units (kg vs N).
