Homeomorphism

Postgraduate guide. Topological equivalence, continuous bijections with continuous inverses, invariants, and classification of surfaces. (Catalog filename: homeomorphicism.)

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Definition of a homeomorphism

Let \((X, \tau_X)\) and \((Y, \tau_Y)\) be topological spaces. A map \(f : X \to Y\) is a homeomorphism if:

  1. \(f\) is bijective
  2. \(f\) is continuous (preimages of open sets are open)
  3. \(f^{-1}\) is continuous

When such an \(f\) exists, \(X\) and \(Y\) are homeomorphic, written \(X \cong Y\). They are the same topological space up to relabelling of points.

Key idea: Homeomorphism preserves all topological properties — connectedness, compactness, separation axioms, and the structure of open sets. It does not preserve metric or geometric data such as angles or distances.

Prerequisite: Topology and continuity on metric or topological spaces.

2) Homeomorphism versus other equivalences

Relation Maps Preserves
Isometry Distance-preserving bijections Metric, angles (in Riemannian setting)
Diffeomorphism Smooth bijections with smooth inverse Smooth structure, differentiable topology
Homeomorphism Continuous bijections with continuous inverse Topology only
Homotopy equivalence Maps inducing isomorphisms on homotopy groups Homotopy type (weaker than homeomorphism)
Worked example: The open interval \((0,1)\) is homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}\) via \(f(t) = \tan\!\bigl(\pi(t - \tfrac{1}{2})\bigr)\), but not isometric — lengths are distorted.
Common mistake: Assuming a continuous bijection is automatically a homeomorphism. The inverse must also be continuous; this fails for bijections between spaces with incompatible topologies.

3) Topological invariants

Properties preserved under homeomorphism are topological invariants. If \(X \cong Y\), then:

  • \(X\) is connected \(\Leftrightarrow\) \(Y\) is connected
  • \(X\) is compact \(\Leftrightarrow\) \(Y\) is compact
  • \(X\) is Hausdorff \(\Leftrightarrow\) \(Y\) is Hausdorff
  • Cardinality and weight of the topology match
Worked example: The circle \(S^1\) and the boundary of a square are homeomorphic in \(\mathbb{R}^2\). Stretch the square's perimeter continuously onto \(S^1\) — a classic "rubber-sheet" deformation that preserves open-set structure.
Algebraic invariants: Fundamental group \(\pi_1(X)\), homology \(H_n(X)\), and Euler characteristic \(\chi(X)\) are homeomorphism invariants (in fact homotopy invariants). If \(\pi_1(X) \not\cong \pi_1(Y)\), then \(X \not\cong Y\).

Related: Homotopy for weaker equivalence.

4) Standard homeomorphisms in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

Open ball \(\cong\) whole space: \(B^n(0,1) = \{x : \|x\| < 1\}\) is homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}^n\) via \(f(x) = x / (1 - \|x\|)\).
Graph of a continuous function: If \(f : X \to \mathbb{R}\) is continuous, the graph \(\Gamma_f = \{(x, f(x)) : x \in X\}\) with subspace topology is homeomorphic to \(X\) via projection \(\pi_1(x, y) = x\).
Product spaces: If \(f : X \to Y\) is a homeomorphism, then \(f \times \mathrm{id} : X \times Z \to Y \times Z\) is a homeomorphism. Hence \([0,1] \times S^1 \cong\) cylinder, and identifying opposite ends yields the torus \(T^2\).
Non-example: \(\mathbb{R}\) is not homeomorphic to \([0,1]\) — the former is not compact, the latter is. Compactness is a homeomorphism invariant.

5) Classification of compact surfaces

Every compact connected surface is homeomorphic to a connected sum of tori and projective planes, or equivalently to a sphere with handles and cross-caps. Orientable surfaces are classified by genus \(g \geq 0\):

\[ S_g = \text{sphere with } g \text{ handles} \]
Worked example: The torus \(T^2 = S^1 \times S^1\) has genus 1 and Euler characteristic \(\chi(T^2) = 0\). The sphere \(S^2\) has genus 0 and \(\chi(S^2) = 2\). Since \(\chi\) is preserved, \(S^2 \not\cong T^2\).
Möbius band: The Möbius strip is not homeomorphic to \(S^1 \times [0,1]\) (cylinder) — removing a core circle from each: the cylinder's complement is disconnected; the Möbius band's is connected.

6) Applications in analysis and geometry

Functional analysis

Homeomorphisms between Banach or Hilbert spaces are topological isomorphisms. The open mapping theorem characterises when a surjective bounded linear map is a homeomorphism.

Dynamical systems

Topological conjugacy — a homeomorphism intertwining two maps — preserves qualitative dynamics (orbit structure, recurrence) without requiring smoothness.

Manifolds

A topological manifold is locally homeomorphic to \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Coordinate charts are homeomorphisms from open subsets to \(\mathbb{R}^n\).

Engineering topology

Mesh quality and CAD surfaces: homeomorphic equivalence ensures connectivity and genus are preserved under deformation; metric distortion may still matter for FEM.

Related: Euclidean Space, Smooth Manifolds.

7) Brouwer invariance of domain and dimension

Brouwer invariance of domain: If \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n\) is open and \(f : U \to \mathbb{R}^n\) is injective and continuous, then \(f(U)\) is open and \(f\) is a homeomorphism onto its image.

Invariance of dimension: \(\mathbb{R}^m \cong \mathbb{R}^n\) (as topological spaces) only if \(m = n\). Thus topological dimension is a homeomorphism invariant — you cannot continuously and bijectively map a plane curve onto a solid region.
Research context: Classification of 3-manifolds (Perelman), knot complements, and low-dimensional topology all rely on detecting non-homeomorphism via invariants (fundamental group, homology, hyperbolic volume).
