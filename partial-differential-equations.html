Partial Differential Equations

Undergraduate guide. Classification of second-order PDEs, heat, wave, and Laplace equations, and separation of variables.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) PDEs and notation

A partial differential equation (PDE) involves partial derivatives of an unknown function \(u(x_1, x_2, \ldots)\) with respect to several independent variables. PDEs model heat flow, vibrations, electrostatics, fluid motion, and quantum systems.

Common notation: \(u_t = \partial u/\partial t\), \(u_{xx} = \partial^2 u/\partial x^2\), \(\nabla^2 u = u_{xx} + u_{yy} + u_{zz}\) (Laplacian).

Key idea: Unlike ODEs, PDEs require boundary conditions (BCs) on spatial boundaries and, for time-dependent problems, initial conditions (ICs). The solution is a function on a region (or region × time), not a curve.

Builds on Differential Equations, Multivariable Calculus, and Vector Calculus.

2) Classification: elliptic, parabolic, hyperbolic

A general second-order linear PDE in two variables has the form: \[ A\,u_{xx} + B\,u_{xy} + C\,u_{yy} + D\,u_x + E\,u_y + F\,u = G \] The discriminant \(\Delta = B^2 - 4AC\) classifies the equation at each point:

Type Condition Canonical example Typical behaviour
Elliptic \(\Delta < 0\) Laplace: \(\nabla^2 u = 0\) Equilibrium, steady state; no time evolution
Parabolic \(\Delta = 0\) Heat: \(u_t = k u_{xx}\) Diffusion, smoothing over time
Hyperbolic \(\Delta > 0\) Wave: \(u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}\) Finite-speed propagation, oscillations
Worked example: Classify \(3u_{xx} + 2u_{xy} - u_{yy} = 0\).
\(A = 3\), \(B = 2\), \(C = -1\). \(\Delta = 4 - 4(3)(-1) = 16 > 0\) — hyperbolic.

3) The heat equation

The one-dimensional heat equation is: \[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad k > 0 \] where \(u(x,t)\) is temperature (or concentration) and \(k\) is the thermal diffusivity. It is parabolic: disturbances diffuse infinitely fast in the model, smoothing sharp gradients.

Physical meaning: Heat flows from hot to cold regions at a rate proportional to the curvature \(\partial^2 u/\partial x^2\) of the temperature profile. In steady state (\(u_t = 0\)), this reduces to \(u_{xx} = 0\) (linear temperature profile in 1D).

Typical problem: rod \(0 \leq x \leq L\) with BCs such as \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\) (fixed zero temperature at ends) and IC \(u(x,0) = f(x)\).

In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), the heat equation is \(u_t = k\nabla^2 u\).

4) The wave equation

The one-dimensional wave equation is: \[ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \] where \(c\) is the wave speed. It is hyperbolic: information propagates along characteristics at speed \(\pm c\).

Physical meaning: Models vibrations of a string, sound waves, and electromagnetic waves. Requires two initial conditions: \(u(x,0)\) and \(u_t(x,0)\).
D'Alembert's solution (unbounded domain): for ICs \(u(x,0) = f(x)\), \(u_t(x,0) = g(x)\), \[ u(x,t) = \tfrac{1}{2}[f(x-ct) + f(x+ct)] + \frac{1}{2c}\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct} g(s)\,ds \] shows left- and right-travelling waves superposed.

Fixed-end string: \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\) — standing-wave modes arise from separation of variables (section 6).

5) Laplace's equation

Laplace's equation is: \[ \nabla^2 u = 0 \] In two dimensions: \(u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0\). It is elliptic and governs steady-state problems where a potential satisfies equilibrium.

Dirichlet problem

Specify \(u\) on the boundary \(\partial\Omega\); find harmonic \(u\) inside \(\Omega\).

Physical contexts

Electrostatic potential in charge-free regions, steady temperature distribution, incompressible irrotational flow velocity potential.

Mean value property: A harmonic function equals the average of its values on any circle (2D) or sphere (3D) centred at the point. Maximum principle: non-constant harmonic functions attain max/min only on the boundary.
Worked example: On the rectangle \(0 \leq x \leq a\), \(0 \leq y \leq b\), with \(u(0,y) = u(a,y) = 0\), \(u(x,0) = 0\), \(u(x,b) = f(x)\), separation of variables yields sine-series solutions in \(x\).

6) Separation of variables

Separation of variables assumes \(u(x,t) = X(x)\,T(t)\) (or \(u(x,y) = X(x)Y(y)\)) and splits the PDE into ODEs linked by a separation constant (often an eigenvalue \(\lambda\)).

Method outline:

  1. Substitute the product form into the PDE.
  2. Separate functions of different variables; introduce separation constant.
  3. Solve the spatial ODE with BCs — non-trivial solutions exist only for certain \(\lambda_n\) (eigenvalues).
  4. Solve the temporal (or remaining) ODE for each \(\lambda_n\).
  5. Superpose: \(u = \sum_n C_n X_n(x) T_n(t)\) and fit ICs via Fourier series.
Worked example (heat equation): \(u_t = k u_{xx}\), \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\), \(u(x,0) = f(x)\).
Let \(u = X(x)T(t)\): \(\dfrac{T'}{kT} = \dfrac{X''}{X} = -\lambda\).
With BCs: \(X_n(x) = \sin\dfrac{n\pi x}{L}\), \(\lambda_n = \left(\dfrac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2\).
\(T_n(t) = e^{-k\lambda_n t}\). Solution: \(u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\dfrac{n\pi x}{L}\, e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}\).

Eigenfunction expansions connect directly to Sturm–Liouville Problems.

7) Boundary and initial conditions

Condition type Specification Typical use
Dirichlet \(u\) given on boundary Fixed temperature, fixed potential
Neumann \(\partial u/\partial n\) given Fixed heat flux, insulated boundary
Robin (mixed) \(\alpha u + \beta\,\partial u/\partial n = \gamma\) Convective heat transfer at surface
Initial \(u\), \(u_t\) at \(t = 0\) Wave equation; parabolic needs \(u(x,0)\) only
Well-posedness: A problem is well-posed (Hadamard) if a solution exists, is unique, and depends continuously on the data. Ill-posed problems (e.g. backwards heat equation) are sensitive to measurement error.
Compatibility: For PDEs with mixed BCs, corner conditions must be consistent (e.g. \(f(0) = 0\) if \(u(0,t) = 0\) for all \(t\) and \(u(x,0) = f(x)\)).
