Projective Variety

Postgraduate guide. Projective space, homogeneous coordinates, Zariski topology, Nullstellensatz, and geometric properties of varieties.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Projective space and homogeneous coordinates

Projective space \(\mathbb{P}^n_k\) over a field \(k\) is the set of lines through the origin in \(k^{n+1}\). Points are written in homogeneous coordinates \([x_0 : x_1 : \cdots : x_n]\), defined up to scaling by nonzero \(\lambda \in k^\times\).

Affine charts: On \(U_i = \{x_i \neq 0\}\), set coordinates \(t_j = x_j / x_i\) (\(j \neq i\)) to identify \(U_i \cong \mathbb{A}^n_k\). Projective space is covered by \(n+1\) affine charts.
Key idea: Projective space compactifies affine space: parallel lines meet at points at infinity, eliminating many special cases in intersection theory.

Prerequisite: Algebraic Geometry and Polynomials.

2) Projective varieties and homogeneous ideals

A projective variety is the zero locus in \(\mathbb{P}^n\) of homogeneous polynomials \(f_1, \ldots, f_r \in k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]\): \[ V(I) = \{[x] \in \mathbb{P}^n : f([x]) = 0 \text{ for all } f \in I\} \] where \(I\) is a homogeneous ideal. Evaluation is well-defined because homogeneous polynomials satisfy \(f(\lambda x) = \lambda^d f(x)\).

Examples:
  • Linear subspaces: \(V(\langle L_1, \ldots, L_{n-k}\rangle)\) of dimension \(k\).
  • Fermat curve: \(x_0^n + x_1^n + x_2^n = 0\) in \(\mathbb{P}^2\).
  • Veronese embedding: \(v_d: \mathbb{P}^n \to \mathbb{P}^N\) via degree-\(d\) monomials.

3) Zariski topology and irreducibility

Closed sets in \(\mathbb{P}^n\) are exactly projective varieties. This Zariski topology is coarser than the Euclidean topology and encodes algebraic structure. A nonempty variety is irreducible if it cannot be written as a union of two proper closed subvarieties.

Projective Nullstellensatz: For a homogeneous ideal \(I \subset k[x_0,\ldots,x_n]\), \[ I(V(I)) = \sqrt{I} \] (with the convention that \(I(\emptyset) = (x_0, \ldots, x_n)\)). Radical ideals correspond to varieties.
Convention: Distinguish \(\mathbb{P}^n\) from its affine cone \(\mathbb{A}^{n+1} \setminus \{0\}\). Homogeneous ideals must be generated in degree \(\geq 1\) to avoid spurious components at the origin.

4) Dimension, degree, and Hilbert polynomial

The Krull dimension of \(V = V(I)\) equals the transcendence degree of its function field \(k(V)\) over \(k\). For a hypersurface \(V(f)\) with \(f\) irreducible of degree \(d\), the degree is \(\deg V = d\).

Hilbert polynomial: For a projective variety \(X \subset \mathbb{P}^n\), the Hilbert function \(h_X(m) = \dim_k H^0(X, \mathcal{O}_X(m))\) is eventually a polynomial whose degree equals \(\dim X\) and whose leading coefficient encodes degree and volume data.
Worked example: A plane curve \(C \subset \mathbb{P}^2\) of degree \(d\) has Hilbert polynomial \(P_C(m) = dm - \frac{d(d-1)}{2} + 1\) (genus formula for smooth curves).

5) Bézout's theorem and intersections

In \(\mathbb{P}^2_k\) over an algebraically closed field, two curves of degrees \(d\) and \(e\) without common components intersect in exactly \(de\) points counted with multiplicity (including complex points and points at infinity).

Intersection multiplicity: Tangency and higher-order contact increase multiplicity; Bézout's theorem counts these weighted contributions.
Worked example: A line (\(d=1\)) and a conic (\(d=2\)) meet in 2 points in \(\mathbb{P}^2_{\mathbb{C}}\), unless the line is tangent (multiplicity 2 at one point).

Related: Grobner Basis for computing intersection ideals.

6) Maps, rational functions, and birational geometry

A regular map \(\varphi: X \to Y\) between varieties is given locally by homogeneous polynomials of the same degree. A rational map is defined on a dense open set and may have indeterminacy loci.

Function field: \(k(X)\) consists of ratios \(f/g\) of homogeneous polynomials of the same degree, modulo the ideal of \(X\). Birational equivalence means \(k(X) \cong k(Y)\).

Blow-ups

Resolve singularities and indeterminacy by replacing a point with a projective space of directions.

Rational normal curves

The Veronese image of \(\mathbb{P}^1\) is a rational curve of degree \(d\) in \(\mathbb{P}^d\).

7) Applications

Coding theory

Algebraic-geometry codes use points on curves over finite fields in \(\mathbb{P}^n_{\mathbb{F}_q}\).

Computer vision

Multiple-view geometry and camera calibration use projective invariants.

Cryptography

Elliptic curves are smooth plane cubics; pairings use divisor theory on projective curves.

String theory

Calabi–Yau varieties in projective spaces model compact extra dimensions.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Define \(\mathbb{P}^n_k\) and explain homogeneous coordinates.
  2. Why must defining polynomials be homogeneous in projective geometry?
  3. State the projective Nullstellensatz.
  4. What does Bézout's theorem predict for a cubic and a line in \(\mathbb{P}^2\)?
  5. Define irreducibility of a variety.
  6. How does the Hilbert polynomial encode dimension?
  7. Give an example of a birational but non-isomorphic pair of varieties.

For marking: work over algebraically closed fields when applying Nullstellensatz and Bézout; distinguish affine and projective charts.
