Divergence Theorem

The Divergence Theorem (also called Gauss’s Theorem) relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence of the field inside the volume enclosed by that surface.

Mathematical Statement

Let \( \mathbf{F} = P\hat{i} + Q\hat{j} + R\hat{k} \) be a continuously differentiable vector field over a solid region \( V \) with boundary surface \( S \), then:

\[ \iint_{S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_{V} (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}) \, dV \]

Where the divergence is:

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z} \]

Interpretation

It says the total outward flow of a vector field through a closed surface is equal to the sum of all sources (divergence) inside the volume.

Example

Let \( \mathbf{F}(x, y, z) = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j} + z\hat{k} \), and \( V \) be the unit ball \( x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq 1 \).

\[ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 \]

\[ \iiint_V 3\, dV = 3 \cdot \text{Vol}(V) = 3 \cdot \frac{4}{3} \pi = 4\pi \]

So the flux through the surface is \( 4\pi \).

Applications

  • Electromagnetism (Gauss’s Law)
  • Fluid flow analysis
  • Thermodynamics and heat transfer
