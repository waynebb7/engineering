Eigenvalues

Undergraduate guide. The characteristic equation, algebraic and geometric multiplicity, and diagonalisation.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Definition and the characteristic equation

Let \(\mathbf{A}\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix over \(\mathbb{R}\) or \(\mathbb{C}\). A scalar \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(\mathbf{A}\) if there exists a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) such that: \[ \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v} \] Equivalently, \((\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\) has a non-trivial solution, so: \[ \det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0 \] This is the characteristic equation. The polynomial \(p(\lambda) = \det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\) is the characteristic polynomial, of degree \(n\).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\): \[ \det\begin{pmatrix} 4-\lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 5) \] Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = 2\) and \(\lambda = 5\).

Builds on Matrices and Linear Transformations. Eigenvectors are covered in Eigenvectors.

2) Finding eigenvalues

Standard method:

  1. Compute \(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\).
  2. Expand to obtain a polynomial in \(\lambda\).
  3. Solve \(p(\lambda) = 0\) (factorisation, quadratic formula, or numerical methods for large \(n\)).
Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) (90° rotation): \[ \det\begin{pmatrix} -\lambda & -1 \\ 1 & -\lambda \end{pmatrix} = \lambda^2 + 1 = 0 \] Eigenvalues: \(\lambda = i\) and \(\lambda = -i\) over \(\mathbb{C}\). No real eigenvalues — rotation has no invariant direction in \(\mathbb{R}^2\).
Useful identities: For an \(n \times n\) matrix:
  • Sum of eigenvalues (with multiplicity) \(= \text{tr}(\mathbf{A})\)
  • Product of eigenvalues \(= \det(\mathbf{A})\)

3) Algebraic multiplicity

The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue \(\lambda\) is its multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial \(p(\lambda)\).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\): \[ p(\lambda) = (2 - \lambda)^2 \] Eigenvalue \(\lambda = 2\) has algebraic multiplicity 2.

The sum of algebraic multiplicities over all distinct eigenvalues equals \(n\) (the degree of \(p\)), counting complex eigenvalues and multiplicities in \(\mathbb{C}\).

Common mistake: Confusing the number of distinct eigenvalues with the sum of multiplicities. A \(3 \times 3\) matrix can have one eigenvalue with algebraic multiplicity 3.

4) Geometric multiplicity

The geometric multiplicity of \(\lambda\) is the dimension of the eigenspace: \[ E_\lambda = \ker(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = \{\mathbf{v} : \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}\} \] It equals the number of linearly independent eigenvectors for \(\lambda\).

Worked example: For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), solve \((\mathbf{A} - 2\mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\): \[ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow y = 0 \] Eigenspace \(= \text{span}\{(1,0)^T\}\), so geometric multiplicity \(= 1\).
Fundamental inequality: For every eigenvalue \(\lambda\): \[ 1 \leq \text{geometric mult.}(\lambda) \leq \text{algebraic mult.}(\lambda) \]

5) Diagonalisation

\(\mathbf{A}\) is diagonalisable if \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{P}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{P}^{-1}\) where \(\mathbf{D}\) is diagonal. This holds if and only if \(\mathbf{A}\) has \(n\) linearly independent eigenvectors — equivalently, geometric multiplicity equals algebraic multiplicity for every eigenvalue.

Diagonalisation test:
  • Compute all eigenvalues and their algebraic multiplicities.
  • For each \(\lambda\), find \(\dim E_\lambda\).
  • If \(\sum \dim E_\lambda = n\), diagonalisable; form \(\mathbf{P}\) from eigenvector basis.
Worked example: \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) has \(\lambda = 2, 5\), each with algebraic multiplicity 1. Two distinct eigenvalues in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) \(\Rightarrow\) automatically diagonalisable (geometric mult. \(= 1\) for each, total \(= 2\)).

When diagonalisation fails, see Jordan Normal Form.

6) Similar matrices and invariants

Matrices \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are similar if \(\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{P}^{-1}\mathbf{A}\mathbf{P}\) for some invertible \(\mathbf{P}\). Similar matrices share:

  • Eigenvalues (with the same algebraic multiplicities)
  • Trace: \(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = \text{tr}(\mathbf{B})\)
  • Determinant: \(\det(\mathbf{A}) = \det(\mathbf{B})\)
  • Characteristic polynomial
Worked example: If \(\mathbf{A}\) has eigenvalues \(1, 3, -2\), then \(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = 1 + 3 + (-2) = 2\) and \(\det(\mathbf{A}) = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot (-2) = -6\). Any matrix similar to \(\mathbf{A}\) has the same trace and determinant.
Exam tip: Use trace and determinant as quick checks when verifying eigenvalue calculations.

7) Symmetric matrices and the spectral theorem

If \(\mathbf{A}\) is real and symmetric (\(\mathbf{A}^T = \mathbf{A}\)), then:

  • All eigenvalues are real
  • \(\mathbf{A}\) is always diagonalisable
  • Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal

The spectral theorem states \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{Q}^T\) where \(\mathbf{Q}\) is orthogonal (\(\mathbf{Q}^T = \mathbf{Q}^{-1}\)) and \(\mathbf{D}\) is diagonal.

Worked example: \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) is symmetric. \(p(\lambda) = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3)\). Eigenvalues \(1\) and \(3\) are real; corresponding eigenvectors \((1,-1)^T\) and \((1,1)^T\) are orthogonal.

This is pure mathematics — engineering applications appear separately in Eigenvalues (Electrical Engineering).

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. State the characteristic equation for eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of matrix \(\mathbf{A}\).
  2. Find eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\).
  3. For \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\), state algebraic and geometric multiplicities of \(\lambda = 3\).
  4. Is \(\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\) diagonalisable? Justify using multiplicities.
  5. If \(\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = 5\) and \(\det(\mathbf{A}) = 6\) for a \(2 \times 2\) matrix, find eigenvalues.
  6. Why must a symmetric matrix have real eigenvalues (informal argument)?
  7. How many eigenvalues (counting multiplicity) does a \(4 \times 4\) matrix have over \(\mathbb{C}\)?

For marking: show \(\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\) expansion; state both multiplicities explicitly.
