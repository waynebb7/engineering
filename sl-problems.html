Sturm–Liouville Problems

Undergraduate guide. Sturm–Liouville form, eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, and an introduction to orthogonal expansions.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) The Sturm–Liouville form

A Sturm–Liouville (S–L) problem is a second-order linear ODE in self-adjoint form: \[ \frac{d}{dx}\left[p(x)\,\frac{dy}{dx}\right] + \left[q(x) + \lambda\,r(x)\right]y = 0, \quad a \leq x \leq b \] with homogeneous boundary conditions at \(x = a\) and \(x = b\). Here \(\lambda\) is a parameter (the eigenvalue), and typically \(p(x) > 0\), \(r(x) > 0\) on \([a,b]\).

Key idea: Only certain values \(\lambda_n\) (eigenvalues) admit non-trivial solutions \(y_n(x)\) (eigenfunctions) satisfying the boundary conditions. This structure underlies separation of variables for PDEs.

Builds on Differential Equations, Eigenvalues, and Integration.

2) The eigenvalue problem

Rewriting the S–L equation: \[ p(x)y'' + p'(x)y' + [q(x) + \lambda r(x)]y = 0 \] we seek pairs \((\lambda_n, y_n)\) with \(y_n \not\equiv 0\) satisfying the BCs. The Sturm–Liouville theorem guarantees (under standard conditions):

  • Eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\) are real and form an infinite sequence \(\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < \cdots \to \infty\)
  • Eigenfunctions \(y_n\) corresponding to distinct \(\lambda_n\) are orthogonal with weight \(r(x)\)
  • The \(y_n\) form a complete basis for expanding functions on \([a,b]\)
Worked example (preview): For \(y'' + \lambda y = 0\), \(y(0) = y(L) = 0\), non-trivial solutions require \(\lambda_n = (n\pi/L)^2\), \(y_n = \sin(n\pi x/L)\).

3) Eigenfunctions

An eigenfunction \(y_n(x)\) is a non-trivial solution for a specific eigenvalue \(\lambda_n\). Each eigenfunction is determined up to a scalar multiple; normalisation fixes the scale.

Problem BCs Eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\) Eigenfunctions
\(y'' + \lambda y = 0\) \(y(0)=y(L)=0\) \((n\pi/L)^2\), \(n=1,2,\ldots\) \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\)
\(y'' + \lambda y = 0\) \(y'(0)=y'(L)=0\) \((n\pi/L)^2\), \(n=0,1,\ldots\) \(\cos(n\pi x/L)\)
\(x^2 y'' + xy' + \lambda y = 0\) Bounded at \(0\), \(y(1)=0\) \(\lambda_n = \alpha_n^2\) (Bessel zeros) \(J_0(\alpha_n x)\)
Common mistake: Including \(n = 0\) for Dirichlet BCs — \(\sin(0) = 0\) gives only the trivial solution.

4) Orthogonality

Eigenfunctions \(y_m, y_n\) (\(m \neq n\)) are orthogonal with weight \(r(x)\): \[ \int_a^b r(x)\,y_m(x)\,y_n(x)\,dx = 0 \] For the standard case \(r(x) = 1\), this is ordinary \(L^2\) inner product orthogonality.

Worked example: Show \(\sin(m\pi x/L)\) and \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\) are orthogonal on \([0,L]\) for \(m \neq n\).
Use \(\sin m\theta\sin n\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(m-n)\theta - \cos(m+n)\theta]\).
Integrating from \(0\) to \(L\) with \(\theta = \pi x/L\) gives zero when \(m \neq n\).

The norm of \(y_n\) is \(\|y_n\|^2 = \int_a^b r(x)\,y_n^2\,dx\). The normalised eigenfunction is \(\phi_n = y_n / \|y_n\|\).

5) Orthogonal expansions — introduction

A function \(f(x)\) on \([a,b]\) can be expanded in eigenfunctions: \[ f(x) \sim \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} c_n\,y_n(x) \] Using orthogonality, the coefficients are: \[ c_n = \frac{\int_a^b r(x)\,f(x)\,y_n(x)\,dx}{\int_a^b r(x)\,y_n^2(x)\,dx} \]

Worked example: Expand \(f(x) = 1\) on \([0,L]\) in \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\).
\(\int_0^L \sin(n\pi x/L)\,dx = \dfrac{L}{n\pi}[1 - (-1)^n]\).
\(\int_0^L \sin^2(n\pi x/L)\,dx = L/2\).
So \(c_n = \dfrac{2}{n\pi}[1-(-1)^n]\): only odd \(n\) contribute, giving the Fourier sine series of a constant.

Fourier sine and cosine series are special cases. See Fourier Series for broader theory.

6) Physical examples

Vibrating string

Wave equation \(u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}\) with fixed ends \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\). Separation gives \(y'' + \lambda y = 0\), yielding standing-wave modes \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\) with frequencies \(\omega_n = n\pi c/L\).

Heat conduction

\(u_t = k u_{xx}\) on a rod with ends held at zero temperature. Eigenfunctions \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\) decay at rates \(k(n\pi/L)^2\). Initial temperature \(f(x)\) is expanded in the eigenfunction basis.

Quantum mechanics: Time-independent Schrödinger equation on a finite interval with BCs is an S–L problem; quantised energy levels are eigenvalues.

Cylindrical and spherical problems lead to Bessel and Legendre equations — classical S–L problems with \(p(x) = x\) or \(p(x) = 1-x^2\).

7) Boundary condition types

Homogeneous BCs for S–L theory take the general form: \[ \alpha_1 y(a) + \alpha_2 y'(a) = 0, \qquad \beta_1 y(b) + \beta_2 y'(b) = 0 \]

Type Condition Effect on eigenvalues
Dirichlet \(y = 0\) at endpoint Quantises \(\lambda\); sine-type modes
Neumann \(y' = 0\) at endpoint Includes \(\lambda = 0\) (constant mode) for Neumann–Neumann
Robin (mixed) \(\alpha y + \beta y' = 0\) Eigenvalues solve transcendental equations (e.g. \(\tan\alpha L = \cdots\))
Periodic \(y(a) = y(b)\), \(y'(a) = y'(b)\) Both sine and cosine modes; \(\lambda_n = (2n\pi/L)^2\)
Regular vs singular endpoints: At singular points (e.g. \(x = 0\) for Bessel's equation), BCs are replaced by boundedness or limit conditions. The S–L theory extends with appropriate modifications.
