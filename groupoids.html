Groupoids

Postgraduate guide. Partial composition, units and inverses, examples from topology and symmetry, and links to higher category theory.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Definition of a groupoid

A groupoid \(\mathcal{G}\) consists of a set of objects \(\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{G})\), a set of morphisms (or arrows) \(\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{G})\), and structure maps:

  • Source and target: \(\mathrm{src}, \mathrm{tgt} : \mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{G}) \to \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{G})\)
  • Identity: for each object \(x\), a morphism \(1_x : x \to x\)
  • Partial composition: if \(f : x \to y\) and \(g : y \to z\), then \(g \circ f : x \to z\) exists
  • Inverses: every morphism \(f : x \to y\) has \(f^{-1} : y \to x\) with \(f^{-1} \circ f = 1_x\) and \(f \circ f^{-1} = 1_y\)

Composition is associative wherever defined. Unlike a group, not every pair of morphisms can be composed — they must be composable (the target of the first equals the source of the second).

Key idea: A groupoid is a category in which every morphism is invertible. A group is a groupoid with exactly one object.

Prerequisite: Group Theory and basic category-theoretic language.

2) Groupoids versus groups and categories

Structure Objects Composition Inverses
Group One object (implicit) Always defined All elements
Groupoid Many objects Only when \(\mathrm{tgt}(f) = \mathrm{src}(g)\) All morphisms
Category Many objects Partial (as above) Not required
Worked example: The discrete groupoid on a set \(X\): objects are elements of \(X\), and the only morphisms are identities \(1_x\). This encodes a set with no non-trivial arrows.
Hom-groupoid of a group: Given a group \(G\), form a groupoid with one object \(*\) and \(\mathrm{Mor}(*,*) = G\). Composition is the group multiplication. Every group arises this way.

3) The fundamental groupoid

For a topological space \(X\) and basepoint \(x_0\), the fundamental group \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) records loops based at \(x_0\). The fundamental groupoid \(\Pi_1(X)\) generalises this:

  • Objects: points of \(X\)
  • Morphisms \(x \to y\): homotopy classes of paths from \(x\) to \(y\)
  • Composition: concatenation of paths (up to homotopy)

Fixing \(x_0\), the endomorphism group \(\mathrm{Aut}_{\Pi_1(X)}(x_0)\) is exactly \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\). The groupoid viewpoint removes dependence on a chosen basepoint when comparing paths between different points.

Worked example: On the circle \(S^1\), paths from \(x\) to \(y\) are classified by winding number relative to a chosen orientation. \(\Pi_1(S^1)\) is equivalent to the groupoid with objects \(\mathbb{Z}\) and unique morphism \(n \to m\) for each integer (encoding net winding).
Convention: Path concatenation order varies by author. Always state whether \(g \circ f\) means "first \(f\), then \(g\)" or the reverse.

4) Equivalence and isomorphism of groupoids

A functor \(F : \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{H}\) between groupoids assigns objects to objects and morphisms to morphisms, preserving sources, targets, identities, composition, and inverses.

A functor is an equivalence of groupoids if it is fully faithful and essentially surjective: every object of \(\mathcal{H}\) is isomorphic to \(F(x)\) for some \(x \in \mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{G})\).

Worked example: The inclusion \(\{x_0\} \hookrightarrow X\) induces a functor \(\pi_1(X, x_0) \to \Pi_1(X)\) (one-object groupoid to full fundamental groupoid). For path-connected \(X\), this is an equivalence — the groupoid captures the same homotopy information without pinning a basepoint.

Related: Homotopy and Topology.

5) Lie groupoids and differentiable structure

A Lie groupoid is a groupoid where \(\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{G})\) and \(\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{G})\) are smooth manifolds, source/target are submersions, and composition and inversion are smooth.

Examples:
  • Pair groupoid of a manifold \(M\): objects are points of \(M\), morphisms \(x \to y\) are ordered pairs — composition is only defined when targets match sources trivially.
  • Action groupoid \(G \ltimes M\): objects are points of \(M\), morphisms encode \(g \cdot x \to y\) for \(g \in G\).
Research context: Lie groupoids underpin symplectic groupoids, Poisson geometry, and the Baum–Connes conjecture. They generalise Lie groups the way groupoids generalise groups.

6) Groupoids in physics and symmetry

Gauge symmetries

In gauge theory, gauge transformations between different field configurations form a groupoid structure: composition is defined only when configurations match on overlaps.

Phase space

Symplectic groupoids encode Poisson brackets and Hamiltonian flows; momentum maps arise as groupoid morphisms.

Algebraic topology

Higher homotopy groupoids (\(\Pi_n\)) and crossed modules appear in obstruction theory and non-abelian cohomology.

Algebra

Groupoid algebras (convolution algebras over \(\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{G})\)) generalise group algebras and appear in representation theory.

Related: E7 Lie Groups, Infinitesimal Transformations.

7) Research directions and higher structures

Groupoids sit at the base of a hierarchy: 2-groupoids (groupoids enriched over groupoids), \(\infty\)-groupoids (every morphism invertible up to coherent higher homotopy), and stacks (sheaves of groupoids). The homotopy hypothesis identifies \(\infty\)-groupoids with topological spaces up to weak homotopy equivalence.

Key theorem (Brown, 1967): The fundamental groupoid \(\Pi_1(X)\) determines the homotopy type of a connected CW-complex whose homotopy groups vanish above dimension 1. For general spaces, \(\Pi_1\) captures first homotopy data but not higher cells.
Common mistake: Treating a groupoid as a group and composing arbitrary morphisms. Always verify \(\mathrm{tgt}(f) = \mathrm{src}(g)\) before forming \(g \circ f\).

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. State the four axioms defining a groupoid and explain why composition is partial.
  2. Show that a group is a groupoid with one object. What are the morphisms?
  3. Define the fundamental groupoid \(\Pi_1(X)\). How does \(\pi_1(X, x_0)\) embed in it?
  4. Give an example of two morphisms that cannot be composed in a non-trivial groupoid.
  5. What does it mean for a functor between groupoids to be an equivalence?
  6. Define a Lie groupoid and give one example beyond a Lie group.
  7. Explain why the fundamental groupoid is more natural than \(\pi_1\) for a space with many basepoints.

For marking: cite composability conditions, state functor properties, and connect examples to topology where relevant.
