Series

Undergraduate guide. Sequences, arithmetic and geometric series, sigma notation, and an introduction to convergence.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Sequences

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers \(\{a_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty}\), where each term \(a_n\) is determined by a rule. In engineering mathematics, sequences model discrete time steps, iterative approximations, and sampled data.

Limit of a sequence: We write \(a_n \to L\) as \(n \to \infty\) if the terms approach the finite value \(L\). If no finite limit exists, the sequence diverges.
Worked example: For \(a_n = \dfrac{2n + 1}{n}\), find \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n\).
\(a_n = 2 + \dfrac{1}{n} \to 2\) as \(n \to \infty\).

Sequences underpin Limits and lead directly to Taylor Series and Convergence theory.

2) Sigma notation

The Greek letter \(\Sigma\) denotes summation. The expression \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=m}^{n} f(k)\) means add \(f(m) + f(m+1) + \cdots + f(n)\).

Rule Formula
Linearity \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} (af(k) + bg(k)) = a\sum_{k=1}^{n} f(k) + b\sum_{k=1}^{n} g(k)\)
Sum of first \(n\) integers \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\)
Sum of first \(n\) squares \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}\)
Index shift \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n} a_k = \displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n+1} a_{k-1}\)
Worked example: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{5} (3k - 1)\).
\(= 3\sum_{k=1}^{5} k - \sum_{k=1}^{5} 1 = 3 \cdot \dfrac{5 \cdot 6}{2} - 5 = 40\).
Common mistake: Confusing the index of summation with the number of terms. \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=3}^{10} 1\) has \(10 - 3 + 1 = 8\) terms, not 10.

3) Arithmetic series

An arithmetic sequence has a constant difference \(d\) between consecutive terms: \(a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d\). The corresponding arithmetic series sums these terms.

Sum formula: \[ S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k = \frac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n) = \frac{n}{2}\bigl[2a_1 + (n-1)d\bigr] \]
Worked example: Sum the first 20 terms of \(5, 8, 11, \ldots\)
Here \(a_1 = 5\), \(d = 3\), \(a_{20} = 5 + 19(3) = 62\).
\(S_{20} = \dfrac{20}{2}(5 + 62) = 670\).

Arithmetic series model linear growth: stepwise voltage ramps, uniform acceleration segments, and simple financial schedules.

4) Geometric series

A geometric sequence has a constant ratio \(r\) between consecutive terms: \(a_n = a_1 r^{n-1}\). The finite sum is:

Finite geometric sum (\(r \neq 1\)): \[ S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_1 r^{k-1} = a_1 \cdot \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} \]
Worked example: Sum the first 6 terms of \(3 + 6 + 12 + \cdots\)
\(a_1 = 3\), \(r = 2\). \(S_6 = 3 \cdot \dfrac{1 - 2^6}{1 - 2} = 3 \cdot 63 = 189\).

When \(r > 1\)

Terms grow; the finite sum is dominated by the largest term.

When \(0 < r < 1\)

Terms decay; the infinite sum converges (next section).

5) Sum to infinity

An infinite geometric series \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} ar^k\) converges to a finite sum if and only if \(|r| < 1\):

Sum to infinity: For \(|r| < 1\), \[ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} ar^k = \frac{a}{1 - r} \]
Worked example: Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{3^k}\).
Here \(a = 1\), \(r = \dfrac{1}{3}\). Sum \(= \dfrac{1}{1 - 1/3} = \dfrac{3}{2}\).
Common mistake: Applying the sum-to-infinity formula when \(|r| \geq 1\). For example, \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} 2^k\) diverges — the partial sums grow without bound.

6) Introduction to convergence

An infinite series \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a_k\) is the limit of its partial sums \(S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k\). The series converges if \(S_n \to S\) for some finite \(S\); otherwise it diverges.

Necessary condition for convergence: If \(\sum a_k\) converges, then \(a_k \to 0\).
Warning: The converse is false — \(a_k \to 0\) does not guarantee convergence (e.g. the harmonic series).

Harmonic series (diverges)

\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{k}\) diverges, even though \(\dfrac{1}{k} \to 0\).

\(p\)-series test

\(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{k^p}\) converges if \(p > 1\), diverges if \(p \leq 1\).

Deeper convergence tests (ratio, comparison, integral) are covered in Convergence and underpin Taylor Series radius of convergence.

7) Engineering applications

Series appear wherever engineers sum discrete contributions: digital signal samples, iterative algorithm error terms, and feedback-system responses.

Digital signal processing

A sampled signal \(x[n] = \sum_k x_k \delta[n-k]\) is a discrete sum. Z-transforms convert series to functions for filter design.

RC charging circuit

Iterative numerical solutions approximate the exponential charging curve as a sum of geometric terms, linking discrete steps to continuous models.

Worked example: A voltage divider network with repeated stages attenuates by factor \(r = 0.5\) each stage. Total signal from an infinite cascade: \(V_{\text{out}} = V_{\text{in}} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \dfrac{V_{\text{in}}}{1 - 0.5} = 2V_{\text{in}}\) (idealised model).

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Write \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=2}^{6} k^2\) as an explicit sum and evaluate it.
  2. Find the sum of the first 15 terms of the arithmetic series \(4, 7, 10, \ldots\)
  3. Find the sum of the first 8 terms of \(2 + 6 + 18 + \cdots\)
  4. Evaluate \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{5}{4^k}\).
  5. Does \(\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{k^2}\) converge? Justify briefly.
  6. Find \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{3n^2 + 1}{n^2 - 4}\).
  7. State the necessary condition for convergence of an infinite series.

For marking: show formula substitution, state whether a geometric series converges before summing to infinity, and cite the relevant test for convergence questions.
