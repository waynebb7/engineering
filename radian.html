Radian

A-Level guide. Radian measure, arc length \(s = r\theta\), sector area, and converting between degrees and radians.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) What is a radian?

A radian is an angle measure based on arc length. One radian is the angle subtended at the centre of a circle when the arc length equals the radius:

\[ 1\,\text{rad} = \text{angle where arc length } s = r \]

A full turn (360°) corresponds to circumference \(2\pi r\), so the angle is \(\frac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi\) radians. Therefore:

\[ 360° = 2\pi\,\text{rad}, \qquad 180° = \pi\,\text{rad} \]

Radians are the natural unit for calculus and trigonometry at A-Level. See Unit Circle and Trig Functions.

2) Converting degrees and radians

To convert, use the proportion \(\frac{\text{degrees}}{180°} = \frac{\text{radians}}{\pi}\).

Degrees → radians

Multiply by \(\frac{\pi}{180°}\).

\(90° = 90 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{2}\,\text{rad}\)

\(60° = \frac{\pi}{3}\,\text{rad}\), \(45° = \frac{\pi}{4}\,\text{rad}\), \(30° = \frac{\pi}{6}\,\text{rad}\)

Radians → degrees

Multiply by \(\frac{180°}{\pi}\).

\(\frac{2\pi}{3}\,\text{rad} = \frac{2\pi}{3} \times \frac{180°}{\pi} = 120°\)

\(\frac{5\pi}{6}\,\text{rad} = 150°\), \(\frac{7\pi}{4}\,\text{rad} = 315°\)

Key angles to know: \(\frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{3}, \frac{\pi}{2}, \pi, \frac{3\pi}{2}, 2\pi\) and their degree equivalents.

Calculators: Degrees to Radians and Radians to Degrees converters are available in the hub.

3) Arc length

For a circle of radius \(r\) and central angle \(\theta\) measured in radians, the arc length is:

\[ s = r\theta \]
Worked example: Find the arc length subtending \(1.5\,\text{rad}\) on a circle of radius \(8\,\text{cm}\).
\(s = r\theta = 8 \times 1.5 = 12\,\text{cm}\).
Worked example: A sector has radius \(5\,\text{m}\) and arc length \(7\,\text{m}\). Find \(\theta\) in radians.
\(7 = 5\theta \Rightarrow \theta = 1.4\,\text{rad}\).
Common mistake: Using \(s = r\theta\) when \(\theta\) is in degrees. Convert to radians first, or use \(s = \frac{\theta}{360°} \times 2\pi r\).

4) Sector area

The area of a sector with radius \(r\) and angle \(\theta\) (in radians) is:

\[ A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \]

This follows from the fraction \(\frac{\theta}{2\pi}\) of the full circle area \(\pi r^2\): \(A = \frac{\theta}{2\pi} \times \pi r^2 = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta\).

Worked example: Find the area of a sector with \(r = 6\,\text{cm}\) and \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}\,\text{rad}\).
\(A = \frac{1}{2} \times 6^2 \times \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{1}{2} \times 36 \times \frac{\pi}{3} = 6\pi\,\text{cm}^2\).

Segment area: Area of segment = area of sector − area of triangle. For a sector with chord, use \(A_{\text{triangle}} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\sin\theta\).

5) Chord length and segment problems

Related formulae often appear in A-Level geometry questions combined with trigonometry.

Chord length (subtending angle \(\theta\) at centre): \(c = 2r\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\)

Strategy

  1. Draw a clear diagram with radius and angle marked
  2. Confirm \(\theta\) is in radians before using \(s = r\theta\) or \(A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta\)
  3. Split complex shapes into sectors and triangles

Example

A lawn is a sector of radius \(10\,\text{m}\) and angle \(0.8\,\text{rad}\). Arc length \(= 10 \times 0.8 = 8\,\text{m}\). Area \(= \frac{1}{2}(100)(0.8) = 40\,\text{m}^2\).

6) Angular speed

In circular motion, angular speed \(\omega\) (in rad s\(^{-1}\)) links angle, time, and linear speed.

\[ \omega = \frac{\theta}{t}, \qquad v = r\omega \]
Worked example: A wheel of radius \(0.4\,\text{m}\) rotates at \(15\,\text{rad s}^{-1}\). Linear speed at the rim: \(v = 0.4 \times 15 = 6\,\text{m s}^{-1}\).

See Circular Motion for fuller mechanics treatment. Frequency \(f\) and \(\omega\) are related by \(\omega = 2\pi f\).

7) Small-angle approximations

For small angles \(\theta\) in radians (typically \(|\theta| < 0.1\)), useful approximations arise from calculus:

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta, \qquad \cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2}, \qquad \tan\theta \approx \theta \]

These are used in physics for pendulums, optics, and vibration analysis, and are justified rigorously using Limits and differentiation of trig functions.

Exam tip: Approximations require \(\theta\) in radians. Using degrees gives wrong results.

8) Quick knowledge check

  1. Convert \(135°\) to radians, giving an exact value.
  2. Convert \(\frac{5\pi}{4}\,\text{rad}\) to degrees.
  3. Find the arc length when \(r = 12\,\text{cm}\) and \(\theta = \frac{2\pi}{3}\,\text{rad}\).
  4. Find the area of a sector with \(r = 5\,\text{m}\) and \(\theta = 1.2\,\text{rad}\).
  5. A sector has arc length \(9\,\text{cm}\) and radius \(6\,\text{cm}\). Find \(\theta\) in radians.
  6. A point on a circle of radius \(3\,\text{m}\) moves through \(2\,\text{rad}\) in \(4\,\text{s}\). Find the angular speed and linear speed.
  7. Explain why \(s = r\theta\) only works when \(\theta\) is in radians.

For marking: include units; leave answers in terms of \(\pi\) when exact values are requested.
