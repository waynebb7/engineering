Boolean Algebra

Postgraduate guide. Boolean algebras as mathematical structures, Stone duality, filters, free algebras, and complete lattices.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Boolean algebras as algebraic structures

A Boolean algebra is a six-tuple \((B, \vee, \wedge, \neg, 0, 1)\) where \((B, \vee, \wedge)\) is a distributive lattice with complement \(\neg\) satisfying \(a \vee \neg a = 1\) and \(a \wedge \neg a = 0\).

Equivalent definition: A Boolean algebra is a bounded distributive lattice in which every element has a complement. The two-element algebra \(\{0,1\}\) and power-set algebras \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) are fundamental examples.
Boolean ring: Define \(a + b = a \triangle b = (a \wedge \neg b) \vee (\neg a \wedge b)\) and \(ab = a \wedge b\). Every Boolean ring satisfies \(x^2 = x\); conversely, such rings yield Boolean algebras.

2) Stone representation theorem

Stone's theorem (1936) states that every Boolean algebra is isomorphic to an algebra of clopen (closed and open) subsets of a compact, totally disconnected Hausdorff space — its Stone space.

Contravariant duality: The category of Boolean algebras is equivalent to the opposite of the category of Stone spaces. Homomorphisms correspond to continuous maps in the reverse direction.
Example: The Boolean algebra \(\mathcal{P}(\{1,2,3\})\) has Stone space the discrete three-point space. Filters correspond to ultrafilter convergence — a bridge to topology and logic.
Significance: Stone duality connects algebra, topology, and propositional logic in a precise categorical framework.

3) Filters, ultrafilters, and ultraproducts

A filter on a Boolean algebra \(B\) is a subset \(F \subseteq B\) closed upward, closed under finite meets, and with \(0 \notin F\). A ultrafilter is a maximal filter.

Ultrafilter lemma: Every filter extends to an ultrafilter (equivalent to the Boolean Prime Ideal Theorem, weaker than AC). Ultrafilters on \(\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})\) underpin non-standard analysis.
Stone space correspondence: Ultrafilters on \(B\) are in bijection with points of the Stone space of \(B\). Homomorphisms \(B \to \{0,1\}\) correspond to ultrafilters.

4) Free Boolean algebras

The free Boolean algebra on a set \(S\) is the algebra of Boolean terms in variables from \(S\), modulo the identities of Boolean algebra. It satisfies a universal mapping property.

Structure: For finite \(|S| = n\), the free algebra on \(n\) generators has \(2^{2^n}\) elements — all Boolean functions \(f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}\).
Clauses and terms: Every element is represented both as a disjunction of conjunctive clauses (DNF) and as a conjunction of disjunctive clauses (CNF). See Karnaugh Mapping for minimisation.

5) Complete Boolean algebras

A Boolean algebra is complete if every subset has a supremum and infimum (arbitrary joins and meets). Complete Boolean algebras model propositional logic with infinite conjunctions and disjunctions.

Examples: \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) is complete. The algebra of regular open sets of a topological space forms a complete Boolean algebra under suitable operations.
Complete homomorphisms: A map preserving arbitrary joins and meets is a complete homomorphism. Stone duality extends to complete Boolean algebras and extremally disconnected compact Hausdorff spaces.

6) Duality, topology, and forcing

Stone duality has deep consequences in logic and set theory. Forcing in set theory uses complete Boolean algebras to construct generic extensions; the Boolean-valued model \(\mathbf{V}^B\) interprets ZFC in a complete Boolean algebra \(B\).

Logic

Propositional logic, completeness, and compactness connect to Boolean algebra via Lindenbaum–Tarski algebras of theories.

Digital design

Two-element homomorphisms evaluate circuits. Minimisation of DNF/CNF is optimisation over the free algebra — see Karnaugh Mapping.
