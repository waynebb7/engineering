Fourier Series

Undergraduate guide. Periodic functions, Fourier coefficients, Parseval's theorem, odd/even extensions, and applications.

← Pure Math Subjects Catalog

1) Periodic functions and period

A function \(f(x)\) is periodic with period \(T > 0\) if \(f(x + T) = f(x)\) for all \(x\) in its domain. The fundamental period is the smallest positive \(T\) with this property.

Examples: \(\sin x\) has period \(2\pi\); a square wave toggling every \(\pi\) has period \(2\pi\). Engineering signals (AC waveforms, clock signals, PWM) are often modelled as periodic over one cycle.

Key idea: Knowing one period \([-T/2,\, T/2]\) or \([0,\, T]\) fully determines the function everywhere. Fourier series exploits this by expanding \(f\) in harmonics of \(\dfrac{2\pi}{T}\).
Worked example: \(f(x) = x\) on \((-\pi,\, \pi]\), extended periodically with period \(2\pi\). This produces a sawtooth wave: linear rise, then jump discontinuities at odd multiples of \(\pi\).

Builds on Trigonometry and Integration.

2) Fourier series definition

A \(2\pi\)-periodic function \(f(x)\) can be represented (under suitable conditions) by: \[ f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\bigl(a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx\bigr) \] For general period \(T\), use \(\omega = \dfrac{2\pi}{T}\) and replace \(\cos nx\) with \(\cos(n\omega x)\), \(\sin nx\) with \(\sin(n\omega x)\).

Terminology:
\(a_0/2\) — DC (average) component.
\(a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx\) — \(n\)th harmonic (frequency \(n\) times the fundamental).
Complex form: \(f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{inx}\) with \(c_n = \dfrac{a_n - jb_n}{2}\).
Convergence note: At jump discontinuities, the series converges to the midpoint of the jump (Dirichlet conditions). At points of continuity, the series equals \(f(x)\).

3) Computing Fourier coefficients

Over one period \([-\pi,\, \pi]\) (or any interval of length \(2\pi\)): \[ a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\,dx, \quad a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos nx\,dx, \quad b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin nx\,dx \]

Worked example: Square wave: \(f(x) = 1\) on \((0,\, \pi)\), \(f(x) = -1\) on \((-\pi,\, 0)\).
\(a_0 = 0\) (odd average over symmetric interval — actually zero by symmetry).
\(a_n = 0\) for all \(n\) (odd function).
\(b_n = \dfrac{2}{n\pi}(1 - (-1)^n)\): non-zero only for odd \(n\). So \(f(x) = \dfrac{4}{\pi}\bigl(\sin x + \tfrac{1}{3}\sin 3x + \tfrac{1}{5}\sin 5x + \cdots\bigr)\).
Shortcut: If \(f\) is even, only cosine terms (\(b_n = 0\)). If \(f\) is odd, only sine terms (\(a_n = 0\), \(a_0 = 0\)).

4) Odd and even extensions

A function defined only on \([0,\, L]\) can be extended to a full period by choosing symmetry:

Even extension

Reflect about \(x = 0\): \(f(-x) = f(x)\). Produces a cosine series (half-range cosine expansion on \([0,L]\)).

Odd extension

Reflect with sign flip: \(f(-x) = -f(x)\). Produces a sine series (half-range sine expansion).

Worked example: \(f(x) = x\) on \([0,\, \pi]\), odd extension to \([-\pi,\,\pi]\).
Sine series only: \(b_n = \dfrac{2}{n}(-1)^{n+1}\).
\(f(x) = 2\bigl(\sin x - \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2x + \tfrac{1}{3}\sin 3x - \cdots\bigr)\) on \([0,\,\pi]\).

Half-range expansions are standard in solving PDEs (heat equation, vibrating string) with boundary conditions at fixed ends.

5) Parseval's theorem

Parseval's theorem relates the energy of a signal in time (or space) to the energy in frequency components: \[ \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |f(x)|^2\,dx = \frac{a_0^2}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(a_n^2 + b_n^2) \]

Physical meaning: Total power in a periodic waveform equals the sum of powers in the DC term and each harmonic. No energy is lost — it is redistributed across the spectrum.
Worked example: For the square wave above, \(\dfrac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}1^2\,dx = 2\).
Series energy: \(\sum_{k\,\text{odd}} \dfrac{16}{k^2\pi^2} = \dfrac{16}{\pi^2}\bigl(1 + \tfrac{1}{9} + \tfrac{1}{25} + \cdots\bigr) = \dfrac{16}{\pi^2}\cdot\dfrac{\pi^2}{8} = 2\). ✓
Common mistake: Forgetting the factor \(\dfrac{a_0^2}{2}\) (not \(a_0^2\)) in the DC term when applying Parseval.

6) Standard series and harmonic content

Waveform (period \(2\pi\)) Series (main terms) Harmonic decay
Square wave (odd) \(\dfrac{4}{\pi}\sum \dfrac{1}{n}\sin nx\), \(n\) odd \(1/n\)
Triangle wave (odd) \(\dfrac{8}{\pi^2}\sum \dfrac{(-1)^{(n-1)/2}}{n^2}\sin nx\), \(n\) odd \(1/n^2\)
Full-wave rectified cosine Cosine series, even harmonics only \(1/n^2\)
Sawtooth \(f(x)=x\) \(2\sum \dfrac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin nx\) \(1/n\)
Engineering insight: Smooth waveforms (triangle) decay faster (\(1/n^2\)) — less high-frequency content, lower EMI. Sharp edges (square, sawtooth) need many harmonics for accurate reconstruction.

7) Engineering applications

AC power and harmonics

Non-linear loads (rectifiers, inverters) draw non-sinusoidal current. Fourier analysis identifies harmonic distortion and guides filter design.

Signal processing

Fourier series is the discrete-frequency precursor to the Fourier transform. Sampling and FFT extend these ideas to digital systems.

Heat and vibration

Separable solutions to PDEs yield spatial Fourier sine/cosine series for temperature and displacement fields.

Antenna and EMC

Periodic switching waveforms radiate at harmonic frequencies. Truncating the series estimates far-field contributions.

Related: Trig Functions, Integration, Calculus (Electrical Engineering).
